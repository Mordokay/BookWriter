# The Forest Inside

*A novel by Pedro Saldanha*

---

# Chapter 1: Threshold

A bird called once from somewhere high in the Douglas fir canopy: a single sustained note, hollow and metallic, like a finger drawn along the rim of a wet glass. It hung in the dusk air and thinned and was gone. The forest filled the space it left with everything else: the tick of condensation sliding from fern tips, the chitter of bats stitching the air above a canopy gap, the soft percussion of something small and unseen moving through salal.

Ada locked the car. The click of the mechanism was sharp and foreign against the layered quiet of the trailhead. She shouldered her pack: water bottle, lighter, rain jacket, car keys. She walked toward the trail without looking back at the parking lot. The lot was empty. Late autumn, a Tuesday, this far from any town. She had not expected otherwise.

Her phone was on the passenger seat. Not forgotten. Placed. Face down, powered off, centered on the leather with the deliberateness of someone setting a table for one. She had decided this days ago. No signal out here anyway, but that was not the point. The point was the leash, and tonight she was cutting it. Or that was how she had framed it to herself on the drive up, hands at ten and two, jaw set against the small voice suggesting she was making a mistake.

The maintained trail was compacted earth and scattered gravel, signposted with distances she did not read. Douglas fir trunks rose on either side in thick-barked columns that vanished upward into a canopy she could no longer see against the dimming sky. Two hundred and fifty feet, maybe more. She knew their Latin name, could recite the taxonomy from memory. She had prepared for this the way she prepared for everything: thoroughly, clinically, as if knowledge were a kind of armor and enough of it could make the unknown safe.

At the fork she took the left path, the narrower one, where boot prints in the soft earth were older and fewer. Ferns closed in on both sides, fronds nearly as tall as her waist, each one a precise green blade furred with spore cases on its underside. Moss covered every horizontal surface: the stones, the fallen wood, the low crooks of bigleaf maple branches that sagged and bent under the weight of it. The moss was the color of old jade in the failing light. Dense, damp, unhurried. It covered things like time covers things. Without effort. Without opinion.

The fallen log was where she remembered it, more or less. A Douglas fir trunk three feet in diameter, fallen and half-consumed by its own becoming. Hemlock seedlings grew from its spine in a row of dark green spires. Bracket fungi, horse-hoof shaped and hard as wood, climbed the near flank in ascending tiers. Moss sealed every crack and wound. The log had been dying and living in equal measure for decades, and it did both without complaint.

Ada sat down. The bark was damp beneath her palms, spongy with rot. She swung the backpack to the ground between her boots and unzipped it. The sound of the zipper was loud in the layered quiet. She reached inside.

The mushrooms were in a small paper bag, folded twice with precise creases. She opened it. A handful of dried psilocybin mushrooms: stems and caps, the color of burnt caramel. She picked one up between thumb and forefinger. The cap was wavy, curled at its edges, tinged with a faint blue where the active compound had met the air. A chemical fact she had read about and was now confirming. She noted the confirmation. It steadied her.

She ate them slowly. The texture was between cork and raw pasta, dry and fibrous. The taste was bitter, earthy, with a faint metallic undertone that lingered at the back of her tongue. She chewed with deliberate thoroughness, washing each mouthful down with water. The bottle was half full when she capped it and set it back in the pack. She folded the paper bag and set it on the moss beside the log's base.

Through the gap in the canopy overhead, the first stars had appeared, faint, in a strip of blue so deep it was almost violet. The moon was up already, nearly full, she had checked, but the canopy hid it.

Twenty to forty minutes for onset. She could sit with twenty to forty minutes.

She pressed her lips together and scanned the clearing. The inventory came without effort: the log beneath her, the ring of ferns, the wall of trunks at the clearing's edge, the darkness gathering between them. Insects had begun their night chorus: a layered pulse she could not parse into species. The click-buzz of beetles, a thin whine rising and falling, and beneath both something lower and continuous she felt in her teeth. Moisture dripped from a fern at irregular intervals, each drop landing on a leaf below with a soft percussive tap. The air smelled of wet stone and damp bark, underlaid with the sweet fungal scent of wood returning to earth. She catalogued all of it. She was having the experience correctly.

Then the toad.

It was sitting on the log, less than two feet from her right hand. It had not made a sound. It was simply there, as if it had been there longer than she had, as if she had sat down beside it without looking.

It was large. The size of her fist, maybe larger. A Western toad. She recognized the species from her research. Its skin was rough with warts, mottled olive-brown and gold, with a pale cream stripe running the length of its back. It sat with its body pressed flat to the bark, heavy and low, with the settled weight of a stone that has occupied its position since before anyone thought to measure time. It did not move. It did not blink. Its eyes were gold, bisected by horizontal pupils, and they were watching her with an expression she had no name for. Something older than curiosity or wariness.

A flicker passed through her chest, below alarm, below recognition, something that lived below language. The toad's stillness was so complete it had its own gravity. She stared at it. It regarded her. The forest's evening sounds continued around them both, indifferent.

Ada looked up at the canopy, at the strip of sky overhead, where the first stars had appeared, faint, precise, each one a cold point in a field of deepening indigo. When she looked back, the log beside her hand was bare. The bark was slightly depressed where the toad had sat, dark with moisture. She ran her fingers across the spot. Damp. Cool. Nothing to confirm what she had seen except the faint impression of weight in soft wood.

She filed it away. Western toads were common in Pacific Northwest old growth. Large specimens were unremarkable. There was nothing unusual about a toad on a log at dusk. She filed the observation and the filing did not work. The gold eyes, patient and unhurried, settled somewhere beneath her inventory and stayed.

The stars multiplied. The strip of sky between the canopy became a dense scatter of light, and then the canopy itself dissolved into darkness and the sky was gone. The temperature dropped. Ada pulled her jacket closer around her ribs and pressed her back against the log.

She breathed. She waited. She counted her breaths and then stopped counting because counting was another form of managing and she had come here to stop managing, hadn't she? She had. That was the plan. She breathed without counting and the breaths came anyway, which should not have been a revelation but briefly, strangely, was.

It began with the moss.

The moss on the log beside her hand grew more vivid. Not brighter, the light was failing, but more present, the resolution on a single patch of the world refined past what her eyes were built for. Individual strands of moss stood distinct, each one beaded with moisture, green shading to gold shading to a deep copper she had not seen before. The texture deepened until the patch of moss was no longer a surface but a landscape: ridges and valleys and miniature forests within the forest, each droplet of water a small clear world reflecting the dark above.

The moss was breathing. Not actually breathing. The surface rose and fell in slow, shallow waves, expanding and contracting the way a sleeping animal's ribs move. She watched it. She noted it. This is onset, said the voice in her head. Visual cortex becomes hyperactive. Surfaces appear to breathe. This is normal. This is expected.

The bark of the log shifted next. The furrows deepened, the ridges sharpened, the pattern of decay became a topography she could have mapped with her fingers: beetle tracks carved in winding lines where the bark had peeled away, the grain of the wood flowing in currents that had taken a century to form. A shelf of bracket fungus she had glanced at earlier pulsed with a slow interior rhythm, a heartbeat inside the woody flesh keeping time.

Then the ferns. They swayed, though no wind stirred. The fronds moved with the lazy precision of kelp in a current, tips curling and uncurling in sequences that repeated but never exactly. The canopy above, what she could see of it through the clearing's gap, had receded. Three hundred feet. Four hundred. The Douglas fir trunks rose into darkness like columns in a cathedral designed by an intelligence that planned in centuries and did not rush.

Colors saturated. The green of the moss became a green she had no word for. A green beyond bright or neon: true. Every green she had seen before had been a diluted print, and this was the original.

Along a fallen log at the clearing's edge, a faint light pulsed.

She stood. The motion came before the decision, her body moving toward the light while her mind was still annotating. Jack-o'-lantern mushrooms clustered along the log's underside, caps the size of small hands, gills catching their own bioluminescence and casting it back. The glow was faint, the color of an ember seen through green glass. In daylight she would have passed without notice. Here, in the full dark, it was the only light the forest offered.

She crouched beside the log. The mushrooms smelled sweet, almost fruity, underlaid with the deep fungal note of rotting wood. The light was chemical, an enzyme reaction in the gill tissue. She knew this, had read the papers, and the knowledge was supposed to make it ordinary. It did not. The glow pulsed with a rhythm she could nearly match to the blood in her wrists.

On her feet again. The forest beyond the clearing was a wall of shapes, fern fronds, trunks, the broken silhouettes of dead branches, and the margins of every shape were softening. Not blurring. Loosening. The outlines of things no longer fully committed to where they had been drawn.

Her skin prickled at the back of her neck and along her forearms, a fine electric awareness that was not unpleasant. The insect chorus had deepened, the individual species separating into distinct layers: beetles clicking on one layer, the thin whine threading above, and beneath it all, a low drone she felt in her jaw more than she heard. A bat flickered overhead, its echolocation a rapid ticking at the edge of hearing. The air tasted different. Mineral and clean, with a sweetness she associated with rain that had not yet arrived.

This is normal, said the voice in her head, but the voice was speaking from a room she was walking out of, and the door between them was swinging shut.

She could not sit still.

The impulse arrived complete: she needed to walk. Sitting on the log felt passive, and passive felt like waiting, and waiting felt like yielding the only thing she still held: the ability to move, to choose, to go somewhere on purpose. She was not ready to yield. She shouldered her backpack and tightened the straps, a small, practiced gesture, the body executing its checklist while the mind was already walking. She stepped past the ferns at the clearing's edge and into the forest.

The ferns brushed her jeans as she passed through them, their fronds cool and damp against the denim. The ground beneath her boots was soft, a thick carpet of decomposing needles and moss that swallowed each footfall. She walked with purpose, though she could not have named what she was walking toward. Deeper. The forest was deeper ahead and she moved into it, and the moving felt necessary. Halfway between chosen and automatic.

The air changed as she left the clearing. Denser, wetter, carrying the sweet-rot scent of fungal decay mixed with something sharper: cedar resin, cold and sharp. Hemlock boughs brushed her shoulders where the understory closed in, their needles leaving faint damp traces on her jacket sleeves. She told herself she was exploring. Just a walk. Just the forest at night, the onset settling in, the experience unfolding as she had planned. The voice delivering these assurances had grown thin, though. It was narrating from a distance, and the distance was widening.

A western red cedar loomed to her left, its trunk wide enough that three of her could not have circled it with arms outstretched. The bark was fibrous, shredding in long vertical strips. She trailed her fingers across it as she passed, and the texture under her skin was so detailed, so ridged and furrowed, that her hand was reading something written there in a language she almost recognized.

The canopy had closed. What moonlight existed arrived in narrow shafts that shifted as the branches shifted, laying silver rectangles on the ground that vanished before she could step into them. She navigated instead by bioluminescence, faint clusters of glowing fungi on downed wood, scattered like a trail of low green embers. Each cluster gave just enough light to find the next footfall. Not the one after that.

Time had gone soft at its edges. She did not trust her sense of duration, and the distrust carried no alarm. Just a loosening, a slippage she could not grip. The moss she stepped on left no footprints. The ferns she parted swung closed behind her. She passed through the forest and the forest sealed itself in her wake: present at the point of contact, gone from every point before.

Then she stopped.

Something was wrong. Not wrong like danger; no sound, no movement, no prickling instinct at the base of her skull. Wrong like geometry. A line that should be straight and was not. Her jaw tightened. She had been walking more or less straight, following a gentle downward slope between the trunks. The clearing with the fallen log should have been behind her, uphill, reachable by turning and retracing.

She turned.

The forest behind her was flat. The slope she had walked down was gone. The ground stretched level between trunks she did not recognize, bigleaf maple draped in curtains of moss, not the Douglas fir she had passed through. The ferns were different: taller, denser, arrayed in formations she was certain she had not parted. A fallen log lay across the ground twenty feet back, but it was the wrong one. Smaller, covered in red huckleberry rather than hemlock seedlings, oriented the wrong direction.

She walked toward it. Her hand found the nearest trunk. She pressed her palm flat against the bark, reading the texture. Smooth, peeling in papery strips. Alder, not Douglas fir. She had not walked past a single alder. Her fingers curled against the bark and pulled away.

The forest around her did not match anything she had walked through. She turned in a slow circle, scanning for the canopy gap where the stars had been visible from the clearing. The canopy was sealed. Every direction was dark trunks, fern walls, the faint scattered glow of fungi she did not remember passing. She looked for the jack-o'-lantern mushrooms she had been following, the trail of low green embers, and found none. Different fungi glowed here, smaller and paler, a blue-green she had not seen before.

Her hands were fists. She had not decided to make them fists.

Her mind reached for explanations and found them. Spatial disorientation was expected; psilocybin disrupts the brain's internal compass, its sense of grid and direction, makes distance and bearing unreliable. She had read this. Had prepared for this. She had walked farther than she realized, or turned without noticing, or the slope had been gentler than she perceived. These were reasonable explanations. She held them like a railing on a staircase that had begun to sway.

But the trees were wrong. The ground was wrong. The undergrowth pressed closer on all sides, as if the space between trunks had quietly narrowed while her back was turned. She spun again. Every direction looked the same, and every direction looked like the first time she had seen it.

The light had not changed; the scattered bioluminescence still pulsed in its faint, unhurried way. The sounds were the same: the insect chorus continued, undisturbed, and somewhere overhead a barred owl released its low rhythmic call. Temperature, smell, every single element she could isolate and name held steady.

What had changed was the forest itself. The arrangement of it. The fact of it. The woods behind her had quietly, without haste or hostility, rearranged themselves while she walked, and now stood in their new positions with the settled certainty of things that had always been exactly where they were.

The voice in her head offered one more explanation: she had moved, not the forest, and she was disoriented and needed to stop and breathe and get her bearings. She reached for it, and the explanation dissolved in her hands like wet paper.

Ada stood in the dark, surrounded by trees she had not passed, on ground she had not walked, in a forest that had closed behind her the way water closes over a dropped stone.

The ferns did not move. The insects kept their layered song. A single drop of condensation fell from a branch above and landed on the back of her neck, cold, precise, and traced a line down her spine beneath her shirt.

She did not move.

The forest breathed around her, slow and vast and patient, and waited for what she would do next.

# Chapter 2: Swarm

She was counting trees.

Forty-three paces north, or what she judged to be north, though the moon had vanished behind the canopy and she had no compass and the word "north" was beginning to feel like a sound she was making with no referent. Forty-three paces, and the Douglas fir with the split trunk she had passed should be on her left. It was not on her left. It was not on her right. There was no split trunk anywhere in the darkness around her.

The voice in her head had not stopped since the trail vanished. It had only shifted register, from clinical notation to operational planning, running through solutions the way a machine runs through diagnostics when it has been designed to solve and cannot be told there is nothing to solve. Turn ninety degrees right. Walk straight. Count paces. Grid the area. The clearing was here. Clearings did not move.

She turned ninety degrees right. She walked. She counted.

The undergrowth thickened with each step, ferns pressing higher against her thighs, salal closing in, its leathery leaves dragging against her jacket with a sound like damp paper being drawn across skin. At one hundred and twelve paces the counting stopped because there was nothing left to count toward. The clearing was not here. Nothing was here except more forest, identical and indifferent, the pale scattered glow of luminous fungi marking the positions of fallen branches she had never seen.

Her fingers found the backpack straps and tightened on them. Behind her, the forest she had just walked through was wrong again. Not the same wrong as before, a new wrong, differently arranged. The bigleaf maple she had passed at step sixty was gone. In its place stood a cluster of young hemlocks that reached her shoulder, their needles beaded with moisture that caught the faint fungal light and held it in pale green constellations.

Okay. Okay. The psilocybin was disrupting spatial memory. Her brain's sense of direction, misfiring. The forest looked the same because old-growth forests look the same: similar species, similar spacing, similar scale. She was not lost in a changing forest. She was lost in a constant forest and her perception was unreliable.

The voice was still clinical. The voice was still making sense. But it was talking faster now, and under the sense there was a current she did not want to name. Her feet had stopped moving while the voice ran. She was standing in the dark between two fallen logs, both furred thick with moss, the moss itself dotted with tiny ferns that had rooted into the decay. The air was cold on the exposed skin of her wrists. Somewhere nearby, water dripped with the slow regularity of a metronome, a single drop striking a leaf or a hollow in wood, marking intervals of a time she could no longer measure.

The moon. She tried the moon. Through a narrow gap in the canopy far above, the first she had found in minutes, a sliver of the waxing gibbous was visible, milky and diminished by a thin veil of cloud. She stared at it. Waxing gibbous, late autumn, rises mid-afternoon, so by now it would be... what time was it? After sunset. After onset. After the trail. The moon was there, in that gap, which meant...

The calculation would not complete. The moon's position required knowing the approximate hour, which required a sense of elapsed time, which the psilocybin had dissolved into something without edges. Each step of the calculation generated two new unknowns, and each unknown generated two more, branching and branching, and her mind was running them all simultaneously, the decision tree expanding in every direction until it was not a tree but a thicket, a tangle of its own, and she could not find the trunk beneath all the branches.

Her eyes dropped from the gap. The gap was gone. The canopy had sealed above her while she was looking through it.

Fingertips pressed into her temples, she stood still. The voice offered three possible directions toward the trailhead, each supported by different assumptions about where the moon had been, each contradicted by the spatial evidence around her. Choosing required data she did not have, and the absence of data was producing not paralysis but acceleration, her mind spinning faster in the void, generating hypotheses and discarding them in the same breath, the cognitive equivalent of wheels on ice.

The paper bag was on the ground at the base of a Sitka spruce, half-hidden beneath a fern frond. Folded twice with precise creases. The paper was damp, dark at the edges where it touched the earth, but the folds were exactly as she had made them. She crouched and picked it up. The mushroom bag. She had set it on the moss beside the fallen log in the clearing, and the clearing was... she turned, scanning the darkness. Nowhere. She had been walking away from the clearing for however long she had been walking. The bag should not be here.

Both hands held it. The paper was cold and damp against her fingers. Her mind reached for an explanation: she had walked in a circle, she was near the clearing without recognizing it, the bag had been moved by an animal, but there was no clearing here, and the forest around her looked nothing like it, and the explanation collapsed before it finished forming, a structure with no foundation.

The bag went back where she had found it. She stood. Her jaw ached from clenching and she made herself open it, working the hinge until the tension released, and the release did not help because the tension was not in her jaw. It was in the spaces between her thoughts.

The voice offered one more plan: pick a direction. Any direction. Walk straight. Eventually the forest has edges. Every forest has edges.

A direction. Any direction. She walked, and the walking was its own dim answer.

The undergrowth grew denser. Ferns gave way to salal pressing close on both sides, stems she had to push through with her forearms. The leaves were slick, waxy, and they parted with a rubbery resistance before springing back behind her, slapping against the backpack as she passed. The darkness here had texture: not the open darkness of the forest behind her but something closer, thicker, a darkness that pressed against her face like humidity. Every few steps her boot would catch on something invisible beneath the fern cover: a root, a branch, the hidden topography of a forest floor that had been building its own archaeology for centuries, layer on decomposing layer.

Then deadfall: a Douglas fir trunk so large it reached her waist, bark furrowed deep, blocking the way forward. Her boots found purchase on a knot of wood as she climbed it. Her jeans caught on a stub of branch at the right knee and she yanked free, a rip, a sound of tearing denim, and kept moving. On the other side the ground dropped into a shallow depression and her boots sank into cold mud. It sucked at her soles with each step, a sound like mouths opening and closing. Hauling herself up the far side required hands gripping roots, fingers digging into wet earth, forearms bracing against the slope. Mud packed beneath her fingernails, cold and grainy. The roots were slick with a thin film of algae, and her grip slipped twice before she found a hold that would bear her weight: a thick cedar root, rough-barked, angled like a rung.

The air smelled different here. Sweeter, heavier, the fungal decay thickened to something almost cloying, the scent of wood so far past death it had become its own kind of life. Spores in the air coated her tongue, metallic and sweet. The bioluminescence had shifted, the orange-green glow of the jack-o'-lantern mushrooms replaced by a paler, cooler light. Tiny delicate mushrooms, caps no wider than her thumbnail, glowing a faint green, clustered on fallen branches and scattered across the leaf litter in pale green points. Their glow turned the undergrowth into a web of faint light and deep shadow, and in the shadows the shapes of things were uncertain. A stump that could have been an animal. A root arch that could have been a doorway. A hanging mass of stair-step moss on a standing dead tree that swayed without wind.

The ground beneath her boots had changed, too. Softer, more yielding. Not the firm leaf-litter of the upper forest but a deep accumulation of decaying matter that compressed with each step and did not fully spring back. Her footprints stayed behind her, dark depressions that slowly pooled with water seeping up from below. The undergrowth here grew not from soil but from the bodies of older plants, the living rooted in the dead, and the psilocybin made her aware of this in a way that bypassed thought and went straight to her feet, each step landing on something that had once been alive and was now the ground for something else that was alive and would, in its turn, become ground for something else. It went all the way down. There was no bottom that was not also a surface.

The voice was narrating this, cataloging species, assigning threat levels, filing each detail into the same organizational structure it had been using since the trail vanished. But the narration was growing thin, a commentary track that no longer matched the images it was describing. The forest was producing details faster than the voice could categorize them.

And then, in one cluster of glowing mushrooms, a dozen caps had fruited along a curved branch in a line so precise and evenly spaced they looked like notes ascending a musical staff. The bioluminescence in each cap pulsed at its own faint rhythm, slightly offset from the others, so the light appeared to travel: a slow, liquid cascade from the lowest cap to the highest and back, a wave of pale green fire breathing along the branch. It was, for one unguarded moment, beautiful. Not the beauty of something safe. The beauty of something so perfectly organized by forces that had nothing to do with her that her mind went quiet in front of it, the sudden hush of a loud room when a single clear note sounds. The light cascaded. The caps pulsed. Her breath slowed by a single beat.

Then a hanging strand of old-man's-beard lichen brushed her cheek and the moment broke.

Bracket fungi grew on the dead trunks in tiers so high she had to tilt her head to see where they ended, and the tilting made the dark canopy reel above her. She stopped tilting. She kept walking. The voice was still talking but its sentences were shorter now, the clinical vocabulary wearing thin, the diagnostic giving way to clipped instruction barked at a body that was not obeying fast enough.

Find a slope. Downhill leads to water. Water leads to trails. That was a survival-manual fact. That had the weight of a fact she had not invented, a wall she could lean against.

She looked for a slope. The ground was level in every direction.

Come on. Think. Find a landmark. Find something distinctive. Find something you have seen before, which was nothing, because everything was new, because the forest was new, every direction the first time she was seeing it, and the map in her head was blank, a white page that her mind kept trying to write on and the ink kept running off the edge...

The first moth arrived.

It drifted past her face, slow, erratic, wings catching the pale green glow as it passed. A large moth, furry-bodied, antennae like feathered combs. A flinch, reflex, nothing more. Then a second moth from the other direction, brushing her hair. A hand raised to wave it away. A third. And then not three, not five, but many. A dozen, two dozen, emerging from the undergrowth as if the dark itself were shedding layers, each one carrying its own faint green charge.

Then the mosquitoes. The whine arrived before they did, thin, metallic, threading into her hearing from all sides at once. Not one mosquito. Hundreds. Impossible for late autumn, wrong for the season, but the wrongness registered only as a flicker before the sheer number overwhelmed it. The individual whines layered into a drone that shifted pitch as she turned her head. They were on her neck, on the backs of her hands, on her face. The pinpoint touch of legs, the brush of needle-mouths against skin. They did not bite. They landed and stayed, each one a point of contact so slight it should have been negligible, but the accumulation was not negligible; it was a second skin assembling itself on top of hers, alive and vibrating.

The beetles came next, clicking. Invisible in the dark, but the percussive clicking wove into the mosquito drone and the moth wing-flutter until the sound was no longer a collection of species but a single complex noise, a texture with presence and weight. It surrounded her. It thickened. The air itself gained density.

Under the psilocybin, the swarm changed.

It happened in stages, each one a further departure from what the clinical voice would have called baseline perception. Individual insects were still identifiable, a moth, a crane fly, a beetle, but the spaces between them pulsed with faint light, as if the swarm had its own bioluminescence. It moved as a fluid, tightening and loosening in patterns she could almost predict and never quite could. A crane fly drifted past her face with its legs trailing like threads, slow enough to seem deliberate. Moths orbited her head in elliptical paths that tightened when she moved and loosened when she stood still. The swarm breathed. It had a rhythm. It was not random.

And the drone was developing harmonics. Overtones that had not been there before, tones too complex for the sum of insect wings and insect bodies, climbing into frequencies that vibrated in her sinuses and behind her eyes. The harmonics layered and shifted, almost resolving into something structured, something with...

Almost words. Almost speech. Fragments. Syllables assembling at the edge of recognition and dissolving before they solidified. The fragments had a cadence she knew. They had a rhythm. They sounded like...

...what if you can't...

A hand swatted at the air around her head. The swarm tightened. Closer. Louder.

...which way which way which...

The fragments were not the swarm's voice. They were hers. Her cadence. The anxious inner monologue she had been running since the trail vanished, the cascading what-ifs and failed calculations, the branching uncertainties, reflected back to her from a hundred small vibrating bodies, distorted into something alien but unmistakably hers. The swarm was not speaking to her. It was speaking as her.

...too late too late you should have...

"Stop," she said. The word came out thin, a sound without authority. The swarm did not stop. The drone intensified. The fragments multiplied, overlapping, arriving faster than she could not-hear them.

...wrong direction wrong direction...

Her palms pressed against her ears. The drone changed but did not diminish; it traveled through her skull bones, through her clenched jaw, arriving at her inner ear by a route her hands could not block. The moths were in her hair. Wings against her scalp, the tiny grip of legs. The mosquitoes covered her forearms, her neck, the patch of skin between her collar and her hairline. They did not bite. They did not need to.

Her breathing had gone wrong. Short bursts that fogged in the cold air. Her breath hung visible in the green glow, the clouds of it merging with the swarm, her lungs feeding the cloud, the distinction between inside and outside dissolving in a way that was nothing like the gentle onset loosening, but frantic, recursive, a system feeding back into itself and amplifying with each cycle.

...you thought you could control this you thought...

The voice in her head and the swarm's fragments were running in parallel now, indistinguishable. Which thoughts were hers and which were reflections; the question had no answer. The swarm was her mind heard from the outside. There was no way to turn the volume down on both.

She ran.

Not a decision. A detonation. One moment kneeling, the next upright and moving, her legs finding a gear they had not been asked for, every muscle firing in the single coordinated panic of an animal that has stopped thinking about where to go and is now just going.

Crashing through the salal with her forearms raised in front of her face, branches snapping against her jacket, stems whipping past. The swarm kept pace. The drone did not diminish; if anything it tightened, compressed by her speed into a tighter weave, the fragments merging into a continuous anxious hum that matched the pounding of her feet. She could not outrun something that was everywhere.

A root caught her boot and her ankle rolled but did not give, and the stumble carried her sideways into a hemlock trunk. Bark against her shoulder, rough even through the jacket. The impact sent a jolt through her collarbone and into the base of her skull. She pushed off and kept running. The forest streaked past in vertical dark columns with pale green smears of bioluminescence between them, the small glowing mushrooms blurring into brushstrokes of light. Her lungs burned. Cold air tore at her throat on each inhale, too fast, too shallow, the kind of breathing that fed the panic instead of starving it, each gasp a signal to her body that something was chasing her, and her body believed the signal even though nothing was chasing her except the air itself.

The ground dipped without warning and her stride overextended, her leading foot landing hard on a downslope she had not seen. The shock traveled up through her knee and into her hip. She kept going. The terrain was fighting her now, every surface angled or soft or both, roots and rocks hidden beneath the fern cover, the forest floor an obstacle course designed by something that did not want her to run. # Chapter 5: Antlers

The voice returned in fragments. A reduced version, a tentative re-entry, like a clerk returning to a desk she wasn't sure still existed.

*The undergrowth has thinned. Clearings opening. Bioluminescence is different here. Brighter. Sustained.*

Ada walked. Her boots squelched with each step, stream water shifting between her toes, the socks long past any useful function. The rain jacket rustled against her soaked shirt. The clinical observations arrived with an even cadence she hadn't heard since before the swarm, each one placed like a fact in a report, precise and detached and useful. The voice was not strong, stammering at the edges, unable to complete a thought if the thought required too much, but it was cataloging again, and the cataloging was familiar, and familiar was warmth she leaned into without examining why. The shelf fungi pulsed on the dead trees, swelling and dimming. Ghost pipe clusters appeared at the bases of the largest cedars, bone-white and curving. The spore-taste thickened with each clearing she passed through, sweet and metallic, coating her throat. The clinical voice was right about all of it, and being right organized the world into a shape she could move through without asking what it meant.

She crossed a clearing where shelf fungi covered an entire fallen Douglas fir, their combined glow bright enough to cast faint shadows. Her own shadow among them, stretching and shrinking with the pulse. She did not pause. Her shoulders were square. Her chin was level. Her hands rested on the backpack straps. The rain jacket's collar pressed against her jaw, nylon against the ache of hours of clenching, an ache she could not release. Her spine was straight. Not straight from relaxation. Straight from the same sustained effort that had held it straight through every difficult meeting and every phone call she did not want to answer and every morning she rose from a bed she'd barely slept in and walked into a world that required her to be fine.

This was how she moved through the world. Upright. Observant. Prepared.

The fly agarics stood at the far edge of the clearing: two of them, red caps vivid against the green glow, white spots catching the fungal light. She had passed them before, at the boundaries between clearings, each time registering them as placed sentinels between one kind of space and another. She walked between them.

The air changed.

The mist thinned. The ambient sounds of the forest, the scattered insect clicks, the shelf fungi's barely audible breathing, the drip of moisture from high branches, pulled back by a degree. Not the predatory silence that had preceded the wolf. Something else. A hush. The quality of a room when someone important enters it and every head turns, not in fear but in recognition.

The clearing opened.

It was different from the others. The canopy had parted directly above, a gap wide enough that the sky was visible through it: dark, clear, the clouds that had carried the rain having thinned enough to let the moon through. The waxing gibbous sat in the opening, three-quarters full, its light falling in a column that flooded the clearing floor with silver. After hours of green darkness and the faint cold glow of fungi, the moonlight was a shock. Almost violent in its brightness. The clearing was perhaps forty feet across, carpeted in fern and moss, bordered on all sides by the dark trunks of old fir and cedar. At its edges, fly agarics stood in a broken ring: red caps with white spots, each one catching the silver light, each one a vivid warm interruption in the palette of silver and green. The caps were larger here. A foot across, some of them. Their red was saturated past anything the green glow could have produced: true red, a mark between the forest she had come through and whatever this space was.

She had walked onto a stage.

The thought was not the clinical voice's. It arrived from somewhere less organized, a recognition that bypassed analysis. The canopy had not opened by accident. The moonlight did not fall here by coincidence. The clearings she had passed through were practice rooms. This was the performance hall. The moss was the carpet. The fir trunks were the wings. The moon was the spotlight.

And something waited on the stage.

Ada stepped into the silver light. The cold hit her immediately. Without the canopy's protection, the temperature had dropped, and the moonlight carried no warmth. Her wet clothing caught the chill and held it. The rain jacket, thin and insufficient, blocked the mist but not the cold that came from every direction at once. Her body tightened against it: shoulders drawing up, muscles contracting, the shivering that had settled to a manageable rhythm on the walk threatening to return. She clenched her jaw harder. Breathed through her nose. Forced the shiver down.

*You're fine. Keep moving.*

Something stood at the far end of the clearing.

On a rise of ground, a ridge where the mossy earth lifted three or four feet above the clearing floor, where the ferns grew taller and the light silvered every surface, a shape. Enormous. Still. Ada's body registered it before the image resolved: the pulse of adrenaline, thin but specific, running through her chest and into her arms. The tightening along her ribs. The involuntary shift of weight to the balls of her feet. The alarm was quieter than the wolf's, almost formal, as if her nervous system recognized that this was not a threat to run from but a presence to stand before.

A smell reached her through the spore-sweetness. Sharp, territorial, layered beneath the moss and cedar resin. Animal musk, heavy and warm in the cold air, the scent of a large animal occupying a space it had claimed through sheer duration.

The stag.

A Roosevelt elk, standing on the ridge with the settled weight of something that had occupied its position long before she arrived and would occupy it long after she left. Massive through the shoulders, the body heavy, deep brown, the coat thick and winter-dense, each guard hair tipped with silver where the light caught it. The chest was broad. The legs planted wide, sunk into the mossy ridge, hooves invisible beneath the fern. The neck was thick, muscular, holding the head at an angle that was neither raised nor lowered but precisely, terribly level. The eyes were dark. Large, glassy, reflecting the moon in two small points of cold light. They held no expression she could classify. No curiosity, no hostility, no interest she could leverage. They simply looked.

The antlers.

Two main beams rose from the skull, thick at the base, curving upward and backward before splitting into tines. In the ordinary world, in the world before the mushrooms and the forest and the dissolution of the trail, the antlers would have been large enough: four, maybe five points on each side, the rack of a mature bull in his prime. But under the psilocybin, the antlers did not stop. Each tine split into two. Each split into two more. The branching continued at the edges of her perception, the structure multiplying upward and outward, fine as winter twigs at the tips, thick as her wrist at the base, until the antlers were no longer antlers but a canopy of their own: bone-colored, intricate, reaching into the moonlit air. The outer tips caught the silver light and held it in the branches, fragmenting and refracting, so the stag wore a crown of cold light that extended above and behind it like the branching of a leafless tree.

A crown. A cage. The same structure serving both functions, depending on who was wearing it.

The stag's breath was visible. Slow exhalations that steamed in the cold air, rising past the antlers and dissolving. Each breath was unhurried. Even. The rhythm of an animal that had never been rushed, that had stood on ridges and met the wind and the weather and the gaze of predators and rivals without adjusting its breathing by a single beat.

Ada recognized the breathing.

She recognized it because she was doing it. Her own breath came through her nose in careful, even draws, each one metered, held, the respiratory equivalent of her straightened spine and level chin. She was breathing the way she breathed in difficult meetings, when someone asked a question she did not want to answer, when the alternative was something she could not afford.

Neither of them moved.

The silence in the clearing was architectural. Built. Ada and the stag occupied it like two people in a room where something important has not yet been said. The shelf fungi at the borders pulsed their green heartbeat, steady and indifferent. The fly agarics held their positions. The mist hung. The moon lit the scene from above with equal, impartial intensity, shadowless and silver, falling on Ada's wet clothing and the stag's fur and the bone-white branches without preference. Beneath her feet, so faint it barely registered through her waterlogged boots, the mycelium hum continued its slow pulse. She was too tense to notice it.

Ada met the stag's gaze and held it. Her hands tightened on the backpack straps. Her jaw set. She planted her feet on the mossy clearing floor and stood. She stood the way she had always stood when she was being evaluated: straight, still, composed. The posture she had built for the world. The posture that said: *I am here. I am equal to this. I do not require your concern.*

The stag matched her. Exceeded her. The stag's composure was not a posture but a fact, not an effort but an inevitability. The difference between Ada's version and the stag's was the difference between a drawing of a mountain and the mountain itself.

The musk was thicker now, filling the clearing's lower air. The scent of a large animal at rest, warm and territorial and ancient, mixing with the spore-sweetness and the crushed-fern smell of the mossy ground. Ada's nostrils flared. Her mouth was dry, the sustained tension drawing moisture from her tongue, leaving the spore-taste metallic and flat.

"Is this who you are?"

The stag's jaw did not move. The dark muzzle remained closed, nostrils flaring slightly with each exhale, steam rising in slow clouds. But the words arrived, assembling in the moonlit space between them. The voice was cold. Formal. Each word placed with the precision of a chess piece by someone who has already seen the end of the game.

"Or who you've practiced being?"

The question settled into the clearing like a stone into still water. Ada's hands tightened on the straps. The right hand's web of spines flared, the swollen ridge burning as her fingers curled into the nylon. Both fists closed now, tight against the straps, the same clenching she had carried through the wolf and the swarm and the descent and the crossing. Fingers curling around nothing because there was nothing to hold.

She stood her ground. This was what she did. She held her posture and breathed evenly and met the gaze and did not break, because she had been looked at and evaluated and measured her entire life and she had always been equal to the examination. She had always held.

The stag waited.

The moonlight moved across the clearing in increments too small to see, the angle shifting by degrees as the moon tracked its arc above the canopy gap. The mist drifted. The stag's breath steamed and dissolved, steamed and dissolved, each cycle identical.

Ada's breath matched the stag's rhythm. Or tried to. She drew each inhale to the same careful length, held it the same fraction of a second, released it through her nose in the same even stream. But the stag's rhythm cost the stag nothing. It was breathing the way a stone sits: effortless, incapable of effort. Ada's rhythm cost her everything. Each breath required a conscious decision not to breathe faster, not to let the pace quicken, not to let the gasping that wanted to start in her chest escape through the gate she had built for it.

The antlers shifted above the stag's head, the outer tines expanding and contracting in time with Ada's heartbeat. Not the stag's heartbeat. Hers. The branches swelled when her chest expanded, drew inward when she exhaled. The bone structure was tracking something in her body that the stag could read and she could only feel. The crown was breathing with her. The cage was breathing with her.

She straightened further. Drew her shoulders back another fraction. Lifted her chin until the rain jacket's collar pressed into the soft skin beneath her jaw. Her body was performing control with every resource it had left: the muscles of her core, the alignment of her spine, the even breathing, the steady gaze. She was composure. She was the woman who had it together. She was fine.

The stag was better.

It stood on its ridge with a stillness that made her performance visible as performance. The stag did not try to be still. It was still. The stag did not hold its head high. Its head was high. The composure she was spending every remaining calorie to maintain, the stag wore like fur: intrinsic, effortless. It had no audience. It was not performing for anything. It simply was what it was, and what it was was the thing she had spent her life pretending to be.

The recognition arrived not as a thought but as a pressure behind her sternum. A tightness that had nothing to do with the cold or the wet clothing or the psilocybin or the adrenaline. The stag was her composure made visible. Given a body and antlers and unreadable eyes. Placed on a ridge above her so she could see it from the outside.

She did not want to see it.

Her jaw tightened. The ache in the joint spread upward into her temples. Every point of tension announced itself: the locked knees, the rigid core, the held breath, the set jaw, the fists. Every joint under load. Every muscle spending energy to maintain its position. The posture of a woman standing easily, which was the most exhausting stance she knew. Her hands had left the backpack straps at some point during the standoff. They hung at her sides now, fists tight, the nylon gone from between her fingers.

The stag saw all of it. Its eyes reflected the silver light unchanged, unsoftened, giving her nothing to argue with or deflect or redirect. The stag looked at her like a mirror looks at what stands in front of it: without opinion, without mercy, without the possibility of looking away.

The ache in her jaw radiated into her temples and behind her eyes. The stag's voice carried the same cold precision as before, each word arriving in the silver space between them and settling there and refusing to leave.

Practiced. The smile she had smiled in a hundred kitchens and offices and phone calls, the smile that said *I'm fine* and *don't worry about me* and *I don't need anything from you*, the smile that was a locked door with a deadbolt and a chain, and behind it something pressed against the wood with both hands and its mouth was open and the sound it was making would have filled the room if the door had not been so well built.

A long, slow breath. Held. Released. The control reasserted, thin as the rain jacket, transparent as the thin ice she was becoming.

"I'm fine."

Her own voice. Ragged, thin, scraped raw by disuse and cold and stream water and hours of silence. Two words. The two words she had spoken more than any others. The body moved with an ease that was the opposite of effort, muscles working beneath the heavy coat with coordinated grace, carrying immense weight without appearing to notice it. Ada looked at the antler fragments scattered across the clearing. Pale shapes on dark moss, catching light, casting shadows. The crown the stag had worn. The crown the stag had shed. Each piece a part of the structure that had made the stag look magnificent, and the crown had been the thing it set down, piece by piece, as it walked away.

She turned from the clearing.

The moonlight narrowed behind her as the canopy closed, the silver column thinning to a shaft, then a bright thread, then gone. The forest ahead was darker. The green glow of the shelf fungi and the scattered mushroom caps replaced the moon's cold light with their warmer, dimmer pulse. The fly agarics at the clearing's edge stood on either side of her as she passed between them, red sentinels marking the threshold, and then they were behind her, and the clearing was behind her, and the fragments were behind her on the moss, pale as bone in the light she could no longer see.

The devil's club scratches pulsed their slow heat along her left forearm, through the swollen web of her right hand. The rain jacket rustled with each step, the sound small and human.

The composure was still there. The straightened spine, the level chin, the hands returning to the backpack straps. The performance reassembling because rebuilding was what it was designed to do. But it fit differently now. Looser. The structure had shifted by a degree, the joints no longer flush, the walls not quite plumb. Not broken. Cracked. A hairline fracture in something she had believed was load-bearing, and through the fracture, a draft. Cold air from somewhere beneath, from the space the composure had been built to cover.

She did not know what had been taken from her in the clearing. She did not know what had been shown. The distinction should have been clear, loss from revelation, and the fact that she could not tell them apart was itself exposure, because Ada had always known. She had always had an answer. A framework. A category for filing what happened into a shape she could manage.

The stag had looked at her filing system. The stag had not been impressed.

The mycelium hum rose through the soles of her waterlogged boots, stronger than before, or she was more attentive to it, or her defenses had thinned enough that signals she had been filtering were reaching her now. It pulsed beneath the moss and the root systems and the dark earth, patient, continuous, indifferent to whether she noticed.

She walked. The forest watched. The clearings ahead were darker, the moonlight unable to follow where the canopy sealed. The bioluminescence pulsed in its heartbeat rhythm. The spore-taste coated her throat. Her fists were at her sides.

And through the crack, the dark water beneath.

# Chapter 6: Skin

The water found her before she found it.

A mineral smell, heavy and cool, rising through the spore-sweetness that had coated her throat since the ravine. Different from the stream. The stream had been clean, sharp, a scent that moved. This was still. Peaty. The smell of water that had been sitting in one place for a long time, collecting what the earth offered: tannin from decaying leaves, iron from the soil beneath, the patient chemistry of groundwater seeping through dark layers of peat. The smell reached her and her feet slowed, adjusting their pace before she had a name for what they sensed.

She was looking down.

Since the stag's clearing she had been looking down. Less from grief than from the effort of not looking at what had cracked. Her gaze tracked the moss and fern and the glowing mushroom clusters that lined the fallen logs, registering them at ankle-height, waist-height, never rising above the middle distance. The composure was back in place, or the shape of it was, the straightened spine and the level chin and the hands on the backpack straps, but the composure had a hairline fracture now and looking up meant risking whatever came through the crack. The forest floor was safer. The forest floor had no mirrors.

The terrain had been narrowing. Not the suffocating press of the Tangle, where salal and devil's club walled every path. This was different. The clearings that had opened wide earlier, arena-spaces lit by green glow and bordered by fly agarics, had grown smaller as she walked, drawing together like a corridor closing on its destination. The trees here were older, their trunks wider, their root systems reaching across the ground in thick, tangled ridges that funneled her movement into a single direction. Ferns crowded the gaps between roots. Coral fungi in pale white and muted purple clustered at the bases of the larger trunks, branching upward in delicate formations she looked away from quickly. They branched like antlers. Everything branched like antlers tonight.

She could not go sideways. The roots and the ferns and the dark press of undergrowth on either side closed the lateral options. She could go forward, following the mineral smell downhill, or she could turn back to the stag's clearing with its fragments of bone on the moss. She went forward.

The fog began at her ankles.

Thin, white, cold. It moved above the ground like something poured, filling the low spaces between roots and settling into the hollows where the moss was deepest. Each step carried her shins through it. The fog had weight, a damp pressure against her soaked jeans, and it smelled of the water she was approaching: peaty, iron-laced, still. The temperature had dropped, gradually, the air around the pond holding its chill, contained by the basin walls and the absence of wind. Her rain jacket, thin and inadequate, blocked nothing but the mist. The shivering that had settled to a manageable rhythm on the walk returned now as a finer frequency, the cold finding the gaps between the nylon and her soaked shirt and exploiting them.

# Chapter 10: Web

Not the owl's mirror-eyes, where her own face had been reflected back with the precision of something that saw every flaw and had no mercy.

This face was hers. Tired. The skin beneath her eyes was dark. The swelling narrowed her gaze to something that looked half-asleep and fully awake at the same time. Tear tracks still lined her cheeks in pale channels where the stream water had rinsed the mud away, the pattern of three separate cryings written on her skin. Her hair was matted, plastered to her temples and jaw, strands of web silk catching the growing light in faint silver lines. Loose strands hung across her forehead. Mud still darkened the hollows beside her nose.

She looked tired. She looked alive. The two observations occupied the same face without contradiction.

The stream carried the reflection downstream, bending it over stones, breaking it into fragments of light and reforming it and breaking it again. It did not hold the image. It let the image move through, carrying it over stones and past the fern-shaded bank and on, the way a stream lets everything move through. Not a mirror. A current.

Ada stood. The standing took effort, her thighs protesting, her knees stiff from kneeling on the bank. The smooth stone in her rain jacket pocket pressed against her thigh when she straightened, a small weight she registered without examining. She adjusted the backpack on her left shoulder.

The forest ahead was opening.

The trees were still large, still old, but they stood farther apart now, generous with space. The canopy had gaps. Through them the sky was no longer gray. A pale gold had entered the east, warm and directional, the first light that came from a specific place rather than filtering through everywhere at once. The gold touched the tops of the tallest firs and traveled slowly down their trunks. It had not reached the forest floor yet, but it would.

The stream led onward, curving gently between the opening trees, its sound steady. Along the bank, a low plant grew in clusters, its leaves shaped like small hearts folded along their center lines, pale green and trembling faintly in the breeze. The first thing she had seen growing here that was not fern or fungus or moss. The frog hopped along the bank, moving when Ada moved, pausing when she paused. It sat on a stone. It sat on a patch of moss. It hopped across a fan of fern that lay across the path, the fronds heavy with dew, and the dew shook loose and caught the new light in brief, falling sparks. The frog was not guiding her. It was with her. The difference was the whole difference, and it was not a difference she needed to think about to understand. She understood it in her legs, in the pace she kept, in the space between them that neither closed nor widened.

Ahead, beyond the thinning trunks, the forest brightened. Not bioluminescence. Natural light. The gold of early dawn filling a space where the canopy broke open and the sky came all the way down to the ground. A clearing. She could not see its shape yet, only its light, only the quality of brightness that meant open ground and a place where the forest chose to stop and let something else begin.

The bird called again. The same note, sustained, metallic, hanging in the dawn air. It was answered by another, farther off, and the two notes overlapped for a moment before the first thinned and was gone. The wren sang. The insects clicked. The stream ran. The wind moved in the canopy in a sound like breathing.

Ada walked toward the light. Her boots squelched. Her jeans were heavy. Her shoulders ached and her forearm throbbed and her throat was raw and her eyes were swollen and the grief in her chest moved like water, steady and unhurried and hers. She walked as she had been walking since she rose from the mycelium mat, unhurried, present, each step a meeting between her boot sole and the ground that received it. She was not rushing. The clearing would be there when she arrived, and she would arrive when she arrived, and the walking itself was not a means to arrival but a continuation of everything that had come before.

The frog hopped ahead, low and deliberate, into the brightening air.

Ada followed.

# Chapter 13: Clearing

Her boot found grass.

After root and mycelium and stream-softened moss, after hours of uneven ground: grass. Short and even, the ground of a meadow that had been holding open space against the trees for longer than she had been alive. Her boot pressed into it and the grass bent and the give of level earth rose through her ankle, her knee, the tired muscles of her lower back. The difference between this ground and every surface she had walked on through the night rose through her legs and settled in her spine before she had a word for it.

She stepped forward. Another step. The trees fell away behind her and the clearing opened.

The meadow was perhaps fifty yards across, bordered on all sides by old-growth forest. Douglas firs and cedars stood at the edge in a ragged line, their trunks massive, their crowns catching the first color of the sky. The grass was short and pale green and studded with the heart-shaped leaves of wood sorrel, each leaf unfolding along its center crease, opening to the light that was arriving. They had been closed through the night, folded inward, and now they were opening. Wild violet grew between them in low clusters, their flowers a soft faded purple against the green, their petals holding beads of dew that caught the gold from the east.

Above her, the sky.

She had not seen it since the trailhead. Hours of canopy, hours of branches and needles pressing close, the flat black absence above the cedars where the crowns were too high to find. Now the sky was there, immense, taking up the entire top of the world. Pale blue in the west, shading to gold in the east, the gold not yet sunlight but the announcement of sunlight, warm and directional, arriving from a specific point on the horizon and reaching across the clearing in long bands that turned the fir trunks at the eastern edge amber. The light had not yet reached the meadow floor. It lay along the highest branches and traveled slowly down, and the slowness was not delay but generosity, and Ada watched it descend.

She stood in the clearing and breathed.

The air was cold and clean. It smelled of dew on grass, of the faint sweetness of violet, of the mineral freshness that fills open ground before the sun warms it. A breeze moved across the meadow, steady and light, carrying behind it the scent of the forest: cedar, moss, the last trace of wet earth. She had not felt moving air since the trailhead. The deep forest sealed out wind layer by layer, canopy and branches and undergrowth and the pressing weight of trees that had been growing for centuries. Here the meadow let the air through. The breeze touched her face, her neck, the raw skin of her cheeks where the dried salt tightened and released. It found the scratches on her left forearm, the inflamed lines from wrist to elbow, and the cold air on the hot skin was sharp and specific and alive.

She was in the open. She was in the light.

The space around her was enormous. After the tangle with its pressing walls of salal and devil's club, after the clearings that served as arenas for encounters she had not chosen, after the massive cedars of Zone 4 pressing close with their root-systems and their humming network, the meadow was pure room. Room above, where the sky went on without interruption. Room around, where the grass stretched to the tree line in every direction without obstacle or barrier. Room to stand without the undergrowth pressing against her legs, without branches catching her hair, without roots catching her boots. The space entered her through every surface, filling the chest and the belly and the spaces between her ribs. Not vibration. Air and light and distance, and it did not press inward. It held open.

Her shoulders dropped, releasing like a held breath to an open window. Her jaw was soft. It had stayed soft since its releasing on the mycelium mat, the tension that had lived there since childhood still absent, and the absence was still strange, a phantom lightness where weight had sat so long it had become the shape of her face. The jaw stayed loose, the eyes stayed easy. The machinery that would once have catalogued every shadow and tested every sound was still off, and what remained in its place was attention without urgency.

Her hands were open at her sides. They had been open since the roots.

The forest was behind her, present without her turning to look. The hum, the pulse, those were gone, or so faint they lived only as memory in her bones, the way a bell lives in the metal after the ringing stops. The forest was there and she was here and the boundary between them was not a wall. She had walked into the forest as a contained thing, sealed, her edges hard and defended. She stood in the clearing as a different kind of creature. The edges had returned during the long walk upward, but they were different now. Permeable. Boundaries that allowed passage in both directions. The meadow was under her boots. The air was on her skin. The light on her face, the forest at her back. None of these were separate from her, and she was not separate from them. It was a fact as ordinary as the grass beneath her feet.

The stream ran through the clearing.

The dawn stream, the same stream she had knelt beside and drunk from and rinsed the night's grime from her face. It crossed the meadow in a gentle curve between the grass and the wood sorrel, the water shallow and clear, catching the gold light from the east in small bright points along its surface. The sound it made over its rounded stones was quiet, musical, steady. A clean sound. A sound that had been running before she found it and would run after she left.

The frog sat on a stone at the stream's edge.

A round stone, half-buried in the bank, the kind the stream had shaped over decades. The frog sat on it with the settled weight she remembered from the nurse log in the first minutes of the night. A lifetime ago. A single night ago. Three or four inches of mottled olive and gold, the pale cream stripe down its back catching the new light. Warty, heavy, sitting in the first warmth of sunrise. Its gold eyes held the morning light. The horizontal pupils were steady. Its throat moved with its own slow rhythm, the pale pouch expanding and contracting in a cadence that belonged to it alone.

Under ordinary light, without bioluminescence below and psilocybin between, it was simply a Western toad. No ancient patterns in its skin. No words forming in its throat. A toad on a stone at dawn, sitting with the patience of something that was here before she arrived and would be here after she left.

Ada watched it. The frog watched Ada.

The watching lasted. Long enough for the gold light to travel another inch down the fir trunks at the clearing's edge. Long enough for a bird to call from somewhere in the trees, one sustained metallic note, the sound of a finger drawn along the rim of a wet glass, the sound that had hung in the dusk air when she locked her car and walked into the forest. It had called at the beginning. It called now. The night between the two calls held everything, and the thrush did not know this, and that was part of it.

Warmth moved through Ada's chest. For the toad on its stone. For the four feet of grass and morning air between them, bridged by nothing except the fact that they were both here, both alive, both looking. The regarding was level, and it was enough.

The frog trilled: a single note, high and clear, rising from the two chambers of its throat and carrying across the meadow. The note traveled over the grass and the wood sorrel and the stream and the trees at the clearing's edge, and it kept going, past the trees, into the forest, farther than a toad's trill should carry. Then the note thinned and faded and was gone.

Silence. Not the alarming silence of the forest, the silence that had preceded the wolf, that had preceded the boar. The silence of a meadow at dawn, filled with small sounds that did not press: the stream over its stones, the breeze bending the grass, the click of an insect in the violet. A silence that was simply the space between sounds, and the space was comfortable, and the silence was full.

The frog blinked once. Its gold eyes closed, slowly, and opened. It sat on its stone and it was a toad and it was everything and these were not different things. The guide had always been just a frog. The frog had always been this. Both were true and had always been true, and Ada stood in the meadow and received this and did not need to understand it any more than she needed to understand the grass or the light or the sound of water over stone.

Ada put her hand in her jacket pocket.

The gesture arrived without intention. Her hand found the pocket by memory. Her fingers closed around something smooth and cool and small. Something that had been there since she did not know when. Since the Black Pond, perhaps, where the shame had moved through her and her hand had closed around a stone on the bank while her attention was elsewhere. She did not remember picking it up.

She drew it out. A stone. Smooth, oval, the size of a large almond, gray with a vein of white quartz running through it. Warm from her body on one side, cool from the morning on the other. She held it in her open palm, the white traces of mycelium still on her skin, and looked at it.

It was real. A stone from the forest, ordinary, the kind a stream tumbles over years until it reaches this smoothness, this particular shape. It smelled faintly of earth. Of the night. She held it and the night was real. The stone was in her hand and the hand was in the morning light and both were real and neither required anything from her except to be held.

She put the stone back in her pocket.

She looked across the clearing. At the far edge, the forest thinned and a gap opened between the trees. Not a trail, exactly. A direction. The ground sloped gently that way and the trees stood thinner there and the gold light was strongest. She had her backpack on her left shoulder. Inside it: car keys, the lighter she had never used, the empty water bottle. Rain jacket streaked with mud, zipper jammed halfway, one sleeve snagged. Boots waterlogged, squelching. Jeans torn at the right knee, dark and heavy. Her hair matted to her temples, strands of web silk catching the morning light. The scratches on her left forearm. The swollen ridge on her right hand. The raw throat and the swollen eyes and the three layers of dried tears on her cheeks. A body that ached in every place a body can ache and a smooth stone in her pocket and something behind her ribs that she could not name and did not need to.

Ada walked.

Across the meadow, through the grass and the wood sorrel and the violet. The gold light reached the meadow floor as she moved, the first direct warmth falling on the crown of her head, her shoulders, the backs of her open hands. After the night's cold the warmth reached her skin and kept reaching, into the muscle, into the bone. Warmth from a source. Warmth that landed on her face like a hand. She did not stop walking but she turned her face toward the east for three steps, and the light was on her closed eyelids and her lashes and the raw skin of her cheeks, and it was the simplest thing in the world and the most remarkable, and then she turned back to the direction she was walking and kept walking.

The stream ran beside her for a while, then curved away. She let it go. The frog stayed on its stone. She did not look back at it. She did not need to. The frog was on its stone and the stone was by the stream and the stream ran through the clearing and the clearing was in the forest and the forest was in her. She carried all of it. Not as weight. As fact.

The grass ended and the trail began, a narrow path of packed earth between salal and sword fern, leading slightly downhill through thinning trees. She stepped onto it and her boots squelched on the packed ground and the sound was the same sound they had been making all night, steady, unchanged, the stubborn evidence of her physical weight on the physical world.

The morning grew around her. The gold deepened and the shafts of light multiplied through the canopy and reached the forest floor in warm pools that shifted with the breeze. A winter wren sang from a tangle of roots beside the trail, its song complex and rapid, tumbling over itself, carrying the entire understory in ten seconds of sound. The same bird called again, answered by another, the two notes overlapping for a moment before one thinned and was gone. The insects clicked in rebuilding chorus. Sound layered on sound, voice on voice, the forest filling with a density that was the opposite of the hum's single vibration and was equally alive.

Ada walked. Her body ached in every place. Her scratches throbbed with their slow heat. Her throat was raw and her eyes were swollen and the grief in her chest moved with each breath, steady and unhurried, not a stone she carried but a current she contained, flowing through the space the boar had opened, finding its own direction and its own speed. She walked with the grief and the exhaustion and the stone in her pocket and the trill still in her ears, a single clear note that would carry farther than this forest, farther than this morning.

She did not look back. The forest was not behind her. It was in her, the way the stream was in the stones it shaped. She carried it because carrying was what bodies did, and hers had carried her through every hour of the dark, and the morning was ordinary and vast and held her without asking anything in return.

Somewhere ahead, through the thinning trees and the brightening air, the trailhead waited. The gravel lot. Her car. Her phone on the passenger seat with its messages and its ordinary demands. The road back to everything she had come from. She would get there. She would sit behind the wheel and the seat would be cool and the world would be ordinary and all of it would be as real as the stone in her pocket and the scratches on her arm and the night she had walked through and the frog on its stone and the trill that was still, even now, carrying.

The trail opened. The light poured in.

Ada walked into the ordinary world, carrying everything, wounded and whole and awake.
