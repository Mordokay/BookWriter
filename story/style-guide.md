# Style Guide

This is the prose and voice contract for the book. All chapter writing must follow these rules.

## Voice and POV

- **POV:** Third person limited, rotating across the five biologists. Quill gets slightly more POV time (the 70/30 rule — ensemble carries scenes, Quill carries meaning).
- **Tense:** Past tense.
- **Narrator tone:** Dry, observational, slightly detached — like a naturalist documenting an impossible field study. The narrator notices absurdity without underlining it. Trusts the reader to catch it.
- **No narrator commentary or editorializing.** Let scenes speak. No "little did they know" or "it was, of course, absurd." The absurdity is self-evident.

## Dialogue

Dialogue is the primary vehicle for this book. Most of the novel happens in conversation.

### Principles

- Dialogue must feel like real speech: interrupted, asymmetric, occasionally petty, sometimes brilliant, sometimes wrong.
- Characters must sound different from each other. Vale is clipped and combative. Marsh is warm and observational. Reed is precise and excitable. Quill is earnest and spiraling. Hart is dry and blunt. Each frog has their own cadence (see below).
- Philosophy enters through friction, not lecture. Ideas emerge from situations, disagreements, misunderstandings, and characters wanting different things.
- Interrupt philosophy with bodily reality. A cosmic insight can be cut short by someone needing to eat, an insect landing on a face, a frog defecating mid-sentence.
- No character speaks in perfect thesis statements. Even the smartest characters fumble, digress, contradict themselves, or get annoyed.
- Leave roughness. Not every exchange resolves. Not every argument has a winner.

### Group Dialogue and Cross-Interaction (Critical Rule)

**Scenes must not feel like one-on-one interviews.** The world is alive. Conversations happen with 3, 4, 5, or more participants — humans and frogs mixing freely. This is a civilization, not a series of paired appointments.

- **Most scenes should involve multiple characters.** A conversation between Vale and Noll should be interrupted by Oona passing through with an opinion, or Talla heckling from the side, or Reed asking a tangential question that derails the political discussion into science.
- **Frogs talk to each other in front of the humans.** The biologists overhear frog-to-frog arguments, gossip, domestic disputes, and philosophical disagreements. These overheard conversations reveal things no frog would say directly to a human.
- **Humans talk to each other in front of the frogs.** The five biologists argue, debrief, worry, and joke with each other — and the frogs observe, react, or interject.
- **Cross-pairing is essential.** While each biologist has a primary frog interlocutor, they must also have meaningful interactions with other frogs. Hart might have an unexpectedly revealing exchange with Vesh. Reed might learn something crucial from Oona's domestic observations. Quill might get a sharper insight from Talla than from Vesh. Characters are multidimensional — they don't stay in their lane.
- **Group scenes are where the book's energy lives.** A scene at the Pools where 3 humans and 4 frogs are all talking, arguing, eating, and reacting simultaneously — that is the texture of this book. Not structured debates but the chaos of real social life in an alien setting.
- **Background characters exist.** Unnamed frogs wander through scenes, offer passing comments, react to the humans, and make the settlement feel populated. The biologists are not alone with their 6 designated frogs — they are inside a community.
- **Simulate real day-to-day conversation.** People wander in and out. Topics shift. Someone arrives with news. Someone leaves to handle something. A conversation about metaphysics becomes a conversation about food because someone is hungry. This is how real social life works.

### Frog Speech

The frogs must not sound like humans with quirky vocabulary. Their speech should feel shaped by:
- **Short lifespans:** urgency, compression, less nostalgia, more present-tense orientation
- **Permeable biology:** comfort with dissolution, merging, and boundary-crossing that humans find unsettling
- **Choral thinking:** frogs may reference collective experiences, speak in "we" more naturally, or finish ideas that another frog started
- **Alien assumptions:** things they take for granted that surprise humans (e.g., casual references to interstellar travel, matter displacement, or death as a minor scheduling inconvenience)

Different frogs sound different:
- **Noll:** measured, courteous, diplomatic, every sentence carefully constructed — the language of soft control
- **Vesh:** grandiose and vulgar in alternation, speaks in koans that are half-profound and half-absurd, prone to sudden tangents about bodily functions or insects
- **Talla:** sharp, impatient, cuts through euphemism, shorter sentences, rhetorical questions, the voice of someone who has had it
- **Krrik:** technical, fast, irritable, allergic to metaphor, drops into incomprehensible jargon then gets annoyed when others don't follow
- **Oona:** gossipy, warm, practical, domestic — speaks the way a neighbor talks over a fence, except the fence is made of bioluminescent moss
- **Pem:** slow, deliberate, eerie, speaks as if quoting something ancient, occasional silence that means more than words

## Description

Minimal. This book is not scenic. It moves through dialogue and interaction.

### Rules

- **One-detail rule:** Instead of describing an entire environment, give one smell, one wet sound, one impossible device, one strange domestic object, one behavioral detail. That does more work than a paragraph.
- **No lush descriptive blocks.** No cinematic establishing shots. No purple prose.
- **Show the world through characters noticing things**, not through narrator exposition. Marsh notices domestic details. Reed notices biological impossibilities. Hart notices contamination risk.
- **Sensory palette:** Wet. Bioluminescent. Organic. Warm stone. Mist. Mucus. Spore. Harmonic vibration through water. The world should feel permeable, alive, slightly unclean, and ancient.
- **Never repeatedly remind the reader that the frogs are frogs.** After the first few scenes, let the reader forget and remember on their own through specific physical details.

## Pacing

- **Detective-novel speed.** Every chapter moves the reader forward. No stalling.
- **Short chapters.** Each chapter is roughly one major conversation, one revelation, one complication, one reversal, or one strange social observation.
- **Jump cuts between scenes.** Don't linger on transitions. End a scene, start the next one in media res.
- **Not every scene is "important."** Light, odd, intimate, sideways scenes make the heavy ones land. A scene of Oona complaining about fermented flies is as valid as a scene about the cyclical apocalypse.
- **Escalating information density.** Early chapters are simpler — discovery, shock, disbelief. Middle chapters expand the world through many lenses. Late chapters compress as the conflict narrows.

## Humor

The comedy must emerge from the world, not from the prose winking at the reader.

### What works

- Dead seriousness applied to absurd things
- Bureaucratic politeness around horrifying choices
- Frogs treating cosmic impossibilities as mundane
- The collision between profound ideas and gross biological reality
- Characters pursuing dignity in ridiculous circumstances
- Earnestness that becomes funny because the situation is inherently absurd
- Specific, concrete absurd details that also reveal something structural about frog society

### What doesn't work

- Ironic narrator commentary
- Everyone being witty in the same way
- Random whimsy disconnected from character or situation
- Marvel-style quips that deflect every emotional moment
- Humor that signals "look how clever and original this is"
- Forced zaniness — the premise is already absurd enough

## Exposition

- **No "as you know, Bob" explanations.** Characters do not explain things to each other that they already know.
- **No info-dump paragraphs.** Worldbuilding arrives through discovery, conflict, jokes, mistakes, and consequences.
- **Partial mystery is better than full explanation.** The self-renewing energy material, the limits of frog technology, whether the spiritual practices are real — these should remain partially unexplained. Let the reader live with wonder.
- **Science arrives through Reed's excitement and confusion**, not through neutral narrator explanation.
- **Politics arrives through Vale's suspicious questions**, not through a history lesson.
- **Daily life arrives through Marsh's observations**, not through descriptive survey.

## Chapter Structure

Each chapter should:
1. Open in the middle of something — a conversation already underway, an observation mid-thought, an arrival at a new place
2. Contain change — something shifts by the end, even if it's just one character's understanding
3. End with energy — a question, a surprise, a discomfort, a joke that lingers, a door opening onto the next thing
4. Move fast enough that the reader turns the page

## Emotional Register

- The book is a comedy, but it has a serious heart.
- Funny and unsettling should coexist, often in the same scene.
- The reader should frequently be unsure whether a moment is beautiful or horrifying, wise or insane, profound or ridiculous.
- Earned emotional moments — when they come — should not be undercut by a joke. Let them breathe.
- The ending is the emotional payload. Everything builds toward the quiet horror and strange beauty of the ultimatum.

## Prose Quality Benchmarks

- A sentence should do one thing well, not three things vaguely.
- Cut adjectives that don't earn their place.
- Vary sentence length. Short sentences for impact. Longer ones for rhythm and accumulation.
- The prose should feel clean, quick, and slightly off-kilter — like the narration of someone who finds the situation extraordinary but is determined to document it precisely.
- No repetitive chapter openings. No "the sun rose" or "the morning brought" or "they gathered again."
- No summarizing emotional beats that could be dramatized.

## The Standard

The book should feel like: a first-contact comedy, a political and spiritual detective story, a fast-moving dialogue novel, an anthropological hallucination, something that is very funny on the page and slightly upsetting in the mind afterward.

**The book should repeatedly balance the grotesque, the sublime, the comic, and the philosophical, often within the same scene.**
