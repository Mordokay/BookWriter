# Style Guide

## Prose Voice

### Register
Literary but accessible. The prose should feel carefully made without feeling labored. Sentences should reward attention but never require a second read to parse basic meaning. The vocabulary is precise and occasionally unusual, but never showy. Think of a naturalist who is also a poet: exact observation rendered with rhythmic care.

### Density
Medium-high. The prose is denser than commercial fiction but never as thick as maximalist literary fiction. Every paragraph earns its length. White space and short sentences are used for impact, not laziness. Long, rolling sentences are used for immersion, not indulgence.

### Rhythm
Variable and intentional. During disorientation and fear, sentences fragment. During immersion and wonder, they lengthen and breathe. During confrontation with the animals, the prose tightens. During moments of surrender, it opens. The rhythm tracks Ada's nervous system.

**Rhythm logic (illustrative, not to be copied literally):**
- Fear: short, clipped, present. Sentence fragments allowed. Hard consonants.
- Awe: longer sentences, dependent clauses, liquid sounds, commas as breath marks.
- Dissolution: syntax loosens. Boundaries between sentences blur. Images connect without logical bridges.
- Integration: clean, warm, direct. Simple subject-verb structures. Room to breathe.

## Dialogue Style

Ada is mostly alone, but the forest speaks to her -- through its creatures. Dialogue in this story operates at the boundary between real speech and psychedelic interpretation. The animals speak, but whether Ada is truly hearing language or projecting meaning through psilocybin is permanently unresolved. The prose presents dialogue as dialogue without resolving whether it is "real."

### Ada's Speech
Ada may speak aloud to herself, the animals, the frog, or the forest. These utterances should be brief, raw, and unpolished. She is not performing for an audience. Her spoken words reveal what her controlled interior voice is trying to suppress.

### The Frog's Voice
The frog speaks in short, patient sentences. Wry, grounded, never preachy. It asks more questions than it answers. Its speech is sideways -- observations, provocations, and questions rather than instructions or explanations. Think a cross between Gandalf's patience and the Cheshire Cat's oblique wisdom. Sample register: "You're still running." "What are you holding?" "Sit down." The frog also communicates through presence, demonstration, and sound -- speech augments the physical teaching, it does not replace it. In early appearances (Ch. 1-2), the frog may not speak at all -- Ada has not gone deep enough into the trip. Speech arrives gradually, becoming more direct as the journey deepens.

### Animal-Demon Voices
Each animal-demon speaks with a distinct voice tied to the wound it embodies. The dialogue is dreamlike and psychedelically inflected -- simultaneously absurd, wise, cutting, and impossible to dismiss. Think Alice in Wonderland's Cheshire Cat or Caterpillar: ancient, alien, other. Not human conversation in animal costume.

What animals must NOT do in dialogue:
- Deliver exposition about Ada's backstory
- Explain their own symbolism ("I am your shame")
- Lecture, moralize, or philosophize abstractly
- Sound like humans wearing animal masks -- they should feel alien, ancient, other

What animals SHOULD do in dialogue:
- Provoke Ada with uncomfortable truths she cannot dismiss
- Ask questions that cut to the bone
- Speak in ways specific to their nature and wound
- Be funny, strange, and unsettling simultaneously
- Create tension through conversation, not just presence

### Dialogue Presentation
- Dialogue tags: "said" only. No exotic tags ("whispered," "growled," "intoned") unless the physical act is plot-relevant. Body language and environmental response accompany speech -- the forest reacts when the animals speak.
- The ambiguity must be preserved: Ada cannot be certain the animals are truly speaking. The psilocybin layer makes this perpetually uncertain. A wolf "speaks" but its jaw does not move in human ways. The words arrive in Ada's mind as the animal watches her. The prose presents the dialogue as real without confirming the mechanism.
- No witty banter. No quips. No sarcasm. Animal dialogue is an exposure of wound, not a social performance. The humor (where it exists) is dark, strange, and unsettling -- the humor of something that knows you better than you know yourself.

## Description Density and Philosophy

### Sensory-first
Every scene opens through the senses. What does Ada smell, feel against her skin, hear in the undergrowth? The reader enters the forest through the body before the mind interprets.

### Grounded symbolism
Symbols emerge from concrete physical details. The reader sees a real frog on a real stone before they feel its symbolic weight. Never lead with abstraction. Never describe a symbol; describe the thing, and let the symbol breathe underneath.

### Selective focus
Not everything is described. Ada's attention is the camera. What she notices reveals her state. Early in the story, she notices threats: thorns, shadows, sounds behind her. Later, she notices textures: the grain of bark, the warmth of mud, the light inside a mushroom cap. The shift in her perception IS the arc.

### Environmental interiority
The forest reflects Ada's psychological state, but this should be shown through environmental detail, not stated. When Ada is anxious, the trees press closer. When she surrenders, the canopy opens. The reader should feel this without being told "the forest responded to her mood."

## Point of View

**Close third person, locked to Ada.** The narrative voice stays inside Ada's perception at all times. We see what she sees, feel what she feels, know only what she knows. The narrator does not editorialize or explain Ada's experience from the outside.

The narrator may, in moments of ego dissolution, blur the boundary between Ada and environment. "She felt the rain" might become "The rain was her skin was the forest was the rain." These moments should be brief, controlled, and reserved for peak psychedelic intensity.

## Tense

**Past tense** for the primary narrative. This provides a subtle sense that Ada is recounting something she survived, which adds a quiet undertow of earned survival even during frightening scenes.

Exception: during the most intense psychedelic moments, present tense may be used for short passages (no more than a few paragraphs) to create immediacy. The shift must be deliberate and infrequent. Returning to past tense signals re-grounding.

## Pacing

### Scene-level
Each scene should have a single dominant emotional movement. Enter late, leave early. Do not narrate transitions unless the journey between locations carries emotional weight (which, in this forest, it often will). Let silence and stillness do work. Not every moment needs action.

### Chapter-level
Each chapter centers on one major encounter or transformation. The structure within a chapter follows a rough pattern: approach (building tension), encounter (confrontation), and aftermath (processing, shift). But this pattern should not feel mechanical. Some chapters may begin in the aftermath of the previous chapter's encounter. Some may end mid-confrontation. Chapters have room for fully developed scenes: extended sensory immersion, longer animal dialogue sequences, deeper interior processing, and environmental transitions that carry emotional weight. The additional space should serve depth, not repetition.

### Overall
The book moves through three phases:
1. **Descent** (chapters 1-3): Entry, disorientation, first encounters. Pacing is uncertain, exploratory.
2. **Confrontation** (chapters 4-10): Escalating encounters. Pacing tightens. Emotional intensity increases with each chapter. Some chapters may feature multiple animals simultaneously or carry an encounter across chapter boundaries.
3. **Integration** (chapters 11-14): Surrender, revelation, emergence. Pacing opens. Scenes become longer and slower. Silence grows.

## Chapter Ending Style

Chapters should end on moments of shift, not cliffhangers. The final image or sentence should reverberate. Preferred endings:

- A single image that carries symbolic weight (Ada sees something that changes what came before)
- A sensory detail that signals transformation (a new sound, a change in light, a shift in temperature)
- A line of interior thought that marks a threshold crossed
- Silence after intensity

Avoid:
- Cliffhanger cutoffs designed to manufacture urgency
- Summary statements that tell the reader what the chapter meant
- Questions posed directly to the reader
- Neat moral conclusions

## Things to Avoid in Prose

### Purple prose
Do not stack adjectives. Do not use three metaphors when one precise image will do. If a sentence is beautiful but says nothing new, cut it. Beauty serves clarity, not the reverse.

### Said-bookisms
No "she murmured," "she breathed," "she intoned," "she whispered" unless the physical act of whispering is plot-relevant. "Said" or no tag.

### Filter words
Minimize "she felt," "she saw," "she heard," "she noticed," "she realized." Instead of "She felt the cold water on her feet," write "The water was cold on her feet." Instead of "She noticed the frog watching her," write "The frog watched her." The close POV already implies Ada's perception. Do not double-frame it.

### Over-attribution of emotion
Do not name emotions when they can be shown through action, sensation, or thought. "She was afraid" is almost always weaker than showing the fear through her body, her breath, her behavior.

### Repetitive openings
No two consecutive chapters should open the same way. Vary: sensory detail, action, interior thought, environmental description, disorientation. Track openings to prevent patterns.

### Em-dashes
Do not use em-dashes (-- or —) in prose. They are heavily overused in AI-generated text and read as artificial. Replace with commas, periods, colons, semicolons, or restructured sentences. Use commas for parenthetical asides, periods or semicolons for dramatic breaks, colons for elaboration. If a sentence needs an em-dash to work, rewrite the sentence.

### Latin/scientific names in prose
Do not use Latin species names (Mycena chlorophos, Omphalotus olearius, Monotropa uniflora, etc.) in the readable prose. Use common names or vivid descriptions instead. Readers should never need specialized knowledge to follow the text. Ada may "know" the species, but the prose renders what she sees, not her taxonomy. Latin names may appear in Continuity Notes for internal reference.

### Cinematic filler
No "she took a deep breath and squared her shoulders." No "time seemed to slow down." No "her heart pounded in her chest." Find fresher, more specific physical correlates for emotion.

### Explaining symbolism
Never have Ada think "the wolf must represent my anger" or anything equivalent. The reader and Ada may sense meaning, but the prose must not decode the symbols explicitly. Trust the reader.

### Gratuitous interiority
Ada is introspective, but not every sensation needs three sentences of reflection. Let some moments land without commentary. The most powerful beats are often the ones where Ada simply experiences without interpreting.

## Language Texture

The prose should feel like it belongs to the same ecosystem as the following, without imitating any of them:

- **Jeff VanderMeer** (Annihilation): the precision of describing something alien and alive, the way nature resists human categories
- **Susanna Clarke** (Piranesi): wonder and strangeness rendered in calm, clear prose, as if the uncanny is simply how things are
- **Hermann Hesse** (Siddhartha): the rhythm of spiritual journey told through sensory experience, with simple sentences that carry enormous weight
- **Robin Wall Kimmerer** (Braiding Sweetgrass): the intimacy of paying close attention to the living world, the reverence that comes from specificity
- **Samanta Schweblin** (Fever Dream): dread built through disorientation and closeness, the feeling that something is wrong but you cannot name it

## Reference Authors for Voice Calibration

When in doubt about whether a sentence or passage is working, ask:

- Would VanderMeer find this specific enough?
- Would Hesse find this emotionally honest?
- Would Clarke find this clear enough?
- Would Kimmerer find this attentive enough to the living world?
- Would Schweblin find this unsettling enough in the early chapters?

If the answer to any of these is no, revise.

## Additional Rules

- Use the forest's real biology when possible. Real fungi, real animal behavior, real plant life. The psychedelic layer warps these details but starts from accuracy.
- Sound matters. The forest is never silent. Layer ambient sound beneath every scene: insects, wind, water, breath, heartbeat, distant animal calls.
- Smell and touch are as important as sight. Ada is in the dark for portions of the story. The prose must work without relying exclusively on visual description.
- When Ada's perception fractures during peak psychedelic moments, the prose may use unconventional syntax, merged images, or synesthesia. But these passages must be brief and surrounded by grounded prose. The reader needs a baseline to feel the departure.
