# Chapter 19: "The Growth"

## Objective
Show the Pruning crisis escalating from background anxiety to genuine, visible danger -- not through a single dramatic scene but through a day in which the crisis touches every layer of settlement life. The reader must feel the crisis in bodies, homes, and relationships, not just in science. Reed's perspective grounds the chapter in operational urgency while the domestic dimension gives it emotional weight.

## POV
Reed. His internal state: focused, increasingly alarmed, oscillating between scientific excitement and genuine fear. He is useful here because he sees mechanism and cost simultaneously -- the data and the displaced family occupy the same field of vision. His characteristic enthusiasm is now strained; the puzzle is no longer abstract.

## Setting
- **Krrik's laboratory:** Now chaotic. Multiple computation arrays running. Junior engineers arguing. The Siberian chief engineer present and audibly disagreeing with Krrik. The lab smells of ozone, fermentation, and stress-sweat from too many frogs in too small a space. The moss beds are glowing brighter than usual -- overworked.
- **Residential warrens (eastern section):** A flooded corridor. Warm luminescent fungus pushing through a wall where voss has grown through. A displaced frog family standing outside their ruined chambers with woven baskets of possessions.
- **Pruning staging grounds:** Open area. Displacement operators running coordination exercises. The physical toll visible -- operators emerging disoriented, needing to be steadied by attendants. The faint ozone smell of displacement practice.

## Characters Present
- **Reed** (POV) -- alarmed, precise, arguing with Hart about operator welfare
- **Hart** -- at the staging grounds running water samples, tracking operator health, blunt about the physical cost
- **Marsh** -- passes through with Oona carrying food to displaced families, grounding the technical crisis in domestic reality
- **Krrik** -- directing the lab under pressure, irritable, privately desperate, accepts Reed's help without thanks
- **The Siberian chief engineer** -- blunt, disagreeing with Krrik's methodology, culturally distinct (more clipped, less tolerant of spiritual framing)
- **Junior engineers** (2-3) -- arguing over modulation parameters, one goes grey at the end
- **Oona** -- organizing cleanup in the residential section, complaining practically ("this never used to happen until the third week")
- **Medical frogs** -- at the staging grounds, tending disoriented operators
- **Displaced frog family** -- a couple and their young, standing outside ruined chambers, a neighbor offering space
- **Background settlement frogs** -- visible throughout, carrying supplies, murmuring, the community mobilized

## Scene Sequence
1. **The lab in chaos.** Reed arrives at Krrik's laboratory to find it transformed. Multiple computation arrays running simultaneously, the thinking moss glowing hot. Junior engineers arguing about modulation parameters. The Siberian chief engineer has arrived and is audibly disagreeing with Krrik about methodology. Krrik finds this infuriating and useful. Reed takes in the controlled panic of a civilization's best minds hitting a wall.
2. **Reed feeds data.** Reed assists, feeding his team's surface canopy data into the arrays. Patterns emerge that neither species' science fully explains. Krrik and the Siberian argue about what the patterns mean. Reed watches two alien scientific frameworks collide and realizes his human data offers a third angle -- a view from above that the frogs' internal sensors cannot replicate.
3. **Krrik's private admission.** Krrik tells Reed, with only one junior engineer present, that the current biological modulators may not be sufficient. He is attempting to modify them. He needs better growth data. He accepts Reed's help without gratitude or ceremony. There is no time for either. This is the first time Krrik has admitted to a human that the science might be failing.
4. **The flooded residential section.** Reed walks through the settlement and sees the crisis in daily life. A residential corridor flooded with warm luminescent fungus -- voss has grown through a wall. A frog family stands outside their ruined chambers, possessions in woven baskets. A neighbor offers space. Oona is organizing a cleanup crew, complaining that "this never used to happen until the third week." Reed sees the voss not as a data point but as the thing that just destroyed someone's home.
5. **Marsh and Oona with the displaced.** Marsh passes through with Oona, carrying food to the displaced families. The domestic dimension: tadpoles moved, routines broken, fear managed through normalcy. Marsh's presence reminds Reed that the crisis has a human-scale cost he keeps abstracting away.
6. **The staging grounds.** Reed and Hart observe displacement operators running coordination exercises. The physical toll is visible -- operators emerge disoriented, some needing to be steadied by attendants. Hart is running water samples and talking to medical frogs about operator health data. She and Reed have a sharp exchange: she thinks the operators are being pushed too hard; he thinks the crisis demands it. Neither convinces the other.
7. **Krrik's closing line.** Back in the lab, or near it. Krrik, with only Reed and one junior engineer present, says quietly: "We have done this before. We have always done this. But 'always' is a comfort, not a guarantee." The junior engineer's skin goes grey -- all color draining at once, an involuntary display of fear that no one comments on.

## Key Dialogue Moments
1. **Krrik to Reed (private):** "The modulators are insufficient. I am modifying them. I need your surface data -- you see from above. We see from within. Neither view is complete." -- Reveals Krrik's desperation and the first genuine acknowledgment that Reed's "primitive" science has strategic value.
2. **Oona to no one in particular, organizing cleanup:** "This never used to happen until the third week. The third week. We are in the second week. Someone should tell the voss it is early." -- Reveals the abnormality of this Pruning through domestic annoyance; the comedy compressed by genuine fear.
3. **Hart to Reed at the staging grounds:** "Three operators needed to be carried out of the last session. Carried. And Noll's scheduled two more sessions today. At what point does 'preparation' become 'damage'?" Reed: "At the point where the alternative is the voss eating the settlement." Hart: "That's what they said about the last three sessions too." -- Shows the ethical cost of the Pruning and the genuine disagreement between two humans who care about different dimensions of the same problem.
4. **Krrik's closing admission:** "We have done this before. We have always done this. But 'always' is a comfort, not a guarantee." -- The chapter's emotional landing. A scientist admitting the limits of precedent.

## Emotional Progression
Opens with **controlled urgency** in the lab (focused, technical, pressured). Moves through **alarm** as the residential flooding makes the abstract threat concrete. Deepens into **ethical discomfort** at the staging grounds as the physical cost to operators becomes visible. Ends on **dread** -- Krrik's quiet admission and the junior engineer's involuntary grey. The comedy is compressed throughout: Oona's complaints, the Siberian's bluntness, the absurdity of a civilization's survival depending on moss that needs watering. But the comedy does not relieve the tension; it coexists with it.

## Canon Facts to Preserve
- The voss is a living substrate that grows in root systems; it generates energy and powers everything in the settlement. During cosmic radiation peaks (~every 30-40 years), growth accelerates dangerously.
- This Pruning is worse than usual -- the worst in living memory (30-35 frog-years), possibly the worst in centuries.
- Biological modulators (Krrik's chemicals that slow voss growth) are his specialty but may not be sufficient this cycle.
- Displacement operators require synchronized consciousness to coordinate quantum displacement. This takes a severe physical toll.
- Reed's surface canopy data provides a perspective the frogs cannot replicate from within.
- The Siberian chief engineer is present -- Siberians are culturally pragmatic, blunt, and consider Amazon spiritual intensity excessive.
- Oona is a nursery caretaker and fermentation hobbyist, not a crisis specialist, but she organizes with quiet competence.
- Krrik does not express gratitude or social niceties; he accepts help and moves on.

## Wet Way Elements
- **Callbacks, not introductions.** By Ch 19, the reader knows the Wet Way well. No new concepts are introduced here.
- Oona's complaints about the voss could carry a "the Croaking is loud today" or equivalent casual invocation -- the religion as ambient language even during crisis.
- The operators' synchronization at the staging grounds is a functional echo of the breathing circles -- the spiritual practice made operational. This parallel should be felt, not stated.
- "Flow, don't push" could surface ironically -- the operators are being pushed, not flowing.

## Desired Ending Energy
Dread held in a quiet sentence. The junior engineer's involuntary grey. The feeling that "we have always done this" is no longer enough. The ground has shifted beneath a civilization that has stood for 200 million years, and the first cracks are visible to the people whose job it is not to crack.

## Constraints and Warnings
- **Do not make this an action chapter.** The crisis escalates through daily life, not through dramatic events. The flooded home matters as much as the failing modulators.
- **Do not let the lab scenes become jargon-heavy.** Reed understands the science; the reader should understand the stakes. Describe what Reed sees, not what he would publish.
- **Do not flatten the Siberian engineer into a stock "disagreeing colleague."** Give him a distinct voice -- blunter than Krrik, less patient with spiritual framing, culturally different.
- **Keep the group dialogue rule alive.** Even in the lab, multiple frogs are talking over each other. Oona passes through. The displaced family is not alone -- neighbors react. Background frogs populate every scene.
- **Quill is not present in this chapter.** Do not shoehorn him in.
- **The comedy is compressed, not absent.** Oona's complaints, the Siberian's bluntness, and the absurdity of moss destroying someone's bedroom should land as darkly funny even as the situation is genuinely frightening.
