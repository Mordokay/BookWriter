# Chapter 10: "The Fungal Audit"

## Objective
Hart's unauthorized epidemiological investigation of the settlement reveals that frog medicine is more advanced than human medicine but operates on radically different priorities. The reader learns that the frogs deliberately chose to keep their fungal infections because eliminating them would require sealing skin permeability, destroying the communication and consciousness-merging that defines their civilization. The chapter delivers the book's sharpest expression of its core question: is this wisdom or philosophy disguised as negligence?

## POV
Hart. She is in full field-biologist mode, treating the settlement as a contaminated site that has never been properly surveyed. Her internal state is a mixture of professional compulsion (she cannot stop assessing biohazards) and growing discomfort that the frogs' medical logic is internally consistent despite violating every principle she was trained on. She thinks in risk assessments and contamination vectors. Her cynicism is her armor and her analytical lens.

## Setting
- **Frog medical treatment chambers:** Open, warm, tide-pool-like spaces where patients are visible to passersby and practitioners move unhurriedly between cases. One sensory detail: the air smells of mineral salts and something astringent, like wet copper mixed with bruised leaves.
- **The biologists' shared quarters (end of chapter):** Cluttered with borrowed frog moss-cushions and the team's scattered equipment. One sensory detail: the constant low vibration of the water channels through the floor.

## Characters Present
- **Hart (POV):** Leading the investigation, asking clinical questions, growing increasingly frustrated that the system leaks on purpose.
- **Reed:** Recruited by Hart for sampling ("You owe me; I catalogued your root toxins for three weeks"). Provides scientific counterpoint, asks about biochemistry.
- **Marsh:** Recruited for behavioral documentation of frog medical practices. Observes how practitioners interact with patients. Takes notes.
- **Frog medical practitioners (2-3 with speaking roles):** The first asks about human surgery and is horrified. A second argues the human approach has brutal logic. A third, older practitioner arrives and settles the argument with: all medicine is violence managed with good intentions.
- **Oona:** Appears at the treatment chambers to visit a neighbor with joint pain. Defends her moss-compress folk remedy with passionate anecdotal evidence.
- **Krrik (brief appearance):** Arrives to collect a biochemical analysis. Sides with Hart about folk remedies being superstition, then accidentally validates the moss compress by mentioning the anti-inflammatory compounds in the moss's mucilage layer.
- **Background frogs in treatment pools:** A construction worker with a crushed digit, an elderly frog with respiratory congestion, a young frog who swallowed something inadvisable during a fermentation experiment.
- **Vale (end of chapter):** Arrives mid-sentence at the debrief.
- **Quill (end of chapter):** Listening from the doorway.

## Scene Sequence

1. **Hart recruits her team.** Hart corners Reed and Marsh in the morning, announcing she is conducting a "field epidemiological survey" of the settlement whether anyone wants to join or not. Reed owes her. Marsh is curious. Hart has already begun mapping contamination vectors in her field notebook. She mentions she has identified at least four fungal species in the corridor walls since arriving, and nobody seems concerned. This is not a request. (Establishes Hart's compulsive professionalism and the comic tone.)

2. **The treatment chambers tour.** Hart, Reed, and Marsh enter the medical infrastructure. The treatment chambers are open warm pools, nothing like hospitals. Practitioners move between patients with unhurried fluidity. Hart finds this both impressive and clinically irresponsible. She begins asking questions. The practitioners find her charming and alarming simultaneously: they can regenerate limbs and manage cancer trivially, but they find the concept of a "sterile environment" philosophically absurd. Multiple practitioners are present, handling routine cases in the background. (Establishes the primitive-impossible contrast in medicine.)

3. **The surgery horror.** Hart explains human surgery to a practitioner who asked. Anesthesia, incisions, sutures, recovery wards. The practitioner's colors cascade through horrified fascination. Two colleagues come over to listen. "You cut the body open? On purpose? And the patient agrees to this?" Hart, defensive: "The patient is unconscious." The practitioner, appalled: "That is not better." A second practitioner argues the human approach has a certain brutal logic. A third, older practitioner settles the debate: all medicine is violence managed with good intentions. The argument resumes anyway. (Comedy from two medical philosophies each finding the other monstrous.)

4. **The deliberate trade-off revealed.** Hart discovers the fungal infections that are everywhere in the settlement. She assumes they are a failure of sanitation. The practitioners explain: engineering immunity would require sealing skin permeability. Sealed skin means no chemical signaling, no chromatophore communication, no neural synchronization, no consciousness-merging. The frogs chose connection over cleanliness. Hart, who believes every system leaks, encounters a system that leaks on purpose and has built its philosophy around the leaking. Reed is fascinated by the biochemistry. Hart is disturbed. (The chapter's central revelation.)

5. **The folk remedy argument.** Oona arrives to visit a neighbor being treated for joint pain. She advocates for her moss compress. Hart and Oona argue: Hart considers the compress indistinguishable from superstition. Oona swears by it. Other frogs in the treatment area offer opinions. This is a genuine domestic dispute about health practices, not a philosophical debate. (Comedy from the collision of Hart's clinical worldview with Oona's practical one.)

6. **Krrik's accidental validation.** Krrik arrives to collect a biochemical analysis from the practitioners. He sides with Hart on folk remedies being superstition. Then he casually mentions a biochemical mechanism involving anti-inflammatory compounds in the moss's mucilage layer that would explain why the compress might actually work. He does not realize he has changed the argument. Hart is frustrated (she wanted to be right). Oona is frustrated (she wanted to be right for different reasons). Krrik leaves, oblivious to the social damage. (Comedy of accidental scientific validation.)

7. **Background medical life.** Hart continues her survey. The chapter reveals the treatment chambers' daily flow: a construction worker with a crushed digit, an elderly frog with respiratory congestion, a young frog who swallowed something inadvisable during a fermentation experiment. The practitioners have their own internal disagreements about dosing philosophy and whether the current generation's health is better or worse than the previous one's (an argument that apparently recurs every generation). Increased injuries from Pruning staging-ground training are visible: operators and staging-ground workers with stress fractures, exhaustion, and the particular disorientation that follows extended consciousness-synchronization practice. (Grounds the medical world in daily life; advances Pruning thread.)

8. **The debrief at quarters.** Hart tells the group back at quarters, with Reed and Marsh present, Vale arriving mid-sentence, Quill listening from the doorway: "The frogs' relationship with disease is not negligence, it is philosophy. And I do not know which is worse." The line lands differently for each listener. (Hart's verdict; the chapter's thesis stated as a character beat, not as narration.)

## Key Dialogue Moments

1. **Practitioner on surgery:** "You cut the body open? On purpose? And the patient agrees to this?" / Hart: "The patient is unconscious." / Practitioner: "That is not better." -- Reveals the gulf between medical philosophies. Both are horrified by the other's approach.

2. **The older practitioner's settlement:** "All medicine is violence managed with good intentions." -- A line that silences the room for a moment and then does not settle anything.

3. **Hart to Oona on the moss compress:** Hart dismisses it as superstition; Oona defends it with anecdotal passion. The exchange should feel like two neighbors arguing about home remedies, not a philosophical debate. Reveals Hart's clinical rigidity and Oona's practical knowledge.

4. **Krrik's accidental validation:** Krrik mentions the anti-inflammatory compounds in the moss's mucilage layer while discussing something else entirely. He does not realize he has validated Oona's remedy. His tone is indifferent. The comic beat is that he frustrates everyone simultaneously.

5. **Hart's verdict:** "The frogs' relationship with disease is not negligence, it is philosophy. And I do not know which is worse." -- Delivered at the debrief. Reveals Hart's growing discomfort that she cannot dismiss the frogs' approach.

## Emotional Progression
**Opening:** Confident, clinical, comedic. Hart is in control, doing what she does best, and the comedy comes from her treating an alien civilization like a contaminated field site. **Middle:** The surgery exchange is pure comedy of mutual horror. The mood shifts when the deliberate trade-off is revealed: the comedy continues but now carries weight. Hart's confidence cracks slightly. She is encountering a medical philosophy that is internally consistent and that she cannot dismiss with clinical superiority. **The folk remedy sequence** returns to lighter comedy, peaking with Krrik's accidental validation. **End:** Hart's debrief verdict lands with a mixture of dry humor and genuine disturbance. She has learned something she did not want to learn: that the frogs' relationship with disease is a civilizational choice, not a civilizational failure.

## Canon Facts to Preserve
- Threnn medicine can regenerate limbs (slowly) and manage cancer trivially. Lifespan extended from 5-8 years to 25-35 years.
- Fungal infections resist treatment but are deliberately maintained because eliminating them would require sealing skin permeability.
- Cutaneous respiration accounts for ~40% of gas exchange. Skin is permeable to water, gases, and chemical signals.
- Chromatophore displays are a communication channel; sealing permeability would destroy this.
- Consciousness-merging requires neural synchronization through the permeable skin.
- Oona swears by a moss compress for joint pain. Krrik considers this barely distinguishable from superstition.
- The settlement's architecture is organic: grown root walls, bioluminescent pathways, water channels.
- The Pruning staging grounds are currently active, producing training injuries.
- Hart's nationality is French-Canadian (Montreal). She is 39, trained at McGill and London School of Hygiene.
- Reed is 34, American (Atlanta, MIT/Stanford). Marsh is 41, Australian (rural Queensland).

## Wet Way Elements
- **Permeability as virtue** is the chapter's central Wet Way concept, though it arrives through medicine, not through explicit religious language. The frogs chose connection over cleanliness because their philosophy and their biology both insist that closed systems die. A practitioner might reference this in practical terms: "A sealed frog is a dead frog. We breathe through our skin. Seal the skin, seal the breathing, seal the communication, seal the self. You have saved the body and killed the person."
- **Morning wetting** may be visible in passing if the chapter opens early in the day.
- **Casual Wet Way reference:** A practitioner treating a patient might say "Flow, don't push" as medical advice, blurring the line between spiritual practice and clinical instruction.

## Desired Ending Energy
Unsettled recognition. Hart has found something she cannot dismiss. The frogs' medical philosophy is not ignorance or negligence; it is a deliberate civilizational choice that makes internal sense given their biology and values. The reader should feel the discomfort of encountering a system that is simultaneously more advanced and more "primitive" than human medicine, and should not be sure which parts are which. The comedy is intact, but it now carries weight. The page turns because Hart's verdict implies deeper questions about what the frogs have chosen and what it costs them.

## Constraints and Warnings
- The medical practitioners must feel competent and alien, never bumbling. Their medicine is more advanced than human medicine. They just have different priorities.
- Do NOT turn this into a lecture about frog medicine. All medical information arrives through Hart's investigation, practitioner dialogue, and character reactions.
- The surgery horror scene must be played as genuine mutual incomprehension, not as one side being obviously right.
- Oona's folk remedy defense must feel domestic and real, not staged as a "tradition vs. science" debate.
- Krrik's accidental validation should feel casual and oblivious, not dramatic.
- Hart must remain funny throughout. Her cynicism is her comedy. Even her disturbed verdict should have dry humor in it.
- Do not over-explain the permeability trade-off. State it clearly through dialogue, let Hart react, and move on. The reader is smart enough.
- Background frogs in the treatment pools must be present and visible. The treatment chambers are open, public, communal spaces.
