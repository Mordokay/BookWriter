# Chapter 9: "Show Me the Mechanism"

## Objective
The scale of frog technology becomes real through Reed's encounter with Krrik's laboratory. Reed's scientific worldview is shaken -- the frogs' framework is so far beyond his that explanations sound mystical not because they are vague but because the concepts have no human equivalents. The Pruning's scientific dimension becomes concrete: voss growth is anomalous, Krrik's models are failing, and Reed's human data might matter. Reed offers his team's surface data, bridging the two sciences. The reader should leave feeling the intellectual vertigo of encountering a superior framework and the first genuine scientific anxiety about the Pruning.

## POV
Reed. His internal state is overwhelmed reverence meeting scientific hunger. Every question opens ten more. He is the most openly awestruck member of the team, and this chapter is where that awe acquires humility. He begins thinking he can understand the frogs' technology. He ends realizing that human science might be a footnote in their archive.

## Setting
**Krrik's laboratory:** A sprawling, cluttered, semi-public workspace near the settlement's eastern edge. Not a sealed private space -- other frogs pass through constantly.
- Sensory anchor: the smell of ozone and fermentation, and the faint electrical feeling in the air near the computation arrays.
- The lab is messy. Half-finished experiments in various states of decay. Equipment that looks like rocks and moss and fungal networks. Nothing looks impressive. Everything is impossible.

## Characters Present
- **Reed (POV):** In the lab, asking rapid questions, overwhelmed, pushing glasses up constantly.
- **Krrik:** In his element -- impatient, brilliant, reluctantly explaining. Mottled brown-green, dusty with spore residue, one shorter hind leg. Eyes always on something else.
- **Vesh (wanders in):** Arrives apparently seeking someone else, overhears Krrik explaining quantum displacement, offers a spiritual interpretation. Provokes a brief, rapid argument with Krrik that is genuinely funny. Eats an insect. Leaves, having contributed nothing practical.
- **A junior engineer:** Arrives with a modulation report Krrik dismisses as "incompetent." Young, visibly nervous.
- **A logistics frog:** Demands Krrik attend a coordination meeting. Krrik refuses with a profanity the logistics frog absorbs with visible practice.
- **Marsh (briefly):** Stops by the lab with Oona.
- **Oona (briefly):** Has never been inside the lab. Comments on the mess. ("This is why engineers should not live alone.") Domestic commentary provides comic counterpoint to scientific awe.
- **Engineering frogs at voss models (cluster):** Looking at growth data, murmuring concern. Their worry is contagious.

## Scene Sequence
1. **Open in the lab, mid-conversation.** Reed and Krrik are already deep into it. The lab is cluttered, semi-public, alive with interruptions. Krrik is showing Reed something -- possibly the computation arrays -- while simultaneously fielding a junior engineer's report, which he dismisses as incompetent. Reed's POV registers the scale of the workspace: half-finished experiments, organic equipment that looks like moss and wet rocks, the faint electrical charge in the air. Nothing looks impressive. Everything is impossible.
2. **The organic computation arrays.** Krrik shows Reed the thinking moss. This is the moment Reed understands that organic computation is not an analogy -- the moss beds are literally thinking. More processing power than every human computer combined, tended by a frog with a watering vessel. Reed's reaction should be a mixture of awe and comic inadequacy. He asks questions in his characteristic pile-up of clauses. Krrik answers with clipped impatience, annoyed when Reed does not follow, occasionally surprised when Reed catches up faster than expected.
3. **The quantum displacement apparatus.** Krrik shows Reed the displacement mechanism. It looks like a ring of wet stones with fungal threading. Reed: "It looks like wet rocks." Krrik: "It looks like what it needs to look like." The explanation involves terminology that sounds like physics filtered through biology filtered through poetry. Reed tries to map it onto human physics and fails. The mechanism has no human equivalent. This is the chapter's intellectual vertigo: not that the frogs are more advanced, but that their framework operates in a dimension human science has not imagined.
4. **The Vesh-Krrik argument.** Vesh wanders in, apparently seeking someone else, overhears Krrik explaining displacement to Reed, and offers a spiritual interpretation of the mechanism. Krrik's colors flash orange with irritation. A brief, rapid argument erupts: is displacement engineering or metaphysics? Vesh: it is both, because the universe does not recognize the distinction. Krrik: the universe does not care about your opinion; the stones care about substrate resonance. Genuinely funny, deeply revealing of both characters. Vesh eats an insect, having contributed nothing practical and annoyed Krrik profoundly. Leaves.
5. **Marsh and Oona's brief visit.** Marsh stops by with Oona. Oona has never been inside the lab ("I do not understand anything in here and Krrik does not want me to touch things"). Her domestic commentary on Krrik's mess ("This is why engineers should not live alone") provides comic counterpoint to the scientific awe. Marsh asks one quiet question about the voss substrate and absorbs the answer. They leave. The brief domestic interruption grounds the chapter.
6. **The voss growth models -- the Pruning escalation.** Krrik shows Reed the voss growth models. A cluster of engineering frogs is gathered around the data, murmuring concern. The current growth is anomalous. Krrik's models are failing. He is worried in a way that makes Reed worried, because Krrik does not seem like someone who worries easily. The voss is growing faster than predicted. The implications are planetary.
7. **Reed offers his surface data.** Reed proposes sharing his team's canopy and root density observations. Data gathered from above, from the surface, from outside. Krrik is skeptical -- human science is primitive by Threnn standards -- but does not refuse. The offer is quiet, not dramatic. Reed is not saving the frogs; he is handing them a puzzle piece from a perspective they lack.
8. **The closing image.** Reed stands alone (or nearly alone) in the lab, looking at the displacement apparatus -- wet stones, fungal threading, the most powerful transportation technology on Earth. He thinks that the entire history of human scientific achievement might be a footnote in a footnote of the archive. This is not despair. It is vertigo. And beneath the vertigo, a guilty excitement: he wants to understand it all.

## Key Dialogue Moments
1. **Reed and Krrik on the displacement apparatus:** Reed: "It looks like wet rocks." Krrik: "It looks like what it needs to look like." This exchange encapsulates their dynamic -- Reed's desire to translate into human terms meeting Krrik's impatience with the very need to translate.
2. **The Vesh-Krrik argument:** Vesh offers a spiritual interpretation of displacement. Krrik rejects it. The argument should be fast, sharp, and genuine -- not a staged debate but two characters with irreconcilable frameworks colliding briefly. Vesh's contribution should be both infuriating and not entirely dismissible. Krrik should find this maddening.
3. **Krrik dismissing the junior engineer's report:** "Incompetent." One word. The junior engineer's reaction (nervousness, deference) reveals the social hierarchy within the lab -- Krrik may reject the idea of hierarchy, but his lab has one.
4. **Krrik refusing the logistics frog's coordination meeting request:** A refusal so blunt it borders on profanity, absorbed by the logistics frog with the visible practice of someone who has heard this before. Krrik's social skills are nonexistent. His work makes him indispensable, which makes his rudeness tolerated.
5. **Oona on the lab:** "This is why engineers should not live alone." Or something about the mess, the smell, the incomprehensibility of Krrik's world. Domestic perspective puncturing scientific intensity.
6. **Krrik on the voss growth, quietly:** Something that conveys real worry from someone who does not worry. Perhaps: "We have done this before. We have always done this. But 'always' is a comfort, not a guarantee." The junior engineer's colors going grey in response.

## Emotional Progression
Opens with scientific excitement and comic friction (Reed's enthusiasm meeting Krrik's impatience) --> builds through wonder as the scale of frog technology becomes apparent --> peaks with intellectual vertigo when the displacement mechanism reveals a framework beyond human comprehension --> shifts to comedy with the Vesh-Krrik argument and Oona's visit --> darkens when the voss growth models show anomalous, frightening patterns --> ends with Reed's closing image of vertigo and guilty excitement. The chapter moves from wonder to humility to anxiety.

## Canon Facts to Preserve
- Reed is Dr. Elias Kwame Reed, 34. Wire-rimmed glasses. Wiry, restless. Talks in piling clauses. Uses "wait, wait, wait" as a verbal placeholder. Fastest talker of the five.
- Krrik: no pool affiliation. 19 frog-years. Mottled brown-green, dusty with spore residue. One shorter hind leg, distinctive hopping gait he refuses to have corrected. Eyes always focused on something other than the speaker. Speaks in fragments when working. Allergic to metaphor.
- Vesh: 22 frog-years. 5 cm. Vivid emerald green with gold flecks. One eye slightly larger. Alternates between grandiose spiritual pronouncements and casual vulgarity.
- Oona: 16 frog-years. Round-bodied, olive-green, orange throat patch.
- The lab is near the settlement's eastern edge. Contains organic computation arrays (neural moss beds), quantum displacement apparatus (ring of wet stones with fungal threading), voss research samples, half-finished experiments.
- Organic computation: biological computing in cultivated moss beds. Neural tissue cultures, fungal mycelial networks. Must be fed, watered, maintained like gardens. Can process more than human computers.
- Quantum displacement: moves matter instantaneously. Requires functional voss networks at origin and destination. The apparatus looks unimpressive.
- Voss: a living substrate in deep forest root systems. Pale, faintly luminous mycelial network. Warm, slightly wet, breathes. Generates energy continuously. Currently growing anomalously fast due to cosmic radiation peaks.
- The Pruning prunes overgrown voss using displacement, synchronized consciousness, and biological modulation. The current Pruning is more severe than usual.
- Reed's surface data (root density, canopy changes) captured voss growth patterns from above -- a perspective the frogs' internal sensors lack.
- Krrik and Vesh actively irritate each other -- Krrik thinks Vesh turns science into religion; Vesh thinks Krrik reduces the sacred to circuits.

## Wet Way Elements
Minimal and indirect. Vesh's spiritual interpretation of displacement during the argument is the main Wet Way beat. He might reference the universe or the Croaking in his interpretation of why displacement works. Krrik should dismiss this with visible irritation. The tension between Krrik's engineering perspective and Vesh's spiritual overlay is itself a dramatization of the theological debate about whether the rituals and the technology are separable.

## Jargon Watch
- **organic computation arrays:** Do NOT use in narration. Describe: "the thinking moss" or "moss beds that were apparently calculating" or "a patch of living green that contained more processing power than MIT."
- **quantum displacement apparatus:** Do NOT use the full term in narration. Describe: "the ring of wet stones with fungal threading" or "the apparatus that folds space, which looks like rocks in a puddle." Reed might use "quantum displacement" in dialogue (he is a scientist), but the narration should translate.
- **voss substrate:** "Voss" can be used as a proper noun (the frogs call it that). In narration, describe what it looks like: "the pale, faintly luminous material threaded through the roots, warm to the touch, faintly breathing."
- **biological modulation:** Do NOT use. Describe: "the chemicals Krrik was brewing to slow the growth."
- **substrate resonance / field harmonics:** These are Krrik's technical terms. He might use them in dialogue (and be annoyed when Reed does not follow). The narration should not use them. Translate through Reed's attempt to understand.
- **chromatophore:** Do NOT use. "Krrik's skin flashed orange with irritation." "The junior engineer's colors went grey."
- **neural synchronization:** Do NOT use. If relevant: "the operators thinking together" or "the consciousness thing Vesh talks about."
- **cutaneous respiration:** Reed might use this term in dialogue (he is a biologist). If he does, let the context translate it.

## Desired Ending Energy
Intellectual vertigo. Reed standing in a lab that looks like a garden and contains the most advanced science on Earth, feeling the scale of what the frogs have achieved and the smallness of human knowledge in comparison. But also: guilty excitement. The worst scientific crisis in frog history is also the most extraordinary scientific environment in human history. And his data might actually matter. The reader should feel both the wonder and the anxiety.

## Constraints and Warnings
- **The science must arrive through Reed's excitement and Krrik's impatience, not through neutral explanation.** This chapter is at high risk for becoming an info-dump about frog technology. Every piece of information must come through the friction between Reed wanting to understand and Krrik wanting to work. No narrator-delivered science lessons.
- **The lab must feel semi-public and interrupted.** Frogs pass through constantly. The junior engineer, the logistics frog, Vesh wandering in, Marsh and Oona stopping by -- the lab is a workplace in a community, not a sealed tutorial space.
- **Krrik is not a kindly teacher.** He is impatient, rude, and brilliant. He explains to Reed only because Reed asks useful questions and because explaining sometimes helps Krrik think. He does not enjoy it.
- **The Vesh-Krrik argument must be genuinely funny.** Two incompatible worldviews colliding at speed. Not a formal debate but a flash of genuine irritation. Vesh should be both annoying and not entirely wrong. Krrik should be both right and slightly narrow.
- **Reed's human data is a small contribution, not a salvation.** He offers it. Krrik is skeptical. The frogs do not need humans to save them. They need a puzzle piece from a perspective they lacked. This must not feel like a chosen-one moment.
- **Constraint 1:** The humans must never become saviors. Reed's data is useful, not essential. The frogs do almost all the work.
- **Constraint 6:** The primitive-impossible contrast is at maximum in the lab: the most powerful technology on Earth looks like moss and wet rocks in a messy room.
- **Constraint 17:** No exposition dumps. This cannot be stressed enough for this chapter.
- **Constraint 24:** Mystery is preserved. Not every technology gets a full explanation. The displacement mechanism remains partially mysterious even after Krrik explains it.
