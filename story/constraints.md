# Constraints

These are hard rules. Violating any of these means the chapter or section needs revision.

## Character Constraints

1. The humans must never become saviors, chosen ones, or moral superiors to the frogs.
2. The humans play a minor supporting role in resolving the central conflict. The frogs do almost all the work.
3. Martin Quill is the soft protagonist (30% arc focus). The ensemble carries the remaining 70%.
4. Every major character must have a distinct voice — recognizable without dialogue tags.
5. The frogs must not sound like contemporary humans with quirky vocabulary. Their speech must reflect alien assumptions, short lifespans, permeable biology, and choral thinking.

## World Constraints

6. The primitive-impossible contrast must be maintained throughout. The frogs cannot become uniformly advanced or uniformly primitive.
7. The self-renewing energy material is casual and mundane to the frogs — never treated as guarded, sacred, or dramatic.
8. Frog lifespans are significantly shorter than human lifespans. This must visibly shape their politics, culture, architecture, education, spirituality, and emotional rhythms.
9. The frog civilization is millions of years old and predates human civilization entirely.

## Plot Constraints

10. The central conflict is world-ending but cyclical — something the frogs handle roughly every generation. It is not a unique crisis.
11. The conflict unravels gradually through the story. It does not arrive as a sudden crisis.
12. The ending must be ambiguous — not purely tragic, not purely peaceful, not purely darkly funny. Each biologist reacts differently to the ultimatum.
13. The frogs must never collapse into saints or villains. They remain admirable and troubling simultaneously.

## Style Constraints

14. Dialogue is the primary vehicle. Most of the book happens in conversation and interaction.
15. **Scenes must not feel like one-on-one interviews.** Most scenes involve 3+ characters — humans and frogs mixing freely. Group conversations, cross-pairing, overheard frog-to-frog arguments, and background characters are essential. The pairings are gravitational tendencies, not rigid assignments. The world must feel alive and populated.
16. Minimal description. One-detail rule: one smell, one sound, one object, one behavioral detail — not a full scenic paragraph.
17. No exposition dumps. Worldbuilding arrives through discovery, conflict, jokes, mistakes, and consequences.
18. No lectures disguised as dialogue. Ideas emerge from friction, not formal debate.
19. Detective-novel pacing. Every chapter moves the reader forward.
20. Comedy comes from earnestness and inherent absurdity, not from narrator winking or forced whimsy.
21. **No jargon the reader would need a glossary for.** Technical terms from canon files (chromatophore, cutaneous respiration, organic computation arrays, etc.) must be translated into concrete, visual, often funny descriptions in prose. Describe what things look like, not what they're called. See `story/humanizer-checklist.md` Section 1 for the full translation table. Scientists in dialogue may use technical terms if the narration translates them.

## Thematic Constraints

22. No one-to-one political allegory. The book attacks certainty, not a specific ideology.
23. No neat moral conclusions. The central question — whether the frogs are wise or merely sophisticated at surviving absurdity — must remain unresolved.
24. Mystery is preserved. Not every technology, practice, or metaphysical question gets a full explanation.
25. Every worldview presented (human and frog) must reveal both insight and absurdity.

## Absolute Do-Not List

The book must never become:
- A TED Talk with amphibians
- A generic first-contact novel with frogs pasted on
- A satire that only mocks one side
- A lore dump
- A chosen-one plot
- A cute animal fantasy
- A grim dystopia reveal
- A philosophy seminar with no appetite, smell, sex, disease, or inconvenience
- A joke machine with no emotional residue
