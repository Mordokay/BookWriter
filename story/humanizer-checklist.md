# Humanizer Agent Checklist

This checklist serves two purposes:
1. **A drafting guide.** Chapter writers must follow these rules while writing. Do not produce jargon-heavy, AI-patterned prose that needs to be cleaned up later. Get it right the first time.
2. **A final-pass checklist.** The humanizer agent runs after the revision-writer as a final quality gate. Fix anything that survived the earlier passes.

The goal: prose that reads as if written by a skilled human novelist influenced by Vonnegut and Adams. Accessible, funny, sharp, natural. No reader should ever need a glossary, feel lectured at, or suspect a machine wrote this.

**Only modify the prose/Draft section. Do not touch Continuity Notes, Revision Log, or Chapter Intent.**

---

## 1. The Jargon Rule (CRITICAL)

This is an absurd comedy. The language must be accessible, concrete, and often funny. No reader should need a science degree or a glossary to follow the story.

### The Core Principle

**If a term would make a reader pause and think "what does that mean?", replace it with a description of what it actually looks like, sounds like, or does.** Prefer concrete, visual, often funny descriptions over technical vocabulary. The comedy lives in describing the extraordinary in ordinary language.

### Frog Biology and Technology Translation Table

### Rules

- [ ] **No term should appear in prose that a casual reader cannot picture.** "Chromatophore" means nothing to most people. "His skin turned orange" means everything.
- [ ] **Technical terms can appear in DIALOGUE from scientists** (Reed might say "cutaneous respiration" — then the narration or another character should translate: "Breathing through their skin, he meant"). This creates comedy: Reed being precise, the situation being absurd.
- [ ] **The frogs can use their own terms** (voss, Threnn-song, the Wet Way) because these are proper nouns in their world. But the narration should always show what these things look, sound, and feel like, not just name them.
- [ ] **Repeat descriptions are fine.** It is better to say "their colors shifted" for the twentieth time than to introduce "chromatophore modulation" for variety. Readers process familiar descriptions faster than new jargon.
- [ ] **When in doubt, describe what the POV character actually sees, hears, and smells.** Not the mechanism. Not the scientific term. The sensory experience.
- [ ] **Comedy first.** The funniest version of a description is usually also the most accessible. "A patch of moss that was apparently smarter than the entire human species" is better than "a neural computation substrate of extraordinary processing density."

### The Glossary Test

After writing a chapter, scan for any word or phrase that would require a glossary entry. If you find one, replace it. This book has no glossary. It doesn't need one. If you can't explain it in the sentence where it appears, you're being too technical.

## 2. Em-dash and punctuation habits

- [ ] Zero em-dashes ("--" or "---") in prose. Replace with commas, periods, colons, semicolons, or restructure the sentence.
- [ ] Semicolons used sparingly (no more than 5-8 per chapter). Most readers experience semicolons as academic. Prefer periods or commas.
- [ ] Colons used sparingly for elaboration. Not every list or explanation needs a colon. Sometimes a period and a new sentence is more natural.
- [ ] Ellipsis used only for interrupted or trailing thought, not as a general pause device.

---

## 3. AI-tell words and phrases

Flag and replace any of these (they appear frequently in AI writing and rarely in published fiction):

**Single words:** delve, tapestry, myriad, whilst, amidst, amongst, furthermore, moreover, nonetheless, interplay, nuance (as adjective), juxtaposition, visceral (overused), palpable, ethereal, ephemeral, testament, cacophony, symphony (when not about music), gossamer, iridescent, luminescent (check frequency), ineffable, corporeal, primordial, eldritch, uncanny (check frequency), liminal (check frequency), profound (check frequency), intricate (overused), resonate/resonated (overused), ponder/pondered, muse/mused, grapple/grappled with

**Phrases:** "a testament to," "a symphony of," "a tapestry of," "a dance of," "the interplay between," "sent a shiver down," "in the grand scheme," "it was as if," "seemed to (verb) with," "couldn't help but," "found himself/herself (verb)ing," "the weight of (abstraction)," "hung heavy in the air," "cut through the silence," "washed over him/her," "the world seemed to," "time stood still," "his/her heart sank," "a knowing look," "spoke volumes," "the silence was deafening," "pregnant pause," "steeled himself/herself," "a moment that stretched," "something shifted in," "the air between them," "the gravity of," "not for the first time," "in ways he/she couldn't quite articulate," "something ancient and," "there was something almost"

**Note:** Some of these words are fine in the right context. The issue is density. If "visceral" appears once in a chapter, it might work. If it appears three times, it's a tell. Use judgment.

## 4. Sentence structure patterns

- [ ] No more than 3 consecutive sentences starting with the same pronoun. Break clusters with environmental subjects, body-part subjects, participial openings, sentence fragments, or dialogue.
- [ ] No more than 2 consecutive sentences using the same structure (e.g., compound with "and," or subject-verb-object). Vary rhythm.
- [ ] Watch for artificially balanced constructions: "Not X but Y. Not A but B. Not C but D." One pair is powerful. Two is a pattern. Three is a machine.
- [ ] Watch for triple-stacked parallel phrases: "the X of Y, the A of B, the C of D." One set per page maximum.
- [ ] Avoid overly symmetrical paragraphs where the last sentence mirrors or answers the first. Humans don't write in such tidy loops.
- [ ] **Choppy comma-staccato sequences.** Watch for runs of short sentences packed with commas that create a robotic, instructional rhythm. Fix by combining short sentences into longer ones, varying clause length, or replacing comma-separated imperatives with flowing thought. The goal is sentence-length variety within every paragraph.
- [ ] **The list-of-three trap.** AI loves listing three things: "the beauty, the horror, and the absurdity." Once per page is fine. More than that and the prose starts to feel generated. Sometimes two things is enough. Sometimes four. Humans don't naturally default to three.

---

## 5. Vocabulary accessibility

- [ ] No Latin species names in prose (use common names or descriptions).
- [ ] No word that a typical adult reader would need to look up, unless the context makes its meaning clear.
- [ ] Technical/botanical terms are acceptable only if they're common enough that most readers have encountered them.
- [ ] If a character recognizes a species or phenomenon, describe what they see. Don't name the taxonomy.
- [ ] **Apply the Jargon Rule (Section 1) aggressively.** Scan every paragraph for terms that would require a glossary. Replace with concrete descriptions.

---

## 6. Description and metaphor

- [ ] No stacked metaphors (two different metaphors for the same thing in the same paragraph).
- [ ] No metaphors that explain themselves: "like a door closing, shutting out the light."
- [ ] Similes should be concrete and unexpected. Flag any simile that could appear in any novel without modification.
- [ ] "The way" as a simile connector: maximum 3 per chapter.
- [ ] "As if" hedging: maximum 3 per chapter. When the description is vivid enough, state it directly.
- [ ] Avoid describing emotions with abstract nouns: "a wave of dread," "a surge of panic." Show the physical sensation instead.
- [ ] **Descriptions should be funny when possible.** This is a comedy. A frog city should be described in ways that make you smile, not in ways that sound like a nature documentary narrated by God.

---

## 7. Filter words and distancing

- [ ] Remove "he/she felt," "he/she noticed," "he/she realized," "he/she could see/hear/feel," "he/she was aware of." Present the sensation directly.
- [ ] Remove "seemed to," "appeared to," "began to" (when the action simply happens).
- [ ] Remove "found himself/herself" constructions.
- [ ] Remove "something like" and "something that was almost" unless genuine ambiguity is the point.

---

## 8. Dialogue and interior voice

- [ ] Interior thoughts should sound like a real person thinking, not like polished prose. Short. Fragmented. Sometimes grammatically rough.
- [ ] Each character's internal register should have its own quality (Reed thinks in technical excitement; Hart thinks in risk assessment; Quill thinks in spiraling questions; Vale thinks in power analysis; Marsh thinks in sensory detail).
- [ ] Frog dialogue should feel alien and strange, never like a therapist or self-help book. But also never incomprehensible. The reader should understand what the frog means while feeling that the frog arrived at the thought from a completely different direction.
- [ ] **Frog dialogue should be funny.** Not joke-funny. Funny because the frog is completely serious about something absurd, or completely casual about something terrifying, or completely precise about something humans find disgusting.

---

## 9. Pacing and padding

- [ ] Flag any sentence that restates what the previous sentence already established.
- [ ] Flag any paragraph where the last 1-2 sentences could be cut without losing information or impact.
- [ ] Flag any description that goes on longer than the moment warrants.
- [ ] Physical actions should take roughly proportional prose-space to their real-time duration.
- [ ] **Every paragraph should earn its place.** If it doesn't reveal character, advance plot, build world, or land a joke, cut it.

---

## 10. Read-aloud test

- [ ] Read the prose mentally as if speaking it aloud. Flag any sentence that feels unnatural to say.
- [ ] Flag any paragraph where the rhythm is monotonously even or monotonously choppy. Good prose varies.
- [ ] **The Vonnegut test:** Could this sentence appear in Cat's Cradle? If it's too ornate, too serious, or too self-impressed, simplify it.

---

## 11. Comedy quality check

- [ ] Does the chapter have moments that are genuinely funny, not just "clever"?
- [ ] Is the humor emerging from earnestness and situation, not from the narrator winking?
- [ ] Are the funny moments also revealing something about character or world?
- [ ] Is there at least one moment where something profound and something ridiculous happen in the same beat?
- [ ] Would a reader smile, or just nod in appreciation? Smiling is better.

---

## 12. Overall naturalness

- [ ] Does this read like a chapter from a published novel you'd find in a bookstore?
- [ ] Would a reader suspicious of AI-generated text flag anything?
- [ ] Is there anything that feels "too perfect," too symmetrical, too neatly constructed? Humans are messier. Good prose has controlled imperfection.
- [ ] Are there any moments where the writing is showing off rather than serving the story?
- [ ] **The friend test:** If you read this passage aloud to a friend, would they be engaged, or would their eyes glaze? If glaze, rewrite.

---

## Output format

The humanizer agent should:
1. Read the full chapter prose
2. Make all fixes directly using Edit calls (do not just report issues)
3. After all fixes, write a brief summary of changes made to the chapter's Revision Log section
4. Report a final count of changes made

## What NOT to change

- Do not flatten the literary voice into generic commercial fiction. The prose should remain sharp and controlled.
- Do not remove all unusual words. Remove only the ones that break immersion or scream "AI."
- Do not simplify complex sentences that are complex for a reason (building tension, mirroring disorientation).
- Do not touch the Continuity Notes, Revision Log headers, or Chapter Intent sections.
- Do not change character voice, plot details, or factual content.
- Preserve the story's existing motifs and callbacks.
- **Do not make the prose bland in pursuit of accessibility.** The goal is vivid, concrete, and funny. Not simple. Not flat. Alive.
