# Chapter 19: The Growth

The lab had acquired a population.

Reed stood in the corridor entrance and counted. Krrik. Two junior engineers. Three frogs he did not recognize from Krrik's regular team, who were crouched over a secondary moss bed that had not been active the last time Reed visited. The Siberian engineer from the coordination meeting, compact and grey, occupying a stone ledge near the largest computation array rigid and still, clearly considering the room's chaos a character flaw. And, at the back, near the water channel, a pair of frogs who appeared to be arguing about whether the nutrient drip on the secondary array needed adjusting or whether adjusting it would compromise the recalibration Krrik had started an hour ago.

The moss beds were glowing brighter than Reed had ever seen them. Not the gentle bioluminescence he had come to associate with their resting state. This was active, strained, the pale green shifted toward something almost white at the edges. The computation arrays were overworked, running simultaneous models across every bed in the room, and the beds were responding the way any living system responded to sustained overload: they were getting hot and complaining about it in light.

The smell was worse than usual. Ozone and fermentation and the sweet metallic scent that frogs produced when stressed, concentrated by too many bodies in an enclosed space. Reed's eyes watered. He wiped his glasses on his shirt, pushed them back up, and stepped into the room.

Krrik was at the largest bed. His forelimb rested flat against the moss surface. The display beneath his touch was dense, branching networks of light pulsing through the green in patterns Reed had learned to read over the past weeks. Voss growth models. But the model he was seeing now bore almost no resemblance to the one Krrik had shown him in the early days. That model had been organized, bounded. An expanding network with clear edges. This one had no edges. The light pushed to the borders of the moss bed and continued, the display folding back on itself to show layers of growth overlapping in three dimensions, and the bright center, which had been the primary substrate mass beneath the settlement, had expanded until it occupied a third of the entire projection.

"Krrik."

"I see you." Krrik did not look up. His mottled brown-green skin was cycling through its unconscious processing patterns faster than Reed had ever seen, a churn of color that made him look like a stone being rotated under shifting light. "Shut the door."

"There is no door."

"Then stand in it and pretend."

One of the junior engineers, a small dark-green frog with anxious yellow at the edges of her coloring, looked up from the secondary bed. "The modulation compounds are degrading faster than we can reformulate them. The eastern sectors absorbed the latest application in, in." She looked at the bed for confirmation. "Forty-one hours."

"Forty-one," Reed said. He did the arithmetic in his head against the timeline he had been tracking. The previous application had lasted three days. Before that, ten. Before that, the projections had called for ten, and reality had shredded them. The compounds were not just failing. They were losing ground in a curve that bent the wrong way. "That is almost half the duration of the last round."

"The voss adapts," Krrik said. His voice was flat. The same operational flatness Reed had heard when the models first began failing weeks ago, but deeper now, stripped further. "Each application selects for resistant growth. The resistant growth propagates. The next application is less effective. The next is less effective than that. This is not complicated biology. This is evolutionary pressure on an accelerated timescale."

"Can you reformulate faster than it adapts?"

"I have been reformulating faster than it adapts. The gap is closing."

The Siberian engineer spoke from her ledge. She had not moved since Reed entered. Her flat grey skin absorbed the moss beds' glow without reflecting any of it, which made her look like a gap cut into the light.

"The gap closed in the northern quadrant two days ago." Her voice was clipped, toneless, stripped of the diplomatic layering that the Amazon frogs applied to every sentence. "Our modulation team exhausted their reformulation options for the deep-root sectors. We have shifted to physical suppression. Cutting. Burning. Your methods from the early cycles."

Reed looked at her. "You're cutting voss. Physically."

"We are cutting voss. Physically. With tools. The irony is not lost."

"Your northern growth was seventeen percent above projections a week ago," Reed said. "Where is it now?"

"Thirty-one."

Krrik's colors stopped cycling. For a moment his entire body went a flat, solid brown that Reed had never seen on him. Then the churn resumed.

"Amazon eastern sectors?" Reed asked.

"Thirty-eight percent above projections as of this morning's reading," the junior engineer said. She said it the way someone reports test results they have checked three times and hope are wrong. "Global growth rate across all monitored sectors is approaching ninety percent above the resting boundary."

Reed's glasses needed pushing up. He pushed them. Ninety percent. Weeks ago, when Krrik had first shown him the growth models, the number had been sixty-seven, and that had made the room go quiet. Ninety was not a room-going-quiet number. Ninety was a number that changed the nature of the questions you were asking.

"Show me the compound degradation curve alongside the growth curve," Reed said.

"You cannot read the arrays."

"I can read a curve. I have been reading curves for twelve years. Show me."

Krrik looked at him. Both eyes, which was rare. Then he touched the moss bed and the display shifted, and two branching patterns of light overlaid each other in the green depths of the living computation medium. One was the growth model, the pulsing expansion Reed had been watching. The other was something new: a fading trail of pale yellow that retreated as the growth advanced. The modulation compounds. Losing ground. The two curves were not parallel. They were converging. And the point where they met, where the yellow trail vanished into the advancing white, was not far.

"Weeks," Reed said.

"Less."

"How much less?"

Krrik removed his forelimb from the moss. "Come with me."

They moved to the back of the lab, past the arguing nutrient-drip frogs, past a table covered in stone dishes containing chemical preparations in various states of development. Krrik stopped at a basin Reed had not seen before. Small, shallow, filled with a thick amber liquid that caught the moss light and held it. Around the basin, seven sealed stone containers sat in a row. Each one was labeled in markings Reed could not read, but the progression was visible. The first container was nearly empty. The seventh was full.

"These are the reformulated compounds," Krrik said. He pointed to the first. "This was the formula active during your first visit to the lab. It held the growth for twelve days." He pointed to the fourth. "This is the formula that failed three days ago. It held for three days." He pointed to the seventh. "This is the latest. Applied this morning. I do not yet know how long it will hold." He looked at the basin. "I am running out of chemical space. The voss is alive. It learns. Not the way you learn. The way an immune system learns. Each compound teaches it what to resist."

Reed stood over the basin. The amber liquid looked warm. It looked like something that should work, that had worked, that was being outpaced by a substrate two hundred million years old that had been adapting to chemical intervention for longer than flowering plants had existed.

"You need my surface data," Reed said.

"I need your surface data."

The junior engineer had followed them. She stood at a respectful distance, her colors grey and uncertain. The Siberian engineer had not followed. She sat on her ledge, studying her own data on a small computation pad she had brought from the northern settlement, her grey skin unchanged, her posture a monument to the Siberian conviction that emotion was a waste of processing capacity.

Krrik spoke without ceremony. "The modulators are insufficient. I am modifying them. I need your surface data. You see from above. We see from within. Neither view is complete."

"What specifically?"

"Growth density at the surface boundaries. Your canopy stress readings correlated with root expansion. Your soil temperature gradients. I can model from inside the substrate but the surface behavior is a variable I cannot observe from down here, and the monitoring stations have not been calibrated for this kind of emergence because surface emergence was not supposed to happen." A flash of orange across his skin. "I am asking for help. Note this. I do not intend to do it again."

Reed was already pulling the dried moss sheets from the satchel he carried everywhere. Six months of surface data, transcribed by hand because human electronics did not survive the settlement's humidity. Crude instruments, primitive methodology, pattern recognition and patience. The broken window that tells you about the wind.

"I need the latest canopy readings correlated against the eastern sector growth data," Reed said. "If the surface emergence is following the root corridors, there should be a stress gradient in the canopy that maps to the underground expansion. I can show you what that looks like from above."

Krrik took the moss sheets without acknowledgment. He carried them to the largest computation array and began feeding the data in, pressing patterns into the moss surface with his forelimbs. The display shifted. New light threaded through the existing models, pale and different, the surface perspective overlaying the underground topology.

The Siberian engineer finally moved. She hopped down from her ledge and crossed the lab to the array, which was the most physical urgency Reed had seen from any Siberian. She studied the combined display for twelve seconds.

"The surface gradient confirms the subsurface expansion rate," she said. "Your underground model is conservative. The actual growth front is further advanced than your projections show."

"I know," Krrik said.

"Then your projections are useless."

"My projections are being revised. That is why I am standing here instead of attending the coordination meeting I was summoned to. Would you like to be helpful or would you like to continue identifying the problem?"

"I would like you to admit that the modulation approach has a failure horizon, and that failure horizon is closer than you have communicated to the coordination office."

Krrik did not respond to this. His silence, which was the loudest sound in the room, lasted four seconds. The junior engineer's skin went three shades paler.

"I have communicated what the data supports," Krrik said. "The data changes hourly. I will update the office when I have something stable enough to update them with."

The Siberian engineer returned to her ledge. The verdict she did not deliver hung in the air alongside the ozone and the stress-sweat: Krrik was not sharing how bad it was. Whether this was scientific caution or political calculation, she did not say. Her grey skin said nothing at all.

Reed left the lab twenty minutes later. He carried three moss sheets covered in Krrik's notations, data requests for surface readings he would need to compile from his field records. His head was full of growth curves and compound degradation rates and a number, ninety percent, that sat in his thoughts like a stone in a shoe.

The residential corridor he walked through on his way back to quarters told the rest of the story.

The eastern section of the warrens was flooded.

Not with water. With voss. The living substrate had pushed through the root wall of a residential corridor, a slow intrusion that had probably been happening for days, but what had been a faint warmth in the stone and a slight luminescence at the seams was now a rupture. The wall had split where a root joint met the carved stone, and through the gap, pale luminous growth spilled into the corridor. Warm, faintly pulsing, it spread across the floor in tendrils that followed the water channels with the patience of something that had been growing for two hundred million years and was not in a hurry but was also not stopping.

A frog family stood in the corridor outside their chambers. Two adults and three juveniles, the juveniles small enough to fit in Reed's palm. Their possessions were in woven baskets beside them: sleeping moss, food containers, a small stone dish that might have been decorative or functional or both. The female's throat patch cycled between amber and grey, a rapid oscillation of worry. The male's colors were flat, controlled, the deliberate stillness of a parent performing calm for children who were not fooled.

A neighbor had emerged from the adjacent chamber and was speaking rapidly, gesturing toward her own space. An offer of room. The family's eldest juvenile, barely large enough to have completed metamorphosis, was staring at the voss growth pushing through the wall. The glow reflected in his eyes.

Two maintenance frogs worked at the rupture. One was applying a thick paste to the edges of the breach, a chemical retardant that Reed recognized from Krrik's lab. The other was physically cutting back the tendrils where they had spread across the floor, using a curved stone tool with a motion that was precise and futile, because behind each cut tendril, the growth continued, slower now where the paste held, but continuing.

Oona arrived from the far end of the corridor with four other frogs, each carrying supplies. Cleaning materials. Food containers. Blankets woven from dried moss. She moved through the small crowd with the unhurried competence of someone who had organized things before and did not need a title to do it. Her orange throat patch was dim, which was unusual. Oona's throat patch was her broadcasting system, brightening with gossip, dimming with boredom. Dim in a crisis meant the crisis had moved past the point where gossip could process it.

"This never used to happen until the third week," Oona said. She was not addressing anyone specifically. She was addressing the corridor, the situation, the voss itself. "The third week. We are in the second week. Someone should tell the voss it is early."

A frog behind her, older, carrying a stack of woven mats, made a sound that was either a laugh or the vocal equivalent of shaking her head. "The Croaking is loud today," the older frog said, which was frog shorthand for everything feels connected, everything feels alive, and in this context carried the additional weight of everything feels connected and the connected thing is eating our homes.

Reed helped. He did not plan to help. He was carrying moss sheets covered in growth data and heading back to quarters to compile surface readings, and then the juvenile frog dropped one of the family's food containers and it rolled into the voss tendrils on the floor, and Reed bent down and picked it up. The container was warm. The tendril it had touched was warm. Everything was warm, the walls and the floor and the air, the settlement running a low fever it could not break.

Marsh appeared at the far end of the corridor with Oona's supplies team, carrying a stone pot that steamed faintly. Food for the displaced. She saw Reed and raised her eyebrows, a question and an acknowledgment, and continued past him toward the family. Behind her, a frog Reed did not know was explaining to the maintenance crew that her sleeping chamber, three doors down, also had warmth coming through the wall, and was that normal, and the maintenance frogs exchanged a look that was not normal, not at all, but what they said was: "We will assess it after the current repair."

The female from the displaced family accepted food from Marsh. She held the container in both forelimbs and looked at the growth pushing through her wall and said, to Oona: "Where do we go?"

"West," Oona said. "The western warrens have space. The families from section four moved there two days ago. It is crowded but the walls are holding."

"The walls are holding," the female repeated. The repetition was not reassurance. It was a question wearing the grammatical clothes of a statement.

"The walls are holding," Oona said again, and her throat patch did not brighten and her voice did not lift and she looked, for a moment, like a frog who was managing a situation she did not believe was manageable.

Reed left the residential section carrying the image of the juvenile staring at the glowing wall and the sound of "the walls are holding" spoken twice without conviction.

The staging grounds were louder.

The open area near the settlement's western perimeter, which during the vibration games had been a place of competition and fermented-fly contests, was now an operational space. Displacement operators sat in the coordination pools, twenty of them in two concentric arcs, their bodies half-submerged, running synchronization drills. Their colors moved in unison, patterns clicking through the green-gold-violet sequence that meant they were thinking together, aligning their cognitive outputs for the displacement operations that would push the voss back when the time came.

Three operators had been pulled from the pool and were sitting on the stone edge, attended by medical frogs. One of them was the young operator Reed remembered from Reed's nocturnal walk through the corridors. No. That was Hart's walk. Hart had told him about the operator who cried three nights out of five. This frog, small and green her coloring washed out, given more than she had, sat on the stone with her forelimbs resting on her knees and her eyes unfocused, looking at something that was not in the room.

Hart was there. She stood near the medical frogs with a water sample in one hand and a questioning expression that Reed recognized. The expression meant she had found something in the data and needed someone to argue with her about it.

"Three operators pulled from the last session," Hart said when she saw him. "Three. Carried out. One of them lost color coherence for six minutes, which their medical team is calling 'transient dissonance,' which is a polite way of saying her brain forgot how to run her body." She held up the water sample. "The pool chemistry after a synchronization session has twice the chemical load of a normal pool. Their skin is absorbing whatever the group produces during merging, and it accumulates. They are not draining between sessions."

"The schedule does not allow draining between sessions," Reed said. "Noll expanded to four sessions per day."

"At what point does preparation become damage?"

Reed looked at the operators in the pool. Twenty frogs, synchronized, their colors moving together, the precision beautiful if you forgot what it cost. He looked at the three on the edge. At the young operator who had not regained focus. At the medical frogs checking chemical levels in her skin with instruments that looked like twigs dipped in moss.

"At the point where the alternative is the voss eating the settlement."

"That is what they said about the last three sessions too."

"Because it is true about the last three sessions too. Hart, the eastern residential section has voss growing through the walls. I just walked through it. Families displaced. Maintenance cutting it back with hand tools. Oona organizing refugees. The growth is at ninety percent above the resting boundary and the modulation compounds are failing in under two days."

Hart set down the water sample. She looked at him. The narrow, still look she reserved for information that confirmed her worst assessment.

"Ninety."

"Ninety. The Siberians' northern quadrant is at thirty-one. The Amazon eastern sectors, thirty-eight. The compounds Krrik is brewing to slow it down are losing effectiveness faster than he can reformulate them. The voss adapts. Each application teaches it what to resist."

Hart looked at the operators. At the medical frogs. At the young frog on the stone edge whose eyes still were not tracking.

"Then the operators are the only thing standing between the settlement and an expanding planetary substrate that has been alive longer than most of the life on this planet," she said. "And the operators are being ground down by daily synchronization drills that their bodies are not recovering from."

"Yes."

"Then we are in trouble."

"We have been in trouble. The trouble got worse while we were arguing about breathing circles."

Hart picked up her water sample. She did not argue further. She did not need to. They stood at the edge of the staging grounds, two human biologists in a frog civilization preparing for a planetary operation that had been conducted roughly every generation for two hundred million years, and watched the operators drill, their colors moving in patterns of green and gold and violet, the beautiful synchronized motion of a species that had learned to think together because thinking alone was not enough to survive what came for them every thirty years.

Reed went back to the lab.

The population had thinned. The arguing nutrient-drip frogs had gone. Two of the unfamiliar engineers had left. The Siberian engineer remained on her ledge, still studying her computation pad with the focused patience of someone for whom data was a form of emotional regulation. Krrik was at the largest moss bed. The junior engineer stood beside him, holding a fresh stone container of modulation compound. The amber liquid caught the light.

The lab was quieter. The hum of the overworked computation arrays filled the space, the living sound of moss thinking harder than it was designed to, and beneath it the constant background murmur of the settlement's water channels, carrying vibrations from everywhere, the sound of twelve hundred frogs going about the business of existing in a civilization that might, for the first time in recorded memory, not be enough.

"Krrik," Reed said.

Krrik touched the moss bed. The display shifted. Reed's surface data, now integrated, showed the combined picture. The growth from below, the stress from above. The modulation compounds retreating. The operators' capacity, represented as a thin line of blue light that was supposed to be the last defense, the synchronized displacement that would push the voss back to its resting boundary.

The blue line was thin.

"We have done this before," Krrik said. He said it quietly. Not to Reed. Not to the Siberian engineer. To the room, maybe. To the moss bed. To himself. "We have always done this."

The junior engineer stood very still. Her stone container of fresh compound rested in her forelimbs. Her colors, which had been cycling through anxious greens and greys all day, went flat. All color drained from her skin in a single, silent wave, leaving her a pale, translucent grey that showed the faint shadow of her internal structures beneath the surface. An involuntary display of fear so total that her body abandoned every other signal to broadcast only this: I am afraid.

Nobody commented on it. Krrik did not look at her. The Siberian engineer did not look at her. Reed did not look at her. They all saw it. They all saw each other not looking. The lab held the silence for three seconds, and the moss beds pulsed their overworked light, and the water channels carried the sound of a settlement that did not know the numbers Reed had spent the day learning.

"But 'always' is a comfort," Krrik said. "Not a guarantee."

## Chapter Intent
This chapter shows the Pruning crisis touching every layer of settlement life through a single day in Reed's eyes. The seven scenes move from the lab's controlled panic through the residential flooding to the staging grounds and back, each scene revealing a different dimension of the same emergency. The lab scenes establish the operational data: the modulation compounds are failing in an accelerating curve, the voss adapts faster than Krrik can reformulate, and the global growth numbers have crossed from alarming to genuinely dangerous. The Siberian engineer provides a second scientific perspective that is blunter and less diplomatic, confirming that the northern quadrant has already fallen back to physical methods. Krrik's private admission to Reed marks a turning point in their relationship and in the crisis: the engineer who refused help is now asking for it. The residential flooding translates the data into bodies, homes, and displaced children. Oona's complaint ("someone should tell the voss it is early") compresses the dark comedy into a single line: a civilization's crisis measured against a schedule the crisis has stopped respecting. The staging grounds show the human cost of the solution: operators ground down by daily drills, medical frogs treating the tools the civilization needs most. Hart and Reed's exchange dramatizes the ethical bind without resolving it, because it cannot be resolved. The chapter ends where it began, in Krrik's lab, with the closing line repeated from Ch 9 but carrying the accumulated weight of everything that has worsened since then. The junior engineer's involuntary grey is the final image: a body that has stopped pretending the numbers are manageable.
