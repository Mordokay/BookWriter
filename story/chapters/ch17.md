# Chapter 17: After Hours

Hart could not sleep.

She lay in the shared quarters listening to the others breathe. Marsh slept on her side, field notebook on the stone beside her like a second pillow. Reed slept restlessly, glasses folded on the ledge, legs tangled in the thin woven blanket the frogs had provided, which was damp because everything was damp. Vale slept on his back with his arms crossed, a posture so controlled it looked rehearsed. Quill's corner was empty. He had been coming back later and later, and Hart had stopped asking where he went because the answer would involve pools and breathing and something she did not want to have an opinion about.

The fungal light pulsed on the walls. The water channels ran their constant low murmur through the stone. The ambient hum of the settlement carried through every surface, a frequency she could feel in her teeth if she pressed her jaw together, which she did now, testing, the way she tested water samples and skin rashes and food stores. Hart tested things. That was what she did. She tested things until she found the contamination, and then she pointed at it until someone listened.

Tonight the thing she could not identify was inside the testing apparatus.

She got up.

The corridors at night were different. During the day: crowded, noisy, frogs moving in clusters, croaking layered so thick it became ambient. At night the crowds were gone. The bioluminescence, which during the day was scattered and absorbed by hundreds of small moving bodies, now had nothing to diffuse it. The light was brighter. The pale blue-green glow clung to every surface with an intensity the daytime concealed, and the walls looked, for the first time since Hart had arrived, like what they were: a living thing, breathing, pulsing, older than anything she had a framework for.

The water channels were louder in the relative quiet. A sound with texture she had not noticed when it was competing with six hundred conversations about fermentation.

Hart walked.

She did not have a destination. She was not conducting a survey or cataloguing a biohazard or following Talla into the parts of the settlement that proved her right. She was walking because the alternative was lying in the dark testing her own teeth against a vibration she could not classify.

The practice pools were three corridors from the residential quarter, down a passage that sloped and opened into a wide, shallow cavern with a low ceiling. She stood at the entrance and watched.

Twelve displacement operators sat in the warm water, arranged in a loose arc, their bodies half-submerged. They were drilling. Hart could tell it was a drill because the movements had the deliberate, repetitive quality of rehearsal, the same pattern cycled and cycled until the body forgot it was being told what to do. Their colors flickered in synchronized patterns, rapid, precise, each frog shifting through the same sequence at the same instant, their skin throwing light across the wet stone walls in moving bands of green and gold and pale violet. The reflections rippled on the ceiling. A light show without an audience, performed in the small hours for no one, because the machinery of planetary defense did not care what time it was.

Hart watched from the corridor entrance. The operators did not notice her or did not care.

The synchronization was tight. Eleven of the twelve held the pattern cleanly, their colors clicking through the sequence with the precision of something mechanical. The twelfth was slower. Half a beat behind, her colors dragging, the greens arriving a fraction late and the violets muddy. She was young and she was tired and her body was broadcasting both. The frog beside her adjusted slightly, compensating, pulling the rhythm back into alignment the way a musician covers for a missed beat. Nobody spoke. Nobody stopped. The drill continued.

She left before anyone looked up.

The fermentation argument was audible from twenty meters away.

Hart followed the sound down a side passage and found four frogs in a shallow alcove lit by a single streak of bioluminescence on the far wall. They were arranged in a rough square, each occupying a wet stone ledge, each leaning forward with the aggressive posture of someone who had been making the same point for a very long time and was not going to stop now.

"Eighteen degrees," said the largest frog, a heavy olive female whose throat patch was flaring orange with conviction. "Eighteen degrees for the first four days, then a gradual reduction to fourteen over the following six. This is the method. This has always been the method."

"This has been your method," said the frog across from her, smaller, darker, his skin cycling through irritated yellows. "Your method produces a larval culture that tastes like mineral sediment. I have been telling you this for nine years."

"You have been wrong for nine years."

"The optimal temperature," said a third frog, this one pale and young and apparently serving as the argument's moderator, "is sixteen point five. I published this in the fermentation collective's seasonal review. The data is clear."

"Your data is from a single batch," said the fourth frog, who had not spoken until now and who delivered his opinion with the weary authority of someone who had outlived several rounds of this dispute. "A single batch in a controlled alcove with artificial humidity. You cannot generalize from a single batch. I have been fermenting beetle larvae for fourteen of my twenty-one years and I can tell you that the optimal temperature depends on the larvae source, the water mineral content, and whether the moon is visible through the canopy vents, which nobody believes but which I have documented."

"The moon," said the olive female.

"The moon."

"You are blaming the moon for your bad fermentation."

Hart had stopped walking. The four frogs noticed her simultaneously. Their colors shifted, a brief collective ripple of surprise that resolved into curiosity.

"Human," said the olive female. "You are the one who studies contamination."

"I am."

"You have a scientific mind. Settle this. Eighteen degrees or sixteen point five?"

"I study parasites and toxins, not fermentation."

"Fermentation is controlled rot," said the dark frog with the irritated yellows. "That is your area."

Hart opened her mouth to decline and found herself considering the question. Controlled rot was, in fact, her area. The four frogs watched her with the focused attention of people who had been waiting years for an impartial judge and were prepared to reject the verdict immediately.

"I would need to see the larvae, the water chemistry, and the temperature controls before forming an opinion."

The four frogs erupted. Not at her. At each other. The olive female declared this proved the need for systematic testing. The dark frog said systematic testing was what he had proposed nine years ago. The young moderator insisted his published data already constituted systematic testing. The fourth frog said the moon was a variable and he would die on that ledge.

Hart left them to it. Behind her, the argument continued at full intensity, four voices overlapping in the dim alcove, the settlement's two-hundred-million-year-old architecture absorbing the sound of four frogs fighting about beetle larvae in the small hours of a night nobody else was using.

The corridor that led toward the settlement's outer edge was quieter. Narrower. The bioluminescence thinned to scattered patches, and the stone underfoot was older, smoother, worn by generations of small feet traveling the same route. Hart's boots were loud here. She was aware of them. She was always aware of them in this place, the heaviness of her steps in a civilization scaled for something lighter.

A sound from ahead. Scraping. Methodical, rhythmic, the sound of a tool against wet stone.

The cleaner was working in a section where the corridor bent and widened into a junction. She was older, her green faded to something between sage and grey, her movements deliberate and unhurried. She held a flat tool, stone-edged, and was scraping mineral buildup from the base of the wall where the water channel met the floor. The scrapings fell into a shallow dish at her feet. The smell was sharp, mineral and astringent, like vinegar cut with wet chalk.

She saw Hart and did not stop working.

"You are the human who walks at night," the cleaner said. "I have heard about you."

"You have heard about me."

"I hear about everyone. I have cleaned this corridor for twelve years. Twelve of my twenty. More than half my life on this stretch. I know who walks here, when they walk, and what their walking means. The families in the eastern alcoves argue on a fourteen-day cycle, which is also their mating cycle, which nobody admits. The engineer from the computation banks comes through at the same hour every night to check the nutrient flow, and he thinks nobody notices, but I notice because he tracks moss residue and I am the one who cleans it. The young frog from the displacement training pools, the one who keeps falling behind in the drills, she comes through here crying three nights out of five, and I leave a clean ledge for her to sit on because that is what I can do."

Hart leaned against the corridor wall. The stone was warm. The cleaner's voice had the steady, unperformative quality of someone who was not trying to impress, instruct, or convert. She was simply talking. Hart had not heard someone simply talk in weeks.

"The archive," the cleaner said, and she scraped a long, curling strip of mineral deposit from the stone and deposited it in her dish with practiced accuracy, "keeps the memories. The songs and the tissue and the spores. Pem tends the dead. That is important. Everyone agrees that is important." She worked another section of the wall, her movements economical, decades of practice compressed into the angle of her wrist. "But who tends the living corridor? Who scrapes the buildup so the water runs clean? Who notices that the young operator cries? Who knows that the family in the third alcove has a sick juvenile they have not brought to the practitioners because they are afraid the practitioners will report the illness to the coordinators, who might reassign them again?" She looked at Hart. "We are the real archive. Pem keeps the dead. I keep the living."

Hart did not respond immediately. The cleaner did not require a response. She returned to her wall, her flat tool finding the next ridge of mineral deposit with the practiced certainty of decades.

"Why do humans not clean communally?" the cleaner asked.

Hart thought about how to explain it. "We hire people to clean. We pay them. Many people consider the work beneath them."

The cleaner stopped scraping. She turned to Hart. Her faded green skin shifted through a slow display of colors that Hart had not seen before, a rippling sequence that moved from her throat to her forelimbs and back, cycling through greens and ambers and finally settling on a pale, bewildered grey.

"Beneath them," the cleaner repeated.

"Yes."

"The work that keeps the corridor alive. The work that keeps the water clean and the walls from growing closed and the mineral buildup from blocking the channels that carry the sound that carries the decisions that keep the settlement functioning. That work."

"That work."

The cleaner stared at her for a long moment. Then she returned to scraping. She did not say anything else about it. Her silence was louder than the fermentation argument.

Hart stayed. She stood in the corridor and watched the cleaner work and listened to the water channels carry their sound through the stone, and the cleaner told her about the family with the sick juvenile, and the engineer who tracked moss, and the displacement trainee who cried, and a maintenance dispute from six years ago that the cleaner considered more important than any Speaking Chamber session, because the Speaking Chamber did not have to live with the consequences of its own drainage policy.

It was the most grounded conversation Hart had had in weeks.

She moved on. The outer edge of the settlement was where the corridors narrowed to single-body width and the bioluminescence dimmed to a faint pulse. The walls here were warm and rough and still growing, root structures pushing outward into the surrounding earth. Hart touched the surface. Warm through her palm. The settlement did not end. It tapered, the architecture softening from civic infrastructure into something more like the edge of a forest, where cultivation faded and wildness resumed.

The water channels were thinner here, carrying fragments of voices from deeper in the settlement, conversations stripped of their words and reduced to rhythm. Hart stood in the oldest, quietest part of a civilization that predated flowering plants and she did not think about what any of it meant because she was not interested in what things meant. She was interested in whether they worked, and whether they leaked, and what grew in the cracks.

The walls were warm. The settlement breathed around her in its sleep.

She found Vesh by accident.

The meditation pool was small, set into an alcove off a corridor Hart had not walked before. The water was still. The bioluminescence was reduced to a faint blue-green glow at the pool's edges, barely enough to distinguish the water from the stone. Hart saw the pool first and the frog second.

Vesh sat at the pool's edge. He was not meditating. He was not performing anything. He was sitting with the stillness of a creature that had run out of motion, his small body hunched forward, his forelimbs resting on the wet stone. Five centimeters of frog in dim light. His colors were wrong. The vivid emerald was gone, the gold flecks dark. His skin had settled to a flat grey-green that made him look like any frog on any stone in any forest, unremarkable, small, old.

He looked old. Hart had not thought of him as old before, because the performance was so large it filled every room he entered, the grandiosity and the vulgar comedy and the gold-flecked brightness swallowing his actual size. Without it he was a small frog in the dark, his skin dull, his posture compressed, his one slightly larger eye catching the faint light and holding it with an expression that was not serene, not philosophical, not cosmic. Tired. Just tired. And beneath the tiredness, something Hart recognized because she had spent her career studying organisms under stress.

He was afraid.

She did not approach. She did not move. She stood in the corridor and looked at the mystic without the mysticism, a creature who would be dead in a few years, who had spent his life preaching that death was a scheduling formality and dissolution was beautiful, and who sat alone in the dark at whatever hour this was with his colors stripped to the grey-green of a frog who was not ready.

Then he noticed her.

The change happened in stages. First the posture. His spine straightened, his forelimbs repositioning on the stone, the hunch dissolving into something more composed. Then the colors. The grey-green warmed. Green deepened. The gold flecks surfaced, slowly at first, then with increasing confidence, the emerald reasserting, the stained-glass brightness returning to his skin like theater lights coming up. He caught an insect from the air beside his head, a motion so quick and practiced it looked casual, and swallowed it without breaking the emerging performance. Then the voice.

"Dr. Hart." The serene rumble was back, rolling through the corridor with the full weight of a frog who had apparently been sitting here contemplating the cosmos rather than whatever Hart had actually seen. "Insomnia is the self refusing to practice dissolution. I recommend the pools. Or the beetle."

Hart looked at him. The performance was complete. The grey-green frog from thirty seconds ago was gone, replaced by Brother Vesh, Ritual Guide, gold-flecked and certain.

"The pools are contaminated and the beetle is dead," she said.

"Two excellent reasons to try both."

She could have walked away. She almost did. But something in the exchange had a weight to it that was not the usual Hart-Vesh register. They were not natural conversation partners. She spoke in systems failure and he spoke in cosmic dissolution and the two languages shared almost no vocabulary. Almost.

"The Pruning," she said. "Can they do it?"

Vesh regarded her. The gold flecks held steady but his larger eye, the one that gave him his permanent expression of lopsided wonder, was still and focused in a way the performance did not quite cover.

"The operators are training. The Siberians have brought their arrays. Krrik is doing what Krrik does, which is to make the impossible merely very difficult. The question is not a simple one."

"Simple questions are the only kind worth asking."

"Then yes. They can do it. They have done it before. They will do it again."

Hart leaned against the corridor wall. "The question is not whether it succeeds but what it costs."

Vesh was quiet for a moment. The pool reflected his colors on its still surface, the emerald and the gold, and the reflection was steady and bright and not quite the same frog she had seen sitting alone in the dark.

"You sound like Talla," he said.

Hart could not tell if this was a compliment. She suspected Vesh could not either.

"I sound like someone who has watched systems survive at the expense of the people inside them. Your operators come home with their minds half-dissolved. Your dissenters get logged by a clerk in an alcove. And the cleaner in Corridor Nine has been scraping mineral deposits for twelve years and knows more about who lives and who suffers in this settlement than Noll will ever hear in the Speaking Chambers."

Vesh ate another insect. He did it slowly, with the deliberate relish of a frog reasserting the physical after too much of the abstract.

"The cleaner in Corridor Nine," he said, "is named Revvi. She has cleaned that section since before I became a ritual guide. She once told me that my breathing circles were too long and that I talked too much about the cosmos and not enough about drainage. I found this observation spiritually clarifying."

Hart almost smiled. She held it. "Good night, Vesh."

"Good night, Dr. Hart. The dawn here is worth seeing, if you can tolerate beauty on an empty stomach."

She walked back through corridors that were beginning to shift. The bioluminescence was changing, the blue-green warming toward amber at the edges. The water channels quickened, carrying the first vibrations of the settlement stirring. Bodies entering the water. The chemical and chromatic exchanges that constituted morning for a species that started each day by absorbing the world through their skin.

The first croaking rose from the warrens. Low, scattered, individual voices joining and overlapping until the sound became collective, a ragged chorus that was not coordinated and not random and not beautiful in any way Hart could defend with data.

She reached the shared quarters. Marsh had turned over. Reed's blanket was on the floor. Vale breathed with his arms still crossed. Quill's corner was still empty.

Hart sat on her moss pad. The bioluminescence was shifting through its dawn sequence, and the settlement was waking around her, and somewhere four frogs were still arguing about beetle-larva temperature because some arguments outlived the frogs who started them.

She did not sleep.

She did not say what she thought.

## Chapter Intent
This chapter uses the settlement's night hours to show what daylight performance conceals. Hart, the character least willing to be moved by anything that does not present as a biohazard, walks the corridors after dark and encounters the civilization stripped of its daytime noise: the displacement operators rehearsing with beautiful, exhausting precision; four frogs engaged in a years-long fermentation dispute that makes the settlement feel absurdly, mundanely alive; a cleaner whose twelve-year intimacy with a single corridor gives her a knowledge of the community that no administrator possesses; and Vesh, alone at his pool, small and grey and afraid, the performance gone, the mystic reduced to a mortal creature sitting with something he does not want to feel. The vignette structure is deliberate. Each encounter gives Hart one sensory detail, one small revelation, one degree of warmth she did not ask for. The emotional arc is a slow erosion of resistance. Hart does not break. She does not become sentimental. She simply walks through a civilization at night and cannot stop noticing that it is held together by the people who scrape mineral deposits and cry after drills and argue about beetle larvae at two in the morning, and that this holding-together is, in a way she will not name, worth seeing. The chapter bridges Act 2A into Act 2B by showing the fragility and exhaustion beneath the managed normalcy. The Pruning is visible in the operators' fatigue but not foregrounded. The real subject is what a civilization looks like when it is not performing for visitors, and what it costs Hart to see it.
