# Chapter 24: The Cost

The recovery pools smelled of copper and crushed herbs and something underneath both, a sweetness Quill could not name, thick and biological, the smell of bodies mending in warm water. The pools were shallow, wider than the regular bathing pools, lined with stone that had been worn smooth by use that predated human agriculture. Dozens of frogs occupied them. Some floated with their eyes closed, their colors dim, their breathing visible in the slow rise and fall of their sides. Others sat at the edges with their legs in the water, their skins flickering in low, irregular patterns that carried no information Quill could read. Partners sat with operators. Friends sat with friends. Medical frogs moved between them carrying stone vessels of something that smelled sharp and herbal, pouring it into the water at intervals that seemed practiced.

Vesh was in the nearest pool.

He moved between the operators with the unhurried attention of someone doing a job he had done before. He sat with each one. He sang, low, in Threnn-song, the sound more vibration than melody, something Quill caught in his chest and the backs of his teeth. He placed his hands on their skin in a gesture Quill could not quite follow and that Vesh did not explain. His emerald green was steady, his gold flecks subdued, the gaudy brightness of his spiritual performances replaced by something quieter and more functional. He was not interpreting the Wet Way. He was doing it. The way a doctor does medicine. The way a plumber does pipes. The body, the hands, the repetition.

A young male near the pool's eastern edge opened his eyes when Vesh reached him. His colors flickered twice, a green so pale it was nearly white, and his mouth moved. Vesh leaned closer. The young male said something Quill could not hear. Vesh said something back. The young male's colors steadied, deepened by a fraction, and his eyes closed again. Vesh moved to the next one.

Marsh was there.

She knelt at the far end of the pool with a stone basin of treated water, helping a medical frog rinse instruments. A disoriented operator had been brought to the pool's edge minutes before Quill arrived, a small brown female whose colors scattered randomly, not communication but static, the visual equivalent of a radio between stations. The operator did not recognize Marsh. She flinched from the medical frog's touch but settled when Marsh put a hand on the wet stone beside her, not touching, just present, a large warm shape that the operator's damaged senses could orient against. Marsh's field notebook was in her back pocket. She was not reaching for it.

Quill watched Marsh steady the operator and thought: she has crossed. The line between studying and belonging. She had crossed it without announcement, without decision, the way water crosses a membrane. He recognized it because he had been watching it happen to himself, in slower motion, through a different door.

Three more operators were brought in while Quill stood at the pool's edge. Their partners followed. One partner, a compact olive female, took position at the waterline and did not move. Her colors were controlled to the point of blankness, the determined stillness of someone holding a shape against pressure. A second partner, older, his skin mottled with age, waded into the shallow water and sat beside the unconscious operator he had come for, adjusting the frog's position in the current with small, precise movements. The third partner did not come. An elderly frog near the entrance told a medical attendant, quietly, that the third partner was at the nursery with their young and could not leave. The medical attendant nodded.

Oona arrived with food.

She carried two stone bowls, one in each hand, balanced with the practiced confidence of someone who had been carrying things to people in crisis for years. Fermented grubs in one bowl. A preparation Quill did not recognize in the other, something dark and dense that smelled of minerals. She distributed portions to the partners at the waterline, moving between them without ceremony, her orange throat patch at a steady dim frequency that Quill had learned to associate with Oona operating in her most serious register. She did not gossip. She did not offer commentary on the grub quality. She put food in front of people and waited until they ate.

The compact olive female at the waterline did not eat. She sat with her controlled blankness and her untouched bowl and watched the pool where her partner floated with closed eyes and scattered colors.

Oona sat down beside her.

Not across from her. Beside her. Close enough that their skins almost touched. The olive female did not look at Oona. Oona did not ask her to. She sat with her own bowl in her lap and ate a grub slowly, watching the pool with the young frog, the two of them facing the same direction, the same water, the same unresponsive body drifting in the treated current.

After a while Oona said, "When my partner died, I kept putting two portions in the bowl for three months. Nobody told me to stop. I stopped when I stopped."

The olive female's blankness cracked. A single flicker of amber crossed her throat, involuntary, the color of something breaking through from underneath. Then back to controlled stillness. She picked up a grub. She ate it. Oona ate another. They sat together and watched the pool.

Quill turned away. The edges of the scene were soft in a way they should not have been. The stone walls, the bioluminescent light, the moving bodies of frogs and the still bodies of operators, all of it had a quality he could not locate in his vocabulary, a blurring at the periphery that was not visual but categorical. He was watching a community process its wounds with the practiced efficiency of a species that had been processing wounds for longer than humans had been planting seeds, and the efficiency was beautiful and the efficiency was terrible and the two assessments occupied the same space in his chest without canceling each other.

Vesh reached the far end of the pool.

The last operator was a young female. Quill had not seen her brought in. She lay in the shallowest section, where the water barely covered her, her body still, her colors not flickering or scattering or broadcasting static but cycling slowly through a sequence that had no pattern Quill could detect. Random. Not the randomness of a damaged signal but the randomness of a signal with no sender. Her body was present. Her eyes were open. She was not there.

Vesh sat with her longer than he had sat with any of the others.

He sang. He placed his hands on her skin. He adjusted her position in the water. He sang again. The medical frogs who had been tending her stood at the pool's edge and did not interrupt. One of them, an older female with faded brown coloring and a professional steadiness that reminded Quill of hospital nurses he had known, watched Vesh with an expression that was not hope.

Quill stood at the pool's edge and watched Vesh sit with the young operator who was alive and not individual, the body that breathed and the self that was gone, dissolved into a collective synchronization that had not given her back. Wet in, wet out. The phrase surfaced in Quill's mind with the cadence of liturgy and the finality of a medical report. The puddle had returned to the water. But the body had not followed.

Vesh stood. Something in his posture changed. It was small. A settling in the shoulders, a fractional lowering of his head, a redistribution of weight that Quill would not have noticed three weeks ago and that he noticed now because he had spent three weeks watching Vesh hold himself with the particular uprightness of a frog who has decided that the universe is fundamentally acceptable. The uprightness was still there. The certainty underneath it was not.

Vesh walked past Quill without stopping. Quill followed.

They moved through the settlement's corridors, dim blue-green light, the sound of water in the walls, the muted colors of a community in collective exhaustion. Frogs passed them. Some nodded to Vesh. A young frog with raw, red-rimmed skin around her eyes stepped aside to let them pass and bowed her head briefly. An older male, carrying a stone dish of food toward the recovery pools, said something in Threnn-song that Quill could not follow, the tone respectful and heavy. Vesh acknowledged each with a gesture or a quiet word. His emerald green was steady. His gold flecks were dim. The performance of serenity was doing its job, and Quill could see now that it was a job, that the performance was labor, that the frog who told others the self was a temporary swamp event was spending considerable effort maintaining his own.

"How many," Quill said.

Vesh walked. The corridor opened into a wider passage where two cleaning crews were restoring a section of wall where the voss growth had been cut back, the scar tissue of the substrate visible as a dark stain on the root surface. One of the cleaners glanced at Vesh and went back to work.

"Over the generations," Quill said. "How many frogs have dissolved into the collective and not come back?"

Vesh considered. His stride did not change. A frog emerged from a side corridor carrying a sleeping child in a water-filled stone bowl and passed them without acknowledgment, intent on something domestic and urgent.

"Enough to matter," Vesh said. "Not enough to stop."

Six words. Quill turned them over and found the entire moral arithmetic of a civilization compressed into a single sentence. The cost was real. The cost was known. The cost was accepted. The accounting had been done, across generations, weighed against what was preserved, and found, each time, to hold. Whether the balance actually held or whether the conclusion was the only one the civilization could afford to reach was a question Quill could not separate from the answer.

They walked. The corridors widened again near the central residential warrens, where the smell of fermentation and the murmur of domestic life filtered through from the eating spaces. Frogs moved around them. A group of three sat on a stone ledge in the corridor, eating in silence, their colors dim, their exhaustion visible in the slowness of their chewing. Two young frogs passed in the opposite direction, one speaking rapidly, the other listening with glazed patience. Normal life. The civilization's pulse, weakened, still beating.

Vesh stopped at an alcove where a small pool reflected the bioluminescent light from the ceiling. He ate an insect. The gesture was familiar, the casual interruption of philosophy by biology that had characterized every conversation Quill had ever had with him. But the timing was different. The insect was not punctuation. It was delay.

Vesh's skin shifted.

The color was something Quill had not seen before. Not the emerald performance, not the gold flecks of spiritual intensity, not the grey-green drain of hidden fear he had glimpsed once in a breathing circle and twice during the Pruning. This was deep. Private. A color between indigo and brown that moved through Vesh's skin like weather through a landscape, slow, involuntary, the chromatic signature of something that was not meant to be seen. It lasted perhaps three seconds. Then the emerald returned, deliberate, controlled, the public self reasserting its shape over the private one.

Vesh looked at Quill with his lopsided eyes, one slightly larger than the other, and said: "You are wondering if we are monsters."

"No," Quill said. Though he had been, or something adjacent.

"That is the wrong question." Vesh swallowed the insect. "We are a species that has found a way to survive that costs us exactly what it should." He paused. The corridor was quiet. The two young frogs had passed. The group of three continued eating on their ledge, not listening, or listening and not caring, because philosophy in corridors was as common as condensation. "Everything worth preserving costs something it should not."

Quill stood in the dim corridor and let the sentence settle. It did not resolve. It sat in the space between consolation and confession, words from a frog who had spent the last hour tending the cost he was now naming, and who was eating an insect and displaying a color he had not chosen to show.

"That is not a comfortable thought," Quill said.

"Comfort is a human obsession," Vesh said. "We prefer accuracy." He blinked. "Also insects. Insects help."

They stood for a moment longer. Then Vesh moved on, back toward the recovery pools, back to the operators, back to the singing and the hands-on-skin and the labor of a religion that was also a medical practice and a grief protocol and an operating system for a civilization that could not afford to stop functioning while it mourned. Quill watched him go. Small, emerald, gold flecks beginning to recover their brightness, the performance resuming because the performance was also the work.

Quill did not follow.

He walked instead toward the staging grounds. The open space that had held hundreds of synchronized operators twelve hours ago was being restored. Cleaning crews moved through it with stone tools and water, scrubbing the displacement platforms, removing the residue of concentrated effort, the biochemical traces of hundreds of frogs who had thought as one and some of whom had not fully come back. The crews worked in small groups, methodical, unhurried, the domestic labor of aftermath.

Hart and Talla were there.

They worked at the near edge of the staging area, picking up debris, moving stone vessels, restoring the space to its non-Pruning function. Hart's sleeves were rolled to her elbows. Her short hair was damp with the settlement's humidity. She moved with the economical efficiency she brought to everything, the competence of a woman whose body had always been her primary instrument and who did not ask permission before using it. Talla worked beside her. Small, dark, rigid with her compensating uprightness, her pale spots controlled, her colors blank. The dissident and the survivalist, cleaning up after a crisis one had opposed and the other had catalogued. They did not speak. They passed stone vessels between them with the coordination of two people who had found a rhythm that did not require words. Hart picked up a water-stained cloth. Talla took it from her and folded it. Hart reached for the next one. The silence between them was not empty.

Quill watched them for a moment and then moved on.

In Krrik's laboratory, through the open entrance, he caught a glimpse of Reed at the secondary computation array, his glasses low on his nose, his pencil moving across a sheet of moss as the Siberian engineer pointed at a data pattern with one grey forelimb. Krrik was at the central array, his mottled brown-green skin cycling through its unconscious processing display, his voice a low rapid murmur directed at a junior engineer who was nodding too quickly to be following. The scientists processing through analysis. The mechanism of continuation.

In the Speaking Chambers, visible through a corridor arch, Noll's muted green-grey was steady and still at the center of a half-submerged gathering, his voice carrying the cadence of facilitative consensus, the shift from emergency command so smooth it might never have happened. Vale stood on a raised ledge at the chamber's edge, arms folded, watching the transition with the set jaw of a man cataloguing something he found both impressive and unforgivable.

Quill stood in the corridor between the laboratory and the Speaking Chambers and looked at his hands. They were human hands. Long-fingered, pale, slightly sunburned from weeks of tropical humidity. They did not change color. They did not broadcast his emotional state to anyone standing nearby. They did not absorb the chemistry of grief from treated water or carry the residual signatures of the dead. They were his. Private. Sealed. The boundaries of his self ended where his skin began, and nothing crossed that boundary without his conscious permission.

He envied the frogs.

The thought arrived without introduction, without the analytical scaffolding he had built over a career of studying consciousness and cooperation and suffering and altruism. He envied their communal grief, the way loss moved through their bodies and their pools and their corridors and was absorbed by the collective. He envied the recovery pools, the singing, the hands on skin. He envied the efficiency of a species that could metabolize its dead within hours and carry the chemical signature of everyone it had ever mourned. He envied the young olive female at the waterline eating a grub beside Oona, absorbing comfort through proximity, the simple animal fact of another body beside her in the dark.

He pitied them.

The operator who was alive and not individual. The cost Vesh had named in six words. The generations of frogs who had dissolved into collective synchronization and not come back, enough to matter, not enough to stop. The institutional calculus that weighed selves against survival and found the exchange acceptable. The theology that called dissolution a homecoming and the bodies that stayed behind after the selves had left.

He was afraid.

Not of the frogs. Of wanting what they had. Of the fact that the envy and the pity were not separate things, that they occupied the same place in him, that the part of him that was horrified by the cost was the same part that recognized, with a clarity that felt like vertigo, that the cost was the point. That anything worth this much suffering to preserve was worth preserving. That the frogs' willingness to pay it was not monstrous but simply honest about a transaction that every civilization made and most refused to name.

He stood in the corridor with his sealed, private, human hands and could not find the boundary between the three feelings. They had collapsed into something that was not envy and not pity and not fear but all of them at once, a single unnamed sensation that sat behind his ribs like a second heartbeat, irregular and persistent.

The bioluminescent light flickered. Water moved in the walls. Somewhere in the recovery pools, Vesh was singing.

Quill stood still and listened and did not know what he was becoming.

## Chapter Intent
This chapter is the Pruning's emotional invoice, and Quill is the right lens because he is the human most permeable to what the frogs are paying. The nine scenes move through an arc from communal recovery to individual crisis: the recovery pools establish the cost as visible, shared, and efficiently processed; Marsh's crossing mirrors Quill's own; Oona's comfort to the bereaved frog is the book's most devastating domestic beat, specific and earned by four years of her own grief compressed into two sentences; the unresponsive operator is the theology's failure point, where "wet in, wet out" meets a body that stayed behind; Vesh's posture change is the only crack in twenty-two chapters of performative serenity, and it is small enough to miss; the walk delivers the philosophical payload through a frog who has earned the right to say it by spending the morning doing the work; Hart and Talla's wordless cleaning is the chapter's quietest and most powerful scene, two skeptics who have nothing to say and everything to share; the brief glimpses of Reed and Vale show the settlement resuming its rhythms; and Quill's crisis closes the chapter on a feeling that has no name because it is three feelings that have forgotten how to be separate. The chapter resists sentimentality because the frogs resist sentimentality. They grieve efficiently. They absorb. They continue. The horror and the beauty are the same thing, and Quill's transformation is complete enough that he can see this, and seeing it is what makes him afraid.
