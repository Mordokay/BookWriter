# Chapter 22: Talla's Wager

The Third Pool was not a secret. That was the point.

Hart sat on a stone ledge above the water, her boots pulled up beneath her because the ledge was scaled for frogs and her feet would otherwise dangle in the pool where a dozen agitated bodies were producing enough color to light the alcove without bioluminescence. The pool was communal, open to the corridor, and anyone walking past could see exactly who was in it. Three frogs had walked past in the ten minutes since Hart arrived, and each had looked in with the particular focus of someone cataloguing faces. Two had continued walking. One had entered and taken a position near the back, where the pool shallowed against the root wall, and had not spoken.

Twelve frogs. Hart counted because counting was what she did when she could not run a diagnostic. Twelve frogs in warm water, their skins throwing rapid shifts across the pool's surface, greens and olives and agitated ambers reflecting off the stone ceiling in overlapping bands. The water rippled with their chemistry. The air smelled of copper and crushed herbs and something sharper underneath, the acrid note Hart had come to associate with frog adrenaline, if frogs had adrenaline, which Reed would correct her about and which she did not care about because the smell meant stress and stress was stress regardless of the molecule.

Talla stood at the pool's center. The water reached her chest. Her dark skin was nearly black in the low light, her small body rigid, held upright with the compensating posture Hart recognized from weeks of watching her refuse to be overlooked. She was not making a speech. She was listing things.

"The coordination office reports seventeen operator casualties across two displacement waves. This is the number released to the general population. The archive records additional deaths among the elderly since preparations began. Oona Reedmoss, who tends the eastern nursery, reports seven mourning rituals in twelve days against a baseline of roughly one per week. The coordination office has not commented on this discrepancy. I am commenting on it now."

A frog near the pool's edge shifted position. Male, older, his skin a deep brown mottled with age spots that did not flicker because age spots did not flicker, a body past the point of involuntary display. He had the look of someone who had attended many meetings and considered most of them a waste of water.

"I want the aggregate mortality data released," Talla said. "Not the operator count. The full cost. Every death that correlates with Pruning preparations. I want the breathing circle mandate examined by practitioners who are not appointed by the coordination office. And I want the Siberian method tested. Technical coordination without the merging. They do it. It works. We could do it."

"The Siberians lose more operators than we do," said a frog to Hart's left, her voice carrying the brisk, certain tone of someone stating a number she had looked up.

"The Siberians lose operators to displacement errors, not to consciousness dissolution. There is a difference between a technical failure and feeding your people into a ritual that sometimes does not give them back."

The pool surface was alive. Colors moved across it in competing patterns, the reflected skins of twelve frogs processing twelve reactions at once. Some of the patterns aligned briefly, clusters of agreement rippling from one body to the next, and some clashed, amber cutting through green. Hart watched the surface the way she watched cultures in a petri dish: looking for what was growing, and which way it was spreading.

An older female near the back, grey-green and faded, spoke without raising her body from the water. "You ask for alternatives during the crisis itself." Her voice was level and tired. "Talla, this is not courage. This is timing."

The pool shifted. Several frogs turned toward the speaker. Colors moved.

"If not during the crisis, when?" The response came from a younger frog near Talla, his olive skin bright with a conviction his voice had not caught up to. "After the crisis, we are told to be grateful. Before the crisis, we are told to wait. There is never a correct time to ask whether the system that saves us is also the system that kills us, because the system is always either saving us or preparing to save us."

The older female did not reply. Her colors held steady. She had heard this argument before. She found it correct and inconvenient.

Two frogs near the pool's edge started speaking at once. Their voices collided, overlapped, separated. A brown male with fading spots on his throat was arguing that the Siberian method had never been tested at the Amazon settlement's scale. A smaller female beside him insisted scale was precisely the argument the coordination office used to prevent any testing at all. A third frog, who had been silent until now, asked whether Talla had actually spoken to the Siberian engineers or was citing secondhand reports. Talla said she had spoken to them. The third frog's colors shifted, processing this.

The pool was loud. Twelve voices finding and losing each other, the water's surface turbulent with their chemistry, colors flashing in combinations Hart could not parse but could read by temperature. Agreement was warm. Disagreement was sharp. The older female at the back watched it all with visible patience. The water would eventually settle. That was the problem Talla was trying to name: settling was what the system relied on.

Talla's pale spots were visible. She controlled them the way Hart controlled her own jaw when she was angry, through effort that was itself a tell. The spots ran across her back in a pattern that broadcast what her voice refused to: that this was costing her, that standing in a public pool calling out the coordination office was not comfortable dissent performed for an audience but the act of someone who had decided the cost was cheaper than the silence.

Hart sat on her ledge and watched a political organism under stress. The supporters, the skeptics, the one frog at the back who had entered without speaking and whose colors were flat and controlled and whose presence would be reported to someone by the end of the day. The young frog whose conviction outpaced his argument. The older female whose objection was not disagreement but exhaustion. Hart had seen this room in Manaus during a dengue response. She had seen it in a hospital boardroom in Montreal. She had seen it in every system she had ever studied, the moment when the people maintaining the machinery started asking whether the machinery was maintaining them.

The pool hummed. A low vibration passed through the water, the bodies processing something collectively, and Hart was not sure whether it was a vote or a feeling or both.

---

Noll's response arrived through intermediaries the next morning.

Hart heard about it from a frog she did not know, a small pale female who stopped her in the corridor outside the fermentation cellars and delivered the message with the cheerful neutrality of someone reading a weather report. Talla's concerns were valued. Her proposals were being considered by the relevant coordinating bodies. In the meantime, the coordination office wished to remind all residents that the breathing circles were a community resource available to everyone, and that attendance during this difficult period was encouraged. Talla specifically was invited to attend the evening session as a gesture of communal solidarity.

The frog delivered this without irony. She believed it. That was the thing. She believed the message she was carrying because the message was constructed to be believed, every word shaped to sound like care, every suggestion framed as invitation, the entire response a sentence made of open doors that led to the same room.

Hart thanked her and went to find Talla.

She found her at the communal meal.

The warren's eating space was large, low-ceilinged, warm. Dozens of frogs occupied the shallow pools and stone ledges that served as tables, and the smell was fermented protein and fresh insects and the mineral tang of standing water. Food passed from group to group in stone dishes. Frogs entered from three corridors, ate, argued, left. The domestic normalcy of shared eating in a civilization running at crisis capacity.

Hart took a position at the ledge where Talla sat with two frogs from the Third Pool, both younger, both eating with the concentrated speed of people who had somewhere to be. Hart picked up a grub. She had learned to eat the grubs. They were not good. They were not bad. They were protein, and Hart respected protein.

Talla ate without speaking. Her spots were controlled. Her jaw worked on something that was probably a beetle larva. The two younger frogs beside her were talking about displacement schedules, routine logistics, their voices low and ordinary.

Then Talla stopped eating.

She did not stand. She did not raise her voice beyond what Hart would have called conversational, though in a space with frog acoustics that meant half the room could hear her.

"The breathing circles are scheduled for this evening. I will not be attending." She spoke toward the pool in front of her, not addressing any particular frog, not performing for the room, simply stating a fact loudly enough that the fact became public. "If my absence endangers the Pruning, then the Pruning has a design problem, not a participation problem."

The meal rippled.

It was not a sound. It was a visual event. Colors shifted across the room in a wave that started near Talla and spread outward through the bodies of forty or fifty frogs, greens darkening or brightening, ambers flaring, throats pulsing with signals Hart could read by feel if not by grammar. Conversations stopped and restarted in clusters. Two frogs at a near table stood and moved to another section without looking at Talla. Three frogs from the corridor entrance moved closer, their colors curious, interested, the skin of people who had heard something they wanted to hear said out loud.

A young frog at the table behind Talla set down her food and left without finishing. An older pair at the far wall did not look up. They continued eating with the deliberate steadiness of people who had lived through previous Prunings and previous dissidents and did not intend to interrupt their meal for either. Near the corridor entrance, a group of three frogs spoke rapidly to each other, their voices too low for Hart to catch, their colors quick and private.

Hart watched the room rearrange itself around Talla's refusal and thought: this is how it works. Not the refusal. The absorption. The system was already processing the disruption, sorting the population into positions, each frog calculating where they stood relative to the line Talla had drawn in the warm water.

Vale was across the room. Hart could see him on a raised ledge near the eastern wall, his long legs folded beneath him, eating nothing, watching with the narrow focus he used when he was cataloguing a power structure in real time. He caught Hart's eye and gave her the smallest nod.

Marsh was closer, two pools away, seated beside Oona. Hart looked at Oona. She looked at Oona because Marsh was looking at Oona, and Marsh looked at things worth looking at.

Oona ate.

Her orange throat patch held at a steady middle frequency. Not bright with gossip, not dim with distress. Controlled. She did not move away from Talla's table, though she easily could have. She did not move closer. She continued eating her grubs with steady, domestic rhythm, and for the first time in Hart's observation, she was choosing to display no opinion at all. Her colors were flat, deliberate, the chromatic equivalent of a woman eating her lunch with great concentration while the table next to her caught fire.

Marsh's pencil was in her hand. She was not writing. She was holding it and watching Oona the way a field naturalist watches a subject doing something new, and Hart knew that the notebook entry, when it came, would be precise and devastating and would describe the most politically sophisticated act in the room: the ordinary frog's refusal to take a position.

---

Evening.

The biologists' quarters were lit by the wall's dim fungal glow, which made five human faces look slightly green. Hart leaned against the root wall with her arms crossed. Vale sat on the stone ledge, legs folded at an angle the room punished. Reed was on the floor with his back against the wall, Krrik's moss sheets in his lap. Marsh stood near the entrance. Quill sat in the far corner, knees drawn up, his back against the stone.

The corridor darkened.

Noll entered their quarters for the first time. He moved through the entrance with careful, deliberate steps, rehearsal and nature indistinguishable. His muted green-grey coloring was steady, calm, controlled. His skin had been a political instrument for longer than most frogs had been alive. His damaged forelimb was tucked inward. He did not duck through the entrance because he did not need to. The room had been built for his species. The humans were the ones who did not fit.

Pem followed. Slow, translucent, bright-eyed. The old archivist moved to the corner opposite Quill and settled there. Quiet. Missing nothing.

"I apologize for the hour," Noll said. His voice filled the small room without effort, the measured cadence of a coordinator addressing a gathering he had not called but somehow chaired. "The day's events have been, I think, distressing for all of us. Talla's concerns are genuine. I wish to be clear about that. Her frustration is heard and her questions are not unwelcome."

Vale's jaw tightened. Hart saw it. Nobody else did. Reed glanced up from his moss sheets and then back down.

"The community is under strain," Noll continued. "The Pruning proceeds. The operators do their work. The breathing circles continue. But cohesion requires more than procedure. It requires trust. And trust, at this moment, is thinner than I would like."

He let this settle. His colors did not flicker. The room's fungal light painted his muted green-grey skin the same shade as the walls, so that he looked, for a moment, like part of the architecture. A feature of the room rather than a visitor to it.

"I come with a request," Noll said. "Not an instruction. Not a requirement. A request."

He paused. The pause was the length of a breath and the weight of a decision being offered as a gift.

"Your perspective as outside observers carries weight. The community knows you have seen our preparations, our rituals, our collective effort. If you were to speak about what you have witnessed, the cooperation, the sacrifice, the beauty of the response, it might help the community find cohesion in a difficult moment."

The silence in the room was the kind Hart associated with the moment between a diagnosis and a reaction. She looked at Noll. He meant it. That was the obscene part. He was not scheming. He was not manipulating. He was a coordinator who had spent his entire adult life aligning collective effort, and he was doing it now, in this room, with these five people, because he genuinely believed their voices could help. He could not see why they would refuse. The request was reasonable. The cause was real. The crisis was real. The community needed cohesion. Outside perspectives carried weight. Every word was true and the sum of the words was a trap, and Noll could not see the trap because to Noll the trap and the truth were the same thing.

"No." Vale. Immediate, clipped, the single syllable carrying the weight of a door closing. He did not elaborate. He did not need to. His jaw was set and his eyes were on Noll and the word was complete.

Marsh spoke next, her voice quiet and firm. "I am not willing to be used as a political instrument. What I have witnessed is real, and it is also not mine to narrate for the coordination office. I am sorry, Noll. No."

Reed looked at Vale. He looked at Marsh. He looked at his hands on Krrik's moss sheets, his pencil still, his glasses low on his nose. His mouth opened, closed. He was calculating something, Hart could see it, the instinct to be helpful warring with a recognition that had arrived too late for comfort and just in time for use. He shook his head. A small motion. He did not trust his voice to say the right thing, so he let his body say no and went back to the moss sheets.

Hart laughed.

It was short, hard, and not amused. The sound of a diagnostic completing. She had been waiting for this since the first pool tour, since Noll's first careful sentence, since the word "entanglement" had been offered as philosophy and accepted as hospitality. The request was the system doing what systems did. She had seen it in every institution she had ever studied, the moment when the outside observer was asked to validate the inside. The laugh said all of it. She did not add words because the laugh was not a response to Noll. It was a response to the fact that she had predicted this in her field notes three weeks ago and had not shown anyone the entry.

Noll received the laugh without flinching. His colors held steady. His damaged forelimb did not move.

Quill said nothing.

The silence expanded. Noll waited. The room waited. Hart watched Quill from her position against the wall. He sat in his corner with his knees drawn up and his pale face lit by fungal glow and his hands still and his eyes open and looking at Noll or through Noll or at something behind Noll that the rest of them could not see. He did not speak. He did not shake his head. He did not nod.

Noll regarded Quill's silence for a long moment, and then he took it. Gently. Completely. Without confirming what it meant.

"I understand," Noll said. He said it to all of them and to none of them, and the phrase covered five different refusals with a single courtesy. "The offer remains, should any of you reconsider. I do not press. I ask. The difference matters to us."

He turned toward the entrance. Pem did not move immediately. The old archivist's bright eyes traveled across the five humans in the small room, resting on each face with unhurried attention. Hart met Pem's gaze and held it. The archivist's faded skin was still, translucent in the dim light. Pem looked at Vale's set jaw and Marsh's steady hands and Reed's downturned face and Hart's crossed arms and Quill's silence, and then rose and followed Noll out of the room with slow, certain steps, having already filed what needed filing.

The corridor took them and the room held the five of them in the fungal light.

Nobody spoke for a while. The water channels murmured in the walls. Reed's pencil did not move. Vale uncrossed his legs, recrossed them, and stared at the entrance where Noll had stood.

Hart looked at the entrance and then at the wall and then at her own hands, crossed over her chest, the hands she had used to catalogue fungal growth and test retardant compounds and take samples of a civilization she had entered as an epidemiologist and was now living inside as a subject.

The walls had always been there. They were just noticing them.

## Chapter Intent
This chapter is the political peak of Act 2B, and Hart is the right lens because she sees systems, not philosophies. Talla's meeting is not a revolutionary rally. It is a public pool where a dozen frogs are having a genuine argument about transparency, and the argument contains people who agree, people who disagree, and people who are there to report back. The energy is political friction, not melodrama. Noll's intermediary response is the system's immune response: absorb the disruption, reframe it as inclusion, neutralize without confrontation. Talla's public refusal at the communal meal is the moment the absorption fails, not because Talla is too strong but because she has chosen a venue where half the community hears, and hearing is harder to manage than a pool discussion. The room's rearrangement around her refusal is the chapter's social earthquake: frogs sorting themselves, Oona choosing visible neutrality, Vale cataloguing from across the room. Then Noll comes to the biologists' quarters, and the chapter shifts from frog politics to the trap. His request is the system extending itself to the humans, asking them to participate in the machinery they have been watching. Each refusal is distinct because each human has a different relationship to the machine. Vale's is political. Marsh's is principled. Reed's is uncertain. Hart's is diagnostic. Quill's silence is the most loaded because it is the one refusal that might not be a refusal at all. Pem's quiet observation at the end is the final turn: the archivist recording the humans the way the archive records everything, selectively, carefully, for a civilization that will outlast them. The walls were always there. They are just noticing them.
