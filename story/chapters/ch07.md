# Chapter 7: Breakfast, Childcare, Sex, Boredom

The fermentation cellars smelled like someone had buried a forest and then disagreed about how long to leave it.

Marsh followed Oona down a root-walled corridor that sloped into warmth, the air thickening with each step until breathing felt like chewing. The cellars opened into a low, broad chamber lit by the usual blue-green glow, and the smell hit her fully, and she breathed through her mouth for about three seconds before giving up and breathing through her nose because it was going to win anyway.

Oona was already talking.

"The new batch is not right. I have been saying this for four days. Nobody listens. They moved my culture stock to the lower level because of the Pruning preparations, and the humidity is wrong down there, and the temperature, and when I tried to explain this to the logistics committee they told me it was temporary." Her orange throat patch was bright. It had been bright since they left the residential warrens ten minutes ago, which Marsh was learning to interpret as Oona's resting state whenever she had an audience. "Temporary. Everything is temporary. My fermentation is also temporary if they keep it at that temperature."

The cellar was not empty. A knot of frogs had gathered around a row of stone basins where dark, wet cultures grew in layered beds. Two elderly frogs were examining a culture bed with the intensity of sommeliers at a tasting they intended to fail.

"This is sour," the grey-green one said. "This is undeniably sour. Before the last Pruning, the batches from this cellar were balanced. Rich. Complex. This is vinegar with ambitions."

"It is not sour," the brown one said. "Your palate has degraded. You are old and your tongue is dying and you are blaming the batch for the failures of your body."

"My tongue is not dying. My tongue is the most reliable instrument in this cellar. I have been tasting since before you were a tadpole, and I am telling you that this culture has turned, and the reason it has turned is that they changed the moss substrate three cycles ago and nobody consulted the people who actually know what they are doing."

Oona waded in. Not physically, though there was some wading involved, the cellar floor being, like everything in the settlement, slightly wet. She positioned herself between the two elderly frogs with the practiced authority of someone who had arbitrated this exact argument before and would arbitrate it again next week.

"The batch is adequate," Oona said. "It is not the best we have produced. It is not the worst. It is adequate, and arguing about it will not change the humidity, which is the actual problem, which I have been telling the logistics committee for four days."

"Adequate," the grey-green frog repeated. "Adequate is not a standard. Adequate is what you say when you have given up."

"Adequate is what I say when I am tired and the grubs need eating regardless of whether they meet your personal specifications for perfection."

Marsh stood at the edge of the group, her notebook out, her pencil moving. She was sketching the culture beds, the stone basins, the way the frogs arranged themselves around the food production like neighbors around a barbecue. Nobody was going to win. Nobody wanted to. The grubs were going to be eaten regardless, and the argument would resume tomorrow with slightly different phrasing and identical passion.

A younger frog at the far end of the cellar, bright green, barely six centimeters, called out without looking up from a culture bed she was tending. "Oona, your last batch tasted like bark. Everyone says so. Nobody tells you because you get loud."

"I do not get loud. I get specific."

"You get loud and specific. It is a combination."

Oona's throat patch flared. The response involved a detailed comparison of the younger frog's technique to something involving stagnant water that Marsh did not entirely follow but that made the two elderly frogs croak in what was unmistakably laughter.

They left the cellars with Oona carrying a sample of the disputed batch in a small stone vessel, still talking. The corridor outside was busy with morning traffic, frogs moving along the root paths and through the shallow water channels with the unhurried rhythm of commuters who had been commuting through the same tunnels for years.

They passed a shallow pool set into the corridor floor, and Marsh stopped.

Eight frogs sat in the water. The pool was barely deep enough to cover their backs. Some had their eyes closed. Others were eating or watching the corridor traffic pass. Their sides moved in a slow, visible rhythm, expanding and contracting in unison. One frog was eating an insect while half-submerged, chewing and breathing and talking to a neighbor simultaneously, with the ease of someone performing three ordinary activities that happened to include absorbing the world through her skin.

"Morning wetting," Oona said, barely pausing. "You sit. You breathe. You remind your body that it is not separate from the water." She said this the way someone might explain brushing teeth. "My sister does it for an hour every morning now. An hour. Since she joined that consciousness circle she does everything slower. Eating, walking, talking. Especially talking. A conversation with my sister now takes twice as long as it used to, and it used to take too long, because she has always had opinions about everything and now she has the same number of opinions delivered at half the speed."

"Is that common? The slowing down?"

"Common enough. The consciousness circles encourage you to settle into the current. Stop rushing. Let the flow move at its pace, not yours." Oona's tone carried the affectionate exasperation of someone describing a family member's diet. "My sister was already slow. Now she is geological. Last week she told me about a dream and it took forty minutes. The dream was about water. All her dreams are about water now. They used to be about food and arguments, which are better dreams."

They turned down a side corridor that grew warmer as it descended. Marsh could hear something ahead. A constant, low, layered sound made up of small movements, small splashes, small clicks. Life at a pitch too quiet to be called noise and too persistent to be called silence.

The tadpole nursery.

The chamber opened wide and low. The floor was a series of interconnected shallow pools, each at a slightly different temperature, the water clear enough that hundreds of tiny dark shapes were visible from the doorway. Tadpoles. Some barely a centimeter long, others closer to two, darting in quick patterns or drifting near the warm stones at the pools' edges. The air smelled like clean water and wet moss.

Two caretakers were working. One, a broad olive frog with steady hands, was sorting tadpoles between pools, lifting each one in a cupped forelimb and placing it in the water that matched its temperature preference. The other, younger, brown with pale yellow markings, was pressing moss beds into the warm stone at the pool edges.

Oona lowered herself into the nearest pool, submerging to her shoulders, and began helping without announcement or instruction. She knew where things went. Marsh watched her settle a cluster of the smallest tadpoles away from a warm-water inlet where the current was too strong for them, her movements gentle, practiced, entirely ordinary.

"The little ones prefer the edges," the olive caretaker said to Marsh. She had the earnest pride of someone who wanted you to understand that what she did mattered. "Too warm and they develop quickly but their coordination suffers. Too cool and they develop slowly but their color range is broader. We find the balance. Every clutch is different. You learn to read them."

Marsh crouched at the pool's edge. The tadpoles nearest to her scattered, then drifted back when she stayed still. They were dark, almost black, with the faintest suggestion of color along their tails.

"How many caretakers work here?"

"Rotating shifts. Twelve of us for this nursery, another fifteen at the western one. Everyone does time in the nurseries, eventually. Even the engineers." The caretaker's colors warmed, a flush of green-gold spreading across her back. "Some people think the nurseries are simple work. They are not simple work. Simple work is what you call the things you do not understand."

Oona, from her position among the tadpoles, added: "Gren's clutch is developing slowly this season. Gren will not admit this. Gren blames the water temperature. The water temperature is fine. Gren's clutch is developing slowly because Gren's clutch is developing slowly. These things happen."

A neighboring frog, a caretaker from the next pool over, leaned in. "Gren's clutch has always been slow. It is not the water. It is genetics, but if you say genetics to Gren she accuses you of judgment."

"Is it judgment?"

"It is observation. Observation is not judgment. But Gren does not see the difference when the observation is about her clutch."

Marsh was helping Oona sort a group of medium-sized tadpoles into a slightly warmer pool when Hart arrived.

She came through the entrance with her field kit over one shoulder and the expression of someone who expected to find something unsatisfactory. Her dark hair was damp. Everything was damp. Hart had stopped commenting on the dampness, which Marsh recognized as a milestone.

"The water supply for these pools," Hart said, addressing the room. "Where does it come from?"

The olive caretaker turned. Her colors, which had been warm and open, shifted to something cooler. "The eastern channel. Fed by the main filtration system."

"And the filtration system. Is it tested? For parasitic load, bacterial contamination, waterborne pathogens?"

The caretaker looked at Hart the way a chef looks at a health inspector who has arrived at a kitchen that has been operating successfully for a hundred years. "The water is clean."

"I'm not asking if it's clean. I'm asking if it's tested."

"The water is clean. The filtration was designed by engineers who understood water before your species understood fire. The tadpoles are healthy. You may observe this."

Hart crouched at the pool's edge and took a water sample in a small vial from her kit. The younger caretaker watched this with open offense.

"You are testing our water."

"I'm collecting a sample."

"In front of the tadpoles."

Hart looked up. "The tadpoles don't know what a sample vial is."

"The tadpoles are in our care. We maintain this nursery. The water is balanced, the temperature is regulated, and the moss is changed on a seven-day cycle. We have not lost a clutch in four seasons. You are standing in a nursery with a perfect record and you are putting our water in a container as though you expect to find something wrong with it."

"I expect to find out what's in it. That's different."

"It is not different to us."

Oona leaned toward Marsh, her throat patch bright. "She does this. Your Hart. She did this at the bathing pools last week. Tested the water and told Renn that the mineral balance was off and Renn has not spoken to her since. Renn has maintained those pools for nine years. You do not tell Renn the minerals are off."

Hart pocketed the vial and stood. The tadpoles swam, indifferent to the diplomatic incident occurring above them.

"Thank you," Hart said, in a tone that was, for Hart, conciliatory. "The nursery is clearly well maintained."

The olive caretaker's colors did not warm. "Yes. It is."

Hart left. The olive caretaker returned to sorting tadpoles with pointed, deliberate competence.

"She means well," Marsh said.

"She means accurately," the olive caretaker said. "That is not the same as well."

They stayed in the nursery for another hour. Marsh stopped taking notes after the first twenty minutes. She helped Oona move a clutch of the smallest tadpoles to a fresh pool, cupping them in her hands, their tiny bodies cool and flickering against her palms. The caretakers watched her do this and said nothing, which felt like approval.

They walked from the nursery into the residential warrens, and the sound changed. The nursery had been a contained, humid quiet. The warrens were everything else. Croaking, clicking, conversations happening through openings between chambers that flowed into each other like rooms in a house where someone had forgotten to install doors.

Oona moved through the warrens like someone walking through her own kitchen. A pair of young frogs on a ledge above the corridor were watching foot traffic with the bored attention of teenagers sitting on a fence, and Oona told Marsh their names and the fact that one of them had recently begun practicing mating calls that the neighbors considered premature and structurally unsound.

"He is too young," Oona said. "His vocal sac is not fully developed and the harmonics are thin. It sounds like a complaint, not a call. His mother has spoken to him about it but he continues, and the frog three chambers down has asked him to stop because her partner finds it distracting, and the partner says he does not find it distracting, he finds it amusing, and now they are arguing about whether finding it amusing is the same as finding it distracting, which it is not, but try telling her that."

"What makes a good mating call?"

Oona's throat patch blazed. This was, apparently, a topic.

"Depth. Resonance. The best calls make the pool vibrate. But that is only the sound. There is also the display. Colors. You want range, control. A good display moves through four, five shades in a sequence that shows you can hold each one without bleeding into the next. Sloppy transitions suggest a sloppy mind. That is what they say. Personally I think sloppy transitions suggest a frog who is nervous, and nervous is not the same as sloppy, but standards are standards."

"And the arguments? About what counts as attractive?"

"Constant. Everyone has an opinion. My partner, when he was alive, he had a very traditional call. Low, steady, three colors held long. Some frogs thought it was old-fashioned. I thought it was honest. He did not need to show off. He just needed to show up." A brief pause. Her colors shifted to something softer. Then back. "That was four years ago. It sounds like a long time in your counting but it is not so long in ours. We live fast. We grieve fast. It does not mean we grieve less."

Quill appeared at the far end of the corridor. He paused when he heard Oona talking, then sat on a root ledge nearby. His hands were in his pockets.

"Is the call taught or instinctive?" he asked.

Oona's throat brightened. Nobody from the coordination committee or the engineering team ever asked about mating calls. "Both. The basic call is instinctive. Every frog can croak. But the shaping, the refinement, that is taught. Mothers teach. Caretakers teach. A frog with no teaching can call, but the call is rough. Like speech without grammar."

Quill's hands moved in his pockets. "So there's a cultural transmission of aesthetic standards. Beauty is partly biological and partly inherited socially."

"I do not know what cultural transmission means. I know that my mother taught me what a good call sounds like and I taught my sister's children and they will teach theirs. If that is what you are saying, then yes."

Quill nodded slowly, the way he nodded when something was settling into a place he had been preparing for it. "Thank you, Oona. That's genuinely fascinating."

"Of course it is fascinating. It is the most important thing we do and nobody ever asks about it." Her throat was very bright. "You are looking for Vesh?"

"I am."

"He is at the Third Pool. He is always at the Third Pool. He will tell you something about the universe being a puddle and you will find it interesting. Go. But come back and ask me more questions. Your friends ask about politics and technology. You ask about how we live. This is better."

Quill smiled, a small, genuine thing, and moved on down the corridor.

Oona watched him go. "I like that one. He listens with his whole body. The tall one, your Vale, he listens with his arguments already prepared. This one listens to hear."

They settled on a root ledge near Oona's sleeping chamber, indistinguishable from every other sleeping space except for a small stone vessel of fermented grubs that Oona kept tucked into a root nook and defended with the territorial instinct of a frog who understood that communal ownership had its limits.

"The engineer three warrens down," Oona said, gesturing vaguely to the left. "She sleeps alone. Alone. She built a root partition across her chamber entrance. Not a wall, technically, but a suggestion of a wall. People talk about it."

"Is that unusual?"

"It is very unusual. We sleep near each other. We always have. The warmth, the sound, the, you know." She gestured at her own skin. "You feel people around you even when you sleep. Their breathing, their chemistry. It is not a choice you make. It is how bodies work when they are close. Sleeping alone means you wake up with only your own chemistry, your own breathing. People say it makes you dry. Closed. She says it helps her think. She is an excellent engineer. She is also strange, and everyone agrees about both of those things."

Marsh wrote in her notebook. Not field observations. Not data. Just the sentence: *sleeping alone makes you dry.* It was the kind of fact that could only arrive through sitting on a root ledge with someone who trusted you enough to say the ordinary thing instead of the impressive one.

A frog passed by in the corridor carrying a bundle of moss. She stopped, looked at Marsh, looked at Oona, and said: "The contentment of daily life is the highest form of the flow."

Oona blinked. "Thank you, Dess. Nobody asked."

"I am just saying."

"You are always just saying. Go and deliver your moss."

Dess went.

"She does that. Pronouncements. She has been attending Vesh's breathing circles three times a week and now she dispenses wisdom like someone handing out damp leaves. Last week she told me that suffering is a temporary shape and I told her my joints hurt and she said the joints are also temporary and I said my joints hurt now, not temporarily, now, and she said now is also temporary, and I asked her to leave."

Marsh laughed. Actually laughed, not the polite exhale of a researcher maintaining rapport, but the real sound, startling in the low warren corridor. Two frogs in a nearby chamber looked up.

"Oona," Marsh said. She was not sure why she asked. It was not in her notebook, not on any list. "Are you happy?"

Oona went still. Her throat patch dimmed, then held at a steady, middling orange.

"I am content," she said. "Is that the same thing? I do not always know. But the fermented grubs are good this season and my joints do not hurt today, so perhaps it does not matter." She looked at Marsh with both eyes, which frogs did when something was serious and worth the focus. "Content is not a small thing. Content is knowing that the water is warm and the nursery is full and the arguments about fermentation will continue tomorrow because nobody has died and nobody has left and the batch, even the inadequate batch, will be eaten. Is that happy? You tell me. You are the one who studies how things live."

A frog passed behind them in the corridor. He overheard enough to stop. "Contentment is the puddle accepting its depth," he said.

"Go away, Mott," Oona said.

Mott went away.

Oona looked at Marsh. "You see what I deal with."

They sat. Somewhere nearby, the young frog practiced his mating call again, the thin harmonics carrying through the root walls like a teenager learning guitar in the next apartment.

Then a caretaker Marsh did not recognize came through the corridor, moving quickly. She was carrying a bundle of bedding and two small containers. Behind her, another frog. Then three more. They were heading toward the eastern section of the warrens, and they were not talking.

Oona watched them pass. Her throat patch dimmed.

"The section past the fourth junction is being cleared," she said. "For the staging preparations. Equipment, coordination, the frogs coming in from the outer districts." Her voice was light. Deliberately light. "They always move us when it is time. It is temporary. We go to the western warrens, or the overflow chambers near the Pools. It is not far."

A family group passed. Two adults and three very young frogs riding on the larger one's back, gripping the wet skin with the tiny, determined hold of creatures who had decided to trust the carrier.

"Is it always like this?" Marsh asked. "Before a Pruning?"

Oona did not answer immediately. Her colors had shifted. The warm olive-green had cooled, and underneath it, flickers of something Marsh could not name.

"It is always something," Oona said. "Every time. Different rooms, different families, but always the moving, always the making space. Always the look on the young ones' faces when you carry them somewhere new and tell them it is fine."

She looked at the corridor where the last of the families had passed. Her throat patch was almost dark.

"It is fine," she said.

## Chapter Intent
The domestic life chapter grounds the frog civilization in the texture of daily existence: food arguments, nursery care, gossip, mating standards, sleeping arrangements, and the small gestures that reveal how a society actually works when nobody is performing. Oona's answer about contentment provides the emotional center, and the quiet ending brings the Pruning's cost into the domestic sphere through displaced families and Oona's worried colors.
