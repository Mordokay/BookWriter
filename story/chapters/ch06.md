# Chapter 6: The Machine for Distributing Power

The session had been running for twenty minutes before Vale understood that nothing was being decided.

He sat on a wet stone ledge at the chamber's edge, his trousers soaked to mid-thigh, his notebook balanced on one knee. Marsh was beside him, her own notebook open, sketching the layout of the amphitheater in quick, sure lines. Below them, in the warm mineral water of the Speaking Chambers, thirty-seven frogs deliberated on the staging logistics of the Pruning. Their colors moved in waves, rolling from one side of the basin to the other, green answering amber answering blue, and the water hummed with a low vibration that settled into Vale's molars and stayed there like a headache that had decided to become a sound.

The agenda item was displacement allocation for incoming delegations. Noll sat at the center of the lowest ring, half-submerged, his skin settled into that steady, calming blue that Vale had come to recognize as a performance of neutrality. His damaged forelimb was tucked inward, still. He had been speaking for three minutes in sentences so balanced they could have been weighed on a scale.

"The coordination framework, as proposed by the western perimeter working group, distributes platform access across a fourteen-cycle rotation. This allows each settlement delegation to arrive within an acceptable margin of."

"The margin is not acceptable," said a frog on the second ring, mottled brown, medium-sized, unremarkable except for the speed at which she interrupted. "The Seventh District has been requesting priority access for three seasons. We have supply dependencies that the rotation does not account for."

"The rotation accounts for supply dependencies through the auxiliary allocation in cycle eight," Noll said. "Your concern was addressed in the revised proposal."

"The revised proposal moved our access window to the night hours. Nobody wants to travel at night. The platform operators refuse to work double shifts."

"The operators' schedule is a separate coordination item."

"It is the same item. You separated them to make both items easier to ignore."

A ripple of amber crossed the chamber. Several frogs shifted on their rings. Two near the back had been having a side conversation in low clicks and were now paying attention, or at least performing attention, their colors tracking the argument with the idle interest of people watching a tennis match from a bar.

Vale wrote: *Procedural partitioning. Same technique as committee chairs in every parliament. Split the problem. Address each half separately. Neither half contains the actual complaint.*

Noll absorbed the objection the way he absorbed everything. His blue held. He offered a sentence that acknowledged the concern, restated the framework, and moved to the next item with the administrative grace of a man closing a door so gently you did not hear it shut.

Between agenda items, Vale leaned forward. "Who drafted the rotation framework?"

Noll turned his head slightly. "The western perimeter working group."

"And who sits on the working group?"

"That information is available in the coordination records."

"I'm asking you."

A pause. Measured. The pause of a man selecting the most boring possible answer. "The working group consists of representatives from each affected district, selected through a process of communal nomination."

"Right. So the consensus drafted a proposal, and the consensus is now debating it, and you're facilitating. Nobody decided anything. The process decided."

"That is an adequate summary."

"It's a circle, Noll. The process produces an outcome that validates the process. I've seen this before. It's called institutional self-justification."

"It is called governance, Dr. Vale. I understand the two can appear similar."

Behind them and to the left, Oona was perched on a root ledge near Marsh's elbow. Her orange throat patch was very bright. It had been bright since the session started and was getting brighter, which Vale had learned to interpret as a reliable indicator that she had something to say about everyone in the room and was waiting for someone to listen.

Marsh leaned slightly toward her. "Who's the one who interrupted? The brown frog on the second ring."

"Gren," Oona said, keeping her voice low. "Seventh District coordinator. She always objects. Not because she disagrees, usually, but because she objects. It is what she does. She has been objecting since before Noll's first cycle. She objected to the previous coordinator too. She objected to the coordinator before that. Gren objects."

"Does it work?"

"Sometimes. Not today. Today the agenda was settled before the session began. Watch the three frogs on the fifth ring, the ones who have not spoken. They will vote with Noll on everything. They always do. Denn, Lorra, and the pale one whose name I should remember but do not. They coordinate their colors before the formal consensus. You can see it if you watch. A flicker of blue, very fast, before each item resolves."

Vale was watching. He caught it on the next item. Three frogs on the fifth ring, unremarkable, silent, and their skins flickered to blue a half-second before the rest of the chamber settled. A pre-consensus. A signal.

He wrote: *Core bloc. Three confirmed. Probably more. Colors coordinate before the formal vote. The consensus is seeded.*

The next agenda item was something about nursery water temperature adjustments. Fourteen frogs weighed in. The discussion was genuine, or close to genuine, the kind of low-stakes domestic policy that democratic bodies handle well because nobody's power is at risk. Vale half-listened. Colors rippled. Water hummed. Two frogs on the upper ring got into a side argument about whether the mediation pool's mineral balance had been altered since last season and whose responsibility that was.

"That's Reth and Vann," Oona murmured to Marsh. "They have been arguing about pool maintenance for six years. Six years. Reth thinks the mineral levels are too low. Vann thinks Reth is measuring wrong. They argue about it at every session. Once they argued about it during a displacement emergency. An actual emergency. Frogs arriving from the Central African settlement with supply shortages, and Reth wanted to discuss magnesium content."

Vale turned. "During an emergency?"

"During an emergency. Noll let them speak for four minutes, then moved on. Reth was furious. Reth is always furious. The minerals are fine. Everyone knows the minerals are fine. Reth's entire identity is built on the minerals not being fine."

On the third ring, Talla sat conspicuously apart.

She was smaller than most of the chamber, dark, nearly black-green, holding herself upright on a stone ledge with the rigid posture of someone who had come prepared to be ignored. A cluster of younger frogs sat near her, four or five of them, their colors shifting in quick, sympathetic patterns that tracked Talla's movements the way an audience tracks a speaker they agree with but are not brave enough to quote.

The agenda moved to displacement platform scheduling for the Pruning staging. Noll introduced it with the same measured phrasing, the same balanced sentences, the same air of administrative inevitability. "The coordination proposes a centralized scheduling authority for platform access during the Pruning preparation period, to be administered by the logistics committee in consultation with."

"In consultation with whom?" Talla said.

The chamber shifted. Colors flickered. Not alarm. More like the collective sigh of a room that knew this was coming.

"In consultation with district representatives and affected settlement liaisons," Noll said.

"So the logistics committee decides, and then consults the districts about what it has already decided. That is not consultation. That is notification with a polite label."

"The consultation is genuine, Talla."

"Then reject the framework and let the districts propose their own schedules."

"That would create coordination conflicts between districts that the centralized authority is designed to prevent."

"The centralized authority is designed to prevent districts from disagreeing with the centralized authority. You have described a system whose purpose is itself." Talla's pale spots were visible across her back now, faint against the dark skin, flaring with the involuntary intensity of someone who had not planned to get this angry this early in the session. "The proposal says 'in the interest of harmonious coordination.' When the coordination says harmonious, it means quiet. When it means quiet, it means agreement. When it means agreement, it means this meeting is a ceremony and we are the audience."

The cluster of younger frogs near her shifted to deeper greens. Agreement. Visible, synchronized, unmistakable.

Noll did not change color. His blue held with that unsettling stability that was, Vale thought, its own kind of display. The display of not displaying. "Your concern is noted and will be incorporated into the revised framework."

"My concern was noted last session. And the session before that. It has been noted for two years. Noting is not incorporating. Noting is filing."

"The incorporation process requires."

"The incorporation process requires that I stop talking, which is the one thing the process is very efficient at producing."

A beat of silence. Two frogs near the back exchanged a glance. One of them, grey-green, elderly, shifted her color to something neutral and looked away. The chamber absorbed Talla's objection the way the chamber absorbed everything. Not with hostility. Not with dismissal. With a kind of gentle, systematic patience that said: you have been heard, you are valued, and now we are moving on. It was the most sophisticated form of silencing Vale had ever witnessed. No one argued with Talla. No one needed to. The process simply continued around her, like water flowing past a stone, and the stone could be as sharp as it liked. The water did not care about sharp.

The younger frogs near Talla held their deep green for a few seconds longer, then gradually settled back to ambient colors. They had agreed. Their agreement had been visible. It would change nothing.

Vale wrote: *Dissent tolerated. Dissent absorbed. The absorption is the tolerance. She is the designated objector. The system needs her to prove it allows objection. Her rebellion is a feature, not a bug.*

Marsh, reading over his shoulder, tapped the page with her pencil and wrote beneath his note: *Or she is right and nobody can do anything about it.*

The session continued. Reth and Vann resumed their mineral argument during a pause in proceedings. Noll waited for them to finish with the patience of a man who had been waiting for Reth and Vann to finish for three consecutive cycles and would continue waiting for the remainder of his natural life. A frog on the upper ring fell asleep and slid gently sideways into the water, causing a small splash that nobody acknowledged. Another frog, young and bright yellow, asked a question about nursery staffing that was off-agenda and was gently redirected, and the redirecting took four minutes and involved three frogs explaining the proper procedure for raising off-agenda items, which was itself off-agenda, and nobody redirected that.

Vale was beginning to appreciate the comedy of governance when the water moved.

Not the normal movement. Not the humming vibration of consensus or the ripples of thirty-seven bodies shifting on stone. Something deeper. A displacement in the water itself, as if the pool had inhaled. Colors cascaded through the chamber, a fast, involuntary wave of reaction that started at the basin's center and rolled outward through every frog in the room. For a moment the chamber was a single organism, all thirty-seven frogs flashing the same startled amber before their individual colors reasserted themselves.

And then there were more frogs.

Five of them, sitting in the basin as though they had always been there, except that they had not been there three seconds ago. The water around them was colder. Vale could see it in the way the nearest frogs flinched and adjusted, a ring of physical discomfort expanding outward from the newcomers. The water temperature of the entire pool dropped by a degree that was small and unmistakable.

The five new frogs were different. Broader. Thicker-skinned, their coloring a flat, dense grey that did not shift or ripple. Their posture was compact, tucked close, built for holding heat rather than displaying it. They sat in the water of a tropical amphitheater with the bearing of animals who considered this temperature self-indulgent.

Siberians.

The chamber adjusted. Colors settled. Noll's blue deepened by a fraction, which Vale filed as the first involuntary shift he had seen from the High Speaker during a formal session. It lasted less than a second. Then the blue was back, steady, controlled, and Noll was turning toward the delegation with the precise courtesy of a diplomat greeting allies he had not expected for another two days.

"The Siberian delegation is welcome. We had anticipated your arrival at the next coordination window."

The largest of the five, a broad grey frog with a scar across her back and eyes that moved like someone checking exits, spoke without diplomatic preamble. "The window was too slow. We came now. The northern substrates are accelerating ahead of the models. Your models. Which are wrong."

The chamber went quiet. Not the quiet of consensus-building. The quiet of a room that had just been told something nobody wanted to discuss in front of guests.

Noll absorbed it. "The models are being revised. Dr. Krrik's latest projections suggest."

"Krrik's projections are based on data we sent him four days ago. The data from this morning is worse. We will present it. Now, or after your pool-maintenance argument. Your choice."

Oona's orange throat patch was blazing. She leaned toward Marsh. "The Siberians. They are always like this. No ceremony. No greeting songs. They arrive and they start talking about the problem. The Amazon frogs find it rude. The Siberians find the Amazon frogs exhausting. This has been going on for about eighty million years."

Vale watched Noll. The courtesy was precise, controlled, and for the first time in Vale's observation, costing something. Noll's damaged forelimb twitched. Something crossed it, a color Vale could not quite identify in the dim light, there and gone. He was managing all of it, and the room had just gotten harder to manage because these frogs did not respond to his color or his cadence or his balanced sentences. They responded to urgency.

Noll yielded the floor. The Siberian leader presented numbers that Vale could not follow, substrate growth rates and displacement energy margins and calibration thresholds, delivered in flat, clipped sentences stripped of every diplomatic courtesy the Amazon frogs wrapped their information in. No "it was felt." No "the coordination suggests." The data is this. The timeline is this. Adjust.

The chamber's colors shifted through a slow, uneasy palette. Several frogs on the upper rings had stopped pretending to be engaged and were staring at the Siberians with the frank discomfort of people whose meeting had been interrupted by someone who did not care about the agenda.

Talla, Vale noticed, was watching the Siberians with a different expression. Not discomfort. Recognition. These were frogs who said what they meant. She leaned toward the cluster of younger frogs beside her and said something low and quick that Vale could not hear but could read in the way two of the younger frogs turned to look at the Siberian leader with renewed interest.

Noll closed the session seven minutes after the Siberians arrived. He closed it the way he did everything, with procedural grace, acknowledging remaining items, scheduling follow-up coordination, thanking participants. But the rhythm was off. The session had been disrupted, genuinely disrupted, not by Talla's objections, which the system was built to absorb, but by frogs from outside the system who did not perform consensus and did not seem interested in learning.

Vale closed his notebook.

"That," he said to Marsh, "was extremely interesting."

They walked back to the biologists' quarters through corridors that Vale was beginning to navigate without thinking about it, which bothered him in the same diffuse way it had days earlier, when he had first stopped noticing the strangeness of the blue-green light. The quarters were a residential chamber that the humans had tried to make livable. Someone, probably Marsh, had arranged their packs along one wall. Reed's equipment covered the stone floor in a pattern that suggested organization but resisted it. The moss sleeping pads were damp. Everything was damp. Vale had stopped expecting otherwise.

Hart was sitting on a stone ledge with her field kit open, running something through a portable test. She had not attended the session. She had, she had told Vale that morning, more productive things to do than watch frogs agree with each other.

"How was parliament?" she said.

"Productive." Vale sat on the opposite ledge, which was wet, and did not react. "In the sense that I now understand how the mechanism works."

"Enlighten me."

"The consensus is real. It's also pre-arranged. About a third of the chamber coordinates before the session. Three frogs on the fifth ring flickered to blue before every vote. Noll facilitates, which means Noll steers. Objections are absorbed, not addressed."

Hart looked up from her test. "So it's a managed democracy. That's not news. Every democracy is managed."

"It's not managed. It's performed. The performance is the management. Her dissent is load-bearing. The system needs it to prove it allows objection."

"What about the Siberians?"

Marsh had come in behind Vale and was sitting cross-legged on a sleeping pad, notebook open. "They arrived mid-session. Through displacement. Five of them. Different from the Amazon frogs. Physically denser, flat coloring, no diplomatic niceties. Their leader told Noll his models were wrong and presented data. No preamble. No ceremony."

"How did Noll handle it?"

"Perfectly," Vale said. "Which is the problem. He yielded the floor, acknowledged their data, closed the session early. It looked like adaptability. It was damage control. The Siberians brought information he didn't want discussed in open session."

Hart set down her test kit. "What information?"

"Something about substrate growth accelerating. The timelines for the Pruning shifting. I couldn't follow the specifics. The point is, the Siberians don't play his game. They don't do consensus theatre. They walk in and say the thing."

"So they're the opposition."

"They're not opposition. They're a different political culture operating within the same system. Noll can manage frogs who share his assumptions about process. He can absorb Talla because she argues within the framework, even when she's arguing against the framework. The Siberians don't argue at all. They present and expect action. It's a different operating system, and his software doesn't run on it."

Hart was quiet for a moment. Then: "So the global coordination is fragile."

"The global coordination is Noll. Which means it's only as stable as his ability to manage cultures he doesn't control."

"And the Pruning requires global coordination."

"Yes."

"And the Pruning is accelerating."

"According to the Siberians, yes."

Hart picked up her test kit. "That's not a governance structure. That's a man holding a building together by being very calm in the lobby."

The entrance to the chamber rustled and Reed came through, damp, excited, his glasses pushed up into his hair and his shirt untucked on one side. He was carrying a portable light that he had apparently borrowed from Krrik's laboratory, and his eyes had the focused, slightly manic quality of someone who had just seen something that reorganized his understanding of a field he had spent ten years in.

"The moss beds," he said.

"We're talking about governance," Vale said.

"The moss beds are doing something. Krrik showed me the calibration arrays for the Pruning. They're alive, obviously, but they're also adapting. The growth patterns are changing in response to the data the Siberians sent. In real time. The computation moss is revising its own models based on new input, and the revision is producing physical changes in the structure of the bed. The moss is thinking about the Pruning and it's growing differently because of what it's thinking."

"Reed," Hart said.

"And the energy draw, Hart, the energy requirements for global displacement coordination during a Pruning are, according to Krrik's estimates, roughly equivalent to the total energy output of."

"Reed."

"What?"

"We're talking about governance."

Reed blinked. He looked at Vale, then at Hart, then at Marsh, who was watching him with an expression that combined affection with the mild exasperation of someone who had seen this exact sequence before. He pushed his glasses down from his hair onto his nose. They were fogged.

"Right," he said. "Governance. Yes. I'll wait."

He sat on the floor, which was damp, and began cleaning his glasses on a shirt that was also damp, which produced no improvement. His leg bounced. He lasted about forty-five seconds.

"The point is that the Pruning is a biological event managed by a computational system that is itself biological, which means the governance and the biology are the same system, which means when you talk about governance you are also talking about the moss beds, which is what I was."

"Reed," said three voices.

Reed cleaned his glasses again.

Quill arrived last, quiet, settling onto a sleeping pad near the entrance. He had been at the Pools. His hands were in his pockets. He listened while Vale summarized the session again for his benefit, shorter this time, the key points only. The pre-coordinated voting. Talla's absorption. The Siberian arrival. Noll's controlled adaptation.

Quill listened. When Vale finished, he said: "And Noll? What's your read?"

Vale had been thinking about this since the session ended. He had been thinking about it since the tour, since the first Speaking Chambers visit, since the first time Noll had paused between sentences and the pause had told Vale more than the sentence.

"He's a dictator who has convinced an entire species he doesn't exist," Vale said. "It's the most sophisticated political trick I've ever seen."

The room was quiet. Reed stopped bouncing his leg. Marsh's pencil paused. Hart looked at Vale with the steady attention of someone storing a sentence for later.

Quill's hands were still in his pockets. "Is it possible he genuinely believes it? The equality. The distributed coordination. Is it possible he believes in the system and the system just happens to make him powerful?"

Vale looked at him. "That would be worse."

## Chapter Intent
The first full-session observation of frog governance reveals the gap between official consensus and actual power dynamics. Through Vale's political lens, Oona's domestic intelligence, Talla's structural critique, and the Siberians' disruptive arrival, the chapter builds a layered portrait of a system that is neither democratic nor authoritarian but something the humans have no framework for. Vale's closing verdict crystallizes the book's central political question while Quill's counter-question opens the philosophical ground beneath it.
