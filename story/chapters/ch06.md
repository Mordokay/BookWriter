# Chapter 6: Skin

## Chapter Intent

Snake encounter at the Black Pond: surface Ada's shame wound for the first time. Introduce the image of shedding as the possibility of transformation. Deploy the frog's 3rd appearance as comforter (presence without action). Deepen the psychedelic intensity and move Ada from exposed (after the stag) to raw.

## Draft

The water found her before she found it.

A mineral smell, heavy and cool, rising through the spore-sweetness that had coated her throat since the ravine. Different from the stream. The stream had been clean, sharp, a scent that moved. This was still. Peaty. The smell of water that had been sitting in one place for a long time, collecting what the earth offered: tannin from decaying leaves, iron from the soil beneath, the patient chemistry of groundwater seeping through dark layers of peat. The smell reached her and her feet slowed, adjusting their pace before she had a name for what they sensed.

She was looking down.

Since the stag's clearing she had been looking down. Less from grief than from the effort of not looking at what had cracked. Her gaze tracked the moss and fern and the glowing mushroom clusters that lined the fallen logs, registering them at ankle-height, waist-height, never rising above the middle distance. The composure was back in place, or the shape of it was, the straightened spine and the level chin and the hands on the backpack straps, but the composure had a hairline fracture now and looking up meant risking whatever came through the crack. The forest floor was safer. The forest floor had no mirrors.

The terrain had been narrowing. Not the suffocating press of the Tangle, where salal and devil's club walled every path. This was different. The clearings that had opened wide earlier, arena-spaces lit by green glow and bordered by fly agarics, had grown smaller as she walked, drawing together like a corridor closing on its destination. The trees here were older, their trunks wider, their root systems reaching across the ground in thick, tangled ridges that funneled her movement into a single direction. Ferns crowded the gaps between roots. Coral fungi in pale white and muted purple clustered at the bases of the larger trunks, branching upward in delicate formations she looked away from quickly. They branched like antlers. Everything branched like antlers tonight.

She could not go sideways. The roots and the ferns and the dark press of undergrowth on either side closed the lateral options. She could go forward, following the mineral smell downhill, or she could turn back to the stag's clearing with its fragments of bone on the moss. She went forward.

The fog began at her ankles.

Thin, white, cold. It moved above the ground like something poured, filling the low spaces between roots and settling into the hollows where the moss was deepest. Each step carried her shins through it. The fog had weight, a damp pressure against her soaked jeans, and it smelled of the water she was approaching: peaty, iron-laced, still. The temperature had dropped, gradually, the air around the pond holding its chill, contained by the basin walls and the absence of wind. Her rain jacket, thin and inadequate, blocked nothing but the mist. The shivering that had settled to a manageable rhythm on the walk returned now as a finer frequency, the cold finding the gaps between the nylon and her soaked shirt and exploiting them.

Then the trees parted and the Black Pond was there.

It lay in a natural basin, a depression ringed by root systems and dense growth, the water dark and flat. The surface was a mirror, precisely, completely. In the green glow of the mushroom clusters lining the surrounding trees, the water reflected the trunks and the canopy and the faint points of bioluminescent light with a fidelity that erased the boundary between real and reflected. The pond was perhaps thirty feet across, its edges lost in fog and root shadow. The water was black. Not dark blue, not deep green. Black, stained by centuries of tannin and organic sediment until it absorbed every color and returned only the surface: a perfect, depthless mirror.

Ghost pipe grew along the near shore, bone-white stems curving from the dark moss like fingers reaching toward the water. The fungal glow was stronger here, concentrated, the shelf fungi on the surrounding standing dead trees pulsing their heartbeat rhythm, the green light doubled in the pond's surface so that every pulse appeared twice, above and below, the basin breathing with twin sets of lungs. The spore-smell was thick, sweeter here, mingling with the pond's mineral scent and the cool, heavy smell of fog.

Ada stopped at the water's edge. The moss was thick and damp beneath her boots. The fog wrapped her ankles and her shins, and she stood above it in the green glow, looking at the pond because the pond was in front of her and looking down was no longer an option.

Her reflection looked back.

It was her. The rain jacket, zipped to the chin, streaked with mud and snagged at one sleeve where a branch had caught it. The hair in wet ropes. The face pale and drawn in the green light, the jaw set, the eyes dark. The reflection was accurate. For a moment it was accurate.

The reflection tilted its head.

Ada had not tilted hers.

The movement was small. Three or four degrees, a slight adjustment to get a better look. Ada stood still. Her boots did not shift on the moss. Her hands did not move from the backpack straps. The reflection held the tilted position for a beat, then its mouth opened, just slightly, the parting of lips making room for a sound building behind the collarbones, something rising from the chest, the jaw dropping to let it through. The expression the reflection wore was raw, unguarded pain. Ada's actual face remained set. Neutral. The jaw clenched. The composure in place.

The face in the water showed what the face above the water would not.

She looked away, her gaze catching instead on the far shore.

The water striders skated the surface near the far shore, six or seven of them, their legs dimpling the tension membrane, each insect leaving circular ripples that spread outward in perfect concentric rings. Under the psilocybin, the rings expanded and intersected and the intersections formed patterns: geometric, repeating, mandala-shapes that radiated from each contact point and merged with the patterns from adjacent striders until the far surface of the pond was covered in a shifting web of expanding circles. The patterns were beautiful and meaningless and she watched them because watching them meant not looking at the mirror.

The terrain held her. The pond on one side. The root systems and dense fern on the others, closing the space into a chamber no larger than a room. The fog sealed the floor. The glow pulsed its twin heartbeat from above and below. She could not move forward. She could not move sideways. She could stay or she could go back.

She stayed.

Time passed. Or the psilocybin stretched what time there was, pulling each minute into something longer, so that standing at the pond's edge became its own era. The fog thickened. The mandala ripples shifted and reformed. The cold bore closer against her wet clothing. The mycelium hum was present here, faint but steadier than before, a low vibration rising through the moss into the soles of her boots.

Something touched her ankle.

The contact was light. A pressure against the denim just above her boot, and she flinched, a jolt sharp and electric traveling from the ankle through her knee and into her hip, before she had registered what touched her. She stepped back, her boot sliding on the wet moss, and looked down through the thinning fog at her feet.

A snake.

Small. Perhaps eighteen inches, mottled brown, nearly invisible against the dark moss and the root tangle. Smooth-scaled and blunt-headed, its body curved in a loose S on the ground where her boot had been. A rubber boa. The recognition arrived from whatever catalog of forest species still operated beneath the surface of her dissolving frameworks. The snake did not retreat from her flinch. It lay on the moss with the patience of a creature that had nowhere else to be.

Then the psilocybin took it.

The snake's body thickened in her perception. Gradually, without the snap of transformation. A slow intensification, like a whisper that grows louder without anyone raising their voice, the outlines filling and expanding until the snake was no longer eighteen inches but two feet, three, its body thick as her forearm, the scales catching the bioluminescent glow and throwing it back in shifting patterns: green to blue to copper and back, the colors moving across the surface like oil on water, each scale a small lens refracting the light. The head was larger now, wedge-shaped, raised two inches off the moss. The tongue emerged, flickered, tasted the air, withdrew. Emerged again. The rhythm was constant, metronomic, each flicker sampling something she could not perceive: temperature, chemistry, the molecules her body was releasing into the space around her.

The snake was tasting her state.

She stood very still. The flinch had used the last of her adrenaline reserve, and what remained was a rigid, electric alertness, every muscle locked, her body unable to choose between flight and freeze and so choosing neither. The snake lay between her and the pond's edge, its body coiled loosely on the moss, the scales catching the glow with each heartbeat of the fungi, as if the snake and the fungi and the mycelium beneath all shared a cadence she was not yet part of. The tongue flickered. The eyes were dark, lidless, fixed on nothing she could name. The snake did not hiss. The silence from it was worse than any sound could have been, a silence that carried no warning because it held no threat. It was waiting.

A new scent reached her beneath the peat and iron of the pond, beneath the spore-sweetness. Faint, reptilian, dry. The musk of scaled skin, warm and ancient, layered under the cold air. Not sharp like the wolf's territorial scent. Intimate. Close. The smell of a creature that lived against the ground, moved through grass and over stone in sustained contact with the earth, knew the underside of every surface.

Ada lowered herself to the ground.

She did not decide to. Her legs folded as they had at the wolf, as they had on the far bank of the stream, the muscles surrendering before the decision arrived. She sat on the moss at the pond's edge with her knees drawn up, three feet from the coiled snake, the dark water behind her, the fog around them both. Her rain jacket rustled as she folded down. The backpack settled against a root behind her. The devil's club scratches on her left forearm throbbed where the denim bore against them, a familiar heat, the night's accumulating record written on her skin.

The snake's tongue flickered toward her. Tasting the air she occupied.

The shame arrived.

It did not announce itself. It came like nausea: from beneath, from the gut, already physical before she could think the word for it. Heat climbed her neck, her face, the skin beneath the rain jacket's collar flushing hot against the cold nylon. Her stomach turned. Not the sharp revolt of poison but the thick rotation of something that had been sitting at the bottom for a long time, disturbed now, stirring, rising through dark water toward a surface it had not reached in years.

The coiling in her gut mirrored the snake's coil on the moss. The correspondence was physical, not intellectual: her body drawing a line between what lay on the ground before her and what lay inside her. The same shape. The same patient weight. Something she had swallowed and carried and refused to look at, buried beneath the composure and the competence and the locked door and the smile. Below everything she had built above it.

Memory arrived in fragments. Not narrative. Not a story she could tell. Pieces.

A phone in her hand. The weight of it. Heavier than a phone should be, the heft of what she was choosing not to do with it, the call she was choosing not to make, the words she was choosing not to say. The screen dark. Her thumb on the glass. And the silence she chose. Deliberate, sustained, the silence of someone who could speak and did not, who had something to say and sealed it behind her teeth, who walked away from a conversation that needed to happen and let the distance do the work of ending it.

Heat in her face, deepening. The specific heat of being caught at the worst angle by your own memory, the internal spotlight swinging to illuminate the thing stored in the darkest room and finding it exactly where you left it, unchanged, unfaded, waiting.

A door. She had walked through it. The door was the end of something, and she walked through it when she should have turned around, should have stayed, should have opened her mouth and said the words she had rehearsed in her car and her shower and her sleepless three-in-the-mornings. The door closed behind her with the soft click of something that would not open again. The corridor beyond was quiet, and in the quiet she had felt relief. Which was the worst part. The relief of escape misread as the relief of resolution, and she had walked down that corridor and gotten into her car and driven home and the relief lasted until the apartment door closed behind her and then the relief was gone and what replaced it had no name but it lived in her gut and it tightened.

A hand. Not hers. Someone's hand, reaching across a distance that was only a few feet but might as well have been geological. The fingers extended. The gesture unmistakable: stay. Don't go. I need you to stay. She had seen the hand. She had registered the hand with the detailed, unflinching perception she had never learned to turn off. She had seen the hand and she had walked through the door anyway, and the hand fell, palm against thigh, and the sound it made was so small it should have been inaudible in the ordinary noise of a room, but she heard it for months afterward. She heard it in her apartment at two in the morning. She heard it over the sound of running water and traffic and the careful conversations she had with the person she had failed, conversations that grew shorter and more careful until they stopped, and the stopping was also a relief, and the relief was also a lie.

The fragments did not connect into a story. They were not meant to. They were the shape of something unforgiven, specific in their weight, approximate in their architecture. A phone not used. A silence chosen. A door walked through. A hand not taken. The shame was not in any single piece. It was in the pattern: the accumulated evidence of someone who left when she should have stayed, who chose the clean exit over the messy truth, who had perfected the art of departure so thoroughly that the departing had become invisible to her, just how she moved, just what she did, leaving rooms and conversations and people with the quiet efficiency of someone who could not bear to stand still long enough to be asked the question the stag had asked.

Her arms wrapped around her knees. Her forehead against the wet denim. The posture of a child in a closet. The posture of someone trying to make herself small enough that the thing looking at her might lose interest. The rain jacket creaked against her bent back. The devil's club spines drove their small fires into her forearm through the fabric. Her breathing had gone shallow, quick, the cadence of a body in retreat.

"You know what you did."

The whisper arrived close enough to feel, intimate as breath against the curve of her ear, though the snake was three feet away on the moss. The voice was quiet, with the stripped plainness of a fact. The volume of a thought you cannot stop thinking at two in the morning, the sentence that plays on repeat beneath every productive hour and every careful smile: flat, certain, stripped of judgment because judgment was unnecessary when the fact was this clear.

Ada pressed her forehead harder against her knees.

"You could have stayed."

The second whisper filled the space the first had opened. The words were not the snake's. They were hers. Her own accusation, the sentence she had spoken to herself in bathrooms and parked cars and the gray hours before sleep, given a body now, given scales and a tongue and a coil on the moss, the inner prosecutor externalized and patient and impossible to dismiss because dismissing it required arguing with her own memory.

Her stomach turned again. The nausea was thick and slow, not the sharp crisis of poison but the chronic, low-grade sickness of a weight that had been sitting in her too long, a mass her body wanted to surface and her mind would not permit. Heat flared in her face. Her fingers dug into her shins through the wet denim, nails finding purchase, the small pain a counterweight to the larger one.

The snake moved.

Not toward her. Alongside her, its body uncoiling from its resting position and extending across the moss in a lateral glide that left a faint trail of glow on the dark ground behind it, the bioluminescent light clinging to the surface the scales had touched. The colors shifted across the snake's body as it moved: green to blue to copper, each scale refracting the glow independently, the whole surface rippling with light like a stream of liquid metal. The snake's path took it to a cluster of root systems at the pond's near edge, thick roots interlocking over stones, and it pushed its body against the rough surface, the head lowered, the movement deliberate.

The process was nothing she would have imagined. Nothing like a clean unzipping or the swift molt of an insect. The snake pushed its snout into the stone, working the old skin backward, leveraging friction to peel the casing from around the jaw. The old skin separated from the head in uneven progress, the thin layer rolling back like a glove turned inside out. Beneath it, the new scales emerged: darker, richer, slick with the fluid that had separated old from new.

The snake worked. It pushed against the stone, against the bark of the roots, against anything rough enough to catch the old skin and hold it while the body inside pulled free. The labor was visible. Each contraction of the body gained another fraction of an inch, the old skin rolling backward in a tube of pale membrane that caught the light and held it in its folds. The snake's muscles strained. The effort was sustained and unglamorous: a body that could not stay inside what it had outgrown, doing the necessary labor of emerging.

Under the psilocybin, the act was almost unbearable to watch. Ada's chest tightened. She was watching a creature strip itself raw, peel away the surface it had worn and lived in and shown to the world, and the shedding was not easy. The snake's body contracted and released in rhythmic pulses, pushing, straining, each cycle freeing another segment of the old skin, the shed sleeve lengthening on the moss behind the snake's retreating body. The closeness of it bore against Ada's ribs. Something being seen that was not meant to be seen. Something private and laborious and necessary.

The shed skin peeled free of the tail in a final release. It lay on the moss beside the stone: translucent, whole, the shape of what it had contained. The scale pattern was visible in the membrane, ghostlike, a record of the surface the snake had worn. In the green glow, the shed skin caught the faint light and held it, empty and delicate, shaped like a snake and containing nothing.

The snake beneath was the same snake. Same body, same proportions, same wedge head and same tongue flickering. But the new scales were raw and glistening, the colors deeper and sharper, and the snake moved away from the shed skin with the unburdened ease of a creature that no longer carried what it had been carrying. Ada looked at the shed skin on the moss, looked at it for only a moment, and when she looked back the snake was gone. No departure, no visible exit. Just the moss where it had been, undisturbed, as if the new skin had made the snake too close to the ground to see, or as if the shedding itself had been the point and the creature had simply ceased to be necessary.

The shed skin remained.

Ada stared at it. The empty sleeve on the dark moss, catching green light in its folds. The snake had lived inside that skin. The snake had outgrown it. The snake had pressed and strained and worked itself free, and the labor had been slow and painful and necessary, and what remained was not a new creature but the same creature without the weight.

Her left hand, resting on the moss beside her knee, closed around something small. A stone, smooth, the size of a large almond, cold and hard against her palm. The motion was not conscious. Her fingers found it and took it and her hand went to the pocket of her rain jacket and the stone went in and her hand returned to her knee and the entire sequence passed beneath the surface of her attention, a body collecting what it needed without asking permission.

She stared at the shed skin. The image lodged somewhere behind her sternum, in the space where the shame sat: the possibility that what you carried could be released. Simply set down, left on the moss, walked away from in a body that was the same body but lighter. She could not follow the image yet. The shame was too thick, the knotting in her gut too heavy, the flush in her face too real. But she had seen it. The snake had shown her a process. Not a lesson. A process. And the process was painful, and it was slow, and it ended with something empty on the ground and something alive moving on.

The silence after the snake was heavy. The fog thickened over the pond's surface, softening the mandala patterns the water striders had drawn, obscuring the mirror that had shown her the wrong face. The green glow pulsed, steady, indifferent to what had happened at its edge. The insect sounds were distant here, muted by the basin's walls and the dense press of root and fern. Ada sat at the pond's edge and shook.

Not from the cold, though the cold was there, the rain jacket doing its thin work over her soaked clothing. This tremor was different. Finer. The shaking that comes not during the crisis but after, when rigidity loosens at the joints, when the body finally registers what the mind refused to acknowledge while it was happening.

She was shaking from what she had looked at.

The frog was simply there.

It sat on a flat stone at the water's edge, four feet to her left, half its body above the stone and half reflected in the dark water below. Large and heavy, the same mottled olive-brown and gold from the fallen log and the wolf's clearing, the same pale cream stripe down its back. The warty skin caught the green glow in the shallow pools between the warts, holding the light like wet stone, without effort.

It had not arrived. It occupied the stone as if it had been sitting there for hours, for years, for the lifetime of the pond.

The frog did not move toward her. It did not lead or guide or demonstrate. It sat. Its sides expanded with each breath, the pale skin of the throat stretching outward and contracting, the movement visible, rhythmic. The breathing was unhurried. Each expansion lasted two seconds, each contraction the same, the cadence as steady as the heartbeat of the shelf fungi but slower, deeper, the pace of a creature that had never been rushed.

Ada watched the breathing. She watched it because she could not look at anything else without seeing the shed skin or the pond or her own hands or the space where the snake had been. The frog's breathing was the only neutral thing in the basin. The only thing that was not asking her to feel something.

"You're still here."

The words arrived without the frog's jaw moving. The same mechanism as the wolf and the stag and the snake: words assembling in the space between them, arriving in Ada's awareness with the quiet certainty of something that had been true before it was spoken. But the voice was different from the snake's intimate whisper, different from the stag's cold precision, different from the wolf's weary recognition. The frog's voice was low. Unhurried. Warm in a way that had nothing to do with temperature. Not kind, not comforting in any way she could have named or refused. Simply present. Like stone. Like ground. Like the thing beneath your feet that you forget is holding you up.

Ada's breathing was ragged. Short, uneven pulls through her nose, the rhythm broken by the trembling that shook her ribs and disrupted each attempt to fill her lungs. Her arms were still wrapped around her knees. The shame was still there: hot, thick, knotted in her gut, a physical weight that no amount of air could shift.

The frog breathed. In. Out. The throat expanded, contracted. The pace continued, unaffected by her presence or her trembling or the shame that filled the basin like fog.

"That's the hardest one to look at," the frog said.

Then it was quiet. It sat on its stone and breathed. The green glow pulsed. The fog drifted over the pond. The words settled into the silence and stayed there, not explaining, not instructing, not offering comfort in any form she could have argued with or rejected. A fact, spoken by a creature that had been in this forest long enough to know.

Ada breathed. Her breath caught, stuttered, caught again. The rhythm would not hold. She was trying to match the frog's cadence, not by decision but by the body's instinct for any available order when its own systems were in disarray. The frog breathed in. She breathed in. The frog breathed out. She tried to follow. The first three attempts failed, each exhale cut short by a tremor that clenched her ribs and forced the air out in thin, uneven bursts.

The fourth held. A full exhale, following the frog's rhythm, the air leaving her lungs in a slow stream that matched the contraction of the frog's pale throat. She drew in again. The inhale came easier, as if the exhale had cleared space for it. Another exhale. The frog set the tempo and Ada followed, not smoothly, not perfectly, but with growing approximation, each cycle a fraction closer to the steady, unhurried pace the frog maintained without cost.

The trembling eased. Not stopped. Eased. The fine vibration that had shaken her ribs and her hands and the muscles along her spine dimmed from a frequency she could feel in her teeth to something slower, deeper, manageable. The shame was still there. The heat in her face. The coiling in her gut. The weight of the unforgiven. But her body had found a tempo that could hold it all without shattering, like a bowl that holds water if it stays steady enough, and the frog's breathing was the steady thing, and Ada's breathing was learning its shape.

The frog sat for a long time. Or what felt long, which in this forest, under this chemistry, could have been five minutes or an hour. Ada did not try to track it. She breathed. The frog breathed. The fog drifted. The glow pulsed. The pond reflected the light from above and below and the fog slowly erased both reflections. Ada did not look at the mirror. She watched the frog's sides expand and contract and she breathed.

Then the frog slipped from the stone. The motion was fluid, a simple forward shift from still to moving, the heavy body sliding from the rock's surface into the dark water with a sound so small and clean it was barely a sound at all: a single, soft break in the surface tension, a circle of ripples expanding outward from the point of entry, and then the frog was beneath the surface, and the ripples widened and flattened and the pond was still again, the mirror reforming over the place where the frog had been.

Ada stayed at the water's edge, alone. The stone in her jacket pocket sat against her thigh, small and hard and unremembered. The rain jacket rustled with each breath. The devil's club scratches throbbed their heat along her forearm, through the swollen web of her right hand. The fog lay thick over the pond's surface now, obscuring the mirror, erasing the reflection that had shown her more than she wanted to hold.

She reached for the water bottle. The backpack, the zipper stiff with damp. The bottle was lighter than the last time she'd held it, the water reduced to a few inches at the bottom. She unscrewed the cap and drank. The water was warm from body heat, flat, tasting of the bottle and the hours it had been closed. It went down her throat in slow swallows: one, two, three, four. The last swallow left maybe an inch at the bottom. She recapped the bottle and held it in her lap for a moment, the warm plastic against her palms, a human object in a world that had stopped being human hours ago.

The bottle went back into the pack, and she zipped it closed with stiff fingers.

The fog was heavy now, thick enough that the far shore of the pond had vanished. The water striders had settled, their mandala ripples stilled. The ghost pipes at the near shore stood in their bone-white cluster, patient, drawing sustenance from the network beneath them. The mycelium hum entered through her sit bones and her palms flat on the ground, stronger now, or she was simply more open to it.

The shame had not gone. Nothing had been resolved. The phone, the door, the hand. They sat inside her where they had always sat, no lighter, no more forgiven. The snake had not healed her. The frog had not fixed her. The forest had not absolved her of what she had done and not done.

But she had looked at it.

She had looked at the coiled thing in her gut, the shape of what she carried, and the looking had not killed her. It had produced nausea and heat and trembling and the desire to disappear, and she had not disappeared. She was still here. The frog had said so. And being still here was, for the moment, the only fact that mattered.

She stood. The fog parted around her boots. The moss compressed and released. She carried two things: the shame, which had surfaced and would not go back under, and the image of the shed skin on the moss, translucent and empty, shaped like what it had contained, left behind by something that was the same creature but lighter.

The forest ahead was dark. The green glow pulsed in its steady beat. The spore-taste coated her throat. The mycelium hum traveled through the soles of her waterlogged boots with each step, patient, continuous, growing. The night was not over. The night had a long way to go.

And somewhere in the darkness ahead, something was watching her with eyes that saw too much.
