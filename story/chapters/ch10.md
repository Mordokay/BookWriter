# Chapter 10: The Fungal Audit

Hart had identified eleven species of fungus in the settlement's corridors before breakfast, and nobody seemed concerned.

She stood in the biologists' shared quarters with her field notebook open on a borrowed moss cushion, her sample kit laid out with the precision of a surgeon who expected the operating room to be filthy. Reed was sitting on the floor retying a boot. Marsh was writing in her own notebook at the low stone table, her pencil moving in the small, steady hand of someone who had been documenting animal behavior in field conditions for fifteen years and did not intend to stop because the animals had started talking back.

"I am conducting a field epidemiological survey of this settlement," Hart said. "Whether anyone wants to join or not."

Reed looked up. "Right now?"

"Right now. I have been here eleven days. I have catalogued fungal growth on every wall I have touched, in the water channels, on the food preparation surfaces, and in the communal pools where everybody sits in each other's chemical runoff and calls it socializing. Nobody has tested any of it. Nobody appears interested in testing any of it. I am interested."

"Hart, we are guests in a civilization that can fold space."

"And that civilization has mold on its breakfast tables. You owe me. I catalogued your root toxins for three weeks in Manaus."

Reed pushed his glasses up. This was true.

Marsh closed her notebook. "I'll come. I want to see how the practitioners interact with patients. Whether they touch them, how close they stand, whether they talk during treatment or stay quiet."

"Bon. Reed, you handle the biochemistry questions. I handle the epidemiology. Marsh documents the behavioral component." Hart snapped her sample kit shut. "We are not asking permission. We are going to walk in and look competent until someone stops us."

Nobody stopped them. The treatment chambers were not chambers at all, not in any sense Hart recognized. They were open, connected pools stepped into the root-wall system, warm and shallow, each one roughly two meters across and separated from the next by low stone ridges worn smooth by centuries of use. The air smelled of mineral salts and something astringent, like wet copper mixed with bruised leaves. Patients were visible to anyone passing through, and anyone passing through had opinions.

A frog with a crushed forelimb sat in the nearest pool while a practitioner applied something thick and dark green to the injury site. Two pools over, an elderly frog with labored breathing soaked in water that had been treated with something that turned it faintly amber and smelled of pine resin. In a shallow basin near the corridor entrance, a young frog sat very still while a second practitioner examined his throat, which had turned an unhealthy grey.

"That one swallowed something during a fermentation experiment," said a passing frog, pausing to observe Hart the way one might observe a large, unusual insect that had wandered into the kitchen. "He is always swallowing things. Last season it was a sample jar lid."

The practitioners moved between patients without hurrying. They had been doing this a long time and did not need to perform urgency. Three were currently working, all roughly the same small size, their skin colors muted to a steady professional green that Hart was beginning to recognize as the frog equivalent of a white coat. They noticed the humans immediately and did not appear alarmed.

Hart began asking questions.

The first practitioner, who introduced herself as Tivven, was treating the crushed forelimb. The treatment involved a paste of biological compounds that, as far as Hart could determine, was stimulating cellular regrowth directly at the injury site.

"The digit will regenerate," Tivven said. "Slowly. Perhaps four of your weeks. The pain is managed through the water's mineral content."

"You can regenerate a lost limb."

"A digit. A full limb takes longer. Several of your months, depending on the patient's age and general condition."

Hart looked at Reed. Reed's glasses had fogged. He was writing in his notebook so fast the pencil was making dents in the paper.

"And cancer?" Hart asked.

Tivven's colors shifted slightly, the way a person might tilt their head at an odd question. "Unregulated cell growth? We identify it early through chemical monitoring. The body's signaling systems usually catch it. When they do not, we intervene biochemically. It is not difficult. Your species finds it difficult?"

"We find it extremely difficult."

"Your skin is very thick," Tivven said. She appeared to mean this literally. "You cannot read your own bodies. We breathe through ours, absorb through ours, signal through ours. If something grows wrong, the body broadcasts it. Your bodies do not broadcast. They. Contain." She said the word "contain" the way someone might say "suffocate." "How do you know when you are ill?"

"We wait until it hurts," Hart said.

Tivven's spots flared with something between alarm and pity.

A second practitioner, younger and slightly larger, had been listening from the adjacent pool. He hopped onto the stone ridge between them, water dripping from his hindquarters.

"Explain your medicine," he said. "How you treat injuries."

Hart looked at Marsh. Marsh gave her the slightest nod. Go ahead.

"Surgery," Hart said. "For serious injuries, we cut the body open. We use anesthesia to render the patient unconscious. We make incisions, repair the internal damage manually, suture the tissue closed, and monitor recovery in a sterile environment."

Tivven stopped applying the paste. Her colors went through a slow cascade, greens to yellow to a pale amber that Hart read as horror. The second practitioner's spots darkened.

"You cut the body open," Tivven said.

"Yes."

"On purpose."

"On purpose."

"And the patient agrees to this."

"The patient is unconscious."

Tivven turned to the second practitioner. Her colors said everything her voice did not.

"That is not better," Tivven said.

The second practitioner leaned forward. "Wait. The logic. You cannot regenerate tissue, so you must access the damage directly. Through the barrier of the skin. Because your skin is a barrier, not a, not a." He searched. "Your skin is a wall. So you go through the wall."

"That is barbaric," Tivven said.

"That is engineering around a constraint," the second practitioner said. "Brutal, but there is logic. If I could not signal through the skin, if the body did not broadcast, if I had to reach the injury blind." His colors shifted through something almost like professional respect. "I would also go through the wall. I would hate it. But I would do it."

"You would not."

"I would. What else could you do? Leave the damage inside and hope?"

A third practitioner arrived, older, moving slowly, her skin faded to a translucent grey-green that reminded Hart of Pem. She had been listening from the corridor.

"All medicine is violence managed with good intentions," she said.

The pool went quiet for a moment.

"You cut to heal," she continued, settling onto a ledge. "We dissolve barriers to heal. Both methods damage something to repair something else. The debate is about which damage you accept." She looked at Hart with clear, bright eyes. "What do you accept?"

"Sterility," Hart said. "Controlled environments. Sealed wounds. Containment."

"Containment." The old practitioner said it the same way Tivven had said it. Like a diagnosis. "You contain the patient. You contain the infection. You contain the body from its environment."

"We keep things clean."

"You keep things closed."

Reed was still writing. Marsh had stopped. She was watching the practitioners' colors with the focused attention she usually reserved for primate mothers handling their young.

Hart moved deeper into the treatment chambers. The crushed-digit frog was flexing his forelimb experimentally, the paste already working. The elderly frog in the amber water was breathing more easily, the labored quality fading. The fermentation-experiment frog was being given something to drink that turned his throat from grey to a cautious green, accompanied by a lecture from the practitioner that sounded identical in tone to every "this is why we do not eat things off the lab floor" speech Hart had heard in graduate school.

She noticed the fungus at the third pool.

It was growing on the stone rim, a thin, persistent film of something pale and fuzzy. She had seen the same species on the corridor walls. On the food surfaces. On the edge of the communal bathing pools. The practitioners were working directly beside it without any apparent concern.

"This," Hart said, crouching beside the pool rim. "This fungal growth. On every surface. In the treatment areas."

Tivven glanced at it. "Yes."

"You are aware of it."

"Of course."

"And you are not treating it."

Tivven's colors did something complicated. "We are aware of it. It is not. It is not what you think it is."

"What I think it is, is a fungal infection colonizing every shared surface in your medical infrastructure. In a human facility, this would be a biohazard. We would shut down the treatment area, sterilize every surface, and investigate the source."

"You would seal everything."

"We would clean everything."

The old practitioner had followed Hart. She stood at the pool's edge, her faded skin catching the bioluminescence.

"The fungal growth persists because we allow it to persist," she said. "We could eliminate it. We have the biochemistry. Krrik's laboratory could produce a compound that would kill every fungal colony in the settlement within a day." She paused. "It would also require sealing the skin."

Hart waited.

"We breathe through our skin. You know this. But the skin does more than breathe. It is how we signal, how we read each other, how we synchronize during the displacement operations. It is how we." She stopped. "It is how we are ourselves. Together."

"And the fungus gets in through the same openness."

"The fungus gets in because the door is open. If you close the door, you stop the fungus. You also stop everything else. No chemical signaling. No color communication. No shared breathing. No synchronization. No merging." The old practitioner looked at Hart with steady, clear eyes. "A sealed frog is a safe frog. A sealed frog is also a deaf frog, a mute frog, and a frog who will never feel another mind touch theirs. We chose. We chose a long time ago."

"You chose infection."

"We chose connection. The infection is the cost."

Reed had stopped writing. His pencil was suspended above the notebook. Marsh was looking at Hart with an expression Hart could not read, or did not want to.

Hart straightened up. The fungal film on the stone rim caught the bioluminescence and glistened, pale and persistent and everywhere.

"Flow, don't push," Tivven said to a patient who was resisting the mineral soak. It sounded like medical advice. It also sounded like a prayer. Hart could not tell the difference.

The argument about the moss compress started because of Oona.

Oona arrived at the treatment pools twenty minutes later, her orange throat patch at maximum brightness, which Hart had learned meant either gossip, strong opinions, or both. She was visiting a neighbor, a squat olive frog soaking in a warm pool with stiff joints.

"Brenk, I brought the compress," Oona said, producing a wad of damp moss from somewhere Hart did not want to think about. "Apply it to the left side. Leave it for an hour. The pain will improve."

"What is in that?" Hart asked.

Oona looked at her. "Moss."

"What kind of moss? Treated with what? Applied based on what evidence?"

"Applied based on the evidence that my grandmother used it, my mother used it, I have used it for nine years, and it works."

"That is not evidence. That is anecdote."

"Anecdote is what evidence looks like before scientists get involved and make it complicated."

Tivven made a sound that might have been a laugh or a professional sigh. Two frogs in adjacent pools turned to watch. The neighbor, Brenk, accepted the compress and applied it to his left side without apparent concern about the medical debate unfolding over his body.

"The moss compress has no demonstrated biochemical mechanism," Tivven said. "We have tested it."

"You have tested it and found nothing because you tested the wrong thing," Oona said. "You tested whether the moss kills infection. It does not kill infection. That is not what it does. It helps with the pain. My grandmother did not care about the mechanism. She cared about the pain."

"Oona, I respect your grandmother."

"You did not know my grandmother."

"I respect the concept of your grandmother. But the compress is not medicine. It is tradition."

"Tradition is what medicine looks like before it has a budget," Oona said. Her throat patch flared. Brenk, beneath the compress, appeared to be improving, or at least was enjoying the attention.

Hart was agreeing with an alien medical practitioner and disagreeing with a frog housewife about moss, which was not a scenario McGill had prepared her for.

"The compress is a placebo," Hart said. "Or the warm water is doing the work, and the moss is decorative."

Oona turned to her with the full force of a personality that had been winning domestic arguments for sixteen years. "You have been here eleven days. I have been applying this compress for nine years. Brenk has had stiff joints for three years. Every time I bring the compress, the stiffness improves. Every time the practitioners give him the mineral soak alone, the stiffness returns within a day. You may call that placebo. I call that a pattern."

Three frogs nearby murmured approval. A fourth, sitting in a shallow pool with what appeared to be a mild skin irritation, offered: "My aunt uses the same compress. She is seventy-four."

"Seventy-four what?" Hart asked.

"Seasons. She is very old and her joints are perfect."

"Her joints are not perfect," Oona said. "But they are better than Brenk's, and she uses the compress twice a week."

Krrik arrived.

He hopped into the treatment area with the lopsided gait Hart recognized from the laboratory, his mottled skin dusted with spore residue, both eyes fixed on a stone tablet he was carrying. He appeared to be there to collect a biochemical analysis from the practitioners, not to participate in anyone's argument.

"Krrik," Hart said. "The moss compress. Superstition or medicine?"

Krrik did not look up from his tablet. "The moss compress is folk practice unsupported by controlled observation. It persists because anecdote is more emotionally satisfying than data. It has no demonstrated mechanism of action. I do not have time for this."

Oona's throat patch dimmed. Tivven looked vindicated.

"However," Krrik said, still not looking up, "the mucilage layer of that particular moss species contains three compounds with measurable anti-inflammatory properties. The concentrations are low but consistent. Whether these compounds are absorbed through the skin at therapeutic levels depends on exposure duration and the patient's individual permeability, which varies. I mention this because it is true, not because it is relevant to anyone's argument."

He collected his analysis from Tivven, exchanged two sentences about a dosing calculation, and left.

The treatment pool was silent for a moment.

"So the compress works," Oona said.

"He said the compounds exist. He did not say they work at therapeutic levels," Tivven said.

"He said anti-inflammatory. He said consistent. He said absorbed through the skin. The compress works."

"The compress might work. Through a mechanism you did not know about and cannot control for."

"I do not need to control for it. I need Brenk's joints to stop hurting."

Brenk, beneath his compress, had closed his eyes. He looked comfortable. Whether this was the anti-inflammatory compounds, the warm water, the placebo effect, or the simple pleasure of being fussed over by someone who cared, Hart could not determine. She found this more annoying than she expected. She had wanted to be right. Oona had wanted to be right. Krrik had made them both right and neither of them happy, and he had done it without noticing. She was fairly sure he had not noticed.

Hart spent the next hour walking the treatment pools, cataloguing what she saw. Reed trailed her with his notebook. Marsh sat with Tivven and the old practitioner, asking questions about how practitioners were trained, how they communicated during treatment, whether they spoke to patients or stayed silent.

The daily flow of the chambers was larger than Hart had expected. Beyond the routine cases, there were injuries from the Pruning staging grounds: a frog with a stress fracture in her hind leg from repeated jumping drills, two displacement operators with the glassy-eyed disorientation that followed extended synchronization practice, a young frog who had strained something in his back carrying equipment. The staging injuries were handled with the same unhurried competence as everything else, but Hart noticed the volume. More injuries than the chambers were designed for. The practitioners absorbed the increase without complaint, without visible strain, and without enough staff.

Two older practitioners argued quietly about dosing philosophy while treating adjacent patients. One favored lower concentrations over longer periods. The other preferred higher concentrations with shorter exposure. The argument had been running for years. It would continue running until one of them died, at which point the survivor would continue it with someone else.

"Is the current generation healthier than the last?" Hart asked the old practitioner.

"Every generation asks this question. Every generation believes the answer is no. The archive contains data suggesting the answer is consistently yes, but nobody reads the archive data because it contradicts the satisfying belief that everything is getting worse."

"So things are getting better."

"Things are getting different. Better and worse are categories I have stopped finding useful." She adjusted the mineral content of a treatment pool with a casual precision that Hart found both impressive and slightly alarming. "Your species measures health by the absence of disease. We measure it by the quality of connection. A frog with a fungal infection who can feel every mind in the breathing circle is healthier, by our measure, than a frog with perfect skin who has sealed herself off."

Hart wrote this down. She wrote it in French, because some things were easier to think about in a language that did not belong to this conversation.

The debrief happened at the biologists' quarters that evening. The low vibration of the water channels hummed through the floor. Hart sat on a root ledge. Reed was cross-legged on the floor with his notebook. Marsh leaned against the wall, her own notebook closed. Vale arrived mid-sentence, settling onto a stone shelf near the entrance with the practiced ease of a man who had been walking into rooms late his entire career and never apologized for it.

Quill was in the doorway. He did not sit. He leaned against the root-frame and listened.

"Their medicine is more advanced than ours," Hart said. "Regeneration. Cancer. Pain management through water chemistry Reed says is centuries ahead of anything we have. And they are covered in fungus. Every surface, every pool, every treatment area. Fungal growth that any hospital on Earth would shut down for."

"Because they can't eliminate it?" Vale asked.

"Because they chose not to. The fungus gets in through the skin. Close the skin, kill the fungus. Close the skin, kill everything else. No signaling, no breathing circles, no synchronization for the Pruning. They have a phrase for it. A sealed frog." Hart paused. "They say it like a diagnosis."

Quill, in the doorway, shifted his weight. He did not speak.

"So they live with the infections," Reed said.

"They live with constant, low-grade fungal colonization of every shared surface and call it the price of being open. Their old practitioner told me that health, by their measure, is not the absence of disease. It is the quality of connection. A frog with a fungal infection who can feel every mind around her is healthier than a frog with clean skin who is sealed off."

"That is philosophy, not medicine," Vale said.

Hart looked at him. "That is what I thought. For about an hour. Then I watched them regenerate a crushed digit in real time, treat cancer like a scheduling problem, and manage pain through water chemistry that Reed says is two centuries ahead of anything at MIT. And the same people, the same practitioners, in the same room, looked at the fungus on their treatment surfaces and said: 'Yes. We know. That is what openness costs.' They are not ignorant. They are not negligent. They made a decision, and the decision is philosophically consistent with everything else about them, and I find that." She stopped. "I find that very uncomfortable."

The water channels hummed beneath the floor. Marsh was watching Hart with the quiet attention she usually gave to things she was not ready to say aloud. Vale's eyes had narrowed, which meant he was processing something he did not like. Reed was writing. Quill stood in the doorway, his hands in his pockets, his face still.

"The frogs' relationship with disease is not negligence, it is philosophy," Hart said. "And I do not know which is worse."

## Chapter Intent
This chapter uses Hart's clinical cynicism as a comedy engine and an investigative lens to reveal the book's sharpest expression of the primitive-impossible contrast: frog medicine is centuries ahead of human medicine, and it operates in open pools covered in fungus because the frogs deliberately chose infection over isolation. Every piece of medical information arrives through dialogue and character reaction, never through narrator exposition. The surgery horror scene plays as mutual incomprehension comedy with both sides genuinely appalled by the other's approach. The permeability trade-off lands through the old practitioner's dialogue and Hart's reaction, giving the book's core theme (biology shapes philosophy) its most concrete expression. The folk-remedy argument lightens the mood while revealing Oona's domestic authority and Krrik's oblivious precision. Hart's closing verdict at the debrief crystallizes the chapter's discovery with dry humor and genuine disturbance: the frogs' relationship with disease is not negligence but philosophy, and Hart cannot decide which unsettles her more.
