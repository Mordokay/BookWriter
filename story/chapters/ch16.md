# Chapter 16: The Siberian Argument

The largest Speaking Chamber in Vreth-in-the-Root could hold two hundred frogs in its tiered mineral pools. Reed counted sixty-three on his way in and stopped counting because more kept arriving.

They came from two directions. The Amazon frogs descended the root-carved steps in clusters of three and four, their colors conversational, shifting through greens and ambers and the occasional flare of orange that meant someone had just said something worth repeating. The Siberians materialized on the displacement stones at the chamber's western end in groups of five, compact and grey and still, stepping off the platform with the unhurried economy of commuters exiting a train they had taken a thousand times. The water temperature dropped a degree each time a new group arrived. Nobody adjusted the heating. The Siberians, apparently, did not consider the cold worth mentioning. The Amazon frogs considered it a cultural aggression too petty to acknowledge and too annoying to ignore.

Reed sat on an upper ledge near the chamber's eastern wall, his notebook on his knee, his pen already moving. The ledge was wet. Every surface in the settlement was wet. He had stopped caring about this roughly nine days ago and now occupied the damp stone with the resigned acceptance of a man who had reclassified "dry" as a theoretical state.

Marsh and Hart were across the chamber, two ledges below. Hart was leaning toward Marsh and saying something that made Marsh glance at the Siberian delegation with the focused calm she used when cataloguing a new variable. Vale was further down, near the second-ring ledges, positioned with a clear sightline to both Noll at the center and the Siberian contingent at the western pools. He had his arms folded and his head tilted at the angle that meant he was mapping power dynamics before the first word was spoken. Quill sat alone near the top of the chamber, watching the room fill, his hands in his lap, his expression the quiet, interior look that Reed had stopped trying to interpret because Quill's interior had become a place Reed could not follow.

Below, in the warm mineral water, sixty-odd frogs arranged themselves across the tiered pools with the practiced informality of a species that had been holding these meetings for longer than mammals had existed. The Amazon frogs spread across the upper and middle tiers. The Siberians occupied the lower western pools in a tight formation that was not exactly military but was not exactly casual. Between the two groups, a strip of unoccupied water ran like a border nobody had drawn but everyone respected.

The hum was enormous. Not loud. Enormous. Sixty-plus frogs vibrating at slightly different frequencies, their bodies producing overlapping pulses that traveled through the mineral water and up through the stone and into Reed's teeth. It was the sound of a building-sized tuning fork operated by a committee that disagreed about the pitch.

Noll opened the meeting with three sentences. Reed could not follow the formal phrasing because Noll's diplomatic register required a fluency in bureaucratic indirection that Reed had never developed and refused to cultivate. Something about coordination. Something about shared purpose. Something about the honored presence of the Permafrost delegation and the sustained collaborative relationship between the Federations.

The Siberian lead did not wait for Noll to finish his fourth sentence. She was the same scarred female who had disrupted the planning session weeks ago, broader than the Amazon frogs, her flat grey coloring absorbing light in a way that made the vivid Amazon greens look theatrical by comparison. She spoke from the western pool without rising, without preamble, without any of the chromatic signals that the Amazon frogs layered onto every public statement.

"The growth models your engineer has shared are inaccurate. The Siberian monitoring network confirms a divergence of nineteen percent from the projections you circulated. This is not a criticism. Your models were built on historical patterns. The patterns have changed."

The chamber's colors shifted. A ripple of amber and pale green passed through the Amazon contingent, moving from the lower tiers upward, a visible wave of reaction that reminded Reed of nothing so much as a stadium crowd responding to a disputed call. Several frogs near Noll turned to look at Krrik, who was sitting at the chamber's edge near a slab of thinking moss, his brown-green skin cycling through rapid unconscious patterns that meant he was processing something and had probably not noticed that sixty frogs were now looking at him.

Noll's blue held. Steady, controlled. His damaged forelimb showed a faint amber flicker, which Reed had learned was the only unrehearsed signal in Noll's entire repertoire.

"The coordination office has noted the divergence," Noll said. "Updated projections are being prepared. The collaborative framework accommodates new data without requiring a reassessment of foundational methodology."

The Siberian lead's grey skin did not shift. "Your foundational methodology includes mandatory consciousness-synchronization for displacement coordination. We coordinate displacement technically, without the synchronization requirement. We have done so for sixty million years. Our displacement accuracy exceeds yours by a factor I will not state in open session because your frogs will find it humiliating."

A sound moved through the Amazon frogs. Not a word. A vibration, low and collective, carrying offense in its frequency. Reed's sternum registered it before his ears did.

The Siberian lead continued without acknowledging the reaction. "You do not need to merge consciousness to coordinate displacement. You need to merge consciousness because you enjoy it and have confused enjoyment with necessity."

The chamber erupted. Not into shouting. Frogs do not shout. Into color and vibration, dozens of bodies producing overlapping signals in the pools, greens brightening, ambers flaring, a physical argument conducted simultaneously through three channels humans could only partially read. Two Amazon frogs on the fourth tier began a rapid exchange in Threnn-song that was plainly combative, their colors flickering through sequences Reed associated with strong disagreement. A cluster of Siberians in the lower western pool sat motionless, their grey untouched, watching the display with the patient boredom of people who had caused this reaction before and intended to cause it again.

On the far side of the chamber, Marsh leaned toward Hart and said something. Hart was watching the Siberians with the narrow, cataloguing attention she gave to organisms she had not yet classified. Reed knew what she was seeing: the physical differences. The Siberians were built differently. Broader, thicker-skinned, their bodies denser and more compact, adapted for temperatures that would kill most Amazon frogs in hours. Their colors did not shift because their skin was engineered for insulation, not communication. They expressed nothing on their surfaces. This, Reed understood suddenly, was why the Amazon frogs found them so irritating. A frog that did not display was a frog that could not be read, and a frog that could not be read could not be trusted, and a frog that could not be trusted was standing in your pool making accurate observations about your foundational methodology.

Then Talla spoke.

She was in the middle tiers, near a group of younger Amazon frogs who shifted slightly to give her space. The small adjustment had a practiced quality. They knew what was coming. Her dark skin caught almost no light. Her pale spots were visible, which meant she was not controlling them, which meant she did not care who knew she was feeling strongly.

"The Siberians coordinate displacement without the merging protocol," Talla said. Her voice was flat, directed at the room, at nobody in particular. "They have been doing this for longer than the current merging requirement has existed. Their accuracy is documented. Their operators are not hospitalized." She paused. "The consciousness-merging may improve coordination. That is debatable. What is not debatable is that the mandatory participation cannot be justified by the operational need when an alternative exists."

The chamber went quiet. Not the vibrating quiet of sixty frogs humming at different frequencies. Actually quiet. The water stopped carrying competing signals. Reed could hear, for the first time since he had entered, the ambient sound of the settlement's water channels behind the walls.

An Amazon frog aligned with the Siberians against her own settlement. Reed did not need Vale's political expertise to understand what had just happened. Across the chamber, Vale leaned forward, hands unclenching from where they had been folded, his head turning between Talla and Noll and back again with the rapid calculation of someone watching a move he had not anticipated.

The Siberian lead looked at Talla. Her grey skin showed no signal, but her posture adjusted, a fractional reorientation toward the dark frog in the middle tiers that carried, in the rigid Siberian body language, the approximate weight of a standing ovation.

Noll's blue did not waver. "Talla-of-the-Third-Pool's perspective is valued and has been registered in the coordination record."

"It has been registered," Talla said. "It has not been addressed."

From the eastern wall of the chamber, near Krrik and the thinking moss, a Siberian frog Reed had not noticed before turned and looked at him. The frog was smaller than the other Siberians, mottled grey with flecks of dark blue at the joints, and was clearly at this meeting under protest because the meeting was happening where the data was. An engineer. Reed recognized the type because he was the type.

The Siberian engineer hopped from the pool edge to the dry stone beside the thinking moss and said, without introduction: "You are the human who collected the surface data."

"I am. Reed. Dr. Elias Reed. I collected root density, soil chemistry, and canopy behavior data during our primate research before we, before we came down here, and the patterns in the soil chemistry were showing accelerated mineral displacement along the root corridors, which at the time I attributed to seasonal variation, but now."

"Show me."

Reed showed him. He had been carrying the data for days, transcribed onto a dried moss sheet Krrik had given him because human electronics did not function in the settlement's humidity and because Krrik considered electronic data storage "touching, in the way a child's drawing is touching." The Siberian engineer studied the transcription while the political argument continued behind them. Noll was saying something about procedural frameworks. The Siberian lead was saying something about procedural frameworks being decorative. Vesh had begun speaking from the upper pools, his gold flecks bright, his voice carrying the serene, rolling cadence of a frog explaining cosmic unity to frogs who considered cosmic unity a quaint regional preference.

"The merging is not a tool," Vesh said, and Reed heard it over his shoulder while the Siberian engineer traced a root-density gradient on the moss sheet. "The merging is the purpose. The displacement coordination is what it does. The Almost is what it is. To say 'you can coordinate without merging' is like saying 'you can eat without tasting.' Correct. Also missing the point of being alive."

The Siberians' response to Vesh was visible even from across the chamber. Their grey surfaces remained unchanged, but three of them turned fractionally away from him mid-sentence, the Siberian equivalent of walking out of a lecture. One Siberian near the front produced a single, brief color change: a flat wash of blue-grey that came and went in half a second. Reed did not know the Siberian chromatic vocabulary, but it looked like the visual equivalent of someone checking their watch.

"Your surface readings," the Siberian engineer said, pulling Reed's attention back. "The depth markers. These indicate root-level voss activity visible from above the soil."

"Yes. Canopy stress, displaced root balls, luminescence through soil cracks. I documented it during our initial survey. I thought it was geological. It is not geological."

"It is not geological." The engineer's grey skin did something Reed had not seen a Siberian's skin do: it shifted. Briefly. A pulse of darker grey that moved across the engineer's back and vanished. "Our subsurface sensors have not registered surface emergence. In any settlement. In the full archive of monitoring data."

Reed's glasses were already in his hair. He pushed them up without thinking, found hair instead of nose, and left them there. "Wait, wait, wait. You're saying surface-level voss emergence has never been recorded?"

"I am saying it has never occurred."

"In how long?"

"In the monitoring record. Which begins approximately one hundred and fifty million years ago, with gaps."

Reed sat with this. The political argument behind them had moved on. Talla was saying something sharp about operational transparency. The Siberian lead was asking Noll a question that was shaped like a question but functioned as an accusation. Forty frogs were producing overlapping vibrations of agreement, disagreement, and what were almost certainly hunger-related complaints from a small group near the food stores who had been there since before the meeting started and were growing restless.

But here, on the stone ledge beside the thinking moss, the Siberian engineer was studying Reed's surface data with a careful, unhappy focus. Her models had just failed in the most informative possible way.

"Your instruments are primitive," the engineer said.

"Extremely."

"Your methodology is crude."

"By your standards, everything we do is crude. By our standards, we mapped the human genome with the same basic approach. Pattern recognition and patience."

The engineer's dark blue joint-flecks pulsed once. "Your primitive instruments looked where ours did not. We monitored from below. You documented from above. The voss has reached the surface. This has never happened." A pause. "Your data is useful. I will not say it is good. It is useful in the way that a broken window is useful for telling you about the wind."

Reed almost smiled. The grudging acknowledgment carried more weight than praise would have. He had lived in academia long enough to know that the most honest compliment a scientist could receive was someone reluctantly admitting they needed your data.

Krrik materialized at the edge of the thinking moss. Reed had not seen him move. Standard for Krrik. The frog navigated the settlement like a chess piece, arriving at positions without appearing to cross the intervening squares.

"You are looking at the surface emergence data," Krrik said to the Siberian engineer.

"I am looking at data that should not exist."

"The growth rate in the eastern sectors has exceeded my projections by twenty-three percent in the last six days. I have revised the model four times. Each revision has been wrong within hours."

The engineer's grey shifted again. That brief, dark pulse. "Our northern quadrant shows seventeen percent. The Rift Pools report twenty-one. The Canopy Clusters have not reported. They do not report on time. They have never reported on time. This is a separate complaint."

A frog from the crowd, small and olive-green, perched on a ledge above them and interjected: "The Canopy Clusters believe timely reporting is a form of spiritual rigidity. They have held this position for forty million years. We have stopped arguing."

Krrik ignored this. "If I combine Reed's surface data with your subsurface readings and the Rift Pool reports, the growth curve suggests this is the most severe Pruning in the accessible archive. Centuries. Possibly millennia."

The word landed in the space between the three of them and sat there.

Reed looked at the chamber. The political argument was still running. Noll was speaking in careful, measured sentences. The Siberian lead was responding in sentences that were neither careful nor measured. Vesh had said something that prompted a Siberian to observe, loudly enough for the entire western pool to hear, that the Amazon frogs had spent eighty million years croaking about oneness while simultaneously traveling to other stars to confirm that nobody out there wanted to hear about it either.

The laughter from the Siberian contingent was brief, controlled, and devastating.

But beneath the political noise, something was happening. The data was converging. Reed knew it the way he always knew good data, in the pattern that emerged when you stopped trying to make the numbers tell a story and let them tell their own. Three independent monitoring networks, plus his surface observations, plus the thinking moss's own pattern analysis, all pointing at the same conclusion. The growth was accelerating. The growth was anomalous. The growth had reached the surface.

"Can I see your northern quadrant data?" Reed asked the engineer.

The engineer looked at him for two seconds. Measured him. Then hopped to the thinking moss and pressed one forelimb against its surface in a sequence practiced and specific, a password on a familiar keyboard. The moss responded. Not visually. The vibrations beneath him changed, sharpened, and the moss surface shifted, patterns forming in its fibrous texture that meant nothing to Reed but that Krrik and the engineer bent toward simultaneously with the focused hunger of two people who spoke the same language.

Krrik produced a sharp orange flash across his skin. "This is wrong."

"It is not wrong," the engineer said. "It is what we measured."

"I do not mean your data is wrong. I mean the growth is wrong. It should not be doing this. The voss should not be at the surface. The voss has never been at the surface. Something in the radiation pattern is different, or something in the substrate response has changed, or both, and I do not know which and I do not have enough computation capacity to model all three variables simultaneously."

"We brought additional arrays. They are being cultivated in the transit pools. They will be operational in two days."

"Two days is adequate. Two days ago would have been better."

"We were traveling. We do not enjoy the process and do not hurry."

The political argument had reached a temporary lull. Noll was proposing something procedural. Reed could hear his voice, calm and structured, threading a path between the Siberian bluntness and the Amazon frogs' chromatic indignation. And then, below the procedural language, below the political performance, Reed saw it happen.

The room changed color.

Not a ripple. Not a wave of chromatic reaction from one faction to another. A change. Sixty-odd frogs, Amazon and Siberian, their colors moving toward the same shade at the same time. A pale, washed green that Reed had never seen in the settlement, a color that no individual frog seemed to choose but that the room arrived at collectively, a field of grass bending in the same wind. The Siberians, whose grey surfaces rarely moved, showed the faintest flush of the same green at their throats and along their forelimbs. For three seconds the entire chamber was the same color, and the hum that ran through the water and the stone and Reed's teeth resolved into a single low frequency, and sixty frogs were feeling the same thing at the same time.

Fear.

Reed had never seen collective fear as a visible phenomenon. He had seen it in herds, in flocks, in primates freezing before a predator. But this was different. This was sixty individual minds arriving at the same conclusion simultaneously and broadcasting it through their skin, and the conclusion was that the thing they had been managing for two hundred million years was, this time, larger than the management.

The color held for three seconds. Four. Then it broke. Individual patterns returned. The Amazon frogs shifted back toward their conversational greens and ambers. The Siberians' grey reasserted. Voices resumed. The political argument picked up where it had paused. But the room had said something together that no individual had said alone, and everyone in the chamber had heard it, and Reed sat on his wet stone ledge and understood that the data he had been carrying in his notebook, the root density measurements and soil chemistry readings he had collected while watching monkeys, the boring surface observations that nobody on his team had thought were relevant, had just become part of something planetary and old and frightened.

Quill, at the top of the chamber, was watching Vesh and Talla. From across the tiered pools, his attention was fixed not on the political argument or the scientific data but on the two frogs who sat three rows apart and did not look at each other. The tension between them was visible in what they did not do. Talla had spoken against the merging protocol, and Vesh had defended it, and now they occupied the same chamber with the practiced distance of two people whose disagreement was older and more personal than any policy debate.

Reed filed it. He filed everything. It was how his mind worked: data first, interpretation later, the accumulation of observations that would eventually arrange themselves into a pattern he could not yet see.

The chamber was emptying. Frogs climbed from the pools in small groups, Amazon and Siberian mixed, still arguing, still producing overlapping vibrations that filled the corridors with the residual hum of unfinished business. Noll left with the Siberian lead beside him, their conversation private, Noll's blue controlled and his damaged forelimb tucked carefully inward. Vale followed them at a distance that was precisely calibrated to look casual. Hart and Marsh descended the eastern steps together, Marsh writing in her notebook as she walked, Hart's field kit bumping against her hip.

Quill sat where he was. He was watching something Reed could not see. He had been doing that more often.

At the chamber's edge, Krrik and the Siberian engineer were still at the thinking moss. The chamber was nearly empty. The water in the pools rippled faintly with the residual vibrations of a meeting that had not resolved anything and had clarified everything. The two engineers were arguing. Krrik's orange flashed in rapid bursts. The Siberian's grey showed its dark pulses. Their forelimbs moved across the moss surface, pulling data, comparing patterns, adjusting models that were already obsolete by the time they ran.

They were not agreeing. They were doing something better than agreeing. They were working.

Reed pushed his glasses down from his hair onto his nose and walked toward them. The moss hummed beneath his boots. The water channels carried their sound through the stone. The civilizational machinery of survival was engaging, and Reed, with his transcribed surface data and his borrowed moss sheet and his primitive human instruments, was going to stand next to it and be useful.

It was the least, and the most, he could do.

## Chapter Intent
This chapter does three things simultaneously: it expands the story to global scale (the Siberians bring data from other settlements, confirming the Pruning's planetary severity), it dramatizes the deepest ideological fracture in frog civilization (whether consciousness-merging is spiritual purpose or operational tool), and it gives Reed's "boring" surface data its strategic payoff. The packed chamber is the book's biggest ensemble scene, and it must feel chaotic, alive, and politically charged. The two parallel tracks run throughout: the political argument (Siberian bluntness vs. Amazon spirituality, Talla's costly alignment, Vesh's sincere and beautifully received rebuttal) and the scientific sidebar (Reed and the Siberian engineer forcing each other to confront data neither expected). The collective fear display is the chapter's hinge. For three seconds, the comedy drops away and sixty frogs broadcast the same terrified recognition through their skin, and the reader understands that the Pruning is not a political abstraction or a recurring inconvenience but something genuinely frightening to a civilization that has been handling it for two hundred million years. The ending is purposeful urgency: Krrik and the Siberian engineer arguing at the computation moss while the politics empties around them. Reed walks toward them with his transcribed data. The science cannot wait. The page turns because the global scope of the threat is now clear and the reader understands that the machinery of survival is engaging at the highest level, with all its internal contradictions intact.
