# Chapter 1: Threshold

## Chapter Intent

Establish Ada as a specific, embodied person whose control is itself a wound; render the Pacific Northwest old-growth forest as a living, sensory presence; dramatize the psychedelic onset as a perceptual threshold crossing; and close with the first crack in Ada's armor when the trail vanishes behind her. Plant the frog.

## Draft

A bird called once from somewhere high in the Douglas fir canopy: a single sustained note, hollow and metallic, like a finger drawn along the rim of a wet glass. It hung in the dusk air and thinned and was gone. The forest filled the space it left with everything else: the tick of condensation sliding from fern tips, the chitter of bats stitching the air above a canopy gap, the soft percussion of something small and unseen moving through salal.

Ada locked the car. The click of the mechanism was sharp and foreign against the layered quiet of the trailhead. She shouldered her pack: water bottle, lighter, rain jacket, car keys. She walked toward the trail without looking back at the parking lot. The lot was empty. Late autumn, a Tuesday, this far from any town. She had not expected otherwise.

Her phone was on the passenger seat. Not forgotten. Placed. Face down, powered off, centered on the leather with the deliberateness of someone setting a table for one. She had decided this days ago. No signal out here anyway, but that was not the point. The point was the leash, and tonight she was cutting it. Or that was how she had framed it to herself on the drive up, hands at ten and two, jaw set against the small voice suggesting she was making a mistake.

The maintained trail was compacted earth and scattered gravel, signposted with distances she did not read. Douglas fir trunks rose on either side in thick-barked columns that vanished upward into a canopy she could no longer see against the dimming sky. Two hundred and fifty feet, maybe more. She knew their Latin name, could recite the taxonomy from memory. She had prepared for this the way she prepared for everything: thoroughly, clinically, as if knowledge were a kind of armor and enough of it could make the unknown safe.

At the fork she took the left path, the narrower one, where boot prints in the soft earth were older and fewer. Ferns closed in on both sides, fronds nearly as tall as her waist, each one a precise green blade furred with spore cases on its underside. Moss covered every horizontal surface: the stones, the fallen wood, the low crooks of bigleaf maple branches that sagged and bent under the weight of it. The moss was the color of old jade in the failing light. Dense, damp, unhurried. It covered things like time covers things. Without effort. Without opinion.

The fallen log was where she remembered it, more or less. A Douglas fir trunk three feet in diameter, fallen and half-consumed by its own becoming. Hemlock seedlings grew from its spine in a row of dark green spires. Bracket fungi, horse-hoof shaped and hard as wood, climbed the near flank in ascending tiers. Moss sealed every crack and wound. The log had been dying and living in equal measure for decades, and it did both without complaint.

Ada sat down. The bark was damp beneath her palms, spongy with rot. She swung the backpack to the ground between her boots and unzipped it. The sound of the zipper was loud in the layered quiet. She reached inside.

The mushrooms were in a small paper bag, folded twice with precise creases. She opened it. A handful of dried psilocybin mushrooms: stems and caps, the color of burnt caramel. She picked one up between thumb and forefinger. The cap was wavy, curled at its edges, tinged with a faint blue where the active compound had met the air. A chemical fact she had read about and was now confirming. She noted the confirmation. It steadied her.

She ate them slowly. The texture was between cork and raw pasta, dry and fibrous. The taste was bitter, earthy, with a faint metallic undertone that lingered at the back of her tongue. She chewed with deliberate thoroughness, washing each mouthful down with water. The bottle was half full when she capped it and set it back in the pack. She folded the paper bag and set it on the moss beside the log's base.

Through the gap in the canopy overhead, the first stars had appeared, faint, in a strip of blue so deep it was almost violet. The moon was up already, nearly full, she had checked, but the canopy hid it.

Twenty to forty minutes for onset. She could sit with twenty to forty minutes.

She pressed her lips together and scanned the clearing. The inventory came without effort: the log beneath her, the ring of ferns, the wall of trunks at the clearing's edge, the darkness gathering between them. Insects had begun their night chorus: a layered pulse she could not parse into species. The click-buzz of beetles, a thin whine rising and falling, and beneath both something lower and continuous she felt in her teeth. Moisture dripped from a fern at irregular intervals, each drop landing on a leaf below with a soft percussive tap. The air smelled of wet stone and damp bark, underlaid with the sweet fungal scent of wood returning to earth. She catalogued all of it. She was having the experience correctly.

Then the toad.

It was sitting on the log, less than two feet from her right hand. It had not made a sound. It was simply there, as if it had been there longer than she had, as if she had sat down beside it without looking.

It was large. The size of her fist, maybe larger. A Western toad. She recognized the species from her research. Its skin was rough with warts, mottled olive-brown and gold, with a pale cream stripe running the length of its back. It sat with its body pressed flat to the bark, heavy and low, with the settled weight of a stone that has occupied its position since before anyone thought to measure time. It did not move. It did not blink. Its eyes were gold, bisected by horizontal pupils, and they were watching her with an expression she had no name for. Something older than curiosity or wariness.

A flicker passed through her chest, below alarm, below recognition, something that lived below language. The toad's stillness was so complete it had its own gravity. She stared at it. It regarded her. The forest's evening sounds continued around them both, indifferent.

Ada looked up at the canopy, at the strip of sky overhead, where the first stars had appeared, faint, precise, each one a cold point in a field of deepening indigo. When she looked back, the log beside her hand was bare. The bark was slightly depressed where the toad had sat, dark with moisture. She ran her fingers across the spot. Damp. Cool. Nothing to confirm what she had seen except the faint impression of weight in soft wood.

She filed it away. Western toads were common in Pacific Northwest old growth. Large specimens were unremarkable. There was nothing unusual about a toad on a log at dusk. She filed the observation and the filing did not work. The gold eyes, patient and unhurried, settled somewhere beneath her inventory and stayed.

The stars multiplied. The strip of sky between the canopy became a dense scatter of light, and then the canopy itself dissolved into darkness and the sky was gone. The temperature dropped. Ada pulled her jacket closer around her ribs and pressed her back against the log.

She breathed. She waited. She counted her breaths and then stopped counting because counting was another form of managing and she had come here to stop managing, hadn't she? She had. That was the plan. She breathed without counting and the breaths came anyway, which should not have been a revelation but briefly, strangely, was.

It began with the moss.

The moss on the log beside her hand grew more vivid. Not brighter, the light was failing, but more present, the resolution on a single patch of the world refined past what her eyes were built for. Individual strands of moss stood distinct, each one beaded with moisture, green shading to gold shading to a deep copper she had not seen before. The texture deepened until the patch of moss was no longer a surface but a landscape: ridges and valleys and miniature forests within the forest, each droplet of water a small clear world reflecting the dark above.

The moss was breathing. Not actually breathing. The surface rose and fell in slow, shallow waves, expanding and contracting the way a sleeping animal's ribs move. She watched it. She noted it. This is onset, said the voice in her head. Visual cortex becomes hyperactive. Surfaces appear to breathe. This is normal. This is expected.

The bark of the log shifted next. The furrows deepened, the ridges sharpened, the pattern of decay became a topography she could have mapped with her fingers: beetle tracks carved in winding lines where the bark had peeled away, the grain of the wood flowing in currents that had taken a century to form. A shelf of bracket fungus she had glanced at earlier pulsed with a slow interior rhythm, a heartbeat inside the woody flesh keeping time.

Then the ferns. They swayed, though no wind stirred. The fronds moved with the lazy precision of kelp in a current, tips curling and uncurling in sequences that repeated but never exactly. The canopy above, what she could see of it through the clearing's gap, had receded. Three hundred feet. Four hundred. The Douglas fir trunks rose into darkness like columns in a cathedral designed by an intelligence that planned in centuries and did not rush.

Colors saturated. The green of the moss became a green she had no word for. A green beyond bright or neon: true. Every green she had seen before had been a diluted print, and this was the original.

Along a fallen log at the clearing's edge, a faint light pulsed.

She stood. The motion came before the decision, her body moving toward the light while her mind was still annotating. Jack-o'-lantern mushrooms clustered along the log's underside, caps the size of small hands, gills catching their own bioluminescence and casting it back. The glow was faint, the color of an ember seen through green glass. In daylight she would have passed without notice. Here, in the full dark, it was the only light the forest offered.

She crouched beside the log. The mushrooms smelled sweet, almost fruity, underlaid with the deep fungal note of rotting wood. The light was chemical, an enzyme reaction in the gill tissue. She knew this, had read the papers, and the knowledge was supposed to make it ordinary. It did not. The glow pulsed with a rhythm she could nearly match to the blood in her wrists.

On her feet again. The forest beyond the clearing was a wall of shapes, fern fronds, trunks, the broken silhouettes of dead branches, and the margins of every shape were softening. Not blurring. Loosening. The outlines of things no longer fully committed to where they had been drawn.

Her skin prickled at the back of her neck and along her forearms, a fine electric awareness that was not unpleasant. The insect chorus had deepened, the individual species separating into distinct layers: beetles clicking on one layer, the thin whine threading above, and beneath it all, a low drone she felt in her jaw more than she heard. A bat flickered overhead, its echolocation a rapid ticking at the edge of hearing. The air tasted different. Mineral and clean, with a sweetness she associated with rain that had not yet arrived.

This is normal, said the voice in her head, but the voice was speaking from a room she was walking out of, and the door between them was swinging shut.

She could not sit still.

The impulse arrived complete: she needed to walk. Sitting on the log felt passive, and passive felt like waiting, and waiting felt like yielding the only thing she still held: the ability to move, to choose, to go somewhere on purpose. She was not ready to yield. She shouldered her backpack and tightened the straps, a small, practiced gesture, the body executing its checklist while the mind was already walking. She stepped past the ferns at the clearing's edge and into the forest.

The ferns brushed her jeans as she passed through them, their fronds cool and damp against the denim. The ground beneath her boots was soft, a thick carpet of decomposing needles and moss that swallowed each footfall. She walked with purpose, though she could not have named what she was walking toward. Deeper. The forest was deeper ahead and she moved into it, and the moving felt necessary. Halfway between chosen and automatic.

The air changed as she left the clearing. Denser, wetter, carrying the sweet-rot scent of fungal decay mixed with something sharper: cedar resin, cold and sharp. Hemlock boughs brushed her shoulders where the understory closed in, their needles leaving faint damp traces on her jacket sleeves. She told herself she was exploring. Just a walk. Just the forest at night, the onset settling in, the experience unfolding as she had planned. The voice delivering these assurances had grown thin, though. It was narrating from a distance, and the distance was widening.

A western red cedar loomed to her left, its trunk wide enough that three of her could not have circled it with arms outstretched. The bark was fibrous, shredding in long vertical strips. She trailed her fingers across it as she passed, and the texture under her skin was so detailed, so ridged and furrowed, that her hand was reading something written there in a language she almost recognized.

The canopy had closed. What moonlight existed arrived in narrow shafts that shifted as the branches shifted, laying silver rectangles on the ground that vanished before she could step into them. She navigated instead by bioluminescence, faint clusters of glowing fungi on downed wood, scattered like a trail of low green embers. Each cluster gave just enough light to find the next footfall. Not the one after that.

Time had gone soft at its edges. She did not trust her sense of duration, and the distrust carried no alarm. Just a loosening, a slippage she could not grip. The moss she stepped on left no footprints. The ferns she parted swung closed behind her. She passed through the forest and the forest sealed itself in her wake: present at the point of contact, gone from every point before.

Then she stopped.

Something was wrong. Not wrong like danger; no sound, no movement, no prickling instinct at the base of her skull. Wrong like geometry. A line that should be straight and was not. Her jaw tightened. She had been walking more or less straight, following a gentle downward slope between the trunks. The clearing with the fallen log should have been behind her, uphill, reachable by turning and retracing.

She turned.

The forest behind her was flat. The slope she had walked down was gone. The ground stretched level between trunks she did not recognize, bigleaf maple draped in curtains of moss, not the Douglas fir she had passed through. The ferns were different: taller, denser, arrayed in formations she was certain she had not parted. A fallen log lay across the ground twenty feet back, but it was the wrong one. Smaller, covered in red huckleberry rather than hemlock seedlings, oriented the wrong direction.

She walked toward it. Her hand found the nearest trunk. She pressed her palm flat against the bark, reading the texture. Smooth, peeling in papery strips. Alder, not Douglas fir. She had not walked past a single alder. Her fingers curled against the bark and pulled away.

The forest around her did not match anything she had walked through. She turned in a slow circle, scanning for the canopy gap where the stars had been visible from the clearing. The canopy was sealed. Every direction was dark trunks, fern walls, the faint scattered glow of fungi she did not remember passing. She looked for the jack-o'-lantern mushrooms she had been following, the trail of low green embers, and found none. Different fungi glowed here, smaller and paler, a blue-green she had not seen before.

Her hands were fists. She had not decided to make them fists.

Her mind reached for explanations and found them. Spatial disorientation was expected; psilocybin disrupts the brain's internal compass, its sense of grid and direction, makes distance and bearing unreliable. She had read this. Had prepared for this. She had walked farther than she realized, or turned without noticing, or the slope had been gentler than she perceived. These were reasonable explanations. She held them like a railing on a staircase that had begun to sway.

But the trees were wrong. The ground was wrong. The undergrowth pressed closer on all sides, as if the space between trunks had quietly narrowed while her back was turned. She spun again. Every direction looked the same, and every direction looked like the first time she had seen it.

The light had not changed; the scattered bioluminescence still pulsed in its faint, unhurried way. The sounds were the same: the insect chorus continued, undisturbed, and somewhere overhead a barred owl released its low rhythmic call. Temperature, smell, every single element she could isolate and name held steady.

What had changed was the forest itself. The arrangement of it. The fact of it. The woods behind her had quietly, without haste or hostility, rearranged themselves while she walked, and now stood in their new positions with the settled certainty of things that had always been exactly where they were.

The voice in her head offered one more explanation: she had moved, not the forest, and she was disoriented and needed to stop and breathe and get her bearings. She reached for it, and the explanation dissolved in her hands like wet paper.

Ada stood in the dark, surrounded by trees she had not passed, on ground she had not walked, in a forest that had closed behind her the way water closes over a dropped stone.

The ferns did not move. The insects kept their layered song. A single drop of condensation fell from a branch above and landed on the back of her neck, cold, precise, and traced a line down her spine beneath her shirt.

She did not move.

The forest breathed around her, slow and vast and patient, and waited for what she would do next.
