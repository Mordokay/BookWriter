# Chapter 12: Wet In, Wet Out

Lenn was dead, and Oona was carrying food.

She arrived at the biologists' quarters with a shallow stone dish balanced on one forearm and her orange throat patch at its dimmest, which Quill had never seen before. She did not knock. The frogs did not knock. Doors, where they existed, were open or absent, and the concept of a closed room struck most Threnn as architecturally hostile.

"Lenn has dissolved," she said. "The mourning pool is being prepared. You should come."

Marsh looked up from her notebook. "Who was Lenn?"

"Elderly. Displacement operator when he was young, before the joints went. Later he worked the fermentation cellars. He burned the first batch of everything he ever made and blamed the humidity. He was seventy-one seasons." Oona set the dish on the stone table. The food was something dense and dark, shaped into small mounds. "These are for after. You will want them. The ritual is short but the walk back is long."

"How did he die?" Reed asked. He was sitting cross-legged on the floor, his notebook open, his glasses pushed up.

"Old age. His breathing slowed over two days and then it stopped. The practitioners offered to extend it. He said the water temperature in the treatment pools was wrong and he would rather dissolve than continue complaining about it."

She said this with a flatness that was not cold. It was the flatness of someone reporting a fact that was sad and finished and did not require decoration.

"When is the mourning?" Quill asked.

"Now. Within the hour. The pool is already filling."

Vale, leaning against the root-frame of the doorway, looked at Hart. Hart looked at the food Oona had brought. Neither of them moved.

"You don't have to come," Oona said. "But you should. Lenn would not have cared either way. He did not care about most things. But the mourning is for the living, and you are living here now, so."

She left the sentence there. The throat patch flickered once, a faint pulse of orange, and went dim again.

They went.

The mourning pool was set apart from the communal bathing areas, deeper in the settlement, down a corridor where the bioluminescent light thinned to a pale wash and the stone walls were older, smoother, worn by water and time into surfaces that looked almost polished. The air changed. Warmer. Denser. A faint smell that Quill could not immediately place, something sharp and green, like crushed stems left in standing water.

Frogs were already gathering when they arrived. Not in a procession. Just arriving, because this was a place they had been before and would come to again. Some were already in the pool, floating at the surface with their eyes half-closed. Others sat on the stone rim, their hind legs trailing in the water. A group near the far edge was talking in low Threnn-song, colors shifting in slow, muted patterns. Two young frogs hovered at the entrance, unsure of their positioning, nudging each other toward the water with the awkwardness of beings attending something solemn for the first time.

Quill counted. Forty frogs, then more arriving. Forty-five. Close to fifty by the time the water stilled.

The five biologists spread out along the pool's edge. Marsh sat near Oona. Reed found a spot where he could see the water clearly. Vale positioned himself where he could see the room, who stood where, who arrived in what order. Hart stood with her arms crossed, examining the pool's surface with the focused restraint of someone who had decided, for once, not to test the water chemistry. She had stopped herself. Quill saw it happen: the instinct, the pause, the hands dropping to her sides.

Quill sat at the pool's rim, his feet on dry stone, his notebook in his lap. He did not open it.

Noll was there, near the center of the gathering, his muted green-grey coloring steady and composed. He stood on a stone ledge that gave him a slight elevation, and the effect was of someone presiding without admitting to presiding. He did not speak. His presence was the statement. One damaged forelimb held inward, as always, the old injury catching the light.

Talla was there, against the far wall, her dark skin nearly invisible in the dim corridor behind her. Pale spots hidden. She had come. Oona had mentioned days ago that Talla attended every mourning without exception. "Even I admit the dead deserve a proper dissolving," Talla had said. "It is the one thing we do that I do not have a complaint about. Give me time."

Krrik stood at the pool's edge nearest the exit, his mottled brown-green dusted with spore residue, his posture rigid in the way of someone who wanted to be elsewhere but could not justify leaving. He had known Lenn. Decades ago, when Lenn ran displacement operations and Krrik was calibrating the systems Lenn operated. They had not been close. They had been colleagues: proximity over time, shared work without shared sentiment. Krrik was there because it would have been noticed if he were not.

Pem was near the water. The archivist's faded, translucent skin caught the bioluminescence and held it like fog holds light, diffuse and sourceless. Pem's eyes were bright and steady. Hands ready. This was work. Sacred, repeated, necessary work, performed for the ten-thousandth time with the same precision as the first.

An older frog, compact and rust-colored, was telling a story to nobody in particular from a ledge near the pool's center.

"Lenn fermented a batch of grubs the year I moved to the Ninth Warren," the older frog said. "He had been working on the recipe for three seasons. It was, he insisted, going to be the finest preparation the eastern cellars had ever produced. He burned it. Completely. The smell lasted six days. He blamed the stone temperature, the ambient moisture, the quality of the grubs, and at one point the Siberians, who had not been involved. He then made a second batch, which was worse."

A few frogs nearby made soft sounds. Not laughter, not quite. Recognition.

"His third batch was acceptable. He was so surprised that he accused the grubs of cooperating out of spite."

A nursery caretaker arrived, slipping into the pool quietly, her small body barely disturbing the surface. She had been waiting for a colleague to take over the tadpoles. She was late and did not apologize. Nobody expected her to. The tadpoles came first. Everyone understood this.

Vesh moved to the pool's center.

He was smaller than most of the frogs around him, five centimeters of compact emerald green, the gold flecks in his skin dimmed to something muted and warm. Not the vivid brightness Quill had seen at the pools the night before. This was a different register. Practiced. Quill thought: he has done this before. Many times. He will do it again. And then someone will do it for him.

Vesh settled into the water until it reached his throat. The pool quieted. Not silence, not the sharp quiet of the merging-deaths revelation the night before. A gentler settling, like a room drawing a breath.

"Wet in, wet out," Vesh said.

His voice carried across the water. Not loudly. The pool's acoustics were designed for this, the warm stone and the still surface carrying vibration like a bell carries its ring.

"Lenn came from the water. He has returned to the water. The puddle has formed. The puddle has dissolved. The water does not mourn the shape."

He paused. Around the pool, frogs were still. The two young frogs near the entrance had stopped nudging each other. One had settled into the shallows with careful, deliberate movements.

"The Croaking was loud in Lenn," Vesh said. "It is quiet now. It will be loud again in others. The note changes. The singing does not stop."

A low harmonic passed through the water. Not from Vesh. From the gathered frogs. A communal vibration, sub-vocal, felt more than heard. Quill felt it in his hands where they rested on the stone rim, a faint tremor that traveled through the rock and into his palms and wrists. Around the pool, the frogs' bodies were transmitting something his ears could not fully capture but his bones registered. A chord. Sustained. Not musical in any way he could describe, but harmonically present, like standing near a cathedral organ, something that made your sternum hum.

Lenn's body was brought to the water.

Two frogs carried it, carefully, the small form cradled between them. Lenn was perhaps six centimeters. His colors had faded to a uniform grey, the vibrancy gone, the signaling skin quiet for the first time in seventy-one seasons. He looked, Quill thought, like what he was: a small dead animal. And then he thought: no. He looked like a person who had stopped. The difference between those two thoughts was the distance his mind had traveled in the last two weeks, and he did not know what to do with that.

They placed Lenn in the water. The body floated, then settled, half-submerged, in the center of the pool where Vesh waited.

"May you dissolve gently," Vesh said.

Then nothing happened. And then something did.

Quill did not see it start. He felt it. A change in the water's smell, the sharp green scent intensifying, becoming more specific, more present. The frogs in the pool shifted. Not in position. In attention. Their skin, their colors, their breathing oriented toward the center where Lenn's body rested.

The chemical sharing. He had read about it in Marsh's notes, heard it referenced in Oona's stories, understood it intellectually as a biological process: the dead frog's body releasing its final chemical signature into the warm water, the living frogs absorbing it through their permeable skin. Chemistry. Not metaphor.

But understanding it intellectually and sitting at the edge of a pool while it happened were not the same thing.

The frogs nearest Lenn's body went still first. Their colors shifted, briefly, through something Quill could not read. Then the stillness spread outward, ring by ring, as the chemical signal moved through the warm water and reached each body in turn. The frogs absorbed it as they absorbed everything, through the skin, through the permeable boundary between self and environment that was not a boundary at all but a door that was always open.

Quill, at the rim, his feet on dry stone, his hands on wet rock, caught a trace.

It was faint. He was not in the water. His skin was not theirs. But the stone was wet, and his palms were flat against it, and something traveled through the moisture and reached him.

A feeling. Mild, specific, and slightly annoyed.

The water was too warm.

That was it. That was Lenn's final broadcast. His last chemical signature, the final biological output of a seventy-one-season life that had included displacement operations and burned fermentation batches and complaints about humidity and stone temperature and the Siberians: irritation about the temperature of the pool.

Several frogs near the center made small sounds. Not quite laughter. Closer to recognition. The sound people make when something is exactly what they expected and still catches them off guard.

Oona, floating near Marsh, whispered something. Quill heard it because the pool was so quiet that whispers carried.

"He always complained about the water temperature. Even at the end."

Marsh made a sound that was half a laugh and half something else. She did not finish it.

And then the colors changed.

Quill saw it happen. The gathered frogs, forty-seven or forty-eight of them, spread across the pool and around its rim, their bodies still processing the chemical trace of a dead frog's last irritation, synchronized. Their colors shifted. Not deliberately. Not as part of the ritual, not as a breathing circle or a merging exercise. Involuntarily. The way a crowd flinches at the same sound or turns toward the same light. Fifty frogs, for perhaps three seconds, turned the same shade of blue-green. A deep, still, luminous blue-green that was not any individual frog's natural coloring but something produced collectively, a frequency none of them owned alone.

The Almost. Appearing uninvited. In grief.

Quill recognized it. He had heard Vesh describe it the night before, the chord of separate selves almost resolving, two hundred million years of reaching. He had heard it as philosophy. Now he was seeing it as something that happened to bodies, involuntarily, in a pool where a dead frog's last complaint about water temperature was still dissolving through the living.

Three seconds. Maybe four. Then it passed. The individual colors returned. The blue-green faded into forty-eight separate patterns of emerald and olive and brown and rust and the dark near-black of Talla against the wall and the faded translucence of Pem near the water's edge.

Reed, across the pool, was leaning forward. His glasses had fogged. He was not writing.

Vale was watching the frogs' faces. The social choreography. Who had expected this. Who had not.

Hart stood with her arms at her sides and her mouth closed.

Marsh had put her hand flat on the stone rim the way Quill had, and he wondered if she had caught the trace too, if her palms had picked up the same mild irritation through the wet stone, and what she had made of it.

Vesh waited until the colors settled. Then he spoke to the young frog who had waded into the shallows at the start of the ritual, the one who had been nudging his companion at the entrance.

"You felt it," Vesh said. Not a question.

The young frog's skin was cycling through confused greens and ambers. "I felt. Something. About the water being wrong."

"That was Lenn. His last feeling. It is in you now. A drop." Vesh's gold flecks were steady, dim, warm. "You carry a drop of everyone who dissolves near you. This is why old frogs are heavy with the dissolved."

The young frog's colors shifted through something that was not quite wonder and not quite nausea and settled on a pale, shaken green.

Pem moved forward.

The archivist entered the water with the slow economy of a body that rationed its movements, each one chosen. Pem's translucent skin flickered, briefly, with colors that were not Pem's own. Faint echoes. Lenn's grey, passing through the keeper's faded membrane and registering there like light through tissue paper. Pem received the biological imprint the way an archivist receives a document: with steady hands and an attention that did not require emotion to be present but did not exclude it either. The record of who Lenn was, compressed into chemistry, into the last traces the water held, absorbed through skin that had absorbed ten thousand such traces before.

Pem said nothing. Pem's bright eyes were open and focused on the water. When it was done, the archivist withdrew, slowly, carrying what had been deposited.

The water was cycled. Fresh water entered through channels Quill had not noticed, and the mourning pool's contents flowed outward, carrying the diluted remnants of Lenn's final broadcast into the settlement's water system, into the channels and pools and walls where it would disperse and thin and eventually be indistinguishable from everything else. The water that had held a life's last feeling would become the water someone bathed in tomorrow, or drank, or soaked tadpoles in. This was not symbolic. It was plumbing. It was also, Quill thought, the Wet Way in four pipes and a drainage channel.

The community began to leave.

Not slowly. Not with the heavy, reluctant movements of human mourners filing out of a church or standing too long at a graveside because leaving felt like an admission that life continued and continuing felt like betrayal. The frogs left the way they had arrived: practically, without ceremony, because there were things to do.

A group near the far edge climbed out of the pool and headed toward the Pruning staging grounds, their conversation shifting to displacement drill schedules before they reached the corridor. A nursery worker left to relieve the colleague who had taken over her tadpoles. Two frogs from the fermentation cellars discussed whether Lenn's last unfinished batch should be completed as a tribute or discarded as a health risk. They appeared to be genuinely divided on this question.

Oona climbed out of the pool and sat on the rim near Marsh, water dripping from her small, round body. Her throat patch was still dim.

"Is that all?" Marsh asked. She said it quietly, not as criticism, not as judgment. As a question she needed answered.

Oona looked at her. "What else would there be? He was here. Now he is in the water and the archive. Holding the shape of a dissolved puddle is unkind to the puddle and to yourself."

Marsh closed her notebook. She had opened it at some point during the ritual. Quill had seen her writing. He had also seen her stop.

Reed was talking to one of the frogs who had carried Lenn's body, asking quiet questions about the chemistry of the release, how the signal propagated through warm water, what determined which emotional state was broadcast. The frog answered patiently. Reed was processing. This was how Reed processed: by asking how the mechanism worked, because the mechanism was easier to hold than the thing the mechanism carried.

Vale had moved to the exit corridor. He stood near Talla, who was leaning against the wall with her forelimbs folded, braced, ready to argue even when there was nothing to argue about.

Hart said something to Talla. The first and only thing Hart had said since arriving at the pool.

"That was the most efficient grief I have ever seen."

Talla did not move. Her pale spots flickered once.

"Efficient grief is what you get when you have thirty years and cannot afford to spend three of them crying."

Hart looked at her. Talla looked at the pool where two maintenance frogs were already checking the water channels.

"Do you grieve him?" Hart asked.

"I did not know him well. I knew his fermented grubs. They were bad." Talla paused. "I grieve the system that makes this necessary. The speed. The moving on. Some of us would like to sit with the dead a little longer, but there is a Pruning to prepare for and displacement drills to run and a civilization that does not stop because one old frog complained about water temperature for the last time."

Her spots flared. She folded them back. She left.

Quill sat at the pool's edge after the others had gone.

Not all the others. A few frogs remained, drifting in the cycled water, not mourning exactly, just present. The older rust-colored frog who had told the fermentation story was still on his ledge, his eyes half-closed. One of the young frogs from the entrance was sitting very still in the shallows, absorbing what he had absorbed, his colors settling slowly into something stable.

The pool was quieter now. The bioluminescence on the walls pulsed in its slow, ambient rhythm. The sharp green smell had faded. The water smelled like water.

Quill had witnessed a civilization's entire relationship with death compressed into an hour.

It was not cold. He kept coming back to this. The efficiency looked like coldness from the outside, from the place where human grief stretched across months and years and sometimes never ended, where the shape of the dead was held and held and held because letting go felt like a second death. But the frogs did not hold the shape. They let the shape dissolve because that was what shapes did, because the water that had been Lenn was still water, because the irritation about pool temperature was now distributed across forty-eight living bodies who would carry that drop of him in their chemistry until they dissolved in turn.

He thought about the Puddle. About Vesh's voice saying "the water does not mourn the shape." He thought about the Almost, the involuntary synchronization, fifty frogs turning the same color for three seconds because even in grief the collective pull was there, the chord reaching for resolution. He thought about Lenn's last feeling passing through wet stone and into his palms, a dead frog's annoyance about temperature registering in the hands of a human biologist from Cork who studied animal consciousness and could not, at this moment, locate the boundary between studying a thing and being inside it.

He did not know if this was wisdom or the most sophisticated way of managing loss that any species had ever developed, a philosophy of grief designed around the biological fact that the grievers would themselves be dead within a decade or two.

He suspected the Wet Way would say those were the same thing. That there was no difference between a religion that helped you live and a truth that happened to be useful. Wet in, wet out. The puddle forms. The puddle dissolves. Holding the shape is unkind.

He sat with the question. He did not answer it. The water moved through the channels, carrying whatever remained of Lenn out into the settlement, into the ancient, damp, glowing infrastructure of a civilization that had been dissolving its dead and resuming its arguments for longer than his species had been walking upright.

Somewhere in the corridors behind him, frogs were returning to work. Somewhere, Oona was distributing food. Somewhere, Krrik was in his laboratory, having attended because it would have been noticed and left because the voss would not wait.

Quill sat at the edge of the pool with his notebook closed and his hands still wet and no idea what to do with what had passed through them.

## Chapter Intent
This chapter dramatizes the Wet Way's mourning ritual from inside, making the religion real and portable by showing every major concept in its native context: the Puddle as framework for understanding the dead, "wet in, wet out" as lifecycle acknowledgment, the Croaking invoked over Lenn's body, the Almost appearing involuntarily when fifty grieving frogs synchronize colors for three seconds without trying. The chapter's tonal challenge is its reason for existing: Lenn's last chemical broadcast is irritation about the pool temperature, and this fact is simultaneously the chapter's funniest moment and its most devastating, a life compressed to one final complaint that is exactly right because it is exactly him. The comedy does not undercut the grief. The grief does not smother the comedy. They are the same substance viewed from different angles, which is the Wet Way's central teaching enacted rather than explained. The community's efficient return to daily life shocks the biologists but follows logically from the frogs' biology and philosophy: thirty-year lifespans cannot afford prolonged mourning, and the Wet Way has built a spirituality around that constraint rather than against it. Hart and Talla's exchange delivers the sharpest line. Oona's "holding the shape of a dissolved puddle is unkind" delivers the warmest one. Quill ends carrying a question he cannot answer, his observer distance thinner than ever, the religion no longer a philosophy he heard at the pools but a practice that passed through his hands.
