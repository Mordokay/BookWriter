# Chapter 9: Show Me the Mechanism

The lab smelled like a compost heap that had been struck by lightning.

Reed stood in the doorway trying to locate the source of the ozone and failing, because the source was everywhere. A faint hum in his sinuses and the fillings of his back teeth, the kind of charge he would have attributed to faulty wiring if anything in this room had wires. Nothing did. The closest thing to a wire was a thread of pale fungus connecting two moss beds across a gap of three meters, and the moss beds were, according to Krrik, thinking.

"Not thinking the way you mean thinking," Krrik said without looking up. He was crouched over a shallow stone basin at the center of the room, adjusting something inside it with both forelimbs. His mottled brown-green skin was dusted with spore residue, the same color as the residue on every surface in the lab, which gave the impression that Krrik and his workspace had been growing into each other for years. His shorter hind leg was braced at an angle against the basin's edge, the asymmetric stance so habitual that it looked more stable than standing straight. "Thinking the way thinking works. Chemical gradients. Electrical propagation through living tissue. Pattern recognition at molecular density. Not thoughts. Processing."

"Right, right, but the output. The output is functionally equivalent to."

"The output is not equivalent to anything you have. Do not compare it to your computers. Your computers are arithmetic performed on sand. This is." Krrik paused. A sharp orange flicker crossed his skin. "There is no analogy in your framework. It is what it is."

Reed pushed his glasses up. The lab stretched in three directions, built into and grown out of the root system, with no clear walls, only gradual transitions from workspace to corridor to what appeared to be another experiment. Half-finished devices sat on stone ledges alongside what looked like lunch. Near the far wall, four moss beds lay in tiered stone containers, each bed a different shade of green, each one faintly luminous, and each one apparently containing more processing power than every computer on the planet combined, being kept alive by a drip-feed irrigation system made of hollowed-out roots.

The drip system was leaking. A bead of nutrient solution fell from a cracked root joint and splashed onto the moss bed. The moss rippled. Not from the impact. The ripple moved outward in a pattern too regular to be hydraulic, too fast to be growth, and too purposeful to be random. The color shifted from pale green to something warmer for about two seconds, then settled back.

"Wait, wait, wait." Reed stepped closer. "Did it just reconfigure its own architecture based on that chemical input? Because that's not a computer, that's a, that's a living system that rewrites itself in response to."

"It is doing what it does. Please do not touch it."

Reed pulled his hand back.

"I was not going to touch it."

"You were reaching. This array is calibrated for voss growth modeling. If you introduce your skin oils it will spend three days processing the chemical signature of primate sweat instead of doing something useful."

A young frog appeared in the corridor opening. Small, bright green, carrying a flat stone tablet covered in markings Reed could not read. Her colors were cycling through nervous greys.

"Dr. Krrik. The modulation report from the western sector."

Krrik did not look up. "Summary."

"Growth rates within projected parameters for subsections one through six. Seven through twelve show fourteen percent above median, with structural anomalies in the deep root layer that."

"Incompetent."

The grey in her colors deepened.

"The anomalies are genuine," she said.

"The anomalies are genuine and the analysis is incompetent. They are measuring surface density when the growth is propagating through the mineral layer. I told Henn this four days ago. Did Henn adjust the sensor depth?"

"I do not know."

"Then find out."

The young frog left. Her colors had not recovered.

Krrik hopped from the basin to a stone ledge beside the largest moss bed. The hop was lopsided, his shorter leg producing a distinctive hitch that he navigated with the automatic ease of someone who had stopped noticing it decades ago. He pressed one forelimb flat against the moss surface.

The bed responded. Colors shifted through the green in a slow wave, and something moved beneath the surface. Tiny threads of light, branching and reconnecting, tracing paths that looked almost like neurons firing in a brain scan, except the brain was a meter-wide patch of living moss in a stone container in a room that smelled like ozone and compost.

"It's modeling the voss growth."

"Obviously."

"In real time."

"In something more useful than real time. It is modeling probability spaces. Fourteen hundred variables. Atmospheric chemistry, geothermal flux, cosmic radiation intensity, root system connectivity, water table depth, spore count, mineral composition. Your species models weather. This models the behavior of a living planetary substrate that has been growing for two hundred million years." Krrik removed his forelimb. "And the models are failing."

He said this the way Reed might say that his coffee was cold. A fact. An inconvenience. But underneath the flatness, his skin was cycling through browns faster than Reed had seen before, an unconscious churn of processing that his body broadcast even when his voice did not.

"Why are they failing?"

"Because the growth is not following established patterns. The substrate is behaving as though the radiation peak is three times the projected intensity, but our astronomical monitoring confirms the peak is within historical range. Something else is accelerating the growth. I do not know what. The models do not know what. This is not a comfortable scientific position."

A thick-bodied frog appeared at the main corridor entrance. Dull yellow, officious coloring held rigid. A middle manager in any species.

"Dr. Krrik. The coordination committee is meeting in forty minutes. Your attendance is requested for the displacement scheduling review."

"No."

"The committee requires input on displacement capacity for the eastern sector Pruning operations."

"The committee can read the capacity report I submitted two days ago. The numbers have not changed. Tell the committee I am working."

The logistics frog absorbed this. She had delivered this message before. She had received this answer before. "I will relay your response."

"Relay also that if they schedule another meeting during peak modeling hours I will submit my capacity reports in a Threnn-song dialect the committee members are too young to read."

The logistics frog left.

Reed sat on a root stool near the largest moss bed. The stool was uncomfortable, built for bodies that squatted rather than slouched.

"The displacement mechanism. Can I see it?"

Krrik looked at him for the first time in several minutes. Both eyes. "Why."

"Because I want to understand how you move matter across a continent using technology that looks like rocks in a puddle."

"The file on your species describes you as intermittently clever primates with an impressive tolerance for self-destruction. Your technology assessment takes up less space than your conflict history." He hopped down from the ledge. "Come."

The displacement apparatus was in an alcove off the main lab, separated by a root wall with a gap sized for frogs. Reed crouched, ducked, and eventually crawled through on his hands and knees into a space he could barely stand upright in.

It was a ring of stones. Dark, wet, each one the size of his fist, arranged in a circle roughly two meters across. Between and across them ran a network of fungal threads, pale and faintly luminous. The threads pulsed with a rhythm so slow it was almost invisible. The whole arrangement sat in a shallow depression filled with about two centimeters of clear water.

The most advanced transportation technology on Earth looked like a rock garden someone had forgotten to drain.

"It looks like wet rocks."

"It looks like what it needs to look like." Krrik settled beside the ring. His forelimbs rested on the nearest stone. "What did you expect?"

"I don't know. Something that looked like it could fold space."

"It does not fold space. That is your analogy. Your species uses 'fold' because you imagine space as a flat thing that bends. Space is not flat and it does not bend. What this does is." His skin went through three shades of brown. "Do you understand resonance at the substrate level? Harmonic correspondence between molecular states across distance?"

"I understand resonance. I do not understand how wet rocks produce it at scales that should require more energy than the sun."

"The energy comes from the voss. The stones are interface. The fungal network is conductor. The water is medium. The operator is the." Orange flashed across his skin. "I do not have a word in your language. The operator is the thing that makes the intention real. Not consciousness. Not will. Something between. Your language does not have a word because your species does not have the capacity."

Reed pushed his glasses up, wiped them on his shirt, pushed them back. "You're saying the mechanism requires a cognitive input that humans cannot produce?"

"I am saying that explaining this to you is like explaining color to a species that sees in two dimensions. I can describe it. You can understand the description. You cannot experience the referent."

A voice from the main lab, rich and unhurried.

"The frustration is the teaching."

Krrik's colors went solid orange. Reed turned. Brother Vesh sat on a ledge near the alcove entrance, compact, vivid emerald green with gold flecks that caught the bioluminescence. One eye slightly larger than the other. He was eating something small and winged.

"I was looking for Pem," Vesh said. "But I heard you explaining displacement and thought I would help."

"You cannot help. You do not understand displacement."

"I understand displacement as the universe understands it. As flow. As the natural tendency of coherent matter to respond to coherent intention. The stones do not move things because of physics. They move things because the universe is already moving, and the stones are how we ask politely."

"That is not what happens."

"That is exactly what happens, described correctly. You describe it with substrate resonance and harmonic correspondence, which are accurate and incomplete. I describe it with flow and intention, which are imprecise and complete. Together we are almost useful."

"We are not together. You wandered in. I did not invite you. The stones operate on measurable principles that do not require your cosmological narration."

"And yet the operators who meditate before displacement achieve fourteen percent better accuracy than those who do not. This is in your own data."

Krrik's entire body went a shade of orange so vivid it was almost red. "That is a correlation. Correlation is not causation. Meditation affects neurochemistry. Neurochemistry affects operator function. This is biology, not the Croaking."

"The Croaking is biology. That is my point." Vesh ate the rest of the insect, chewing with serene deliberation. "The universe does not recognize the distinctions you insist on making. Engineering, metaphysics, biology. These are filing cabinets. The universe does not file."

"The universe also does not eat insects during scientific discussions."

"The universe does whatever the universe does. I am part of the universe. The insect was part of the universe. Now the insect is part of me. This is also displacement, of a kind." Vesh's gold flecks intensified. He looked enormously pleased with himself. "I will leave you. Your human is very attentive. He reminds me of a tadpole discovering water. Tell him about the growth problem. He may see it differently."

Vesh hopped from the ledge and vanished into the corridor, having contributed nothing to the engineering discussion and having irritated Krrik with a precision that suggested long practice.

Krrik sat still. His colors cooled slowly from orange through amber to his usual mottled brown.

"I apologize," he said.

"For what?"

"For Vesh. For his existence. For the fact that he says things that are wrong in ways that sound right, and I cannot refute him without engaging his framework, and engaging his framework requires accepting premises I do not accept. He knows this. It delights him."

Reed grinned. "He's not entirely wrong, though. About the meditation correlation."

"Do not start."

They went back to the main lab. Two things had changed. First, a new cluster of frogs had gathered around the largest moss bed, five of them, all engineers by the look of them, skin cycling through anxious greens as they studied what the moss was displaying. Second, Marsh was standing near the entrance with Oona, who was looking at the lab the way someone might look at a room where a small, controlled disaster had been lovingly curated over many years.

"Krrik," Oona said. Her orange throat patch was very bright. "This is why engineers should not live alone. I have seen cleaner moss trenches."

"I do not live here."

"You do not live anywhere. You exist near equipment. There is something growing in that water channel that I do not think you put there on purpose."

"It is a controlled mycological experiment."

"It smells like an uncontrolled mycological experiment." She turned to Marsh. "He is brilliant and his workspace looks like something died in it. He will tell you it is all intentional and labeled. He will be lying about the labeled part."

Krrik's skin flickered. "The labeling system is internal."

"The labeling system is in your head. When you die, nobody will find anything in here." She looked around the lab with frank assessment. "I do not understand anything in here and I do not want to. Marsh wanted to see the moss beds."

Marsh was already at the tiered stone containers, crouching to look at the largest bed. She did not reach for it. She had heard the skin-oils lecture from the corridor, apparently.

"It's warm," she said. "Is it supposed to be warm?"

"It is alive," Krrik said. "Living things are warm. This is not a revelation."

Marsh looked at it for a long moment. Marsh was not trying to understand the mechanism. She was trying to understand what it was like to tend a living thing that thought.

"Do you talk to them?" she asked.

Krrik's orange flashed so fast it barely registered. "They are computation media. They do not have opinions. They do not have feelings. They process inputs and generate outputs. I do not talk to them."

"He talks to them," Oona said. "Everyone knows this."

Krrik said nothing, which was, Reed thought, louder than anything he could have said.

Oona and Marsh left. The lab settled back into its baseline hum, the moss beds pulsing, the drip system leaking, the fungal threads carrying information or nutrients or both between surfaces that looked dead and were not.

Reed joined the cluster of engineers at the voss models.

He could not read the moss bed's display directly, but he could read the engineers. Their colors were wrong. The anxious greens had deepened into grey. Reed had seen it before, in labs at MIT, in conference rooms when the numbers went bad. The look of people staring at data they had trusted, and that was now telling them something they did not want to hear.

"Show me," Reed said.

Krrik did not look at him. He was looking at the bed. His forelimb rested on the surface, and the display shifted, and Reed could see, in the branching patterns of light through the moss, a shape that was either a map or a model or both. Root networks spreading outward from a central mass. The central mass was bright, almost white. The outer networks dimmed as they extended, fading into nothing at the edges of the display. Except that the edges were not where they should have been.

"This is the voss growth pattern for the current cycle," Krrik said. His voice was flat. No irritation. No impatience. Just flat. "The bright center is the primary substrate mass beneath us. The network is its extension through the root system. Normally, during a radiation peak, the growth extends approximately forty percent beyond its resting boundary. That is manageable. That is what the Pruning manages."

He touched the moss, and the display shifted. The bright center expanded. The network pushed outward, further, brighter, thicker.

"This is the current growth. Sixty-seven percent beyond resting boundary and accelerating. At this rate, within two of your months, the substrate will have consumed the primary root system of this section of forest. Within four months, the growth will be visible at the surface. Within a year."

He stopped.

"Within a year?" Reed prompted.

"Within a year we will know whether our methods are sufficient. We have done this before. We have always done this." Krrik looked at the display. The junior engineer from earlier had returned and was standing at the cluster's edge, her colors grey and still. "But 'always' is a comfort, not a guarantee."

Reed sat with that. The engineers around the moss bed were quiet. Their colors said what their voices did not.

"I have surface data," Reed said.

Krrik looked at him.

"Root density measurements. Canopy health assessments. Soil chemistry profiles. Six months of data collected from the surface. We documented changes in root density and soil composition without knowing what was causing them. We were looking at voss growth from outside without knowing what voss was."

"Your instruments are crude."

"My instruments measure what they measure. They measured something you did not measure, because you were inside it."

Krrik's colors cycled. Brown, green, brown, the rapid unconscious processing pattern Reed had learned to recognize as Krrik thinking hard about something he did not want to think about.

"The data would need calibration. Your measurement scales are not compatible with ours."

"I can provide the raw numbers. Your moss beds can handle the conversion."

"My computation arrays can handle the conversion." A pause. Something very like a smile crossed Krrik's face, which on a frog was a slight relaxation of the jaw muscles and a brightening of the skin around the eyes. "Your species' instruments are rudimentary. But a perspective from outside the system is. Not useless."

"High praise."

"It is not praise. It is a statement of non-dismissal. There is a difference."

Reed stood alone in the lab after the engineers dispersed to relay Krrik's instructions about sensor recalibration. Krrik had gone to examine a voss sample in a side chamber, his lopsided hop carrying him through the low doorway without hesitation.

The displacement apparatus was visible through the gap in the root wall. Wet stones. Fungal threads. Two centimeters of still water. The thing that moved matter across continents, between hemispheres, between star systems. Built by a species that lived thirty-five years and argued about fermented insects and could not keep fungus out of their bathing chambers.

Reed's entire career, the accumulated scientific knowledge of his species, was a subset. Not a rival tradition. A subset. The frogs understood evolution better than he did, because they had been doing it for two hundred million years, because they had visited other solar systems and found them disappointing and come home to argue about food.

His hands were shaking. Not from fear. Everything he thought he knew was still true, but in the context of what surrounded him, it was so small that the word "small" did not do it justice. Footnote. His work was a footnote. And the footnote might be useful, which was its own guilty thrill, because the most terrifying scientific crisis in the history of a two-hundred-million-year-old civilization was also, from where Reed stood squinting at wet rocks through fogged glasses, the most extraordinary laboratory on Earth.

The glasses went up. The notebook came out. He began writing down every question he did not yet know how to ask.

## Chapter Intent
This chapter makes the scale of frog technology viscerally real through Reed's overwhelmed encounter with Krrik's laboratory, while establishing the scientific dimension of the Pruning crisis. Every piece of information arrives through friction: Reed's desperate curiosity meeting Krrik's brilliant impatience, Vesh's infuriating spiritual tangent, Oona's domestic puncturing, the junior engineer's anxious report. The primitive-impossible contrast is at maximum: the most powerful technology on Earth is moss and wet rocks in a messy room tended by an irritable frog with a shorter leg. Reed's offer of surface data is positioned correctly as a small contribution from an outside perspective, not salvation. The chapter moves from scientific excitement through wonder and intellectual vertigo, through comedy (the Vesh-Krrik argument, Oona's visit), into the darkening realization that the voss growth models are failing, and ends with Reed standing alone in the lab feeling the guilty thrill of being inside the most extraordinary scientific environment in human history at the worst possible time.
