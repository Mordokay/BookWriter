# Chapter 2: Swarm

## Chapter Intent

First animal encounter. Externalize Ada's anxiety through the insect swarm. Exhaust her first defense mechanism (strategic thinking / problem-solving). Transition from Zone 1 into Zone 2 (The Tangle). Plant the lesson that fighting the experience feeds it: stopping -- even involuntarily -- is what releases it.

## Draft

She was counting trees.

Forty-three paces north, or what she judged to be north, though the moon had vanished behind the canopy and she had no compass and the word "north" was beginning to feel like a sound she was making with no referent. Forty-three paces, and the Douglas fir with the split trunk she had passed should be on her left. It was not on her left. It was not on her right. There was no split trunk anywhere in the darkness around her.

The voice in her head had not stopped since the trail vanished. It had only shifted register, from clinical notation to operational planning, running through solutions the way a machine runs through diagnostics when it has been designed to solve and cannot be told there is nothing to solve. Turn ninety degrees right. Walk straight. Count paces. Grid the area. The clearing was here. Clearings did not move.

She turned ninety degrees right. She walked. She counted.

The undergrowth thickened with each step, ferns pressing higher against her thighs, salal closing in, its leathery leaves dragging against her jacket with a sound like damp paper being drawn across skin. At one hundred and twelve paces the counting stopped because there was nothing left to count toward. The clearing was not here. Nothing was here except more forest, identical and indifferent, the pale scattered glow of luminous fungi marking the positions of fallen branches she had never seen.

Her fingers found the backpack straps and tightened on them. Behind her, the forest she had just walked through was wrong again. Not the same wrong as before, a new wrong, differently arranged. The bigleaf maple she had passed at step sixty was gone. In its place stood a cluster of young hemlocks that reached her shoulder, their needles beaded with moisture that caught the faint fungal light and held it in pale green constellations.

Okay. Okay. The psilocybin was disrupting spatial memory. Something in her brain's sense of direction, misfiring. The forest looked the same because old-growth forests look the same: similar species, similar spacing, similar scale. She was not lost in a changing forest. She was lost in a constant forest and her perception was unreliable.

The voice was still clinical. The voice was still making sense. But it was talking faster now, and under the sense there was a current she did not want to name. Her feet had stopped moving while the voice ran. She was standing in the dark between two fallen logs, both furred thick with moss, the moss itself dotted with tiny ferns that had rooted into the decay. The air was cold on the exposed skin of her wrists. Somewhere nearby, water dripped with the slow regularity of a metronome, a single drop striking a leaf or a hollow in wood, marking intervals of a time she could no longer measure.

The moon. She tried the moon. Through a narrow gap in the canopy far above, the first she had found in minutes, a sliver of the waxing gibbous was visible, milky and diminished by a thin veil of cloud. She stared at it. Waxing gibbous, late autumn, rises mid-afternoon, so by now it would be... what time was it? After sunset. After onset. After the trail. The moon was there, in that gap, which meant...

The calculation would not complete. The moon's position required knowing the approximate hour, which required a sense of elapsed time, which the psilocybin had dissolved into something without edges. Each step of the calculation generated two new unknowns, and each unknown generated two more, branching and branching, and her mind was running them all simultaneously, the decision tree expanding in every direction until it was not a tree but a thicket, a tangle of its own, and she could not find the trunk beneath all the branches.

Her eyes dropped from the gap. The gap was gone. The canopy had sealed above her while she was looking through it.

Fingertips pressed into her temples, she stood still. The voice offered three possible directions toward the trailhead, each supported by different assumptions about where the moon had been, each contradicted by the spatial evidence around her. Choosing required data she did not have, and the absence of data was producing not paralysis but acceleration, her mind spinning faster in the void, generating hypotheses and discarding them in the same breath, the cognitive equivalent of wheels on ice.

The paper bag was on the ground at the base of a Sitka spruce, half-hidden beneath a fern frond. Folded twice with precise creases. The paper was damp, dark at the edges where it touched the earth, but the folds were exactly as she had made them. She crouched and picked it up. The mushroom bag. She had set it on the moss beside the fallen log in the clearing, and the clearing was... she turned, scanning the darkness. Nowhere. She had been walking away from the clearing for however long she had been walking. The bag should not be here.

Both hands held it. The paper was cold and damp against her fingers. Her mind reached for an explanation: she had walked in a circle, she was near the clearing without recognizing it, the bag had been moved by an animal, but there was no clearing here, and the forest around her looked nothing like it, and the explanation collapsed before it finished forming, a structure with no foundation.

The bag went back where she had found it. She stood. Her jaw ached from clenching and she made herself open it, working the hinge until the tension released, and the release did not help because the tension was not in her jaw. It was in the spaces between her thoughts.

The voice offered one more plan: pick a direction. Any direction. Walk straight. Eventually the forest has edges. Every forest has edges.

A direction. Any direction. She walked.

The undergrowth grew denser. Ferns gave way to salal pressing close on both sides, stems she had to push through with her forearms. The leaves were slick, waxy, and they parted with a rubbery resistance before springing back behind her, slapping against the backpack as she passed. The darkness here had texture: not the open darkness of the forest behind her but something closer, thicker, a darkness that pressed against her face like humidity. Every few steps her boot would catch on something invisible beneath the fern cover: a root, a branch, the hidden topography of a forest floor that had been building its own archaeology for centuries, layer on decomposing layer.

Then deadfall: a Douglas fir trunk so large it reached her waist, bark furrowed deep, blocking the way forward. Her boots found purchase on a knot of wood as she climbed it. Her jeans caught on a stub of branch at the right knee and she yanked free, a rip, a sound of tearing denim, and kept moving. On the other side the ground dropped into a shallow depression and her boots sank into cold mud. It sucked at her soles with each step, a sound like mouths opening and closing. Hauling herself up the far side required hands gripping roots, fingers digging into wet earth, forearms bracing against the slope. Mud packed beneath her fingernails, cold and grainy. The roots were slick with a thin film of algae, and her grip slipped twice before she found a hold that would bear her weight: a thick cedar root, rough-barked, angled like a rung.

The air smelled different here. Sweeter, heavier, the fungal decay thickened to something almost cloying, the scent of wood so far past death it had become its own kind of life. Spores in the air coated her tongue, metallic and sweet. The bioluminescence had shifted, the orange-green glow of the jack-o'-lantern mushrooms replaced by something paler and cooler. Tiny delicate mushrooms, caps no wider than her thumbnail, glowing a faint green, clustered on fallen branches and scattered across the leaf litter in pale green points. Their glow turned the undergrowth into a web of faint light and deep shadow, and in the shadows the shapes of things were uncertain. A stump that could have been an animal. A root arch that could have been a doorway. A hanging mass of stair-step moss on a standing dead tree that swayed without wind.

The ground beneath her boots had changed, too. Softer, more yielding. Not the firm leaf-litter of the upper forest but a deep accumulation of decaying matter that compressed with each step and did not fully spring back. Her footprints stayed behind her, dark depressions that slowly pooled with water seeping up from below. The undergrowth here grew not from soil but from the bodies of older plants, the living rooted in the dead, and the psilocybin made her aware of this in a way that bypassed thought and went straight to her feet, each step landing on something that had once been alive and was now the ground for something else that was alive and would, in its turn, become ground for something else. It went all the way down. There was no bottom that was not also a surface.

The voice was narrating this, cataloging species, assigning threat levels, filing each detail into the same organizational structure it had been using since the trail vanished. But the narration was growing thin, a commentary track that no longer matched the images it was describing. The forest was producing details faster than the voice could categorize them.

And then, in one cluster of glowing mushrooms, a dozen caps had fruited along a curved branch in a line so precise and evenly spaced they looked like notes ascending a musical staff. The bioluminescence in each cap pulsed at its own faint rhythm, slightly offset from the others, so the light appeared to travel: a slow, liquid cascade from the lowest cap to the highest and back, a wave of pale green fire breathing along the branch. It was, for one unguarded moment, beautiful. Not the beauty of something safe. The beauty of something so perfectly organized by forces that had nothing to do with her that her mind went quiet in front of it, the sudden hush of a loud room when a single clear note sounds. The light cascaded. The caps pulsed. Her breath slowed by a single beat.

Then a hanging strand of old-man's-beard lichen brushed her cheek and the moment broke.

Bracket fungi grew on the dead trunks in tiers so high she had to tilt her head to see where they ended, and the tilting made the dark canopy reel above her. She stopped tilting. She kept walking. The voice was still talking but its sentences were shorter now, the clinical vocabulary wearing thin, the diagnostic giving way to something more like instruction barked at a body that was not obeying fast enough.

Find a slope. Downhill leads to water. Water leads to trails. That was a survival-manual fact. That had the weight of something external, something she had not invented, something she could lean against.

She looked for a slope. The ground was level in every direction.

Come on. Think. Find a landmark. Find something distinctive. Find something you have seen before, which was nothing, because everything was new, because the forest was new, every direction the first time she was seeing it, and the map in her head was blank, a white page that her mind kept trying to write on and the ink kept running off the edge...

The first moth arrived.

It drifted past her face, slow, erratic, wings catching the pale green glow as it passed. A large moth, furry-bodied, antennae like feathered combs. A flinch, reflex, nothing more. Then a second moth from the other direction, brushing her hair. A hand raised to wave it away. A third. And then not three, not five, but many. A dozen, two dozen, emerging from the undergrowth as if the dark itself were shedding layers, each one carrying its own faint green charge.

Then the mosquitoes. The whine arrived before they did, thin, metallic, threading into her hearing from all sides at once. Not one mosquito. Hundreds. Impossible for late autumn, wrong for the season, but the wrongness registered only as a flicker before the sheer number overwhelmed it. The individual whines layered into a drone that shifted pitch as she turned her head. They were on her neck, on the backs of her hands, on her face. The pinpoint touch of legs, the brush of needle-mouths against skin. They did not bite. They landed and stayed, each one a point of contact so slight it should have been negligible, but the accumulation was not negligible; it was a second skin assembling itself on top of hers, alive and vibrating.

The beetles came next, clicking. Invisible in the dark, but the percussive clicking wove into the mosquito drone and the moth wing-flutter until the sound was no longer a collection of species but a single complex noise, a texture with presence and weight. It surrounded her. It thickened. The air itself gained density.

Under the psilocybin, the swarm changed.

It happened in stages, each one a further departure from what the clinical voice would have called baseline perception. Individual insects were still identifiable, a moth, a crane fly, a beetle, but the spaces between them pulsed with faint light, as if the swarm had its own bioluminescence. It moved as a fluid, tightening and loosening in patterns she could almost predict and never quite could. A crane fly drifted past her face with its legs trailing like threads, slow enough to seem deliberate. Moths orbited her head in elliptical paths that tightened when she moved and loosened when she stood still. The swarm breathed. It had a rhythm. It was not random.

And the drone was developing harmonics. Overtones that had not been there before, tones too complex for the sum of insect wings and insect bodies, climbing into frequencies that vibrated in her sinuses and behind her eyes. The harmonics layered and shifted, almost resolving into something structured, something with...

Not words. Not speech. But fragments. Syllables assembling at the edge of recognition and dissolving before they solidified. The fragments had a cadence she knew. They had a rhythm. They sounded like...

...what if you can't...

A hand swatted at the air around her head. The swarm tightened. Closer. Louder.

...which way which way which...

The fragments were not the swarm's voice. They were hers. Her cadence. The anxious inner monologue she had been running since the trail vanished, the cascading what-ifs and failed calculations, the branching uncertainties, reflected back to her from a hundred small vibrating bodies, distorted into something alien but unmistakably hers. The swarm was not speaking to her. It was speaking as her.

...too late too late you should have...

"Stop," she said. The word came out thin, a sound without authority. The swarm did not stop. The drone intensified. The fragments multiplied, overlapping, arriving faster than she could not-hear them.

...wrong direction wrong direction...

Her palms pressed against her ears. The drone changed but did not diminish; it traveled through her skull bones, through her clenched jaw, arriving at her inner ear by a route her hands could not block. The moths were in her hair. Wings against her scalp, the tiny grip of legs. The mosquitoes covered her forearms, her neck, the patch of skin between her collar and her hairline. They did not bite. They did not need to.

Her breathing had gone wrong. Short bursts that fogged in the cold air. Her breath hung visible in the green glow, the clouds of it merging with the swarm, her lungs feeding the cloud, the distinction between inside and outside dissolving in a way that was nothing like the gentle onset loosening, but frantic, recursive, a system feeding back into itself and amplifying with each cycle.

...you thought you could control this you thought...

The voice in her head and the swarm's fragments were running in parallel now, indistinguishable. Which thoughts were hers and which were reflections; the question had no answer. The swarm was her mind heard from the outside. There was no way to turn the volume down on both.

She ran.

Not a decision. A detonation. One moment kneeling, the next upright and moving, her legs finding a gear they had not been asked for, every muscle firing in the single coordinated panic of an animal that has stopped thinking about where to go and is now just going.

Crashing through the salal with her forearms raised in front of her face, branches snapping against her jacket, stems whipping past. The swarm kept pace. The drone did not diminish; if anything it tightened, compressed by her speed into something denser, the fragments merging into a continuous anxious hum that matched the pounding of her feet. She could not outrun something that was everywhere.

A root caught her boot and her ankle rolled but did not give, and the stumble carried her sideways into a hemlock trunk. Bark against her shoulder, rough even through the jacket. The impact sent a jolt through her collarbone and into the base of her skull. She pushed off and kept running. The forest streaked past in vertical dark columns with pale green smears of bioluminescence between them, the small glowing mushrooms blurring into brushstrokes of light. Her lungs burned. Cold air tore at her throat on each inhale, too fast, too shallow, the kind of breathing that fed the panic instead of starving it, each gasp a signal to her body that something was chasing her, and her body believed the signal even though nothing was chasing her except the air itself.

The ground dipped without warning and her stride overextended, her leading foot landing hard on a downslope she had not seen. The shock traveled up through her knee and into her hip. She kept going. The terrain was fighting her now, every surface angled or soft or both, roots and rocks hidden beneath the fern cover, the forest floor an obstacle course designed by something that did not want her to run. A branch caught the top of her backpack and yanked her backward for half a step before the branch snapped. Fern fronds slapped against her thighs, cold and wet, and the cold was beginning to register now, the sweat cooling against her skin beneath the jacket, her body unable to decide if it was hot or freezing.

Deadfall caught her shin and she stumbled forward, hands finding a trunk, bark rough against her palms, and she pushed off and kept moving. The glow streaked past in pale green blurs. She could not see where she was going. She was not going anywhere. Moving away from where she stood, but where she stood was inside the swarm, and the swarm was inside her, and the running was making both worse but the alternative to running was standing still and she was not capable of standing still because standing still meant being inside it without the distraction of motion.

Devil's club.

The thicket hit at a run, or she hit it, the distinction between running into something and something appearing in her path no longer clear. Devil's club: broad hand-shaped leaves she barely registered, stems thick with fine barbed spines she did not see until they were in her skin. Her left forearm took the worst of it, raking across a stem at speed. The pain arrived distant, muffled by adrenaline: heat, not sharpness, a line of warmth from wrist to elbow. She plunged through. More stems caught her right hand, her jacket sleeves, the denim at her thighs. The spines were engineered for exactly this: fine enough to pierce, barbed to break off in the flesh, too small to grip and pull. They lodged beneath her skin and began their slow inflammatory work, a delayed reaction, a time bomb of histamine that would bloom into misery long after the moment of contact had passed. She did not feel the individual spines yet. The adrenaline had drawn a curtain over the pain, and behind the curtain the spines were already swelling, the skin already tightening into the hours-long itch that would outlast everything else this night would do to her. The swarm was still with her. The drone was in her ears, in her teeth, in the spaces behind her eyes.

Another fallen trunk, larger, slick with moss, her hands finding no grip. She hauled herself over and dropped down the far side, landing hard enough to jar her spine. The ground here was root-crossed and her boot caught and she went down. Hands and knees in cold earth. The impact emptied her lungs. For a moment she was only a body on the ground, a shape among shapes, no different from the deadfall and the roots and the moss. Damp soil beneath her palms, grainy and cold, the smell of it flooding her nostrils, mineral, deep, the scent of things that had been decomposed past all recognition of what they once were. Small stones pressed into her kneecaps through the torn denim. A gasp. Another. Air came back in ragged, insufficient mouthfuls.

One foot under her. The thigh muscle shook, a fine involuntary vibration she could not will away. The other foot. Standing again, but the standing was a negotiation now, not a command. Three steps, and her body refused a fourth.

Back down. Knees first, then hands, and then she was kneeling on the forest floor in the dark with her palms pressed into the earth and her head bowed and the swarm pulsing around her in its layered, chattering, unending drone.

Hands over her ears again. Eyes squeezed shut. The moths were on her shoulders, on her back, their combined weight no more than a scattered handful of leaves but their presence enormous. The mosquitoes mantled her wrists, her neck. Beetles clicked and crawled across the roots near her knees, their hard bodies navigating the terrain of her stillness. The drone filled the space behind her closed eyelids, and in the drone the fragments were still arriving, faster now, arriving not as language but as pressure behind her forehead and a taste like copper on her tongue.

And then she was too tired to fight.

It was not a decision. It was not surrender; she would not have used that word, did not have access to that word yet. She simply ran out of the resource that kept her arms raised and her hands pressed and her body braced against the sound. The resource depleted and her arms dropped to her sides and her hands opened, palms up on her thighs, mud on her fingers, the fine devil's club spines already pulsing their hot itch beneath the skin, and she knelt there. Head bowed. Breathing.

The swarm did not leave immediately.

It stayed, circling, the drone still present, still layered. But the pitch dropped. A half-tone, then another. The fragments lost their rhythm, lost their almost-word quality, and became what they may have always been: the overlapping sounds of many small creatures in flight, meaningless and purposeless and no more directed at her than rain is directed at the ground. The swarm loosened. The dense cloud thinned and broke apart, individual insects peeling away the way thoughts peel away from a mind that has stopped grasping at them. A cluster of moths drifted toward the pale mushroom glow on a fallen branch. The mosquitoes thinned. The beetles on the ground clicked once more and crawled into the leaf litter and were absorbed.

Within minutes, or something shaped like minutes, the swarm had dispersed. The last crane fly wobbled upward into the dark canopy and was gone.

The silence was enormous. Not silence. The forest was never silent. Water still dripped somewhere to her left. A branch creaked in the canopy, adjusting to a weight she could not see. But the absence of the drone left a space so vast and ringing it had physical dimensions, a hollow carved in the exact shape of what had filled it. Ada heard her own breathing: ragged, wet, catching in her throat on the inhale. Her heartbeat heavy and fast in her ears. The drip of condensation from a branch above. The faint clicks and rustles of the forest floor settling around her knees, accepting her weight as it accepted the weight of everything that fell.

Her eyes opened. The lids were heavy, gummed with something: tears, or condensation, or the fine residue of spores that had been settling on her face while she knelt with her eyes closed. The bioluminescent glow swam for a moment, then steadied.

The Tangle.

The undergrowth pressed close on every side: devil's club and salal and ferns and deadfall and standing dead trees mossed to their shoulders in thick cascading shelves. Visibility reached ten feet, maybe less. The canopy above was sealed, a solid dark ceiling with no sky showing through. The only light came from the green-capped fungi on the fallen wood, their pale green glow turning her arms and hands into something unearthly, the limbs of a creature that lived underground, something that had never known daylight.

Her arms lifted into the glow. The devil's club had left its record.

The left forearm, from wrist to the bunched-up sleeve above her elbow, was scored with thin red lines that seeped but did not freely bleed. Along the scratches, tiny spines were embedded in her skin, barely visible, each one a thread-fine barb that had broken from the stem and lodged beneath the surface. A fingertip touched one and the itch flared, localized, burning, spreading outward from the spine to connect with the next spine and the next until her entire forearm pulsed with it. Her right hand was worse: a cluster of spines across the back and between the fingers, where she had grabbed a stem while running without seeing it. The skin between her thumb and index finger was already swelling, tight and hot.

Pulling the spines out was not possible. Her fingers were shaking too badly. The light was too dim. There were too many. Her arms lowered.

Trembling. Not from cold, although the cold was real, the temperature having dropped well into the thirties, her breath a pale cloud in the fungal glow, but from the withdrawal of adrenaline. Her muscles clenched and released in small involuntary spasms: thighs, jaw, the webbing between her fingers. Fists pressed against her knees to still them and the pressing did not help.

The voice in her head tried to reassemble.

What was that. Just a panic response. Amplified by the psilocybin. The insects were attracted to warmth, to CO2 in her breath, to the subtle electrical charge of a mammalian body in the dark. The rest was pattern recognition gone haywire, the psilocybin amplifying the brain's tendency to find signal in noise, hearing words in a mosquito drone as people hear voices in white noise or faces in tree bark. There was an explanation for all of it. There was an explanation...

The explanation sounded like a hand waving in front of something it did not want to see.

Running from bugs. That was all. Moths and mosquitoes and beetles in a dark forest while on psilocybin, and she had panicked and run and torn herself on devil's club, and the swarm had not attacked her, had not bitten or stung, had not done anything except be present, be everywhere, be so close and so loud that the boundary between the sound and her own thoughts had...

The voice trailed off. It could not finish the sentence because the sentence was trying to contain something that did not fit inside a sentence. The swarm had known her cadence. It had spoken, or almost-spoken, or she had almost-heard, in the rhythm of her own anxious mind. It had felt the way her thoughts felt at three in the morning when she could not sleep and the what-ifs came faster and louder and more accusatory until the only options were to get up and do something productive or to lie in the dark and be consumed by the noise of herself. She had felt that a thousand times in her apartment, in her bed, staring at the ceiling. She had never heard it from the outside.

She sat back on her heels. The mud was cold beneath her knees, soaking through the torn denim at the right knee. The itch in her forearms pulsed steadily, a small persistent pain that was, in its physical specificity, almost a relief. It was a body-problem. A problem with edges and dimensions. Everything else that had happened since she ate the mushrooms had no edges at all.

Thinking could not solve this. The recognition arrived not as a thought but as a depletion. Her mind, which had been running at full capacity since the trail vanished, spinning plans, generating hypotheses, constructing and discarding maps, had hit something the swarm had shown her: the engine of her thinking, pushed past its tolerances, produced not clarity but noise. The faster she thought, the less she understood, and the forest only closed tighter around her.

And when she had stopped, not chosen to stop, been too exhausted to continue, the swarm had thinned. The noise had dropped. The almost-words had dissolved back into the drone and the drone had dissolved back into the individual sounds of individual creatures and the creatures had drifted away, leaving her kneeling in the dirt with nothing solved and nothing worse. The stopping had done what the running could not. She held this in her body, in the trembling muscles, in the stinging forearms, in the lungs still pulling air in shallow draws, and did not analyze it. She just knelt in the mud and let herself be tired.

After a while she stood.

The motion was slow, deliberate. Nothing like the efficient, backpack-adjusted rising of the woman who had left the fallen log however long ago. One leg straightened, then the other. The backpack shifted on her shoulders, its weight suddenly noticeable again; it had been part of her body during the run, forgotten, and now it announced itself as a thing she was carrying, a burden with straps and buckles and a water bottle she had not touched. Her knees ached. Her hands throbbed. Mud wiped from her palms left streaks across her jeans, which were already dark with it at the knees, and she did not bother to brush the streaks away.

Every direction looked the same. Dense, lightless except for the scattered pale green points of bioluminescent mushrooms, impenetrable. No sky. No slope. No landmark. No trail. No evidence that the forest had an edge or a center or a direction that led anywhere other than deeper into itself. The cold had settled into her now. Not the sharp cold of the first hour but something heavier, a damp weight that had found her bones through the layers of sweat-dampened clothing and was drawing the heat out of her slowly, patiently, as it drew the heat out of everything.

A direction, then. Not because it was right, there was no right, but because standing still in the Tangle felt like sinking, and she was not ready to sink. Her forearms held tight against her body to protect the scratched skin, she pushed through the nearest gap in the undergrowth. The salal parted reluctantly, its stems bending rather than breaking, and she had to lean her weight into the gap before it widened enough to pass through. On the other side, the ground was softer, mossier, the faint fungal glow slightly brighter, or her eyes had adjusted, or the psilocybin had adjusted her eyes. She walked.

A single moth circled her head once, slow, unhurried, its wings catching the pale green light. Then it spiraled upward, found a gap between branches, and was gone.

Ada walked. The forest pressed close and the darkness pressed closer and her breath made small pale clouds that drifted ahead of her and dissolved before she could reach them.
