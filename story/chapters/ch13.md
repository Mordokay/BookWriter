# Chapter 13: Every Elegant System Leaks

Hart had been awake since before the fungal light shifted, which she had started using as a clock because her actual watch had died on Day 9 and nobody in a civilization capable of folding space had offered her a battery.

She met Talla at the junction where the main corridor split toward the communal pools and the narrower passages that led, as far as Hart could tell, to the parts of the settlement that did not appear in Noll's curated tour. Talla was waiting with the rigid posture of someone who had been standing still for exactly as long as her patience allowed.

"You said you wanted to see the edges," Talla said. "The edges are this way."

"Bon. Lead."

They walked. The corridors narrowed within fifty meters, the root walls closer together, the bioluminescent growth thinner and patchier. The light went from the settlement's usual dim blue-green to something sparser, concentrated in streaks rather than the even wash Hart had gotten used to. The stone underfoot was damper. Not the controlled dampness of the communal areas, where the water channels were engineered and the humidity managed. This was leaking. Moisture on walls that should have been dry. A faint smell Hart recognized from fieldwork in flooded forest: cellular stress, the sharp green chemical tang of plant tissue breaking down under pressure.

Frog life continued around them. This was not an abandoned sector. A family group occupied a shallow alcove to the left, two adults and three juveniles, the juveniles chasing something small and alive across the wet stone floor. Further on, an older frog sat on a ledge sorting dried insect preparations into shallow dishes, his movements unhurried, his olive skin showing the kind of chronic pallor that Hart associated with sustained low-level stress. He glanced at them as they passed. His colors did not change. He went back to sorting.

"This section houses roughly two hundred," Talla said. "Mostly families relocated from the western perimeter when the staging grounds expanded. The coordinators moved them here because the space was available. The space was available because nobody wanted it."

"Why not?"

Talla pointed at the wall. Hart looked. The root structure, which in the settlement's center was smooth and hardened to a warm, living solidity, was here swollen. Distended. Pale threads of something luminous pushed through the surface, pulsing faintly with a rhythm that was too slow to be mechanical and too regular to be random. The voss. Growing where it should not be growing, pressing into the architecture the way tree roots pressed into old foundations, except these roots were warm to proximity and pulsing with their own rhythm.

Hart crouched. The growth was warm to the touch. Warmer than the surrounding stone. She could feel it through her fingertips: a low, steady vibration, biological, unmistakable.

"How fast?" she asked.

"Two centimeters in the last four days along this corridor. Faster near the water channels. Slower where the stone is denser."

Hart stood. She wiped her hand on her trousers. Force of habit. Contamination protocol for a substance she could not classify using any framework McGill or the London School had given her.

The maintenance crew was three corridors further, where the voss growth had pushed through a wall joint and was reaching across the ceiling in pale, branching tendrils that caught the thin bioluminescence and threw it back in soft, pulsing waves. Two frogs were working on the growth directly, one applying a dark paste to the base of the intrusion while the other redirected a water channel away from the affected area. A third sat on a stone shelf nearby, eating, watching them work, and offering commentary.

"You are applying too much," the third frog said. He was compact, brownish, and clearly considered himself the authority on every aspect of maintenance work.

"I am applying the correct amount," said the frog with the paste, not looking up.

"The correct amount is less than that. If you saturate the base, the retardant seeps into the water channel and we spend the next two days flushing it out. I have told you this."

"You have told me this. You are wrong."

Hart watched them for a moment. She recognized the argument. She had witnessed identical disputes in every field hospital, every water treatment facility, every decontamination unit she had worked in. Two professionals, one methodology, no agreement. The specifics were alien. The dynamic was universal.

"What are you using?" she asked the frog with the paste.

Both workers turned. The frog on the shelf stopped eating.

"Chemical retardant," said the one applying it. "It slows the growth by disrupting the substrate's nutrient uptake. Temporarily. The effect lasts perhaps six to eight days. Then we reapply."

"And has it been working?"

"It has been working for eleven cycles." The frog sat back on her haunches. Her skin was a tired, washed-out green. Long shifts. "It has been working less well this cycle. The growth rate is faster. The retardant concentration was designed for standard expansion. This is not standard."

The frog on the shelf made a sound. "Tell her the rest."

The worker looked at him. Then at Hart. "The coordinators do not visit this side."

She said it without bitterness. Without drama. The way someone reports that the supply truck does not come on Tuesdays. A fact about how things are organized, observed without expectation that it would change.

Talla did not comment. She stood at the corridor's edge, her dark skin nearly invisible against the dimmer walls, her pale spots hidden. She let the worker's observation sit where it landed.

Hart took a sample of the retardant paste and a small scraping of the voss growth and put both in her field kit. She asked two more questions about the growth pattern and the application schedule. The workers answered directly, with the frankness of people who were used to explaining their problems to each other and had no practice explaining them to administrators.

The third frog finished eating. "Last cycle, maintenance had six teams on the outer sections. This cycle we have four. Two were reassigned to staging-ground support. The growth is faster and we have fewer people. This is not a complaint. It is arithmetic."

"It sounds like a complaint," said the frog with the paste.

"Arithmetic and complaints have the same structure. Both describe a situation where the numbers do not add up."

Hart looked at Talla. Talla's expression was carefully blank. She did not say I told you so. She did not need to.

They moved on. The breathing circle attendance station was not where Hart expected it to be. She had imagined something institutional, a desk or a counter or at least a visible administrative presence. It was an alcove. A small, recessed space off the main corridor that connected the outer residential sections to the communal pool complex. A single frog sat in it on a moss cushion, with what appeared to be a flat stone tablet and a stylus made from a hardened reed. He was small, pale green, with the mild, unremarkable coloring of someone who blended into any room he occupied.

Hart almost walked past it. That was, she realized, the point.

"What is this?" she asked.

Talla stopped. "Ask him."

Hart looked at the frog. "What are you recording?"

The frog looked up. His expression was pleasant. Patient. He was not thinking about the task very hard. "Participation," he said. "I note who attends the breathing circles. Names, pool affiliation, frequency."

"Why?"

"Because it is noted." He said this with the simplicity of someone stating that water was wet. "The coordinators review the patterns. Community wellness. It is helpful to know who participates and who does not."

"And what happens to the frogs who do not participate?"

The frog blinked. His colors did not shift. "I simply record. What is done with the records is not my concern."

The words hung in the damp air. Hart looked at Talla. Talla looked back. Neither of them needed to speak. The logging frog returned to his tablet and made a small notation about a name Hart could not read.

Hart had encountered this before. Not the specific mechanism. The specific quality. The mild, procedural compliance of someone performing a function whose consequences were someone else's responsibility. She had seen it in pharmaceutical companies, in hospital administrations, in government health agencies where the person filing the report and the person acting on it existed in separate moral universes connected by paperwork. The logging frog was not sinister. He was clerical. The system did not require sinister. It required bland.

They were past the attendance station and into the corridors leading to the relocated residential areas when Marsh found them.

She came from the direction of the eastern nursery, her field notebook in her back pocket, carrying a shallow stone dish of something dark and fermented that smelled sharp enough to make Hart's eyes water.

"Oona's grubs," Marsh said. "For the families in the relocated section. She sends them every other day. She says the children need the protein and the coordinators have not adjusted the food distribution to account for the new residents yet."

"How long have they been relocated?" Hart asked.

"Three weeks. Some longer."

"And the food distribution has not been adjusted."

Marsh set the dish down on a ledge near a family alcove, where two young frogs immediately approached it with the cautious enthusiasm of children assessing something they had been told was good for them. "The official line is that the relocation is temporary and the standard distribution will resume when they return to their original sections. The families say they have been told this twice before."

Hart looked at the alcove. It was smaller than the residential spaces she had seen in the settlement's center. Three adults and four juveniles shared it. The adults' skin showed the muted, unsettled patterns she had noticed on the older frog sorting insects earlier. Stress colors. Not dramatic. Not the flaring alarm signals she had seen in the treatment chambers. The low, persistent color shifts of people living with sustained uncertainty. Their children's colors were brighter, less affected, but the juveniles pressed closer to the adults than Hart had observed in the main nursery areas, where tadpoles and young frogs moved freely and independently.

"They look like refugee housing," Marsh said quietly. "I have seen this in Queensland after floods. The temporary camps that become permanent because nobody makes the decision to move them back."

Talla had been silent since the attendance station. She spoke now, and her voice was flatter than Hart had heard it. Not angry. Tired. "The relocation is real. The staging grounds need the space. The Pruning is real. The voss growth is real. Everything is real. That does not mean everything is fair."

"The two are not related," Hart said.

"The two are always related. The emergency is the reason. The unfairness is the cost. And the cost is paid by the people who live on the edges, not the people who coordinate from the center."

They stood in the corridor, the three of them, the muted sounds of displaced families behind them, the faint pulse of voss growth audible if you listened for it, a low hum transmitted through the damp walls.

"So what is the alternative?" Hart asked. "No breathing circles. No tracking. No synchronized displacement. What happens when the voss grows through this wall and the one behind it and the nursery floor?"

Talla's spots flared. She controlled them. "You sound like Noll."

"I sound like someone who has run disease response in four countries and learned that the people who complain about the quarantine are sometimes right and sometimes dead. I am asking you a real question, Talla. If you dismantle the chain, what holds?"

"I do not want to dismantle the chain."

"Then what do you want?"

Talla was quiet for a moment. In the alcove behind them, one of the young frogs had taken a grub from the dish and was eating it with total concentration, not yet old enough to eat and think at the same time.

"I want the chain to be visible," Talla said. "I want the frog who logs attendance to know what is done with his records. I want the families in these corridors to know when they will go home, or to be told honestly that they will not. I want the breathing circles to be what they claim to be, voluntary, or to stop claiming it." She paused. "That is the cruelty of it. The chain is also the rope that pulls you from the water."

Marsh, who had been listening with the particular stillness she used when she was thinking, said: "Maybe the tracking is not for control. Maybe it is for care. If you know who is not participating, you know who is struggling. Who is isolated. Who needs help."

Talla looked at her. "That is a kind reading."

"It is a possible reading."

"It is also the reading the coordinators prefer, because it makes surveillance sound like love. Hart, tell her."

Hart did not want to take sides. She took one anyway, because she had been thinking about it since the logging frog and the thought had arrived fully formed. "Tracking is control. It does not matter what the stated intent is. The moment you record who participates and who does not, you have created a mechanism for pressure, whether or not anyone applies the pressure today. The infrastructure is the coercion. The filing system is the threat. You do not need to punish anyone. You just need them to know the file exists."

Marsh did not argue. She did not concede. Her face was careful, listening. Not ready to accept or reject. She looked at the families in the alcove, at the children eating Oona's grubs, at the walls where the voss growth pulsed faintly through root and stone.

"Both things can be true," Marsh said. "Care and control. At the same time. In the same system."

"Both things are always true," Hart said. "That is what makes it difficult."

A frog passed them in the corridor, older, her skin a deep rust-brown mottled with age spots, her gait unhurried. She had been listening. Hart had not noticed her approach, which meant either the frog moved quietly or Hart had been too focused on the conversation. Probably both.

The older frog stopped. Her look at Talla carried the particular patience of a neighbor who had been overhearing these arguments for years.

"Talla, the chain also held when the water rose last cycle. My children are alive because of the chain."

Talla said nothing. Her spots flared and she let them. The older frog did not wait for a response. She continued down the corridor toward the family alcoves, carrying a bundle of dried moss under one forelimb.

The silence between the three of them held for five seconds. Six. The hum of the voss growth filled the space that words would have occupied.

Hart did not break it. There was nothing to break it with. The older frog was not wrong. The chain had held. Her children were alive. The breathing circles worked. The displacement operations saved the settlement every generation. The system that tracked attendance and relocated families and ran on the labor of four maintenance teams instead of six also kept two hundred million years of civilization from being swallowed by its own energy source.

Talla resumed walking. Hart and Marsh followed.

They walked back through the narrowing corridors, past the maintenance crew still arguing about retardant application, past the attendance station where the logging frog was making another notation, past the family alcoves where displaced frogs were eating and talking and living with the compressed patience of people waiting for a temporary situation to end.

Hart said nothing for the rest of the walk. She was composing.

The debrief happened that evening in the biologists' quarters. The stone floor was cool. The water channels hummed their low, constant note beneath the walls. Hart sat on the root ledge she had claimed as hers on the second day, her field notebook open, her sample kit beside her. The voss scraping and retardant paste sat in sealed containers. She did not know what she would do with them. She had taken them because taking samples was what she did when she did not know what else to do.

Reed was on the floor, cross-legged, his glasses pushed up, his notebook open to a page covered in diagrams that appeared to involve the voss growth rate and three different mathematical models. Vale sat against the far wall, his long frame folded into the low space with angular discomfort. He had been designed for lecture halls, not root chambers. Marsh was at the stone table, her own notebook closed, her pencil behind her ear.

Quill was in the doorway.

Hart told them. She started with the maintenance crew and the understaffing. She described the voss growth rate, two centimeters in four days along the outer corridors, faster near water. She described the relocated families, the unchanged food distribution, the stress patterns visible in every adult's skin. She described the attendance station and the logging frog and his flat, unremarkable compliance.

"The breathing circles are tracked," she said. "Participation is recorded. Non-participants are noted. The system is voluntary the way tax filing is voluntary. Nobody will arrest you. Everyone will know."

Vale nodded. His eyes were narrow and his mouth was set in the thin line that meant he had already arrived at the conclusion and was waiting for everyone else to catch up.

"And Talla?" Reed asked. "What does she propose instead?"

"She does not have an alternative. That is the problem. The Pruning requires the merging. The merging requires the circles. The circles require participation. She cannot oppose the mechanism without opposing the survival tool. She knows this." Hart closed her notebook. "The frogs are not lying about the Pruning. But they are using it. And the people who see that most clearly are the ones with the least power to do anything about it."

Vale's nod was small. He did not add anything. He did not need to. Hart saw it in his face: recognition. He had been mapping this structure since the first Speaking Chambers session. She had just walked through it.

Reed looked at the voss sample in Hart's kit. His discomfort was visible in the way he adjusted his glasses, pushed them up, adjusted them again. The cracks complicated his scientific admiration, and he was not ready to let the admiration crack.

Marsh said nothing. She was looking at her closed notebook. Hart suspected she was thinking about the children eating Oona's grubs in the alcove, about the care that existed alongside the control, about both things being true at the same time.

Quill looked out through the doorway at the bioluminescent pathways beyond.

He did not speak.

The fungal light pulsed its slow rhythm on the corridor walls. The water channels carried their quiet sound through the stone. Somewhere deeper in the settlement, a breathing circle was beginning, and somewhere on the outer edges, a maintenance frog was reapplying retardant to a wall that would need it again in six days, and somewhere in a recessed alcove, a mild green frog was recording who attended and who did not, and he was not concerned about what was done with the records, because that was not his job, and he was right, and that was the problem.

## Chapter Intent
This chapter uses Hart's clinical eye to walk the settlement's edges and find what the center hides: understaffed maintenance crews, encroaching voss growth, relocated families in unchanged conditions, and a breathing circle attendance station run by a frog whose bland compliance is more unsettling than any overt coercion. The chapter's discipline is in what it does not do: it does not make Talla a simple truth-teller (she admits she has no clean alternative), it does not make Hart morally superior (she sees the cracks but has no better system), and it does not make the frog system obviously wrong (the older frog's children are alive because of the chain). Three perspectives on coercion converge without resolving: Hart's systems analysis, Talla's lived experience, and Marsh's genuine suggestion that care and control can coexist. The logging frog is the chapter's most important figure precisely because he is the least remarkable, a mild functionary performing a task whose consequences belong to someone else. The debrief ends on Quill's silence, which carries the accumulated weight of philosophy (Ch 11), mourning (Ch 12), and now political critique (Ch 13), and which the narration does not explain because explaining it would reduce it. The chapter earns its title by making Hart's philosophical angle literal: every elegant system leaks, and the leaks are structural, and the people who see them most clearly are the ones least positioned to fix them.
