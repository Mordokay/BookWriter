# Chapter 3: Teeth

## Chapter Intent

Wolf encounter -- confront Ada's chronic fear and hypervigilance through their mirror image. Demonstrate that yielding (not fighting) releases the wolf. Introduce the frog as active guide (2nd appearance) who leads Ada from the Tangle to the ravine threshold. End at the ravine's edge with threshold energy. Backstory fragment: childhood hallway, listening for footsteps. Rain begins.

## Draft

A branch caught the shoulder strap of her backpack and held. Ada twisted against it, felt the nylon stretch and the branch flex, and for two full seconds she was pinned between the pack's weight pulling backward and her own momentum pulling forward. She wrenched sideways. The branch snapped. The crack was loud enough to scatter something small and unseen in the undergrowth to her left, a quick rustling through fern and then silence.

She kept moving.

The Tangle did not thin. If anything it had thickened since she chose her direction at the end of the swarm's dispersal: the salal pressing close on both sides, stems waxy and unyielding, each one requiring her forearm to push through. Her forearms burned. The devil's club spines had bloomed into a continuous itch, swollen beneath the skin, pulsing with her heartbeat. Every time a salal branch dragged across the scratches on her left arm the itch flared into something sharper, a line of heat that made her clench her jaw and push harder into the gap. The right hand was worse. The cluster of spines between her thumb and index finger had tightened the skin into a hot ridge, and gripping anything, a branch for balance, a root to haul herself over deadfall, sent a sharp jab up through the web of her hand and into her wrist.

Her boots found soft ground and sank. Cold mud, ankle-deep, the kind that gripped the sole and held. She pulled one foot free with a sucking sound and placed it on what looked like firmer ground, a root crossing, and the root was slick with condensation and her boot slid sideways. Her knee caught the edge of a standing dead tree. The bark was soft with rot and crumbled on impact, leaving a wet smear on her jeans. She steadied herself against the trunk. Bracket fungi grew from it in high shelving tiers, their undersides pale in the glow of small green-capped fungi, layered one atop the other like stairs built for something that climbed without feet.

The clinical voice had gone quiet. Not silent; it was still there, a radio left on in another room, audible if she listened for it but no longer offering anything she could use. It had given its last instruction at the end of the swarm: pick a direction. She had picked one. The instruction was exhausted. What remained was the body's own navigation: the arms pushing through, the legs finding purchase, the lungs pulling air in steady draws that fogged in the bioluminescent light and drifted ahead of her. Keep moving. The imperative had dropped from the voice to the muscles, from language to repetition, from strategy to stubbornness.

Deadfall blocked the path. A Douglas fir, chest-high, bark furrowed deep. She climbed it like the last one: hands finding the top edge, forearms scraping, one boot jammed into a crack. Her jacket caught on a knot of wood. She pulled until the fabric groaned and freed itself and dropped down the far side into more mud, colder this time, the cold traveling through the denim at her already-muddied knees.

The rain arrived without announcement.

Not wind-driven, not a downpour. A thin cold drizzle that came through the canopy in intermittent drops, each one a small shock on her face, her neck, the backs of her hands. The canopy was nearly sealed above her. She could not see it, only feel the occasional drop that found a gap and fell the full distance to land on her with the precision of something aimed. The drops accumulated on the salal leaves around her, beading on the waxy surfaces, running together into larger drops that slid to the leaf tips and hung there, catching the faint bioluminescent light before letting go. The rain had no sound of its own against the dense canopy, it was too light, too scattered, but the dripping from the leaves had a rhythm now that it hadn't before, each drop landing on the next leaf down in a slow percussion that spread through the understory like a pulse.

Her hair was damp within minutes. The cold sharpened. The temperature had been dropping since full dark and the drizzle accelerated the work, the water finding the gap between her collar and her neck and tracing a line along her spine, cold and slow. Her hands were wet. The mud under her boots was softening further, each step leaving prints that filled with water before she could take the next.

She pushed through a curtain of old-man's-beard lichen that hung from a bigleaf maple and caught across her face like cobweb. The lichen was wet, fibrous, and it stuck to her lips and eyelashes. She wiped it away with the back of her hand and the motion dragged the swollen ridge of spines across her mouth. The taste was forest: wet bark, mineral earth, the faint metallic sweetness of spores.

A gap in the undergrowth ahead. A thinning of the salal, the deadfall opening into a space where the ground was flat and the ferns grew lower. She pushed toward it, boots squelching, arms held tight against her body to protect the scratches. Ten steps. Eight. The gap widened into a small clearing, not wide, perhaps twenty feet across, the floor carpeted in sword fern and stair-step moss, the surrounding trees leaning inward like the walls of a room that had been built slightly too small.

She stepped into it. The rain fell more freely here. The canopy had thinned enough to let the drizzle through in a fine, steady mist. Clusters of small glowing mushrooms clung to two fallen branches at the clearing's edge, their pale green light barely reaching the center. The darkness between the trees was total. Impenetrable. Darkness that had texture and weight, that pressed against the edges of the small lit space like something leaning in to look.

The forest went silent.

Not the silence of quiet. The silence of absence. The insect chorus, that layered pulse of clicks and whines and drones that had been the night's constant underscore since the first hour, stopped. All of it. At once. The dripping continued for a few seconds longer and then even that thinned, the drops spacing further apart until the clearing held nothing but the sound of Ada's own breathing and the faint wet creak of a branch adjusting overhead.

Her body knew before her mind did.

The muscles in her back drew tight, pulling her shoulders up and in. Her fingers curled toward her palms. Her weight shifted to the balls of her feet, an old, automatic adjustment, the body distributing itself for speed or impact, whichever came first. Her eyes moved across the clearing's perimeter. Left to right, then back. Scanning. The scanning she had done since she was seven years old, walking down the hallway at home, reading the quality of the silence before she reached the living room. Listening for footsteps. Gauging the weight of a closed door. Calculating what waited on the other side by the density of the quiet.

The hallway flickered and was gone, not a memory she had invited but a sensation, the specific feeling of bare feet on cold floorboards, the smell of carpet cleaner and something older underneath. It passed through her and left. The clearing was the clearing. The darkness between the trees was the darkness between the trees.

Something was in it.

At the clearing's northern edge, where the sword ferns met the dense black wall of the Tangle, the darkness had a shape. A shape she could feel rather than draw, like air pressure dropping before a storm, like a room shifting when someone enters it from behind. The shape was low, wide across the shoulders, and still. Absolutely still. Stiller than the trees. Stiller than the moss. The kind of stillness that was not the absence of movement but the suppression of it: everything coiled, everything held.

Two points of light.

Amber. Pale. Set wide apart at the height of her waist, unblinking, fixed on her with an attention that had no curiosity in it. The eyes reflected the fungal glow the way an animal's eyes reflect headlights on a back road, except these did not flash and vanish. They held. They burned steadily, twin points of cold light in the black mass of the body they belonged to.

The wolf stepped into the clearing.

It materialized from shadow in a way that made the boundary between shadow and flesh uncertain. One moment the darkness had a shape. The next the shape had a body: gray-brown fur over a frame that was wrong. Too large. The shoulders stood higher than her hip. The legs were too long, the proportions distorted in a way she could not have articulated but which her body registered as an error, a departure from the photographs and documentary footage she had filed in the rational part of her mind. Under the pale green light the wolf's coat rippled, the edges of its body softening and re-solidifying as it moved, as if the animal were not entirely committed to being solid. The muzzle was long, dark, wet at the nostrils. The ears were forward. Those eyes did not leave her.

It did not charge.

It circled.

The first arc was wide, twelve feet, maybe fifteen, skirting the clearing's perimeter with a fluid economy that left no wasted motion. Each paw placement was deliberate, soundless, the pads compressing the moss without registering. Ada turned to track it, her boots grinding in the soft earth, locked in the instinctive rotation of prey following predator. The wolf completed the arc. Paused. Changed direction and began again, this time two feet closer.

She backed into the nearest tree. Bark against her spine, rough through the jacket. The tree was solid behind her, one direction she did not have to watch. Her hands found the bark and pressed flat against it, and the pressing steadied her, a fixed point in the slow geometry of the wolf's orbit.

Hold eye contact. A rule from a survival manual, filed away, now arriving with the force of an instruction from outside herself. She locked her gaze on the wolf's eyes. The wolf met the lock and did not flinch. Its eyes were not hostile. They were not predatory in any way she had expected predatory to look. They were patient. Intelligent. The intelligence was the worst part. Not calculation; recognition. The wolf looked at her... no, through her, the look she caught in her own mirror on the bad mornings. With knowledge. With something that in a human she would have called exhaustion.

She broke eye contact. Another instruction: don't look them in the eyes. Make yourself big. She raised her arms, the jacket spreading, and the movement cost her: the devil's club scratches lit up along both forearms, the spines dragging against the inner lining of her sleeves. The wolf paused its circling. It regarded her with her arms raised, her body pressed against the tree, her chest heaving, and the regard held no fear and no aggression and nothing that her response catalog could match. It circled again. Closer. Ten feet.

She edged sideways along the tree's trunk, bark scraping her back. The wolf adjusted. Its arc tightened. She moved right; the wolf drifted right. She stopped; the wolf stopped. The choreography was precise, and she was not leading it.

Stand your ground. Don't run. She stood. Her boots rooted in the mud, her hands behind her gripping bark. The wolf circled.

Make noise. She opened her mouth and nothing came. The fear had settled in her throat, a physical obstruction that compressed the airway and turned breath into something she had to force through a narrowing passage. A sound came out, not a shout, not a word. A thin, pressed vocalization that died between her teeth.

The wolf's circuit had brought it to within eight feet. Close enough that the bioluminescent light caught individual details: guard hairs tipped silver, the muscular ridge of the shoulder, a scar or fold of skin above the left eye. The smell reached her: musky, acrid, layered over something older, something like hot stone and rain-soaked earth. The scent filled the clearing and replaced the forest's fungal sweetness with the sharp fact of a large animal in close proximity.

A growl.

Not heard. Felt. The sound began below the frequency of hearing, a vibration that entered through her sternum and spread outward through her ribs and into her spine where it pressed against the tree. The tree carried the vibration, or the ground did, or Ada's body was tuned to a register it had always been capable of receiving but had never had reason to until now. The growl carried neither warning nor threat; it was the bass note of something massive and alive, standing close in the dark, and the note insisted on presence, on duration, on the immovable fact of something that had been here long before she arrived.

"You've been running your whole life."

The wolf's jaw did not move. The muzzle stayed closed, wet nostrils flaring with each exhale, breath steaming in faint clouds that caught the pale green light and dissolved. But the words arrived, not through her ears but in the space between the growl's vibration and her own recognition, in the gap where sound becomes meaning and how it got there stops mattering. The words were low, toneless, flat as a struck stone.

Ada pressed harder against the tree. The bark bit into her shoulder blades.

The wolf circled. Seven feet. The clearing was shrinking. Not literally, the trees had not moved, the ferns had not closed in, but the space available to her was contracting with every orbit, the wolf's path tightening like a spiral drawn by a hand that never lifts, each loop bringing the pale yellow eyes and the dark muzzle and the impossible wrong-proportioned body closer. She tried to think. The thinking produced nothing. Every survival strategy she had ever read or been told or absorbed through cultural osmosis had been deployed, eye contact, no eye contact, making herself large, standing ground, making noise, and each one had landed against the wolf's patience like a fist against water. The wolf absorbed every tactic and continued.

Her legs were trembling. The quadriceps first, the same fine involuntary vibration that had started after the swarm, except now it was deeper, originating not in muscle fatigue but in something more fundamental: the nervous system running a current it could not discharge, the voltage of sustained fear with no outlet. Her hands shook against the bark. The rain continued its thin cold drizzle on her face and the water ran into her eyes and she could not wipe it because she could not take her hands from the tree because the tree was the last solid thing.

"What are you bracing for?"

The words assembled in the clearing like the pale green light: sourceless, pervasive, belonging to no single point. The wolf was six feet away. Five. Its pace had slowed. It was no longer circling in the sense of traveling; it was circling in the sense of tightening, a knot pulling closed, each pass drawing the available space smaller. Ada could see the individual whiskers along the muzzle. The steam of its breath reached her before dissipating, hot, damp, carrying the animal musk so close that it coated the back of her throat.

She tried to slide around the tree. Her boots skidded in the mud. One ankle caught a root and she stumbled, her hand leaving the bark, and for a half-second she was unsupported in the clearing with the wolf between her and every direction. The wolf stopped. It stood in the path she had been trying to take. Gold eyes level with hers. Close enough to read the texture of the iris: not uniform gold but layered, the color of old sap over darker rings. Under the psilocybin the patterns in those irises shifted, reorganized, formed concentric patterns that held and released and held again.

She found the tree again. Pressed back against it. The bark was wet now, the rain having soaked through the moss that grew on the trunk, and the moisture seeped through her jacket at the shoulders, cold against skin already cold.

The wolf had stopped circling. It stood in the center of the clearing, five feet from her, and regarded her with an expression that she could not name because her vocabulary for animal expressions had been built from a lifetime of human projection and this was not that. This was something stripped of projection. The wolf's face held nothing she could argue with, nothing she could interpret away. It was the face of the thing she had been scanning for since childhood. The threat behind the quiet door. The danger she had trained her entire nervous system to detect, to anticipate, to brace against.

And here it was. Standing in front of her. Patient. Enormous. Real in a way that made every other fear she had ever managed feel like rehearsal.

Her hands were fists against the bark. Her jaw ached from the clenching she could not release. The rain fell on her upturned face and ran down her cheeks in cold lines. Her breath was shallow, rapid, inadequate, each inhale catching at the top, never filling the lungs, each insufficient breath a signal to the body that the danger was still here, still here, still...

The wolf took a step toward her.

The distance between them compressed. Four feet. The wolf's head was at the level of her chest. Its breath reached her in rhythmic pulses, hot and damp against her collarbones, each exhale visible as a thin cloud that briefly merged with her own. The musk was overwhelming now, not unpleasant exactly, but absolute, a presence filling every sense until no sense had room for anything else. The taste coated her tongue. The pressure lay against her skin. The wolf's body blocked the fungi's light from the fallen branches behind it, and its silhouette was enormous, the shoulders and skull and ears cutting a shape from the darkness that was darker than the dark.

Time had stopped working.

Not the loose-edged slipping of duration since the onset. Something different. A contraction. Each second was a room she entered and could not leave, the wolf filling every corner of it. She stood inside the moment like she stood against the tree: pressed, small, with nowhere left to retreat.

The wolf's eyes held hers. The amber glow was steady. The rain fell between them, each drop catching the light as it passed through the five feet of charged air.

Her legs gave.

Not a decision. The muscles simply stopped holding. The quadriceps released their contraction and her knees folded and she went down, hands finding the ground, palms pressing into cold mud and wet moss and the tiny stems of plants she was crushing without knowing their names. The impact was soft. The forest floor absorbed her weight like it absorbed everything, and then she was kneeling in the mud in the rain with her head bowed and her hands in the earth and the wolf standing above her.

The position was depletion, nothing more. The body had burned through every chemical resource that kept it upright and braced, and when the resources were gone the architecture they supported simply folded. She knelt the way a building settles: not all at once but joint by joint, system by system, the engineering of vigilance collapsing from the feet up.

The ground was cold and wet under her palms. The mud pushed between her fingers. The sword fern fronds brushed her wrists. The rain fell on the back of her neck and ran into the collar of her jacket. The devil's club spines pulsed their slow swollen burn along her forearms, hot against the cold.

The wolf approached.

The footsteps were audible now. Not soundless: the soft compression of moss under weight, the faint wet give of mud. Close. Closer. The sound stopped and the wolf's breath was on the back of her neck, hot and rhythmic, each exhale ruffling the wet hairs at her nape. She could have reached behind her and touched the muzzle. She could have felt the coarse fur, the wet nose, the warmth of the mouth. She did not move. She knelt in the mud and breathed and the wolf stood over her and breathed and for a span of time that had no measurement both of them existed in the same small circle of rain and darkness and exhalation.

The hallway again. The floorboards. Bare feet. The specific quality of silence that meant something was about to happen: not noise, not a door slamming, just the atmospheric pressure change of a mood shifting in another room. The child scanning without moving. The child making herself small, making herself still, making herself a shape that a bad moment might pass over without noticing. The vigilance that had started there. The scanning that had never stopped.

The wolf's breath was warm on her skin. It did not feel like threat. It felt like proximity. The proximity of something too large and too old to lie about what it was.

"I'm tired too."

The words arrived as the others had: without a mouth, without a source, assembling in the space between the wolf's exhalation and Ada's recognition. Three words. Low, toneless, and carrying a weight that was not hers alone.

The wolf lowered its head. The motion was slow, the great skull descending until the muzzle nearly touched the earth beside Ada's hands. Not submission. Not dominance. Something she had no frame for. The eyes, close enough now that the individual hairs of the brow were visible, held a quality she could only call recognition: the thing that looked back at her was as exhausted by the vigil as she was.

Then the wolf turned.

It did not hurry. It walked across the clearing with the same easy grace it had used to circle, each step measured, and at the edge where the sword ferns met the wall of the Tangle it paused. The amber eyes reflected the pale mushroom glow one final time, twin points of pale light, and then the wolf moved into the undergrowth and was absorbed by it, fur becoming shadow becoming darkness, and the forest took the wolf back and sealed the gap behind it as if nothing had been standing there at all.

The silence held.

Ada stayed on her knees. Her hands were in the mud. The rain fell on the back of her neck. Her breath came in ragged, wet draws that she could hear because the forest was still withholding its usual sound: no insects, no dripping, no creaking branches. Just her. Just the air going in and coming out and the rain collecting on her bowed head and running off.

The trembling started in her shoulders and worked downward. A whole-body shaking, not the scattered muscle spasms of adrenaline withdrawal but a full-body vibration, every cell releasing a charge it had been holding, the voltage draining all at once. Her arms shook. Her thighs. Her jaw. The shaking felt involuntary and necessary, the body processing something the mind had no mechanism for. She let it happen because she did not have the resources to stop it.

Her hands were still in the mud. Fists. Both closed, knuckles pressing into the cold earth, tendons standing rigid along the backs of her wrists. She looked at them. In the green glow they were pale and streaked with mud and scored with the thin red lines of devil's club scratches and they were clenched so tight the fingernails were cutting into her palms. She looked at them the way she would look at someone else's hands. Hands doing what they had always done.

Something had happened that she could not think about yet. The wolf was gone and the clearing was empty and she was alive and uninjured, or no more injured than she had been before, and the fear was still in her but the pitch of it had dropped. Not gone. Dropped: a sustained note falling a half-tone when the player's breath runs thin. The hypervigilance that had been a constant, cycling hum beneath every other sensation since she walked into the forest, since before the forest, since before the car, since before the planning and the research and the decision, that hum had decreased by a fraction. Not enough to call calm. Enough to notice. Like a sound she had been hearing so long she forgot it was a sound, and now she could hear its edge, could hear where the sound ended and something quieter began.

The wolf had known her cadence. Not her thoughts; the swarm had been her thoughts. The wolf had known the rhythm of her body, the frequency her muscles hummed at, how she braced against a room before she entered it and against a silence before she heard what followed. The wolf had circled her as she circled every space she occupied, scanning, testing, looking for the threat. And when she stopped, not because she chose to, because the body could not sustain the vigil, the wolf had stopped too. Had lowered its massive head. Had spoken three words that were not about her but about itself, or about the space between them, or about the fact that vigilance exhausted everything it touched.

She did not understand it. She held the shape of it without understanding, as her open palms had held the mud after the swarm without understanding. Her body was collecting lessons her mind could not yet read.

The sounds returned.

First the dripping. Rain through the canopy, leaf to leaf to ground, the percussion rebuilding one drop at a time. Then the insects, thin, tentative, a single click from the dark too brief to identify, testing the air before committing. Then more. The layered chorus rebuilding itself from component parts, each species reentering at its own pace, the soundscape of the forest knitting back together around her like tissue closing over a wound. The chorus was thinner than before. The composition had changed: fewer layers, or quieter layers, the forest's ambient life not yet fully recovered from whatever the wolf's presence had suppressed.

Ada leaned back on her heels. The mud released her hands with a soft sucking sound. She wiped them on her jeans, but the denim was already so darkened with mud and rain that the motion accomplished nothing, just redistributed the grime. Her forearms burned. The rain had found the scratches and the water in the scratches felt like a thin acid, each drop reigniting the itch the spines had planted.

Her knees ached when she shifted her weight. The cold had settled deep into the joints during the kneeling, and the ache was the heavy, grinding kind that would worsen if she stayed still. She placed one foot flat on the ground. Pushed. The quadricep shook but held, and she rose to standing with the slow, deliberate caution of someone who was not sure the ground would take her weight.

The clearing was empty. The ferns swayed in the thin rain. The glowing mushrooms pulsed at the edges, green and patient. The wolf's path had left no tracks in the moss.

A croak.

Sharp, clear, singular. It cut through the rebuilt chorus and the rain's soft percussion like a stone dropping into still water, cleanly, leaving a silence behind it that was not absence but attention. Ada's head turned toward the sound.

The frog was sitting on a stone at the far edge of the clearing, where the sword ferns parted and a gap in the undergrowth was visible, narrow, but navigable, a seam in the Tangle's wall. The stone was half-buried in moss, and the frog sat on its crown with the settled weight Ada remembered from the nurse log. Mottled olive-brown and gold. Warty skin catching the bioluminescent light in a pattern of tiny raised shadows. A pale cream stripe running the length of its back. Gold eyes with horizontal pupils, watching her. Not with the wolf's exhausted patience. With something simpler. Direction.

It hopped from the stone. One careful, unhurried hop into the gap. Paused. Half-turned on the moss, one gold eye still visible.

Ada followed.

The decision was not a decision. Her body moved as it had during the wolf's circling: automatically, in response to a force that preceded thought. One boot in front of the other. Into the gap. The frog hopped again. Two feet forward. Paused.

She followed.

The gap in the Tangle was not a path. There were no cleared edges, no worn ground, no evidence that anything had passed through before. But the undergrowth had arranged itself with just enough space: a salal stem bent this way rather than that, a sword fern frond angled to leave a corridor at hip height, a deadfall branch broken at a joint that created a step. Nothing obvious, nothing engineered, but navigable. A gap that existed only if you were looking for it, and she had not been looking. The frog had.

It moved with the steady, measured gait of its kind, walking rather than leaping, placing each wide foot with an intention that had nothing to do with speed. Ada matched the pace. The soft green glow of the fungi followed them along the fallen branches on either side, pale green light pulsing in its slow rhythm, and the frog's warty skin caught the glow and held it in the depressions between warts, so the frog itself seemed to carry small pools of green light on its back.

"This way."

Ada stopped. The frog sat two feet ahead of her on a raised root, its gold eye turned toward her, throat pouch deflating from the croak it had released a moment before. A beat of silence. Then the words, low, unhurried, arriving simply, as the wolf's had, though the quality was nothing alike. Flat in a way that was not tonelessness but simplicity, language stripped to its barest function.

The frog hopped forward. Ada followed.

They moved through the Tangle for a span of time she could not measure. The rain continued, lighter now, a fine mist that coated every surface without pooling. The gap never widened into a path and never narrowed into a dead end. It remained exactly navigable: tight in places, requiring her to turn sideways or duck beneath a low-hanging hemlock bough, but always passable. The frog never hesitated. It knew where the gap went. Or the gap followed the frog. The distinction felt irrelevant.

The undergrowth began to change. The salal thinned. The devil's club disappeared; its absence hit her like the sudden silence after a noise you forgot you were bracing against, a sudden lack of the specific tension that came from knowing spines were close. Red huckleberry replaced it, the bare winter stems ghostly in the bioluminescent light. The ground sloped downward. Not steeply, but enough that her weight shifted to her toes, and the shift told her something the darkness could not: she was descending.

The air changed. Cooler, moving now, a faint draft from below, carrying the mineral smell of cold water over stone. The insect chorus thinned as the temperature dropped with the descent. The luminous mushroom clusters grew denser along the downward slope, their pale green glow pooling in the low ground, as if the light itself were running downhill.

The frog stopped.

It sat on a flat stone at the edge of a sudden absence. The undergrowth ended. The ground ended. Ada's boots found the lip of a ravine and she leaned forward, hands gripping a cedar root that grew along the edge.

Below, how far below she could not tell in the dark, but the slope was steep and the sound of running water rose from the bottom, loud enough that it filled the space the insect chorus had left. Cold, fast water over stone. The ravine walls were dark and slick with moss, and rare moonlight, the first open sky she had seen since the Tangle closed around her, angled down through a break in the canopy above the ravine and caught the stream at the bottom in a ribbon of silver that moved and flashed and disappeared around a bend. The moon was lower than it had been. Shifted in its arc. The light fell at an angle that painted one wall of the ravine in faint silver and left the other in blackness.

The sound of the water was large. Not a trickle; a stream with weight and speed, pushing through the ravine's narrow channel. The sound entered her body through her feet and her hands where they gripped the root, a vibration that was both the water's and the earth's, transmitted through the rock and the root system and the dark moss-covered walls and arriving in her bones as a low, continuous hum.

Beneath the stream's sound, beneath the vibration of water on stone, there was something else. Barely there. A resonance so low she could not hear it, only feel it: a tremor in the ground beneath the cedar root, fainter than the stream, steadier, the earth itself humming at a frequency just below perception. It registered not in her ears but in her kneecaps, in the palms of her hands, in the loosened joints of her wrists. She did not notice it. It noticed her, or it had always been there and she had walked into its range, and it continued beneath everything else, a foundation beneath a house: unnoticed, load-bearing, old.

She turned to look at the frog.

The stone was bare. The moss where the frog had been sitting was compressed, a shallow depression dark with moisture. The gold eyes, the warty skin, the pale stripe down its back. Gone. The forest had taken the frog back as it had taken the wolf, silently and completely, leaving only the impression of weight on soft ground.

Ada stood at the edge of the ravine. Below, the stream ran silver and black in the rare moonlight, and the sound of it rose to meet her: not asking, not welcoming, not hostile. The sound of water doing what water does, which is to move downhill through whatever stands in the way.

Behind her, the Tangle. The wolf's clearing somewhere in the labyrinth she had already passed through. The swarm's territory. The disappeared trail. Everything she had already survived, which was another way of saying everything she could not go back to.

Below, the ravine. The descent she had not chosen. The water she could hear but not yet reach. A crossing she would have to make alone, because the frog had brought her here and the frog was gone and the threshold was hers.

The rain fell on her face. The stream roared in the darkness below. The faint hum beneath the water's sound continued, patient and old and unaware of her standing above it, at the edge.
