# Chapter 3: First Contact

Reed found it first, which he would mention later, and often.

The formation sat at the base of a strangler fig where the ground had cracked open along a seam that ran roughly north-south for about four meters. It looked geological. Most things in the Amazon looked geological if you squinted. But Reed was not squinting. He was on his knees with his face six inches from the rock, his glasses fogged, his headlamp illuminating a surface that should not have been smooth.

"This is not erosion," he said. "This is not water-carved, this is not wind-worn, this is not any process I can identify that would produce a surface this uniform at this depth in this substrate. The mineral composition alone is, wait, wait, wait." He ran his fingers along the seam. "Feel this."

Nobody moved to feel it.

"The edges are beveled. Beveled. Not fractured. Something shaped this."

"Something shaped a rock," Vale said. He was standing four meters back with his arms folded, which was his default position for receiving information he considered premature. "In the Amazon. Where geological processes have been shaping rocks for approximately four billion years."

"Not like this."

"Reed, everything you have shown me for the past three days has been 'not like this.' The roots are not like this. The soil is not like this. The mineral composition is not like this. At some point, the simplest explanation is that your model of 'this' is wrong."

"My model is not wrong. My model is incomplete. There is a difference."

"The difference is funding."

Hart was crouched near the base of the seam, running a strip of litmus paper along the rock face. She sniffed the paper, frowned, and sniffed the air itself. "There's something in the air here. Organic. Not decomposition. Not floral. Something I haven't smelled before." She paused. "Actually, that's not true. It's the copper smell. Stronger. Much stronger. And something underneath it. Sweet, almost. Like overripe fruit stored in a mineral cave."

"Fermentation," Marsh said.

Everyone looked at her. She was standing slightly apart from the group, her field notebook in her left hand, her pencil behind her ear. She had not written anything down since they arrived at the formation. She was looking at the ground.

"What?" Hart said.

"It smells like fermentation. Not rot. Fermentation. Something is being broken down on purpose."

"By what?"

"I don't know." Marsh looked up. "I know what fermentation smells like. That's what this is. Something underground is fermenting."

"Lovely," Vale said. "Underground fermentation. Add it to the list."

Reed was already moving along the seam, tracing it with his fingers. His headlamp caught something and he stopped. "There's an opening."

This produced a silence of the variety that occurs when five people simultaneously recognize that a situation has changed category. Reed was pointing his headlamp into a gap in the rock, perhaps a meter wide at the base, narrowing upward into the root lattice. Beyond the light's reach, the gap continued downward.

"That's a passage," Reed said.

"That's a hole," Hart said.

"It's a passage. Look at the walls. Smooth. Beveled. The same surface treatment as the seam. Something made this."

"Something made a hole in the ground," Vale said. "Perhaps we should call National Geographic."

"We should go down," Reed said.

This initiated a conversation that Marsh would later describe, in her field notebook, as "the fastest argument in the history of scientific fieldwork." It lasted perhaps forty-five seconds. Marsh timed it because she was curious and because nobody else would think to.

Vale said yes immediately, which surprised everyone including Vale. He phrased it as a command rather than an opinion. "We go down. We document everything. If this is what Reed thinks it is, someone else will find it eventually, and I want our names on the data before that happens."

Hart said: "No. Absolutely not. We have no idea what the air quality is below the surface. We have no gas detection equipment rated for confined spaces. We do not know the structural integrity of that passage. If someone falls and breaks a leg down there, the nearest hospital is a two-day boat ride and a helicopter. Nobody goes anywhere until I can confirm we won't asphyxiate."

Reed said nothing because Reed was already three steps into the passage, his headlamp bobbing downward.

"Reed!" Hart said.

"The air is fine," Reed called back, his voice already acquiring the flat quality of enclosed spaces. "Better than fine. It's cool. There's a breeze. A breeze means ventilation, which means another opening."

"A breeze means a pressure differential, which means nothing about toxicity."

Quill was standing at the edge of the formation with his hands at his sides. He looked at the passage, then at Marsh, then at his own feet. His mouth opened and closed. He did not speak.

Marsh put her pencil back behind her ear and closed her notebook. "We're scientists," she said. "There's a hole in the ground that shouldn't exist, and it smells like an underground brewery. The question answers itself."

She walked into the passage.

Vale followed. Hart said something in French that Marsh did not catch and followed. Quill stood for another few seconds, his hands opening and closing at his sides, and then he followed too, which left the forest empty and indifferent above them.

The passage went down at perhaps thirty degrees, steep enough that Marsh had to brace her hand against the wall. The stone was cool and smooth under her palm. Too smooth. Not polished, exactly, but worn, the way a banister gets worn by a century of hands. Except these grooves were small. Narrow. Spaced close together, running in parallel tracks along the lower wall at about ankle height.

Something had walked this path. Something small, walking the same route, over and over, for a very long time.

The air changed as they descended. The temperature dropped by what felt like ten degrees in perhaps twenty meters. Underneath the cool: wet stone, mineral water, the fermentation smell Marsh had identified, and something green and sharp she could not name. Her skin prickled.

"The air is cooler here than the surface," Reed said from ahead, as if this were not obvious to everyone currently experiencing it.

"Thank you, Reed," Vale said.

"Which means temperature regulation. Active temperature regulation. Something is managing the airflow in this system."

Hart was running her hand along the wall. "There's moisture on these surfaces. Condensation, maybe. Or something excreted." She brought her fingers to her nose. "Copper. Faint, but it's there."

"Don't taste it," Marsh said.

"I was not going to taste it."

"You were moving your fingers toward your mouth."

"I was bringing them to my nose. There is a six-centimeter difference that I would think a field biologist could distinguish."

The glow began gradually. Marsh almost missed it. A faint blue-green glow in the walls, so dim at first that it might have been an afterimage from Reed's headlamp. But it persisted. And it spread. Within another ten meters of descent, the walls on both sides were producing their own light, a soft fungal glow embedded in the root structures that lined the passage. The light shifted with the air currents, brightening and dimming in slow pulses.

Reed turned off his headlamp. The blue-green light was enough to see by, barely. It turned their faces into something from a painting nobody would buy.

"That is bioluminescence," Reed said. "In the walls. Generated by a fungal network integrated into the root system. The energy requirements for sustained luminescence at this level would be, actually, I don't know what they would be, because this shouldn't be possible at this scale without."

"Reed," Vale said.

"Right. Yes. Moving."

Marsh stopped. Ahead, she could hear Reed's voice continuing its monologue and the others' footsteps on stone. But she was looking at the floor of the passage.

A channel ran along the left wall, perhaps ten centimeters wide, carrying a thin stream of water that moved with purpose, not trickling but flowing as though it had somewhere to be. In the water, small objects moved. Regular shapes. Not stones. Things that had been placed in the current to be carried somewhere. They were too small to identify in the dim light, but they were uniform and deliberate and they were floating downstream toward whatever was at the bottom of this passage.

Marsh crouched and watched them pass. Six, seven, eight identical shapes moving in a line. Deliveries.

She stood and kept walking.

The sound reached them before anything else did. A low, constant murmur, like distant conversation heard through walls. Water sounds underneath: flowing, dripping, the hollow echo of liquid moving through stone chambers. And something else. Something tonal. Not the harmonic from the surface. This was higher, more complex, and it came from multiple sources, as if several things were communicating at once in frequencies that sat just at the edge of what Marsh could hear.

The passage opened.

Marsh stepped out of the corridor and stopped, and behind her Vale stopped, and behind him Hart stopped, and for a few seconds nobody said anything because the space they had entered did not match any expectation they had carried into it.

The corridor opened into a junction where several passages met, and the ceiling rose to perhaps four meters, and the walls were lined with roots that had been shaped into something between architecture and gardening. Water channels ran along every surface, converging at a shallow basin in the center of the floor. The blue-green light was brighter here, bright enough that details emerged: the texture of the root walls, the moisture beading on stone surfaces, the worn paths on the floor where something had walked the same routes so many times the stone had grooved.

And in the center of the chamber, arranged in a loose semicircle facing the passage the biologists had just emerged from, seven frogs sat in shallow water and waited.

The largest was perhaps eight centimeters long. Muted green-grey. Very still. One forelimb held slightly inward against its body. Flanking it on either side, two slightly smaller frogs whose skin shifted between shades of brown and grey in slow, controlled patterns. Behind them, four more, their colors less controlled, flickering between curiosity and something that might have been alarm.

The large frog's skin shifted to a steady, muted blue.

"You are the research team from the surface," it said, in English. "We have been discussing you."

The voice was small. Of course it was small. The frog was eight centimeters long. But the words were precise, each consonant placed with care, each vowel given its full value. The accent was not quite anything Marsh could identify. It sounded like someone who had learned English from books written a long time ago and had never had the opportunity to practice with a native speaker. Victorian. Administrative. The voice of a colonial filing clerk from 1890, if the clerk had been a frog.

Five things happened at once.

Reed stepped forward and his glasses caught the blue-green light and his voice came out at a pitch Marsh had never heard from him, somewhere between elation and cardiac arrest. "How. Wait. You speak English? How long have you. The root density, is that yours? The mineral uptake, the branching patterns, we measured seventeen standard deviations, seventeen, and the bioluminescence, is this a cultivated network or a symbiotic, and how are you, how is this, wait, wait, wait."

Vale's arms uncrossed. He stepped to Reed's left and his voice dropped into the register he used when he was talking to someone he suspected of lying. "Who authorized this contact? Who decided what we would be shown? I want to know who is running this."

Hart had not moved from the passage entrance. Her hands were on the straps of her field kit. "Nobody touch anything," she said. "Nobody breathe through your mouth. We don't know what's in this air. We don't know what's on these surfaces. If this is a biologically active environment with species we have never encountered, the contamination risk goes in both directions."

Quill stood very still. His hands were at his sides. His mouth was closed. His eyes moved. He did not speak.

Marsh watched all of this, and filed it, and looked at the frogs. Not at the large one who had spoken. At the others. The four behind the delegation were shifting colors rapidly, patterns she could not read, their small bodies angled toward each other in postures that looked like commentary. One of them, the smallest, flickered a bright orange across its throat and the frog beside it responded with a color Marsh could not name, something between green and gold, and they both turned to look at the biologists with an attention that felt less like scientific curiosity and more like the way a crowd watches a street performer before deciding whether to stay or leave.

The large frog waited through all of this with the patience of something that had learned, over a very long time, exactly how much silence it took for other beings to finish panicking.

"My name is Noll," it said. "I coordinate, loosely, for this settlement. These are my colleagues." A small gesture with the undamaged forelimb toward the flanking frogs. "Your questions are understandable. Many of them have answers. Some do not. We have been monitoring your work since you arrived in this region. Your data, if I may say, has been quite thorough, though your mineral uptake model requires a correction I am sure Dr. Reed will find interesting."

Reed made a sound.

"We had not planned to make contact at this time," Noll continued. "You have been more persistent than we anticipated. The current circumstances make delay impractical. We felt it better to meet than to continue redirecting your path."

"Redirecting," Vale said. The word landed like a dropped tool.

"Your navigation through the forest has been subtly guided for three days. Chemical gradients. Adjusted root structures. Minor atmospheric changes. I regret the discourtesy."

Vale's jaw moved but no sound came out, which was, Marsh thought, probably a first.

"You've been herding us," Hart said.

"Guiding," Noll said. "Herding implies livestock. We intended no disrespect."

"You intended no disrespect while chemically manipulating our movement through a forest without our knowledge or consent."

Noll's blue coloring deepened slightly. "Yes. I can see how that would be objectionable from your perspective."

"From any perspective."

"Perspectives vary more than you might expect."

Behind the delegation, the chamber was filling. Marsh saw it happening in the way she noticed most things, through accumulated detail rather than sudden recognition. More frogs were arriving. Not from the passage the biologists had used but from other corridors, other openings in the root walls that Marsh had not initially seen. Small bodies moving through shallow water, climbing damp stone, settling on root ledges at various heights around the chamber. Some were the muted greens and browns of the delegation. Others showed colors she had not expected on frogs: deep rust, pale gold, a violet so dark it was almost black. Their skin shifted as they settled, colors rippling across their bodies in patterns that looked like conversation.

There were perhaps twenty of them now. Then thirty. A crowd, forming the way crowds form anywhere: a few curious arrivals, then a few more drawn by the presence of the first few, then a general drift toward the spectacle. Two of them, perched on a root shelf to Marsh's right, were murmuring to each other in sounds she could not parse, clicks and low harmonics that traveled through the water channels and resonated faintly in her teeth. One of them made a short, sharp sound and the other responded with a flicker of pale green, and they both leaned forward to get a better look at Hart's field kit.

They were, Marsh realized, rubbernecking.

"How many of you are there?" Reed asked. His voice had steadied slightly. His glasses were still fogged but he did not seem to have noticed.

"In this settlement, approximately twelve hundred," Noll said. "Globally, the number is somewhat larger. I would be happy to discuss demographics at a more suitable time."

"Twelve hundred." Reed's voice climbed back up. "Twelve hundred individuals of an undiscovered intelligent species living in a structured settlement beneath the Amazon rainforest. With bioluminescent infrastructure. And language. And you speak English. You speak English."

"We speak several of your languages. English is among the more practical, if architecturally limited. Four bricks." He paused. "Forgive me. That is an internal reference."

One of the flanking frogs shifted colors briefly. Noll's undamaged forelimb moved, a gesture so small Marsh almost missed it. The flanking frog settled.

"Architecturally limited," Reed repeated.

"Your language encodes meaning in one channel. Sound. We find this, I believe the word is, quaint."

On the root shelf to the right, the two watching frogs exchanged a rapid series of clicks. One of them shifted to a bright yellow-orange and then back to brown, very quickly. The frog equivalent, Marsh suspected, of nudging your friend and saying, "Did you hear that?"

"You've been monitoring us," Vale said. He had recovered from the shock faster than anyone, which Marsh noted because it confirmed something she had always suspected about Vale: his suspicion was structural, not emotional, and when the world rearranged itself he simply applied the same suspicion to the new arrangement. "You knew we were coming. You chose to meet us here. You arranged this. Which means you have an objective. What do you want?"

Noll regarded Vale for a moment. His colors remained that steady, controlled blue. "You assume that because we prepared for your arrival, we must have an agenda beyond the arrival itself. This is a reasonable assumption. It is also, in this case, incorrect. We are meeting you because you found us. The agenda, such as it is, is yours."

"Nobody meets anyone without an agenda."

"That has not been my experience."

"Then your experience is incomplete."

"I have been coordinating for this settlement for eleven years. I have lived twenty-eight. In that time I have attended perhaps four thousand meetings. Some had agendas. Some did not. Some had agendas that became apparent only afterward, which I find is the most honest kind."

The number landed on Marsh a beat later than it should have. Twenty-eight years. He said it the way a person might say a number that was obviously, self-evidently, not a long time. Because for him it was not. Twenty-eight years was old. Twenty-eight years was most of a life.

She filed that away and kept watching.

Reed was vibrating. Not literally, though it was close. "The bioluminescent network in the walls. Is it cultivated? Is it symbiotic? What is the energy source? Because the sustained output I'm observing here is not consistent with any known fungal metabolism and the spectral properties suggest a substrate interaction that would require, conservatively, a Nobel Prize in three separate categories to explain."

Noll blinked, which on a frog was a slower, more deliberate action than on a human, and gave the impression of a government official carefully not responding to a question he found endearing.

"Dr. Reed. You are standing in a settlement that your species has not detected for the entirety of its existence. I would gently suggest that the questions you have may benefit from context, and that context may benefit from patience."

"I have context. I have three years of anomalous data."

"You have three years of questions. We have two hundred million years of answers. Perhaps we might find a pace that accommodates both."

Reed's glasses had fogged completely. He took them off, cleaned them on his shirt, and put them back on. They were immediately worse.

Hart had moved two steps into the chamber. She was looking not at the frogs but at the air. Marsh could see her nostrils working, cataloguing smells. "The air in here," Hart said. "The copper compound. The organic volatiles. Are those produced by you? By your bodies?"

"Some, yes," Noll said. "Others are produced by the settlement itself. The walls, as you may have observed, are alive."

"I had observed that. Are they dangerous?"

"To you? Almost certainly not. To us? Occasionally, if maintenance is neglected. We are, I should note, as concerned about contamination from your presence as you are about contamination from ours. You are very large. You are very warm. You are breathing out an extraordinary volume of carbon dioxide and foreign microorganisms. Several of our physicians are, at this moment, extremely unhappy with me."

A frog near the back of the growing crowd, barely visible in the dim light, made a sound that Marsh could only describe as a harrumph. Several nearby frogs shifted colors in what was clearly amusement.

"May I suggest," Noll said, "that we continue this conversation inside the settlement proper. The corridors here are functional, not social. We have spaces that are better suited to extended discussion. The water is warm. The light is better. And my colleagues behind me have been waiting quite patiently, though I suspect their patience is reaching its natural limit."

He gestured with his undamaged forelimb toward the corridors beyond the chamber. In the blue-green light, Marsh could see them branching outward, some rising, some descending, all carrying that constant sound of flowing water. From deeper inside, the murmur of what could only be called activity. Life, happening at a scale she had not expected.

"Dissolve gently," Noll added, turning toward the nearest corridor. He said it to the two flanking frogs, lightly, the way a person might say "shall we" when leaving a restaurant. The flanking frogs shifted colors in acknowledgment and began to move.

Marsh heard it. Filed it. Did not understand it. It sounded like a pleasantry from a language she had not been introduced to.

Reed was already moving forward. Vale followed, his arms folded again, his face set in the expression of a man who intended to find the power structure or die trying. Hart adjusted her field kit and followed, scanning the walls as she walked. Quill had not spoken. He looked at Marsh and she looked at him and something passed between them that was not a word. An acknowledgment that whatever they had been doing before this moment had ended, and whatever came next had not yet acquired a name.

They entered the settlement.

The corridors branched. The water channels widened. The blue-green light spread and deepened and the sound of life grew louder, the murmur of a community going about its day that had now paused to watch five large mammals walk through its streets. An older frog, dark brown with pale yellow spots, sat in a water channel and watched them pass with the frank, unhurried interest of a retiree watching a parade.

The crowd was behind them now. And ahead of them. And on both sides.

Marsh walked, and watched, and listened to the water, and noticed that the corridor they had entered through was no longer visible behind them. Not because anything had closed. The turns had simply accumulated. She could not, if asked, have described the route back to the surface.

She put her hand in her pocket. The stone was warm.

## Chapter Intent
The first contact lands as a collision between bureaucratic courtesy and genuine shock. The frogs are alien from their first words, the settlement is populated and alive, and the comedy comes from the absurd specificity of five scientists reacting simultaneously to something impossible. The chapter ends with the biologists inside the settlement, surrounded, unable to retrace their steps, the door having closed without anything closing.
