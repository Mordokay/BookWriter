# Chapter 8: Competitive Vibrations

The arena had been designed by someone who understood that the best sporting events are the ones you can feel in your kidneys.

Marsh sat on a root ledge above the largest pool she had seen in the settlement, her notebook balanced on her knee, her trousers soaked past the point of caring. Below her, packed into interconnected basins that stepped down toward a central channel, somewhere around eighty frogs watched the competition with the focused intensity of a crowd that had been arguing about the rules for forty million years and intended to keep arguing for forty million more.

The game, as far as she could piece together, worked like this: two teams of four frogs positioned themselves at opposite ends of a water channel, and one team produced a vibrational pattern, a sequence of pulses that traveled through the water with enough force to make the stone ledge hum beneath Marsh's thighs. The opposing team had to decode the pattern, replicate it with a variation, and send it back. The variation had to be, according to the rules, "structurally responsive but creatively distinct."

What counted as "structurally responsive but creatively distinct" was, as far as Marsh could tell, the subject of ongoing litigation.

A frog on the near team pressed low into the water and produced a rolling pulse that Marsh felt in her sternum. The counter came back within seconds, a modified rhythm with a syncopated accent on the third beat. The crowd turned shades of approving green. A cluster of elderly frogs went blue with technical admiration. One of them leaned toward his neighbor and said something that made the neighbor turn orange with disagreement so vivid it was practically a rebuttal.

"That was clean," Oona said from beside Marsh, her throat patch glowing. She had been providing commentary since they arrived. Silence during a sporting event was, for Oona, a moral failure. "The return variation was clean. Good accent on the third beat. Now watch, because the judges will call a foul."

"Why?"

"Because the third-beat accent is disputed. The current rules say structural accents must fall on even beats. But the previous rule committee changed it to odd beats, and the committee before that did not specify, and the committee before that dissolved in a jurisdictional argument about whether beats should be counted from the pulse origin or the pulse arrival, which are not the same thing because water has drag, and the drag varies with temperature, and." She stopped. "Nobody knows the rules. The rules have been under continuous revision for approximately forty million years. The rule committee meets once a season and produces a document that contradicts the previous document, and then a new committee produces a document explaining that the contradiction was intentional, and then a third committee forms to investigate whether the explanation was authorized."

An officiating frog at the pool's edge raised a forelimb. A low click carried across the arena.

The crowd erupted.

Not in sound, or not only in sound. Colors rolled through the packed basins like weather, waves of outraged amber and indignant yellow rippling from three separate sections simultaneously. A frog on the upper ledge turned a shade of orange so intense it looked like a medical event. Three frogs in the middle section began croaking in overlapping rhythms that were unmistakably the frog equivalent of "you cannot be serious." The officiating frog absorbed it all with the flat composure of someone who had made this call before and did not care about any of them.

"I told you," Oona said. "Foul. Third-beat accent. Even beats only."

"But you just said the rule was changed to odd beats."

"It was changed back. Or forward. There is some disagreement about whether the current revision is the revision or the reversion. The rule committee has opinions. Nobody reads the opinions. The games continue." She watched the officiating frog with open admiration. "Fenn is very brave. She officiated the last three tournaments. After the second one, someone left a dead beetle in her sleeping chamber as a protest. She ate it."

Reed was two ledges below, sitting at the water's edge with his feet in the pool, glasses fogged. He was talking to a medium-sized green frog who was not so much watching the competition as sitting near it.

"The propagation rate," Reed was saying. "Through interconnected water bodies at this temperature, with this mineral content, the vibrational frequency should dissipate within, what, eight meters? Ten? But the channel extends at least twenty meters and the signal clarity at the far end is still." He paused. "How is the signal clarity still that high?"

The green frog looked at him. "Because the channel is good."

"Right, but physically. The medium is water. Water absorbs vibrational energy at a rate proportional to."

"The channel is very good. They built it well."

"Who built it?"

"Someone. Before my time. It works well. Are you enjoying the game?"

Reed pushed his glasses up. "Enormously. But the propagation."

"You are thinking too hard about the water. Watch the game. Bett is about to do the descending spiral. Nobody does the descending spiral anymore. It is old-fashioned but when it works it is beautiful."

Reed turned to watch. He lasted about four seconds before reaching for his notebook.

On the far side of the arena, on a dry stone ledge conspicuously above the waterline, Hart sat with her legs crossed and her field kit on her lap. The only person in the arena, human or frog, who was not wet. Two frogs sat near her, maintaining a safe but curious distance.

Hart's left hand was gesturing at the communal pools below. The two frogs were looking at the pools, then at Hart, then at each other.

One of them turned alarmed green. The other turned amused brown.

"She is telling them about parasites," Oona said. "Your Hart. She told Menn last week that communal pools at this temperature are ideal breeding conditions for seventeen different parasitic organisms. Menn has not been in the pools since. Menn's partner is furious."

"Is she wrong?"

"She is probably not wrong. She is also not helpful. We have been sitting in these pools for two hundred million years. If the parasites were going to kill us, they would have done it. But your Hart says it as though she has just discovered death and wants to make sure everyone knows."

The alarmed frog on Hart's ledge stood up, looked at the pools, and sat back down. His colors had gone a dull, grieving grey. The amused frog had not moved.

"That one is Gorr," Oona said. "He sits on dry ledges at every competition. Not because of parasites. Because he does not like being touched. He will tell your Hart she is the first sensible person he has met, and they will sit together being dry, and it will be the most intimate friendship either of them has had in years."

Below them, teams rotated. The new team on the near end included a small, very dark frog who produced vibrations with a precision that made the water surface shiver in geometric patterns. The crowd went quiet. Colors shifted to attentive blue-green across the arena, a wave of focus passing through the audience like a breath. For a moment, everyone was the same color.

Quill was sitting three ledges away, visible in profile, his hands in his pockets, watching the crowd instead of the game. His head moved slowly across the packed basins, tracking the color shifts, the synchronized waves that rolled through the spectators whenever something happened in the channel. The crowd's colors moving together, involuntarily, like a current passing through them.

His face had gone still. Whatever he was recognizing in the crowd's colors, it did not seem to please him.

Marsh filed it. Quill got it when something that should be simple turned out to contain something else, and he was not sure yet what.

The game ended. Or paused. Immediately a knot of frogs near the middle basin began arguing about whether the final sequence had been a legal variation or an illegal repetition, and the argument generated its own audience, and the audience generated opinions, and the opinions branched into secondary arguments like tributaries from a river that had no interest in reaching the sea.

A frog climbed onto a ledge near the central basin and began delivering a formal complaint about scheduling. The vibration organizers, four frogs wearing their authority in the form of colors so rigid they looked painted on, listened with the patience of officials already composing their response.

"The fermentation competition," Oona said, standing up. "This is Grenn. He organizes the vibration tournaments. He is upset because the fermentation tasting was scheduled the same evening, which splits the attendance. He has been upset about this for three seasons. The fermentation committee says the conflict is unavoidable. Grenn says they are doing it on purpose. They are not. But try telling Grenn. He will argue about this until one of them dies, and the fermentation committee has more members, so the numbers are against him, but Grenn has conviction, which is worse."

The adjacent chamber was connected to the arena by a waterway that smelled like someone had concentrated every fermentation cellar in the settlement into a single room and then disagreed about the results.

Dozens of small batches in carved stone vessels lined the chamber's edges, each tended by a frog who stood beside their entry with the nervous pride of a baker at a county fair. Three judges moved among the entries with slow deliberation, tasting everything, enjoying nothing.

Oona positioned herself beside her entry, both forelimbs on the stone slab, throat patch at maximum brightness. "This batch is exceptional. I adjusted the moss substrate after they moved my culture stock. The lower level has worse humidity but better mineral seepage, and the minerals changed the fermentation in ways that were initially catastrophic and then became interesting and then became, I believe, superior."

"What does it taste like?"

"Perseverance," Oona said. "Also, slightly of copper."

Three vessels down, Krrik stood beside a colleague presenting a batch. His mottled skin was cycling through rapid, irritated color shifts. The colleague did not appear to notice. Or had stopped noticing years ago.

"This is not chemistry," Krrik said. He was looking at the tasting entries with the expression of a physicist at a horoscope convention. "This is decay with pretensions. You are cultivating rot and calling it cuisine."

"It is fermentation," the colleague said.

"Fermentation is controlled decomposition. This is not controlled. This is optimistic decomposition. You have placed organic material in a vessel and hoped."

"I have placed organic material in a vessel and managed the conditions for six weeks."

"You have managed nothing. You have observed. Observation is not management. The microorganisms did the work. You watched." Krrik's shorter hind leg shifted, and he resettled himself with the asymmetric precision that was as much his signature as his irritability. "If you presented this in my laboratory, I would ask you to identify the variables you controlled, and you would say 'the vessel' and 'the location,' and I would ask you to leave."

The colleague tasted his own batch, considered it, and offered some to Krrik.

Krrik recoiled. "No."

"It is good."

"It is decomposed insects in mineral water."

"Yes. That is what fermentation is."

"I know what fermentation is. I object to calling it food."

"You object to everything."

"I object to imprecision. This is imprecise food."

The colleague ate his batch with visible satisfaction. Krrik watched him eat it, every color on his body communicating that he was witnessing a grave and voluntary error.

One of the judges arrived at Oona's entry, a small pale frog whose spots shifted through careful greens as she tasted. She held the sample for a long moment. Oona's throat patch was so bright it was nearly yellow.

"The mineral profile is unusual," the judge said.

"The lower cellar," Oona said. "Better seepage."

"The acidity is high."

"The acidity is intentional."

"I did not say it was not intentional. I said it was high."

Oona's colors shifted in the rapid sequence of a defense attorney's opening statement. "The acidity complements the copper notes. The copper comes from the mineral seepage. The seepage is a function of the location, which was forced on me by the logistics committee, which moved my culture stock without consulting me, so the acidity is, in a sense, the logistics committee's contribution, and I think we can all agree that the logistics committee's contributions are rarely intentional but occasionally excellent."

The judge moved on without committing to an opinion. Oona watched her go.

"She liked it," Oona said.

"How do you know?"

"Her spots held green. If she disliked it, the spots would have gone grey. If she was neutral, she would have said 'adequate,' which is what judges say when they want to move on without insulting you to your face."

A frog at the far end of the tasting chamber dropped a sample vessel into the water channel. The vessel sank. The frog looked at it. A neighboring frog looked at it.

"Wet in, wet out," the neighboring frog said.

Several frogs nearby turned the color of suppressed laughter.

They drifted back toward the arena, where the vibration games had resumed, or had never stopped, or had evolved into something that was partly competition and partly argument and partly the natural state of eighty frogs in a shared space with strong opinions about rhythm.

The courtship display was happening at the arena's southern edge, in a shallow pool separated from the main channel by a low stone ridge. A young frog, bright green and trembling with effort, was performing.

His skin moved through colors in a sequence that was partly rehearsed and partly improvised. Greens deepening to blue, blue cycling to a warm amber, the amber held for a long moment before shifting through three shades of violet in quick succession. The transitions between amber and violet wobbled slightly, the way a singer's voice wavers on a high note they are not quite sure they can reach.

A female frog on the far ledge watched him. Her colors held neutral. You watched, you assessed, you gave nothing away. The neutrality was itself a display: impress me.

Three frogs near the young suitor were coaching. Loudly. Publicly. Without any indication that this was unusual.

"Too much amber in the third sequence," said the first, an older frog with faded green coloring that suggested she had been coaching since before the suitor was a tadpole. "The amber overwhelms. Pull it back."

"Your grandmother did the cascade better," said the second, a compact frog with an air of personal investment in the outcome. "She did the violet transition in two beats, not three. Three looks hesitant."

"Ignore them," said the third, younger, brighter, leaning forward with the conviction of someone who knew exactly what they were talking about regardless of whether they did. "The amber is bold. Bold is good. Nobody remembers the cautious ones."

The young frog held his amber. It shivered at the edges.

"Pull it back," said the first coach.

"Bolder," said the third.

"Your grandmother," said the second, "would be mortified."

The young frog let the amber go and cycled into a rapid descent through blues and greens that was either a deliberate stylistic choice or a panicked retreat. The crowd near the courtship pool turned various shades of opinion. A frog two ledges up offered an unsolicited critique to nobody in particular. A frog beside her disagreed. A third told them both to be quiet because the boy was doing his best.

Oona leaned toward Marsh. "The amber was good. Not traditional, but good. The cascade was sloppy, but he is young, and young frogs always rush the cascade because they think speed compensates for control, which it does not, but by the time they learn this they are old and their color range has narrowed and they do the cascade slowly and call it dignity."

"What does she think?"

"She thinks he is interesting. Her forelimbs are forward. If she was bored, they would be tucked. If she was offended, her spots would darken. She is watching closely, which is the best a young frog can hope for on a first display."

"Is it always this public?"

"How else would you do it? You display. People see. People help. A private display is just practice. A public display is a commitment. You are saying: this is who I am, and I am willing to be seen being it, and if the amber wobbles then the amber wobbles and that is part of the honesty."

Marsh watched the young frog try again. The three coaches offered conflicting advice simultaneously. The young frog's colors flickered with what might have been irritation or gratitude or both.

On the far side of the arena, Vale was sitting on a ledge with his notebook. He had not been watching the games or the courtship. He had been watching where the frogs sat.

The frogs did not have assigned seating. The best positions were claimed by arrival, except that the same frogs arrived first every time. When Marsh had asked Oona about this, Oona had said that certain frogs simply preferred certain spots and everyone knew this and it was not a ranking, it was a habit, and habits are not the same as hierarchy, except that they produce exactly the same result, which Oona said without appearing to notice she had said it.

Vale would be writing that down. His note would say something about informal seniority systems, and it would be correct, and it would also miss something, which was that the frogs at the edges were having just as good a time as the frogs near the center.

She turned back to the courtship pool. The young frog had finished his display, or been advised to stop by the first coach, who had placed a forelimb on his back with the firm gentleness of someone ending an audition. The neutral frog on the far ledge held her composure for another moment, then let her colors shift, just slightly, to a warmer green. The second coach said something about the grandmother. The third clapped the young frog on the back. His skin went through six colors in a second, none of them deliberate.

The evening wound down. The vibration games continued at reduced intensity, the remaining competitors playing to a crowd that had thinned and softened. The fermentation tasting produced a winner that Marsh did not catch because Oona was too busy objecting to the judging criteria to report the results. Conversations broke into smaller clusters along the ledges and in the shallow pools.

The attendance was lower than usual. Oona had mentioned this early in the evening, casually, the way she said things she wanted to seem casual about. "Fewer people this season. The staging preparations. People are tired." Her throat patch had dimmed when she said it, and she had brightened it deliberately. Near the back of the arena, a group of frogs sat together in the upper basin, quiet, colors muted. Operators from the staging grounds. They were not watching the games. They were present because the community was gathering and this is what you did, even when your body wanted to be somewhere horizontal and dark. One of them had her eyes closed. They had come. That was the thing.

Marsh put her notebook away.

Quill was still watching the crowd. During the final moments of the last game, when the winning team produced a vibration that made the central channel ring like a bell, the audience had reacted. All of them. At once. Their colors had shifted together, dozens of frogs turning the same shade of surprised gold in the same instant, involuntary and gone before it fully registered.

Like the breathing circles, Marsh thought. Except this was a game.

Quill had seen it. His hands were in his pockets, his body very still, watching the crowd's colors settle back into their separate patterns.

She stood. Her legs were stiff. Oona was arguing with a neighboring frog about whether the fermentation judges had confused complexity with acidity, a distinction both frogs considered critically important and that the judges would probably have to answer for at next season's committee meeting and possibly for the rest of their natural lives.

The young suitor from the courtship pool walked past with two of his coaches. His colors were calm. The amber was gone. He looked tired and pleased and slightly dazed.

## Chapter Intent
The funniest chapter in Act 2A makes the frog civilization feel fully alive through play, competition, food, and courtship. Each biologist's reaction to the communal event reveals character through a different lens. The comedy comes from the frogs' absolute sincerity about their games, their food, and their romantic advice. Underneath the warmth, two quieter notes: the Pruning's cost visible in thin attendance and exhausted operators, and Quill's observation that the crowd's involuntary color synchronization echoes the merging rituals, introducing philosophical unease beneath the comedy.
