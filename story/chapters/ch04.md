# Chapter 4: Temporary Coordinators of Distributed Harmonies

Noll had arranged them in a line, which Vale found telling.

The frog had not said "follow me in single file." He had said "the corridors are narrow in places, and your comfort will be greater if you proceed in sequence." The result was the same. They were in a line, Noll in front, and the line moved at Noll's pace, which was the pace of an eight-centimeter frog navigating root corridors designed for eight-centimeter frogs. Vale, who was six foot one and had already hit his head on a ceiling root twice, found his comfort was not notably greater.

Behind him, the tour had acquired passengers. Two frogs trailed at the rear of the group, small and grey-brown, officially there to observe. They were not observing. They were having a conversation in quick clicks and color changes that Vale could hear but not parse, and from their posture and the way they kept glancing at Hart's field kit and then at each other, they were gossiping. He recognized the body language. He had spent twenty years in faculty departments.

The other passenger was worse.

"And this," Noll said, pausing at the entrance to a wide, low chamber, "is one of our Speaking Chambers. You will observe that it is partially flooded. This is by design. The mineral water facilitates communication and promotes a hormonal environment conducive to measured deliberation."

"The water is also warm," said a voice from behind Vale's left knee, "which is why everyone falls asleep during the long sessions."

Oona Reedmoss had appeared at some point between the residential corridor and here. Nobody had invited her. Nobody had objected to her arrival, either, which Vale noted. Noll's colors did not shift. His damaged forelimb did not move. He simply absorbed her presence the way a river absorbs a thrown stone, closing around it without acknowledging the splash.

Oona was round-bodied, olive-green, with an orange patch at her throat that was currently very bright. She had the energy of a neighbor who had seen the tour group pass her window and come outside to contribute.

"Please," Noll said, to the biologists and to nobody in particular. "Observe."

Vale observed.

The Speaking Chamber was an amphitheater carved from living root, descending in concentric rings to a central basin of dark water. Perhaps forty frogs sat on the rings, half-submerged, arranged in no pattern Vale could identify as hierarchical. Their colors moved. Not flickering, not random. Shifting in waves that passed from one side of the chamber to the other, like wind through grass, except the grass was alive and opinionated. A sustained harmonic came through the water and the stone and the soles of Vale's boots, settling in his chest. It was not a sound he was hearing. It was a sound he was standing in.

"What are they deciding?" he asked.

"They are coordinating resource allocation for the western perimeter," Noll said. "The specifics would require context I am not certain we have time to provide."

"Who decides the outcome?"

"The coordination emerges."

"From what?"

Noll paused. The pause was perfectly measured. "From the process."

"Right." Vale watched a ripple of amber pass through the assembled frogs, answered by a wave of deep green from the opposite side. "So nobody has power. Nobody makes decisions. The moss just arranges itself into policy."

"There is no moss in this chamber," Noll said. "But I take your meaning. You believe that consensus is a performance behind which a smaller group exercises actual control."

"I believe that in every system I have ever studied."

"Then your studies have been thorough. Whether they have been complete is a separate question."

Vale filed that. He filed the pause before it, too. The pause was the more interesting data.

Reed had broken from the line. He was crouched at the edge of the basin, his glasses catching the blue-green light, one hand hovering over the water, almost touching it, pulling back, hovering again. "The vibration in the water. Is that how they vote? Through vibration?"

"It is how they communicate. Voting is a human metaphor that does not precisely apply."

"The water hummed," Marsh said quietly, from behind her notebook. She was sketching the concentric rings. "They deliberated and the water hummed."

Oona had positioned herself on a root ledge near Vale's ankle. "The Third Pool district tried to get a drainage maintenance vote through this chamber last season. The coordination emerged that it was not a priority. The Third Pool district still has drainage problems. The coordination did not emerge for them." Her orange throat patch flared. "But as Noll says, the process is the process."

Noll did not respond to this. His blue coloring held steady.

"We should continue," he said.

They continued.

The corridors branched, narrowed, opened into spaces that Vale's brain wanted to categorize and could not. Not rooms exactly. Not hallways. Grown structures that followed a logic he did not have the vocabulary for, shaped by a species that squatted and clung and floated. Water ran through every surface, along channels carved by centuries of flow. The light was the dim blue-green of the fungal networks, pulsing faintly with air currents, and Vale had stopped noticing it was strange about ten minutes ago. That was more disturbing than the strangeness itself.

Noll stopped at a low opening. "This is a residential section. You may find the noise startling."

The noise was already audible. Croaking, clicking, the ambient percussion of many small bodies in enclosed damp spaces. And, cutting through it, what was clearly an argument. Two frogs, somewhere in the warren, were going at each other in sharp bursts of sound that needed no translation. The rhythm was universal. Someone was wrong about something and someone else was making sure they knew it.

Noll walked past the argument as though it were furniture.

"Sleeping chamber allocation," Oona said, keeping pace with the humans. "It happens every season. Everyone wants the chambers near the warm channels. Nobody wants the upper levels, because they drip. It is the same argument every year. My sister had the warm chamber for three seasons running and people still bring it up."

"Do they have a formal process for allocating chambers?" Vale asked.

"They have a coordination."

"And does the coordination work?"

"The coordination," Oona said, "produces an outcome that nobody is satisfied with, which is then adjusted informally until people get roughly what they got last year. This is called harmony."

Hart had fallen behind. Vale looked back and found her standing at the edge of what appeared to be a trench running along the corridor floor, filled with a dark, glistening material that was growing. The growth was alive. It was doing something to the water that passed through it. It was, as far as Vale could determine, the sanitation.

"That's your sanitation," Hart said.

Noll turned. His blue coloring did not waver. "That is our relationship with biology."

"That is a trench of living organic material processing waste in an open corridor next to where people sleep."

"It is an effective system."

"It's right there. In the hallway."

"We do not have hallways in your sense. We have continuous living space. The processing systems are integrated into the living space. Separation would require introducing barriers to the biological flow, which would be, in our terms, counterproductive."

Hart stared at the trench. The trench pulsed faintly. "In my terms, it's a disease vector."

"The trenches in the Third Pool district are worse," Oona said. "Much worse. Nobody wants to coordinate the maintenance schedule. The last coordinator resigned after two seasons and nobody replaced her. If you think this is bad, you should see the eastern warrens after the wet months."

Noll's forelimb twitched. It was the smallest motion Vale had seen from him, and it was the first he had seen that Noll did not appear to have chosen.

They passed through the warrens. Brief rooms, moss-padded sleeping surfaces, the sound of domestic life through root-shaped walls. In one chamber, three frogs were eating something from a communal stone dish, their colors shifting in lazy, social patterns. They looked up as the humans passed. One of them turned a vivid, fascinated yellow. The other two pointedly did not look.

Marsh was writing in her notebook. Not fast. Precise entries, one per space, her pencil moving in the careful strokes of someone cataloguing specimens. Temperature. Humidity. The angle of the water channels. The wear patterns on the root-shaped seating. She was, Vale thought, the only one of them doing anything useful.

Quill was not taking notes. He was not asking questions. He was watching the frogs. Not the ones Noll presented. Not the ones who reacted to the humans. The ones in the background, the ones going about their day, the ones who were talking to each other in the rapid, layered communication the humans could hear and not understand. Quill was watching the way a frog at a water channel deferred to an older frog approaching from the other direction. The way two frogs on a root ledge shifted their colors in synchrony without appearing to notice they were doing it. The domestic choreography of a civilization that had been doing this for longer than mammals had existed.

He had not spoken since the tour began. Vale kept track of that.

Behind the group, the two monitoring frogs had stopped gossiping about Hart's field kit and started gossiping about Quill. Vale could not understand the sounds, but he recognized the glances, the way one tilted toward the other while looking at the silent human. He had seen the same thing at a hundred conferences. Two people in the back row, watching the quiet one, trying to work out what he was thinking.

"Dr. Vale." Noll had stopped at a junction where three corridors met. Water flowed from all three into a common channel, warmer here, smelling of minerals and something faintly herbal. "You have questions about governance."

"I have a question about the title you gave us. Temporary Coordinator of Distributed Harmonies. For the Marsh Federations."

"That is my formal designation, yes."

"Who gave it to you?"

"The designation rotates. I was selected through a consensus process that I can describe if you are interested."

"I'm interested in how temporary a temporary coordinator is."

Noll's blue held. Steady, controlled. The color of a man who has heard this question before. "The coordination cycle is five years. I am in my third cycle. Continuation is a matter of general feeling, not personal ambition."

"Eleven years. Three cycles. You've been coordinating for eleven years."

"The general feeling has persisted."

"Right. So the general feeling is that nobody else should do your job. That's power, Noll. That's a mandate wearing a modest hat."

"That is one interpretation."

"Name another."

"Service. Obligation. The willingness to do work that nobody else wishes to do. These are also interpretations. Whether they are more flattering to me or more accurate is a question I am not the appropriate person to answer."

The two monitoring frogs behind them exchanged a quick burst of clicks. One of them shifted briefly to a color Vale had not seen before, something between amber and pink. The other responded with what looked, to Vale's eye, like suppressed amusement.

"Can I touch this?" Reed said, from somewhere ahead of the group.

He had found Krrik's laboratory.

Or rather, he had found the entrance to Krrik's laboratory, which was marked by the smell of ozone and fermentation and by the presence of Krrik, who was standing in the doorway with the expression of a frog who had not been expecting visitors and was not pleased to have received them. He was smaller than Noll, mottled brown-green, dusted with something pale and powdery that might have been spores, and one of his hind legs was noticeably shorter than the other. His eyes were focused on something inside the laboratory that was apparently more interesting than five humans.

"No," Krrik said.

"Just the surface. The texture of this growth medium, the structural density is unlike anything I have ever."

"I said no."

"I won't remove anything. I just want to feel the."

"You are warm, you are damp, you are shedding microorganisms at a rate I find distressing, and the moss you are reaching toward contains more processing power than every computer your species has ever built. It is thinking right now. It is solving a calibration sequence I have been working on for three seasons. If you touch it, you will introduce biological contaminants and thermal disruption into a system that is, at this moment, smarter than both of us. So no. You may not touch it."

Reed pulled his hand back. His glasses were fogged. "More processing power than every computer we've ever built."

"That is what I said."

"And it's being tended by." Reed looked at the moss bed, then at the watering vessel sitting beside it on a stone ledge, then at Krrik. "You water it."

"It requires hydration. The hydration schedule is precise. I am the one who maintains it. If you are about to make a joke about gardening, I have heard it. The last human who was here made the same joke. It was not funny then either."

This landed on the group in stages. Reed first, because Reed processed fast. "The last human who was."

"We should continue," Noll said.

"Wait," Vale said. "The last human? There have been other humans here?"

"That is a topic we will address at a more suitable point in the tour."

"Noll."

"Dr. Vale, there are many things to see. The tour is structured for a reason. Context accumulates."

"Context is being managed. That's different from accumulating."

Noll regarded him. The blue held. The damaged forelimb was perfectly still. "You are correct that I am managing the sequence in which you receive information. I would hope this is understood as courtesy rather than concealment. The distinction is, I recognize, a fine one."

Vale stared at the frog. The frog stared at Vale. The silence had a texture to it, the quality of two people who both understood exactly what was happening and neither of whom was willing to blink.

"Fine," Vale said. "Continue the tour."

They moved on.

In the corridor outside Krrik's lab, the biologists fell apart. Not dramatically. Not with shouting. With the particular quiet disintegration of a group that has absorbed too much in too little time and needs to process it in smaller clusters.

Vale and Hart dropped to the back. "The sanitation," Hart said.

"I know."

"It's an open biological processing system in a residential corridor. The contamination potential is extraordinary. And he called it a relationship with biology."

"He calls everything a relationship with something."

"And the moss bed. Computing. In moss. Tended with a watering can."

"While the sewer runs through the kitchen."

"It's not a sewer. It's worse than a sewer. A sewer at least acknowledges the concept of separation between waste and living space."

Ahead, Reed had cornered Marsh and was talking at a speed that suggested his brain had overtaken his mouth and both had given up on his body. "The computation density in that moss bed, Marsh, the neural architecture, the substrate alone would require a revision of every model we have for biological information processing, and the watering, the watering is the thing, because it means the system is alive, it's not a machine, it's a living system that thinks and also needs to drink, and that means."

"Reed," Marsh said.

"And that means."

"Reed. Write it down. You'll lose half of it by dinner."

Reed patted his pockets, found no notebook, and looked stricken.

Marsh tore a page from hers and handed it to him.

Quill walked alone, between the two clusters, watching a frog in a water channel carefully arrange a series of small objects in a line. The objects were uniform, pale, about the size of rice grains. They were being placed with deliberate care into the flow of water and carried downstream. Deliveries. The frog arranging them had the focused, unhurried attention of someone doing a task that was important and tedious and had been done the same way for a very long time.

Quill watched this. He did not take notes. His hands were at his sides.

"Does the distributed consensus actually work?" he said.

Vale turned. Quill was not looking at him. He was still watching the frog with the deliveries.

"No," Vale said.

"You're certain about that."

"I'm certain that no system in history has distributed power without concentrating it somewhere. The question is not whether there is hierarchy. The question is whether the hierarchy admits it exists."

"And if it genuinely doesn't? If the consensus is real?"

"Then it's the first time in the history of organized life. And I would want to know what's holding it together, because something always is."

Quill turned from the water channel. "You don't think it's possible that a system could be genuinely equal."

"I think a system can be genuinely committed to the idea of equality while being structurally unequal. Which is most systems. Including this one."

Noll, who had been waiting ahead of them with the patience of a man who has spent eleven years waiting for people to finish talking, turned his head slightly. The motion was small. Vale caught it. Noll had been listening. Noll's colors had not changed, which meant Noll was choosing not to respond, which was its own response.

"Shall we proceed?" Noll said. "There is one more thing I believe will interest you."

He led them to a wider space, a junction where the ceiling opened and the blue-green light was brighter. Roots descended from above in shapes that looked architectural, deliberate, grown into pillars and arches over what might have been centuries. The water here was deeper, warmer, and the smell of minerals was strong enough to taste.

"This settlement is called Vreth-in-the-Root," Noll said. "It is one of several dozen across the globe. We are connected by displacement, though the specifics of that need not concern you today."

"Several dozen," Reed said.

"The population across all settlements is estimated at four to six million. We have never been numerous. We prefer this."

"Four to six million." Reed's voice had gone flat. The flatness of a number too large to process. "How long have you been here?"

"In this settlement? Approximately fourteen thousand years, in your counting."

"And as a civilization?"

"Roughly two hundred million years."

The corridor was very quiet.

"Two hundred million years," Hart said.

"Give or take. We predate flowering plants. We found them a pleasant development."

"And humans?" Vale said. "How long have you been aware of us?"

Noll's blue coloring did not change. His body did not shift. He delivered the next sentence like a weather forecast. "Approximately three hundred thousand years."

The five reactions happened in the space of about four seconds.

Vale: "Why the hell didn't you make contact?"

Reed: "How did you monitor us? What instruments? Over what range?"

Hart: "Has there been cross-species pathogen exposure? Three hundred thousand years of proximity without containment protocols is an epidemiological."

Marsh: "What did you think of us?"

Quill said nothing. His hands opened and closed at his sides.

Noll let them finish. He let the silence hold for a beat. Then he addressed Vale, and only Vale.

"Discovery is a form of entanglement," he said. "We preferred to remain disentangled. Until, of course, you made that impossible."

He said it like he said everything. Measured. Courteous. Final. Then he turned and continued down the corridor, and the tour was apparently over, or at least this part of it was over, and behind him five biologists stood in the blue-green light of a civilization that had been watching them for longer than their species had existed.

Oona's throat patch dimmed. She looked at the humans, then at Noll's retreating form, then back at the humans.

"He does that," she said. "Says the enormous thing and walks away. Very dramatic. I tell him it's rude. He tells me it's pacing." She hopped down from her root ledge. "The fermentation cellars are this way, if anyone wants something to drink. You look like you need it."

Nobody moved for a moment. Then Marsh closed her notebook and followed Oona, and the others followed Marsh, and the two monitoring frogs trailed behind them, clicking softly to each other about something the humans would never understand.

## Chapter Intent
The tour of Vreth-in-the-Root delivers world-building entirely through character interaction: Vale's interrogation of governance, Oona's domestic contradictions, Hart's biological horror at the sanitation, Reed's scientific overload at the computing moss, Quill's silent observation of real frog social behavior, and Krrik's territorial impatience. The chapter's depth charge is the 300,000-year monitoring revelation, which reframes the entire first-contact dynamic and lands through Noll's casual, devastating delivery.
