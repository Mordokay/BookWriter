# Continuity Review -- Chapter 26: "Five Answers"

## Verdict: PASS WITH NOTES

Two critical issues with the herbalist anecdote in Reed's scene. The rest of the chapter is exceptionally clean -- character voices, physical tells, frog behaviors, and the emotional architecture all align with 25 chapters of established canon.

## Issues Found

### Critical

**1. The herbalist's location: Africa vs. "here"**

Ch 26 line 113: Krrik says "The human from Africa. The herbalist. She was here eleven years."

"Here" in context means Vreth-in-the-Root (the Amazon settlement), because Krrik is speaking inside his own laboratory in Vreth-in-the-Root, and he says "her soil chemistry observations improved our eastern nursery yields by nine percent" -- implying the Amazon settlement's nurseries.

Canon says otherwise:
- Timeline.md line 73: "Approximately 4,000 years ago (Central Africa): A human herbalist stumbles into the Rift Pool settlement while collecting plants."
- Ch 15 line 97: Pem says "The woman from the continent you call Africa. She was found by the Rift Pool settlement approximately four thousand of your years ago."
- Timeline.md line 73: "Her biological imprint survives in the African archive."
- World.md line 26: "Central African Rift Pools" is a separate settlement.
- The continuity review for Ch 15 verified this: "Pem says the woman was 'from the continent you call Africa' and was 'found by the Rift Pool settlement.'"

The herbalist lived among the Threnn at the Rift Pool settlement in Central Africa, not at Vreth-in-the-Root. Krrik saying "She was here" and referencing "our eastern nursery yields" places her at the Amazon settlement, which contradicts established canon.

**Fix:** Either (a) change "here" to "at the Rift Pool settlement" and adjust the nursery yields reference to say "Rift Pool eastern nursery yields" or "the Rift settlement's yields," or (b) change the human referenced to the Tupi trader or the cartographer, who were actually at Vreth-in-the-Root. Option (a) is simpler and preserves the scene's emotional structure. Krrik, as the chief biophysicist with inter-settlement access, would plausibly know Rift Pool data.

---

**2. Krrik's "two hundred years" does not match any previous-human timeline.**

Ch 26 line 117: "Content is an approximation. I am translating across species and two hundred years."

The herbalist (subject of this paragraph) lived approximately 4,000 years ago. "Two hundred years" does not apply to her.

The cartographer from Pem's sample line in characters.md lived "two hundred and forty years past." That is the only previous human near the 200-year mark. But (a) the cartographer was in Northern Europe's Bog Networks (timeline.md line 77), not the Amazon or Africa, and (b) the cartographer is not the subject of this passage -- the "human from Africa" is.

The Tupi trader lived approximately 800 years ago. Also not "two hundred years."

**Fix:** Change "two hundred years" to "four thousand years" to match the herbalist's timeline. Alternatively, if the chapter brief's "cartographer (240 years ago, lived 11 years)" was intended as a conflation, this is the wrong human -- the cartographer was in Europe. The simplest repair: "I am translating across species and four thousand of your years."

---

**3. The junior engineer's "grandmother's grandmother" generational math.**

Ch 26 line 121: "My grandmother's grandmother knew the human."

"Grandmother's grandmother" = great-great-grandmother = roughly 4 generations back. With frog lifespans of 25-35 years (world.md line 148/474), and assuming reproductive maturity at roughly 5-8 frog-years, four generations back covers approximately 80-140 frog-years, which is 80-140 human years.

The herbalist lived approximately 4,000 human years ago. Four frog generations cannot span 4,000 years. This is off by a factor of roughly 30-50.

Even the cartographer (240 years ago) would require approximately 7-10 frog generations, not 4. The Tupi trader (800 years ago) would require approximately 25-30 generations.

**Fix:** This is tied to Issue 2. If the "two hundred years" is corrected to reference the correct timeline for the herbalist (4,000 years), then "grandmother's grandmother" must also change. Options:
- (a) Replace with a vague generational reference: "The archive preserves a memory from a frog who knew the human" or "A predecessor of my lineage knew the human."
- (b) Switch the referenced human to the cartographer (~240 years ago), who fits "grandmother's grandmother" much better (240 years / ~30 years per generation = ~8 generations, still more than 4 but closer). However, the cartographer was in Northern Europe, not the Amazon.
- (c) The cleanest fix: keep "grandmother's grandmother," change the human from the herbalist to a more recent absorbed human at Vreth-in-the-Root. The chapter brief mentions "a cartographer (240 years ago, lived 11 years)" -- if this cartographer is recanonized as being at Vreth-in-the-Root instead of Europe, the generational math is closer (though still slightly long at ~8 generations for 240 years).
- (d) Alternatively, keep the herbalist at the Rift Pool settlement and have Krrik reference her work through the inter-settlement archive, then drop the junior engineer's personal-connection anecdote entirely and replace it with an archival detail. This preserves the 4,000-year timeline but loses the warm "singing badly" beat. The singing detail is already established in Ch 15 as coming from Pem's archive, so a junior engineer having personal family knowledge of a 4,000-year-old event is the real problem.

**Recommendation:** The simplest coherent fix is to change the referenced human from the African herbalist to the Tupi trader (~800 years ago, at Vreth-in-the-Root). The trader lived 22 years among the frogs. Krrik could reference the trader's contributions. The junior engineer's "grandmother's grandmother" still does not work for 800 years, but a phrase like "the old songs remember the human" or similar archival framing would. Alternatively, invent a more recent absorbed human at Vreth-in-the-Root (within 100-140 years) to make the generational math and location both work. The chapter brief already implies flexibility -- it says "the previous humans include a cartographer (240 years ago)... others are less specified."

## Summary

The chapter's character work is outstanding. Five voices are distinct and earned by 25 chapters of established characterization. Physical tells (Reed's glasses, Talla's rigid posture, Vesh's lopsided eyes, Noll's muted green-grey, Oona's throat patch) are all accurate. Frog behaviors (chromatophore displays, biological curiosity about tears, food-as-comfort, background settlement life) are consistent with world.md and prior chapters. The emotional architecture -- loudest to quietest, Vale to Quill -- matches the chapter outline exactly.

The three critical issues all stem from the herbalist anecdote in Reed's scene. The herbalist's location (Africa's Rift Pool, not Vreth-in-the-Root), the timeline ("two hundred years" vs. 4,000 years), and the generational math ("grandmother's grandmother" spanning 4,000 years when frog generations cover ~30 years each) form a single interconnected problem. The cleanest fix is likely to replace the herbalist with a more recent absorbed human at Vreth-in-the-Root whose timeline fits both the "grandmother's grandmother" connection and the "here" location, or to reframe Krrik's knowledge as coming through inter-settlement data and drop the junior engineer's personal family connection.
