### Minor

1. **Timeline.md not updated to reflect Ch 12's actual content.**
   Timeline.md (lines 182-183) says Day 17-18 (Ch 12) covers "The full Siberian delegation arrives. Cultural clash between Amazon and Siberian frogs..." The actual chapter is entirely the Mourning Dissolution for Lenn. The chapter outline was updated to make Ch 12 the mourning ritual chapter, but timeline.md still carries the old Siberian-delegation summary. This does not affect the chapter prose -- it is a canon-file maintenance issue. **Action:** Update timeline.md Day 17-18 entry to reflect the mourning ritual.

2. **Talla described with "arms folded" (line 165).**
   Talla is a 4.5 cm frog. "Arms folded" is anthropomorphic shorthand. Canon uses "forelimb" for frog anatomy in precise contexts (Noll's "damaged forelimb," Krrik's forelimbs). The book does not rigidly avoid "arms" for frogs -- Pem on line 143 has "Hands ready" -- but "arms folded" specifically evokes a human posture that is physically implausible for a small frog. Consider revising to "forelimbs crossed" or a posture description that conveys the same emotional register (braced, closed off) without the anatomical stretch.

3. **World.md anecdote overlap.**
   World.md (line 336) describes a historical incident: "one elderly frog's last feeling was apparently annoyance about the temperature of the pool, which became a joke that lasted several generations." Ch 12 dramatizes an extremely similar event with Lenn. This is not technically a contradiction -- the world doc describes a past event, and Ch 12 creates a new instance. But a reader who consults the world bible might wonder whether Lenn IS the historical frog or a separate case. Recommend adding a brief note to the chapter's continuity notes or to world.md clarifying that this is a recurring phenomenon (multiple frogs have died with temperature complaints) or that Lenn is the source of the anecdote referenced in world.md.

4. **Vesh's ritual phrasing: minor variation from brief.**
   The chapter brief and outline specify Vesh says "The puddle has returned to the water." In the prose (line 69), Vesh says: "Lenn came from the water. He has returned to the water. The puddle has formed. The puddle has dissolved. The water does not mourn the shape." The meaning is equivalent and arguably more powerful, but the chapter's own continuity notes (line 215) claim Vesh said "The puddle has returned to the water" as a direct quote, which does not appear verbatim in the prose. Not a story-level issue, but the continuity notes should match the actual text.

5. **Day count arithmetic.**
   Ch 12 continuity notes say "Day 17 of the biologists' time (Day 14 since entering Vreth-in-the-Root)." If the biologists entered on Day 3 (per Ch 10's note: "entering Vreth-in-the-Root on Day 3") and Day 17 is the current day, then time since entering = 17 - 3 = 14 days. This checks out. However, the timeline.md places entry on Day 4 (Ch 3), not Day 3. This is a pre-existing discrepancy between the chapter notes (which use Day 3) and timeline.md (which uses Day 4). Ch 12 follows the convention already established in prior chapters, so this is consistent within the chapter sequence, though the underlying timeline.md disagreement remains unresolved. **Action:** Reconcile timeline.md entry date with the chapter-note convention when performing the timeline update.

## New Facts Established in Ch 12

These facts are introduced or confirmed for the first time in Chapter 12 and should be treated as canon going forward:

1. **Lenn** -- elderly frog, 71 seasons. Former displacement operator (before "the joints went"), later worked fermentation cellars. Known for burning first batches and blaming the humidity. Died of natural causes (old age, breathing slowed over two days). Declined treatment extension; complained about pool temperature. His final chemical signature broadcast: irritation about the water temperature.

2. **The mourning pool** -- a specific, separate pool deeper in the settlement, set apart from communal bathing areas. Older stone, worn smooth. Warmer, denser air. Dim bioluminescence. A sharp green smell associated with the dead frog's chemical signature. Designed acoustics that carry vibration across the water.

3. **Mourning ritual sequence (dramatized):**
   - Community gathers within hours of death. No formal procession; practical arrival.
   - Ritual guide (Vesh) enters water to chest-depth, speaks traditional phrases.
   - A low communal sub-vocal harmonic precedes the body's placement. Felt through stone and water, not fully audible.
   - Body is placed in the water by two carriers. Floats, then settles half-submerged.
   - Chemical signature releases; living frogs absorb it through skin. Emotional content of the final broadcast is felt briefly.
   - The Almost can appear involuntarily during the sharing (color synchronization, 3-4 seconds).
   - Pem enters the water to receive the biological imprint with deliberate, practiced economy. Pem's translucent skin briefly flickers with the dead frog's colors.
   - Water is cycled through channels. Remnants disperse into the settlement's water system.
   - Community disperses within the hour. Grief is processed; daily life resumes.

4. **Quill caught a faint trace of Lenn's chemical signature through wet stone** (not through direct water contact). His palms were flat on wet rock at the pool's rim. The sensation was "mild, specific, and slightly annoyed" -- irritation about the water temperature. This establishes that the chemical sharing can weakly bridge to humans through moisture contact, even without direct skin immersion.

5. **Oona's line on grief:** "What else would there be? He was here. Now he is in the water and the archive. Holding the shape of a dissolved puddle is unkind to the puddle and to yourself." -- establishes this as a domestic, personal articulation of the Wet Way's grief philosophy.

6. **Vesh to young frog:** "You carry a drop of everyone who dissolves near you. This is why old frogs are heavy with the dissolved." -- new Wet Way teaching, not previously stated in canon. Implies accumulative chemical imprinting across a lifetime.

7. **Talla on efficient grief:** "Efficient grief is what you get when you have thirty years and cannot afford to spend three of them crying." -- new line, consistent with her character, connecting biology to philosophy from the skeptic's angle.

8. **Talla's critique of the mourning's speed** (lines 179-181): "I grieve the system that makes this necessary. The speed. The moving on. Some of us would like to sit with the dead a little longer, but there is a Pruning to prepare for..." -- Talla's dissidence extends to the pace of grief, not just the breathing circles. She is not criticizing the ritual itself, but the broader system that compresses mourning.

9. **Hart's only line in the chapter:** "That was the most efficient grief I have ever seen." -- Hart's silence during the ritual itself and her single post-ritual observation are character beats: she held back her clinical instincts for the duration.

10. **Marsh opened and then closed her notebook during the ritual** (lines 160-161). She began documenting and then stopped. This is a continuation of her arc from observer to participant.

11. **Lenn's body described as "perhaps six centimeters" and "uniform grey" after death** (line 79). Colors fade to grey upon death. Signaling skin goes quiet.

12. **Water cycling mechanics:** Fresh water enters through built-in channels; mourning pool contents flow outward into the settlement's general water system. The diluted chemical remnants become part of communal water (bathing, drinking, tadpole pools). This is described as "not symbolic. It is plumbing. It is also the Wet Way in four pipes and a drainage channel" (line 147).
