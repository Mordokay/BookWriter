### MODERATE

#### Issue 1
- **Severity:** MODERATE
- **Location:** Line 35 -- "the clouds that had carried the rain having thinned to nothing"
- **Problem:** Ch. 4's close describes rain "thinning to fine mist" (not stopping, and not clouds clearing). The timeline says "Light intermittent rain is present during portions of the journey. It begins during Ch. 3-4 territory and stops. May return briefly in Ch. 6-7." The ch05 brief says "Rain has stopped (or paused)." However, line 35 says the clouds "thinned to nothing" and the sky is "dark, clear." This is stronger than "stopped or paused." If the clouds have completely cleared, it needs to be reconciled with the possibility of rain returning in Ch. 6-7. Clouds that have "thinned to nothing" would need to reform for rain to return.
- **Why it matters:** The weather system needs to be plausible across chapters. If the sky is completely clear here, a return of rain in Ch. 6-7 requires new cloud cover to form, which is meteorologically possible (Pacific Northwest weather is variable) but should be handled carefully.
- **Suggested fix:** Soften the cloud description slightly. Instead of "thinned to nothing," consider "thinned enough to let the moon through" or "drawn apart." This preserves the moonlit clearing's visual impact while leaving room for clouds to return. Alternatively, describe the gap as localized: the canopy gap reveals a patch of clear sky, but clouds may still be present elsewhere. The current text says "the sky was visible: dark, clear" -- this could be narrowed to "the sky visible through the gap was clear" to keep the description local.

#### Issue 2
- **Severity:** MODERATE
- **Location:** Line 55 -- "deep chestnut-brown, the coat thick and winter-dense"
- **Problem:** Roosevelt elk in late autumn in the Pacific Northwest would indeed have a winter coat. However, the color "deep chestnut-brown" may be slightly more appropriate for summer/early fall pelage. In winter, Roosevelt elk tend toward a darker brown to grayish-brown body with a notably lighter, tawny rump patch. The "chestnut-brown" is not wrong, but for a winter-dense coat the color could be slightly darker or grayer to be maximally accurate. This is a minor natural-history point that most readers would not catch, but the world.md instruction to "use the forest's real biology when possible" makes it worth noting.
- **Why it matters:** The story prides itself on naturalistic accuracy. The stag is described in vivid detail, and the color is prominent.
- **Suggested fix:** Optional. "Deep brown" or "dark brown" without "chestnut" would be slightly more accurate for winter pelage. The "silver where the moonlight caught it" detail on the guard hairs is excellent and would still work with a darker base color.

#### Issue 3
- **Severity:** MODERATE
- **Location:** Line 15 -- "a measured cadence she hadn't heard since before the swarm"
- **Problem:** The clinical voice's attempted re-engagement in this chapter is described as something Ada "hadn't heard since before the swarm." But in Ch. 4, the clinical voice stirred and attempted a sentence ("Okay. You crossed the stream. You're on the other side. You can --"). Ada did hear a version of the clinical voice in Ch. 4, even though it failed to complete its thought. The Ch. 5 opening implies the voice has been entirely absent since before the swarm (Ch. 2), which contradicts the Ch. 4 partial re-emergence.
- **Why it matters:** The clinical voice degradation arc is carefully tracked across chapters. Describing the Ch. 5 voice as unheard "since before the swarm" skips over the Ch. 4 stirring, which could confuse attentive readers.
- **Suggested fix:** Revise to acknowledge the Ch. 4 partial attempt. For example: "a measured cadence she hadn't heard since before the swarm, not really, not the incomplete sentence that had stirred on the far bank and failed" or simply "a measured cadence she had not heard in hours, not since the voice's last attempt at the far bank crumbled before it could finish." This preserves the sense of return while honoring the Ch. 4 moment.

#### Issue 4
- **Severity:** MODERATE
- **Location:** Line 149 -- "Her arms, clenched at her sides with fists tight"
- **Problem:** Earlier in the encounter, Ada's hands are consistently described as gripping the backpack straps (lines 23, 73, 85). Lines 23 and 97 show her hands on the straps. But at line 149, her arms are suddenly "at her sides with fists tight," and by line 153, "Her hand reached for something to brace against" and "Her hand closed on air." The transition from hands-on-straps to arms-at-sides is not narrated. The hands moved off the straps at some unspecified point during the standoff.
- **Why it matters:** The fist/hand motif and Ada's physical positioning are tracked with high precision. The reader who has been imagining Ada gripping the straps would notice the unexplained shift to fists at her sides.
- **Suggested fix:** Add a brief transitional beat earlier in the standoff showing the hands leaving the straps. For example, somewhere around lines 105-110, a sentence like "Her hands had dropped from the straps at some point, she couldn't say when" or simply narrate the release: "Her hands left the backpack straps and hung at her sides, closing into fists." Alternatively, adjust line 149 to "Her arms, still clenched on the straps" if the intention is to keep the hands on the straps throughout. However, the reaching-for-something gesture at line 153 works better if her hands are free, so the first option (transitional beat) is preferable.

- **Problem:** This is a simile worth noting: "like a door closing in an empty house." It is evocative and works well.
