1. **Timeline entry divergence (pre-existing, not introduced by Ch 14).** The timeline.md entry for Day 21 / Ch 14 describes events that do not appear in the chapter as written: "Krrik's revised models project growth exceeding anything in the accessible archive. Voss grows through a residential section wall. Displacement operators run increasingly complex coordination exercises. Krrik privately tells Reed the biological modulators may not be sufficient. Reed's surface observation data becomes a critical input to Krrik's modulation research." None of these events are dramatized in Ch 14. The chapter instead follows the chapter-outline.md brief, which calls for displacement travel, the monitoring station, and surface voss growth. The timeline entry appears to describe background/off-screen events or content that was redistributed during planning. **Recommendation:** Update timeline.md Day 21 entry to match the chapter's actual content (displacement, monitoring station, surface voss growth), or add a note that the Krrik-Reed events occur off-page on this day.

2. **Vale's reference to "what Krrik had told Reed" (line 171).** Vale thinks: "He was thinking about what Krrik had told Reed. About growth rates. About projections that kept failing because the voss kept exceeding them." This is grounded in established canon -- Ch 9 (Reed POV in Krrik's lab) establishes Krrik saying "the models are failing" and growth is anomalous (ch09 lines 59-65), and Reed relays information at the Ch 6 debrief (ch06 line 203). However, we have no on-page scene where Vale was directly present for Krrik telling Reed about projections failing. He would know this from group debriefs. This is plausible but slightly loose -- it reads as if Vale personally heard Krrik speak. **Recommendation:** No change required; Vale knowing this from Reed's debriefs is natural. Note for future reference only.

3. **Talla's "business at the station" framing.** Talla claims she has "business at the station" (line 13), and the guide immediately contradicts her: "You do not" (line 15). This is a strong character beat. However, no explanation is given for why Talla has access to accompany them on the displacement trip at all. In a civilization where breathing circle attendance is tracked and dissidents are "gently set aside," Talla's ability to join a guided displacement excursion to a monitoring station without coordination approval is slightly unexplained. **Recommendation:** Minor. Talla's freedom of movement has been consistent throughout (she moved freely in Ch 10, Ch 13). The frogs tolerate her, they do not restrict her. No fix needed, but this is worth noting as part of the broader "dissent is tolerated" pattern.

4. **"The tracking Hart had found in the quiet alcove with the logging frog" (line 173).** Vale's internal reference to Hart's Ch 13 discovery is accurate -- Hart found the attendance tracking station in Ch 13. However, the phrase "quiet alcove" slightly understates what was described in Ch 13 (a "recessed" alcove, described as easy to miss). Minor word-choice variance, not a factual error. **Recommendation:** No change needed.

## New Facts Established in Ch 14

1. **Displacement experience details.** The displacement process involves standing on a platform of six wet stones connected by fungal threading. The traveler experiences complete dissolution and reassembly. During the transit, the traveler temporarily loses their name/identity. The effect is briefer on subsequent trips ("the body learns"). The displacement field carries "residue" -- sensory traces of previous travelers, described by the guide as "like a fingerprint on a doorknob."

2. **Marsh's memory fragment.** During displacement, Marsh catches a residual impression: sunset over canopy from a low perspective, warmth of stone against permeable skin, a feeling of great age and patience. Origin unknown.

3. **The displacement guide.** Unnamed, compact (~4 cm), flat muted coloring that does not shift or signal. Deadpan, practiced. Her skin flickers once when Talla asks about inconvenient observations -- otherwise completely controlled. Treats displacement with bureaucratic boredom.

4. **Monitoring station.** A long, low alcove carved into a root system at an outer location reachable by displacement. Staffed by six frogs. Contains biological sensors embedded in root walls (twelve visible) and a small thinking moss array nicknamed "Mott" (who once classified a toucan as a military reconnaissance aircraft due to a "nutrient fluctuation"). Coverage: approximately nine hundred square kilometers. Monitors radio frequencies, satellite transmissions, ground-penetrating radar surveys, logging activity, mining prospectors, research expeditions, indigenous community movements, and individual human behavioral patterns. Real-time data integration.

5. **Station frog details.** An olive-green monitor with brown flecks. A frog who tends the moss (disputes that Mott has "moods"). A frog on break. A frog named Giss who works night shift and decorates her nesting hollow with dried insects in geometric patterns (one bright wing fixed with resin). A frog who has carved private poetry into the root wall -- subject: "distance."

6. **Monitoring station reports to "Coordination" (i.e., Noll's office).** The monitor refuses to answer Talla's question about who reviews the reviewers. The guide says: "Nobody. That is the point."

7. **Surface voss growth observed.** Root systems swollen, pushing soil upward in regular ridges. Soil warm to touch (internal metabolic heat, ~5 degrees above ambient per Marsh). Trees stressed: canopy gaps, tilted trunks, exposed root balls. Faint luminescence through soil cracks. This specific manifestation extends approximately six kilometers along a root corridor. Forty-seven active growth zones tracked in the Amazon basin alone. Siberian and African settlements report similar patterns. No global figures available.

8. **Talla's political observation.** "Every generation, they push it back. It costs them. They push it back. It grows again. And every time, the response requires more coordination. More merging. More control... The emergency is always real. The power it justifies is always permanent."

9. **Vale's private displacement response.** For a few seconds during dissolution, Vale experienced the absence of his name as relief, not terror. He does not share this with anyone. This connects to Wet Way themes (dissolution as temporary selfhood) without forcing the theology.

10. **Return displacement is easier.** The guide's line: "The body learns that dissolving is temporary. Whether the self agrees is a personal matter."
