# Continuity Review: Chapter 6 "Skin"

## Summary

Chapter 6 is a strong, emotionally precise draft that handles the snake encounter, shame surfacing, and frog's third appearance with restraint and physical specificity. The vast majority of continuity points are correct. The chapter maintains Ada's accumulated physical state, honors the emotional progression from the stag encounter, deploys the frog and snake within their established rules, and plants the forest object subtly. There are no critical continuity failures. A handful of moderate issues require attention, mostly involving a water bottle location inconsistency, an owl forward-reference that breaks POV, and minor terminology matters.

## Issues Found

### CRITICAL (must fix before proceeding)

None.

### MODERATE (should fix in revision)

1. **Water bottle location inconsistency.** In Ch. 4 (line 69), the water bottle is in the backpack's main compartment ("The zipper on the main compartment resisted, stiff, then gave. Inside: the water bottle, wedged against the balled-up rain jacket."). In Ch. 6 (line 177), Ada reaches for it in "the backpack's side pocket." This is a direct physical contradiction. Ada put the rain jacket on in Ch. 4 and repacked the water bottle into the backpack, but the text does not establish that she moved the bottle to a side pocket. Either revise Ch. 6 to say "main compartment" or add a brief moment earlier (Ch. 4 or Ch. 5) where Ada moves the bottle for easier access.

2. **Owl forward-reference breaks POV and approaches symbol-decoding.** Line 93 reads: "She had registered the hand the way she registered everything, with the detailed, unflinching perception that the owl would later illuminate as its own wound." This is a proleptic narrator intrusion. The close third-person POV locked to Ada should not know what happens later in the story. Ada has not encountered the owl yet (Ch. 7). This also approaches decoding the owl's symbolism ("perception as wound"), which violates constraints.md ("Symbols are never decoded in the text") and the style-guide's POV rules. Recommend cutting the clause "that the owl would later illuminate as its own wound" and replacing with something grounded in the present moment, e.g., "with the detailed, unflinching perception she had never been able to turn off."

3. **Frog/toad terminology inconsistency within chapter.** On line 143, the creature is called "The toad" ("The toad was large and heavy"), but every other reference in the chapter (and throughout the series) uses "frog." While Anaxyrus boreas is technically a toad, the story has established "frog" as the consistent common-name reference. Using "toad" once in the middle of the chapter creates a jarring inconsistency. Recommend changing to "The frog was large and heavy" or, if the intent is to use the accurate term, commit to "toad" more consistently (which would require changing the entire series -- not recommended).

4. **Water bottle level arithmetic.** In Ch. 4, the bottle goes from half full to approximately one-third. In Ch. 6 (line 177), before drinking, the water is described as "reduced to a few inches at the bottom." Ada did not drink in Ch. 5. If the bottle was one-third full after Ch. 4, "a few inches" is plausible but vague. After drinking in Ch. 6, "maybe an inch at the bottom" remains. The running-state says bottle was "approximately one-third full" after Ch. 4. "A few inches at the bottom" for one-third of a standard bottle is reasonable but could be tightened. Consider: was the bottle sloshing around in the wet backpack during the stag encounter, potentially losing water? If not, clarify: "reduced to what had been a third of the bottle" or similar to maintain the tracking chain.

5. **Snake has two lines, not three as suggested in the brief's dialogue examples.** The brief (line 92) provides three example lines: "You know what you did," "You could have stayed," and "Tell me what you told yourself afterward." The draft uses only the first two. The brief says "two or three whispered lines," so two is within range and not a violation. However, the third line ("Tell me what you told yourself afterward") is more provocative and would add texture to the encounter. Consider whether adding it would strengthen the scene. This is a suggestion, not a required fix.

### MINOR (note for tracking)

1. **Filter words in prose.** Two filter constructions appear in the prose:
   - Line 93: "She had seen the hand. She had registered the hand..." -- the "seen/registered" construction is used deliberately here as part of a memory fragment, so it serves the scene. Borderline acceptable.
   - Line 141: "The frog was there before she noticed it." -- "she noticed" is a filter word per the style guide. Could be revised to "The frog was there before she saw it" or "The frog was simply there" or restructured.

2. **"She remembered" implicit in frog description.** Line 143 says "the same mottled olive-brown and gold she remembered from the nurse log and the wolf's clearing." The verb "remembered" is a mild filter. Consider: "the same mottled olive-brown and gold from the nurse log and the wolf's clearing" -- the recognition is implied by the detail.

3. **Coral fungi "branching like antlers" -- effective callback but check for over-explanation.** Line 17: "They branched like antlers. Everything branched like antlers tonight." The second sentence is Ada's interior awareness. This is close to the line of the character recognizing a pattern explicitly, but it stays on the right side (Ada is not interpreting symbolism, just noting a visual echo). Worth flagging for the literary review to confirm it does not feel too explicit.

4. **The chapter brief estimated 4,000-4,500 words; the draft prose runs approximately 5,550 words.** This exceeds the brief's estimate but falls well within the constraints.md target of 5,000-8,000 words per chapter. Since constraints.md is the hard constraint, the word count is within range. The extra length serves the scene well -- the snake encounter and frog breathing sequence both benefit from the space.

5. **"The way" simile connector.** The construction appears several times in the prose (e.g., "the way nausea comes," "the way she moved," "the way a bowl holds water"). The style guide flags this connector as something to watch. The humanizer pass on Ch. 4 reduced it. Ch. 6 uses it frequently but each instance is distinct and functional. Worth noting for the humanizer pass.

6. **Legs folding callback references wolf's clearing (Ch. 3) and "the far bank of the stream" (Ch. 4).** Line 75: "Her legs folded the way they had folded at the wolf, the way they had folded on the far bank of the stream." In Ch. 4, Ada's legs do not fold in the same way -- she crawls over the bank lip and collapses. In Ch. 3, she kneels/collapses at the wolf. The "far bank of the stream" reference is slightly imprecise: Ada did not fold her legs there; she collapsed face-down, then pushed herself up to all fours, then eventually stood. Consider revising to reference a moment where her legs genuinely gave out rather than the stream bank, or adjust to "the way her body had given beneath her at the wolf, the way it had dropped to the moss on the far bank."

## Recommendations

1. **Fix the water bottle location** (side pocket vs. main compartment). Simplest fix: change "side pocket" to "main compartment" on line 177, or add a brief beat showing Ada moved the bottle for easier access.

2. **Remove the owl forward-reference** on line 93. Replace "the detailed, unflinching perception that the owl would later illuminate as its own wound" with language that stays in Ada's present tense, e.g., "the detailed, unflinching perception she had never been able to turn off" or "the detailed, unflinching perception that cost her more than she had ever been willing to name."

3. **Harmonize frog/toad terminology** on line 143. Change "The toad was large and heavy" to "The frog was large and heavy" to maintain series consistency.

4. **Consider tightening the "legs folding" callback** on line 75 to be more precise about what happened at the far bank in Ch. 4.

5. **Run the humanizer pass** to check for "the way" connector density and any remaining filter words ("she noticed," "she remembered").

6. **Consider whether adding the third snake line** ("Tell me what you told yourself afterward") would deepen the encounter. The current two lines are effective, but the third would add a provocative invitation that Ada refuses -- which would underscore her inability to articulate the shame even to herself.

7. **The forest object planting** is slightly longer than the brief's "half a sentence" instruction (it runs about two full sentences). It is still subtle and effective, but the revision pass could consider compressing it to a single sentence to make the moment even more invisible.
