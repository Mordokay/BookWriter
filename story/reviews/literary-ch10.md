## Strengths

**1. Info-dump check: PASSED with distinction.**
Every piece of medical information arrives through dialogue and investigation. Hart asks questions; practitioners answer. Reed writes in his notebook; readers see him writing, not what he writes. The permeability trade-off is delivered entirely through the old practitioner's speech and Hart's reactions. The debrief recaps through Hart's voice to her colleagues, not through narrator summary. At no point does the narrator step in to explain frog biology. This is exactly what the brief demanded and what the style guide requires.

**2. The surgery horror sequence is the chapter's best comedy.**
The escalation from "You cut the body open" to "On purpose" to "And the patient agrees to this?" to Hart's deadpan "The patient is unconscious" to Tivven's appalled "That is not better" is perfectly paced. The comedy works because both sides are genuinely horrified. The second practitioner's grudging respect ("I would also go through the wall. I would hate it. But I would do it.") adds dimensionality -- it prevents the scene from becoming one-sided mockery. The old practitioner's arrival with "All medicine is violence managed with good intentions" is the chapter's single best line: it silences the room, shifts the register, and then does not resolve anything. The argument resumes anyway, as it should.

**3. The Oona-Hart-Krrik triangle is genuinely funny and structurally smart.**
Three different epistemologies collide: Hart's clinical empiricism, Oona's anecdotal pragmatism, and Krrik's accidental validation. The comedy works because Krrik does not realize he has changed the argument. His "I mention this because it is true, not because it is relevant to anyone's argument" is perfect Krrik -- technically precise, socially oblivious. Hart's internal reaction ("She had wanted to be right. Oona had wanted to be right. Krrik had made them both right and neither of them happy, and he had done it without noticing.") is the kind of observation that earns a smile and reveals character simultaneously. The line "She was fairly sure he had not noticed" is a good comic button.

**4. The permeability trade-off is delivered cleanly and with restraint.**
The old practitioner's speech is the chapter's emotional center, and it avoids over-explanation. The key line -- "A sealed frog is a safe frog. A sealed frog is also a deaf frog, a mute frog, and a frog who will never feel another mind touch theirs. We chose. We chose a long time ago." -- is specific, concrete, and lands hard. The brief's warning ("Do not over-explain the permeability trade-off") is respected. Hart's reaction is physical (she straightens up) and observational (the fungal film glistens), not philosophical. The narrator does not editorialize. Clean.

**5. Hart's POV is distinct from Reed (Ch 9) and Marsh (Ch 7-8).**
Hart thinks in contamination vectors, risk assessments, and clinical categories. She counts fungal species. She notes the practitioners' muted-green-as-white-coat detail. She identifies the compound on Tivven's forelimb as "thick and dark green" rather than analyzing its biochemistry (that is Reed's job). She writes notes in French when something disturbs her, a character-specific detail that appears once, does its work, and does not repeat. Her cynicism is her comedy and her lens, and the chapter sustains both without making her one-note. Crucially, Hart is not just cynical -- she is uncomfortable about being unable to dismiss the frogs, and that discomfort is what gives the chapter its closing weight. This is a different register from Reed's intellectual vertigo (Ch 9) and Marsh's participatory warmth (Ch 7-8).

**6. Background frogs populate the treatment chambers.**
The crushed-digit frog, the respiratory-congestion frog, the fermentation-swallower, the passing frog who comments on the swallower ("He is always swallowing things. Last season it was a sample jar lid"), Brenk under his compress, the frog offering testimony about her seventy-four-season aunt -- the treatment chambers feel like a real, busy, communal medical space. The constraint requiring 3+ characters and populated scenes is thoroughly met. The two older practitioners arguing about dosing philosophy is a nice background detail that grounds the medical world without demanding attention.

**7. The debrief works as a closing beat.**
Hart's verdict -- "The frogs' relationship with disease is not negligence, it is philosophy. And I do not know which is worse" -- is the right ending line. It is dry, funny, genuinely disturbed, and entirely in Hart's voice. The brief's required presence of Vale, Quill, Reed, and Marsh is met. Quill's silence in the doorway is correctly handled: noted, not interpreted. Vale's single challenge ("That is philosophy, not medicine") prompts Hart's best extended speech. The debrief does not overstay.

## Issues

### Must Fix

**1. The old practitioner's speech on permeability is slightly too long and lists too many consequences.**

**Location:** Lines 139-143. The speech beginning "We breathe through our skin" and ending "We chose. We chose a long time ago."

The speech lists seven consequences of sealed skin: no chemical signaling, no color communication, no shared breathing, no synchronization, no merging, no being "ourselves, together." This is a list-of-seven trap -- it reads like a comprehensive catalogue of everything the canon files say about skin permeability, delivered through a character's mouth. The old practitioner's voice is otherwise excellent (deliberate, eerie, earned), but the middle of this speech tips into exposition-by-enumeration. The style guide warns against "lectures disguised as dialogue," and while this is not a lecture, the accumulation of consequences feels slightly systematic for a character who has been presented as someone who speaks like she is quoting something ancient.

**Fix:** Cut two or three items from the list. The speech is strongest at its bookends: "We breathe through our skin" and "It is how we are ourselves. Together." The middle items (chemical signaling, color communication, shared breathing, synchronization, merging) can be compressed into fewer, more evocative phrases. For example, collapse the signaling/communication/breathing into a single clause and keep "merging" as the culmination. The fewer items listed, the more weight each one carries. A line like "Seal the skin, and you stop everything that makes us what we are" does more than enumerating each capability individually.

**2. Hart's debrief speech partially restates the old practitioner's earlier dialogue.**

**Location:** Lines 249-277. Hart's explanation to the team covers: regeneration, cancer management, fungal infections, the permeability choice, the sealed-frog concept, and the old practitioner's health-as-connection philosophy. Most of this is material the reader already experienced in real time earlier in the chapter.

The debrief is structurally necessary (it brings all five biologists together and delivers Hart's verdict), but approximately 40% of the content is recap. Lines 249-253 (regeneration, cancer, pain management) restate what the reader learned in lines 35-51. Lines 256-267 (the permeability choice) restate lines 135-148. Lines 273-274 (the old practitioner's quote about health) restate line 241. The reader has already absorbed these facts. The debrief should add new perspective (Hart's interpretation, the group's reactions), not re-explain what was dramatized.

**Fix:** Compress the recap portions significantly. Hart can reference the facts without re-explaining them: "Their medicine is more advanced than ours. Regeneration. Cancer. Pain management through water chemistry Reed says is centuries ahead of anything we have." That is one sentence covering what currently takes five. Then move quickly to the new material: the philosophical interpretation, Hart's discomfort, and the group's differentiated reactions. The debrief should be approximately 30% shorter, with the saved space given to character reactions (Vale's narrowed eyes, Marsh's silence, Quill's stillness, Reed still writing) rather than information the reader already has.

**3. "With the X of someone who" simile construction appears too frequently.**

The following instances appear in the chapter:

- Line 4: "with the precision of a surgeon who expected the operating room to be filthy"
- Line 5: "in the small, steady hand of someone who had been documenting animal behavior in field conditions for fifteen years and did not intend to stop because the animals had started talking back"
- Line 29: "with the unhurried competence of people who had been doing this work for a long time and did not need to perform urgency"
- Line 233: "with the comfortable rhythm of a disagreement that had been running for years and would continue running until one of them died"
- Line 245: "with the practiced ease of a man who had been walking into rooms late his entire career and never apologized for it"

Five instances is too many. The Ch 8 revision log specifically notes reducing this construction from 11 to 5, so the project has already flagged this as a recurring pattern. Two or three per chapter is the maximum before it becomes a stylistic tic. The first instance (Hart's surgeon simile) is the strongest and most character-specific. The second (Marsh's hand) is good but very long. The third (practitioners' unhurried competence) is functional but generic. The fourth (dosing argument) is the weakest -- it is a near-duplicate of a construction that appeared in Ch 8 about the fermentation argument. The fifth (Vale arriving late) is good character work.

**Fix:** Keep the first (surgeon/filthy operating room -- it is Hart's voice), the second (Marsh's hand -- it sets the scene), and the fifth (Vale arriving late -- it is character-specific). Rewrite the third and fourth. For the practitioners: "The practitioners moved between patients without hurrying. They had been doing this a long time and did not need to perform urgency." For the dosing argument: simply cut the simile and let the argument's tone convey its longevity -- the sentence already says "two older practitioners argued quietly," which establishes the dynamic.

### Consider Fixing

**4. Sentence beginning "She had seen the same species on the corridor walls" (line 113) -- slight redundancy.**

Lines 112-113: "It was growing on the stone rim, a thin, persistent film of something pale and fuzzy. She had seen the same species on the corridor walls. On the food surfaces. On the edge of the communal bathing pools. It was everywhere in the settlement, and the practitioners were working directly beside it without any apparent concern."

The short-sentence staccato ("On the corridor walls. On the food surfaces. On the edge of the communal bathing pools.") is effective rhythmically, but "It was everywhere in the settlement" then restates what the list just demonstrated. Consider cutting "It was everywhere in the settlement" and going straight to "the practitioners were working directly beside it without any apparent concern." The list already says "everywhere." Saying it again is the prose explaining itself.

**5. "Chromatophore tells" are well-handled but one instance slightly over-explains.**

Line 53: "Tivven's spots flared with something between alarm and pity."

This is clean and accessible. But line 63 reads: "Her colors went through a slow cascade, greens to yellow to a pale, horrified amber." The phrase "horrified amber" assigns an emotion to a color in narrator voice. Throughout the book, the standard is to describe the color shift and let the reader (and the POV character) interpret. Consider: "greens to yellow to a pale amber that Hart read as horror." This keeps the interpretation in Hart's POV rather than the narrator's.

**6. The phrase "filed it" (not present in this chapter but the closing paragraph uses similar Marsh-like observation patterns).**

Line 107: "Marsh was watching the practitioners' colors with the focused attention she usually reserved for primate mothers handling their young."

This is Marsh-characteristic observation being described in Hart's POV, which is slightly off. Hart would not know what Marsh "usually reserved" her focused attention for unless Hart had observed this pattern before. It works as close-third-person shorthand between teammates who know each other well, but it does slightly blur the POV boundary. Consider grounding it more firmly in Hart's perception: "Marsh was watching the practitioners' colors with a focus Hart recognized from the field -- the same expression she wore watching primate mothers handle young."

**7. "Hart found herself in the unexpected position of agreeing with an alien medical practitioner" (line 187).**

"Found herself" is flagged in the humanizer checklist as a filter-word construction. The observation is good -- Hart's alliance with Tivven against Oona is the right comic beat -- but the phrasing distances the reader from the moment. Consider: "Hart was agreeing with an alien medical practitioner and disagreeing with a frog housewife about moss, which was not a scenario McGill had prepared her for." Dropping "found herself in the unexpected position of" tightens the sentence and keeps Hart's dry voice.

**8. "Flow, don't push" (line 153) -- the Wet Way moment is slightly signposted.**

"It sounded like medical advice. It also sounded like a prayer. Hart could not tell the difference, and she was beginning to suspect there was not one."

This is the narrator explaining the significance of the Wet Way blurring into medicine. The moment would be stronger if the observation stopped at "Hart could not tell the difference." The clause "and she was beginning to suspect there was not one" is Hart arriving at a conclusion the reader has already reached. Trust the reader. Cut the final clause.

## Comedy Assessment

**Strong. The chapter delivers genuine comedy through three distinct mechanisms:**

**1. Surgery horror (mutual incomprehension comedy):**
The exchange between Hart and the practitioners is the chapter's comedic centerpiece and it works completely. "On purpose?" / "On purpose." is the kind of exchange that makes a reader smile because both sides are being perfectly reasonable within their own framework. The second practitioner's "I would also go through the wall" provides the turn that prevents the scene from being one-sided. The comedy here reveals the book's core theme (biology shapes philosophy) while being genuinely funny. Best comedy beat: "The patient is unconscious." / "That is not better."

**2. Folk remedy argument (domestic comedy):**
Oona vs. Hart is a different register -- less horror, more neighborhood dispute. Oona's "Anecdote is what evidence looks like before scientists get involved and make it complicated" is her best line. Brenk accepting the compress without concern while two people argue over his body is a nice physical comedy detail. Krrik's drive-by validation is perfectly executed: he enters, dismisses, accidentally validates, and leaves without noticing. The comic architecture (setup: both sides entrenched -> disruption: Krrik's "however" -> aftermath: both sides frustrated for different reasons) is well-constructed.

**3. Background comedy (the treatment chambers as a living space):**
The fermentation-swallower, the passing frog's commentary ("Last season it was a sample jar lid"), the practitioner's lecture that sounds identical to every graduate-school safety talk, the frog offering testimony about her seventy-four-season aunt, the two practitioners arguing about dosing philosophy -- all of this makes the treatment chambers feel alive and funny without demanding the reader's primary attention. This is the background comedy the style guide asks for: specific details that reveal something structural about frog society.

**Missing comedy:** The brief mentions that Hart's growing discomfort should have "dry humor" even in the disturbing moments. The debrief mostly delivers this (Hart's sarcasm is intact), but the permeability revelation itself (lines 135-152) is played straight with no comedy beat. This is defensible -- the moment earns its seriousness -- but one small comic interruption (a background frog, a physical detail, something that reminds the reader this is happening in a room full of frogs sitting in pools) would keep the chapter's comedic texture consistent through its heaviest passage.

## Summary

Chapter 10 is a strong draft that achieves its central mission: making frog medicine viscerally real through Hart's clinical lens while delivering the book's sharpest expression of the permeability trade-off. The surgery horror sequence is the chapter's comic highlight and one of the funniest exchanges in the book so far. The Oona-Hart-Krrik triangle works as both comedy and characterization. Hart's POV is distinct from Reed and Marsh. The info-dump check passes cleanly. The prose is sharp, concrete, and accessible.

The three must-fix issues are targeted: (1) compress the old practitioner's permeability speech to avoid enumeration, (2) shorten the debrief to eliminate recap, and (3) reduce the "with the X of someone who" simile pattern from five to three instances. None of these require structural changes. The chapter's architecture, emotional arc, and comedic engine are all working. A single revision pass should bring it to final quality.
