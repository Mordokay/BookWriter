## Confirmed Strengths

1. **Sensory-first opening.** The chapter opens with the varied thrush's call and layers of forest sound, exactly as the style guide mandates ("Every scene opens through the senses. What does Ada smell, feel against her skin, hear in the undergrowth?"). Ada enters the frame only after the forest is established. This matches the chapter brief's directive to open with a sensory detail rather than interior thought.

2. **Species accuracy.** Botanical and zoological names are correct throughout: Pseudotsuga menziesii (Douglas fir), Psilocybe cyanescens (wavy cap), Fomes fomentarius (bracket fungi), Omphalotus olearius (jack-o'-lantern mushrooms), Anaxyrus boreas (Western toad), Polystichum munitum (sword fern implied by description). The blue bruising of psilocin on contact is chemically accurate. Luciferase/luciferin bioluminescence mechanism is correctly attributed.

3. **Frog appearance executed well.** The Western toad appears on the nurse log with correct physical description: mottled olive-brown and gold, pale cream dorsal stripe, gold eyes with horizontal pupils, size of Ada's fist, heavy and still. The frog does not speak (canon: "In early appearances (Ch. 1-2), the frog may not speak at all -- Ada has not gone deep enough into the trip"). It vanishes when Ada looks away, leaving only a damp impression. The appearance is naturalistic enough to avoid signposting, but charged enough to lodge with the reader. This matches the chapter brief's "balance between too obvious and too invisible."

4. **Ada's character voice.** The clinical interior voice is established clearly: she knows Latin names, she notes the psilocin oxidation as "a chemical fact she had read about and was now confirming," she tells herself "This is onset. This is normal. This is expected." She prepares for everything "thoroughly, clinically, as if knowledge were a kind of armor." This matches the character sheet's description of her performative composure and the chapter outline's note that the "clinical interior voice is still intact."

5. **Emotional arc matches brief.** The progression moves from controlled determination (the car, the walk) through the onset loosening to the final crack when the trail vanishes. Hands close into fists, jaw clenches, the clinical voice "dissolved in her hands like wet paper." The ending state matches: "Unsettled. The trail is gone. Confidence fractured."

6. **Phone placement.** Correctly rendered as deliberate: "face down, powered off, centered on the leather with the deliberateness of someone setting a table for one. She had decided this days ago." Matches canon exactly.

7. **Backpack inventory.** Water bottle, lighter, rain jacket, car keys -- all present and accounted for. Matches world.md and chapter brief.

8. **Paper bag discarded.** Ada folds the paper bag and sets it on moss beside the nurse log's base after eating the mushrooms. Properly planted for potential reappearance in Ch. 2-3 as a spatial-disorientation signal.

9. **No mycelium hum in Zone 1.** Correctly absent. The continuity notes at the chapter's end explicitly confirm this. Matches timeline.md ("Absent in Zone 1").

10. **Psilocybin phenomenology grounded in reality.** Surfaces breathing, color saturation, enhanced detail resolution, spatial disorientation, time approximation -- all are documented psilocybin effects, not pop-culture hallucination tropes. Matches world.md's phenomenology section.

11. **Trail vanishing preserves ambiguity.** The chapter offers both readings (psilocybin spatial disorientation vs. genuine forest rearrangement) without resolving either. The clinical voice's explanation about entorhinal cortex grid cell disruption is accurate pharmacology, and its failure to comfort Ada preserves the structural ambiguity demanded by world.md.

12. **Prose avoids banned cliches.** No "ancient trees loomed," no "shadows danced," no "eerie silence fell," no "she didn't know how long she had been walking," no "magic mushrooms," no "everything went black," no "she took a deep breath." The constraints.md banned list appears fully respected.

13. **Chapter ending matches brief.** Ends on a quiet, irreversible shift -- not a cliffhanger but a recognition that "the forest behind her is not the forest she walked through." The final image (the forest breathing around her, waiting) matches the style guide's preference for endings that "reverberate" rather than manufacture urgency.

14. **Filter words minimized.** The prose largely avoids "she felt," "she saw," "she noticed" constructions. The frog "watched her," the moss "grew more vivid," the bark "shifted." The style guide's directive is well observed.

## Issues

### Issue 1
- **severity:** low
- **location:** Paragraph 3 (the toad on the nurse log): "A Western toad, Anaxyrus boreas."
- **problem:** The chapter refers to the creature as both a "toad" (correctly, Anaxyrus boreas is a toad) and, in the continuity notes and foreshadowing section, as a "frog." This dual terminology is consistent with the canon files, which themselves alternate between "frog" and "toad" (e.g., world.md: "The Frog's Special Rules" then "Western toad (Anaxyrus boreas)"). However, within the prose itself, the creature is called only "toad," while the continuity notes header reads "Frog Appearance." This is not a contradiction -- the canon intentionally uses "frog" as the character name and "toad" as the species name -- but for internal chapter consistency, be aware that the prose-level terminology differs from the canon-level character name. This could become a continuity issue if later chapters call it "the frog" in prose without bridging the terminology.
- **why it matters:** Readers will notice if the same creature shifts from "toad" to "frog" between chapters without explanation. The canon's dual naming is structural, but the prose should either acknowledge the distinction or settle on one term.
- **suggested fix:** No fix needed for Ch. 1 itself -- "toad" is correct in-scene. In later chapters, when Ada's perception deepens under psilocybin, a natural shift from the clinical "toad" to the more mythic "frog" could serve the character arc (clinical language giving way to intuitive language). Flag this for the writer of Ch. 3 onward.

### Issue 2
- **severity:** low
- **location:** Paragraph 5 (bioluminescent mushrooms): "Jack-o'-lantern mushrooms -- Omphalotus olearius -- clustered along the log's underside"
- **problem:** Omphalotus olearius is native to Europe and is not found in the Pacific Northwest. The North American species is Omphalotus olivascens (western jack-o'-lantern). However, this naming is consistent with world.md and the chapter brief, both of which use "Omphalotus olearius." The error originates in the canon files, not in the chapter draft.
- **why it matters:** A mycologically literate reader could flag this. The bioluminescence detail is correct regardless of species -- both species exhibit it. The visual and sensory description (caps the size of small hands, gills catching bioluminescence, ember-through-green-glass color) is accurate to the genus.
- **suggested fix:** Consider updating world.md and all downstream references to Omphalotus olivascens for taxonomic accuracy in the Pacific Northwest setting. Alternatively, accept the species name as a minor liberty and leave it. Either way, this is a canon-level decision, not a chapter-level fix. The chapter is faithful to its canon as written.

### Issue 3
- **severity:** low
- **location:** Paragraph 5 (bioluminescent mushrooms): "Along a fallen log at the clearing's edge, something glowed."
- **problem:** Ada discovers the bioluminescent Omphalotus mushrooms after onset has begun but while she is still near the nurse log clearing. She then stands, walks to the log, crouches, and examines them. This is fine, but the chapter brief describes the first bioluminescence as appearing earlier, during the walk-in: "the first faint glow of jack-o'-lantern mushrooms along a fallen log." In the draft, the bioluminescence is not noticed until after the frog sighting and after onset begins. This is a minor resequencing.
- **why it matters:** The brief positions the first bioluminescent sighting as part of the pre-onset atmosphere. The draft positions it as part of the onset experience. The draft's version is arguably stronger -- the enhanced perception of onset makes the discovery more dramatic -- but it departs from the brief's intended sequence.
- **suggested fix:** No fix necessary. The draft's version works better narratively. The brief is a guide, not a transcript. Note the deviation for record-keeping.

### Issue 4
- **severity:** low
- **location:** Paragraph 1 (the nurse log): "Bracket fungi -- Fomes fomentarius, horse-hoof shaped, hard as wood -- climbed the near flank"
- **problem:** Fomes fomentarius is described in world.md as growing "on dead birch and beech." There is no birch or beech in this Pacific Northwest old-growth forest. The canon itself creates this inconsistency -- the species is listed in both the Zone 1 flora notes and the detailed fungi section, where the birch/beech substrate is mentioned. In the draft, Fomes fomentarius grows on the nurse log (a Douglas fir). Fomes fomentarius can grow on conifers, though it is more typically associated with hardwoods. In the PNW, Fomitopsis pinicola (red-belted conifer polypore) would be the more common horse-hoof bracket on a Douglas fir nurse log.
- **why it matters:** Minor mycological accuracy issue. Most readers will not notice, but the same mycologically literate readers who might catch the Omphalotus issue could catch this.
- **suggested fix:** Consider changing to Fomitopsis pinicola in world.md and the chapter. Or accept the liberty. The visual description (horse-hoof shaped, hard as wood, ascending tiers) is accurate to both species. This is again a canon-level decision.

### Issue 5
- **severity:** low
- **location:** Paragraph on insect sounds: "a layered drone of crickets and something higher, a rhythmic clicking that rose and fell"
- **problem:** Late autumn in the Pacific Northwest (the story's setting) is very late for cricket activity. Most cricket species in the PNW cease calling by mid-October. In late autumn (likely November), night-active insect sound would be sparse -- primarily winter-active species like winter crane flies, some moths, and a few beetle species. The "layered drone of crickets" is ecologically improbable for the stated season.
- **why it matters:** The insect chorus is an important ambient layer throughout the book. Timeline.md notes "The insect chorus changes through the night. Different species are active at different times." If the base insect population is established incorrectly for the season, it compounds across all 13 chapters.
- **suggested fix:** Replace "crickets" with a more seasonally appropriate sound source: the click-buzz of beetles, the whine of crane flies, or a more generic reference to "insects she could not identify." Alternatively, given that the forest is "subtly wrong" and exists on the boundary between physical and psychic reality, the cricket sounds could be one more signal that the forest is not quite a normal Pacific Northwest forest. If this reading is intended, no change is needed, but the choice should be deliberate.

### Issue 6
- **severity:** low
- **location:** Ada's description of the moss: "Individual sphagnum strands stood distinct"
- **problem:** Sphagnum moss grows in bogs and very wet, acidic environments. It is not the dominant moss on nurse logs in PNW old-growth forest. The moss on nurse logs would typically be Hylocomium splendens (stair-step moss), Leucolepis acanthoneuron, or Kindbergia oregana. The chapter brief mentions Hylocomium splendens for Zone 2, and world.md does not specify sphagnum in Zone 1.
- **why it matters:** This is a small botanical misplacement. Ada is characterized as someone who has "prepared for this the way she prepared for everything -- thoroughly, clinically" and who knows Latin names. Her perception identifying sphagnum on a nurse log is slightly inconsistent with her established botanical knowledge, since a prepared naturalist would know sphagnum belongs in bogs.
- **suggested fix:** Replace "sphagnum strands" with a more location-appropriate moss, or simply describe the moss without naming the genus. Under onset, Ada's clinical naming faculty may be loosening, which could justify a vaguer description.

### Issue 7
- **severity:** low
- **location:** Chapter continuity notes, "Backpack contains: water bottle (half full), lighter, rain jacket, car keys."
- **problem:** The prose itself mentions only "water bottle, lighter, rain jacket, car keys" when Ada shoulders the backpack. The chapter brief and canon specify the water bottle is "half full." The prose does not mention the water level at this point. This is not a contradiction -- the prose does not say it is full -- but the specific detail of "half full" should ideally be established in the text, since timeline.md tracks water consumption precisely across chapters.
- **why it matters:** The water bottle's level is tracked as a continuity element. Establishing it as half full in Ch. 1 creates the baseline for Ch. 4 and Ch. 6 drinks. Without the detail, later references to diminishing water supply lack their anchor point.
- **suggested fix:** Add a brief physical detail when Ada drinks water -- something like "the bottle was half full" or a description that implies partial volume (the water sloshing, the weight of it). This is a minor insertion.
