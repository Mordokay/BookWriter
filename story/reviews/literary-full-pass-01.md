# Full-Manuscript Literary Review

- scope reviewed: Chapters 1-13 (full manuscript)
- overall impression: strong

## Cross-Chapter Patterns

### Repetition Across Chapters

The most significant repetition issue visible only at manuscript scale is the recurrence of certain structural phrases, sentence shapes, and conceptual formulations that individually feel fresh but collectively form a noticeable fingerprint across 13 chapters.

**1. "The way" simile connector.** Per-chapter reviews caught this and each chapter reduced its count, but at manuscript scale the pattern still accumulates. Across 13 chapters, even 2-3 instances per chapter yields 26-39 total uses. The construction itself is fine in moderation, but a reader encountering "the way water closes," "the way thoughts peel away," "the way a building settles," "the way a bell lives in the metal" across thousands of pages of continuous reading begins to hear the authorial machinery. Direction: reduce further in Chs. 2, 3, 6, and 8, where counts are highest. Reserve the construction for the manuscript's strongest similes and vary connectors ("as," "like," or restructured sentences) elsewhere.

**2. "Not X. Not Y. [Something else.]" negation cascades.** This rhetorical move appears in nearly every chapter. It is effective when used for emphasis ("Not the silence of quiet. The silence of absence." -- Ch. 3), but it has become a structural tic across the manuscript. The pattern appears in descriptions of the wolf, the stag, the snake, the owl, the boar, the spider, the frog, the clearing, the silence, the tears, the grief, the composure, and Ada's physical state. At manuscript scale, this starts to feel like a prose engine the author reaches for whenever something needs to be defined by what it is not. Direction: conduct a full-manuscript search for sentences beginning with "Not" that follow another sentence beginning with "Not." Reduce total count by approximately 30-40%. Keep the strongest instances (the wolf's silence in Ch. 3, the three kinds of tears in Ch. 11, the stream reflection in Ch. 12). Cut or restructure the rest into affirmative descriptions.

**3. "Something" as vague referent.** Across the manuscript, "something" appears at extremely high frequency as a placeholder for sensations, emotions, and perceptions Ada cannot name. This is thematically appropriate for a psychedelic journey where language fails, and many instances are well-deployed. But at manuscript scale, the word's recurrence (estimated 150+ uses across 13 chapters) dilutes its power. When everything is "something," nothing is specific. The style guide's first rule is "prefer specificity over vague drama." Direction: audit "something" across all chapters. Replace approximately 30% with either a more specific descriptor or a restructured sentence that presents the sensation directly without the hedging indefinite pronoun.

**4. The forest-absorbing-its-creatures departure formula.** Each animal encounter ends with a variation of: the animal walks into the darkness, its body merges with the forest, the gap seals, and the clearing is empty. This is deliberate (the animals are manifestations that the forest reabsorbs), but by the boar departure in Ch. 9, the pattern has become so predictable that the reader can see the next departure coming paragraphs in advance. The spider's quieter departure (Ch. 10) is a welcome variation, but it arrives late. Direction: vary at least two of the earlier departures. The snake (Ch. 6) could simply not be there when Ada looks up. The stag's departure already has the antler-shedding differentiation, which works. The wolf and owl departures could be shortened. The formula does not need to be eliminated, but it needs more variation within the template.

**5. "Her body knew before her mind did" / body-before-mind formulations.** This concept is central to the manuscript's philosophy (somatic knowledge over cognitive processing), and it earns its first use in Ch. 3. But the formulation recurs in some version across nearly every chapter: "her body registered it before her mind," "the body decided," "something her body already knew," "arrived not as thought but as a physical sensation." At manuscript scale, this becomes a philosophical refrain that risks turning into a thesis statement repeated in every encounter. Direction: trust the reader to absorb the body-mind theme from the first three instances. After Ch. 5, present the somatic knowledge directly (show the body acting) without the explicit narrative commentary about the body knowing before the mind. The reader will understand. Let the prose demonstrate the principle rather than announcing it.

**6. Encounter structure repetition.** Every animal encounter follows a similar arc: (a) environmental hush or shift, (b) animal appears, (c) confrontation/standoff, (d) Ada's body gives out, (e) animal speaks, (f) animal departs, (g) aftermath/processing. This structure is most rigid in Chs. 3 (wolf), 5 (stag), and 9 (boar). It is effectively broken in Chs. 6 (snake, where the shedding replaces the standoff), 7 (owl, where the confrontation is perceptual rather than spatial), and 10 (spider, where Ada walks into the web rather than facing an animal across a clearing). The early-to-mid chapters would benefit from more structural variation. Direction: in revision, consider whether the wolf encounter (Ch. 3) could begin differently from the stag (Ch. 5), perhaps with less formal "clearing + silence + creature at the edge" staging.

### Pacing Arc

The pacing across the full manuscript is one of its strongest qualities. The three-phase structure (Descent: Chs. 1-3; Confrontation: Chs. 4-10; Integration: Chs. 11-13) is executed with confidence.

**Where it grips:**
- Ch. 1 is an exceptional opening. The measured slowness of Ada's preparation, the precise sensory detail of the onset, and the sudden disorientation when the forest rearranges create a controlled build that hooks the reader through earned unease rather than cheap tension.
- Ch. 2 (swarm) escalates effectively and delivers the first physical stakes. The running-through-devil's-club sequence is visceral and kinetic.
- Ch. 3 (wolf) is the tightest chapter. The circling-and-tightening geometry creates almost unbearable tension.
- Ch. 9 (boar) is the emotional peak. The three-charge destruction followed by the three waves of grief is structurally perfect. The boar lying beside Ada is the manuscript's most powerful image.

**Where it sags:**
- Ch. 4 (crossing) is a necessary valley chapter, but the middle section (stream crossing and far-bank recovery) runs approximately 15% longer than it needs to. The inventory of Ada's physical state on the far bank, while grounding and necessary for continuity, contains details that could be compressed. The composure-reassembling passage at the chapter's end is essential thematic setup, but it arrives after the reader has been in a lower-tension state for some time. Direction: tighten the far-bank recovery by approximately 200 words, focusing the cuts on Ada's physical self-assessment.
- Ch. 5 (stag) takes slightly too long to reach the confrontation. The opening passage of the clinical voice's re-engagement, while thematically necessary, stretches for about three paragraphs too many before the clearing arrives. The standoff itself is excellent.
- Ch. 8 (roots) is the longest chapter and risks losing momentum. The mycelium revelation and frog teaching are essential, but the chapter could lose approximately 500 words from the middle passages (the extended sitting-in-the-hollow section) without sacrificing any essential content. The hum description, in particular, has been established by this point and does not need another full-paragraph rendering.
- Ch. 10 (spider) is emotionally quieter than Ch. 9, which is correct, but the web-struggle section repeats the lesson of "fighting feeds it" too explicitly. The callback to prior encounters is handled well in the prose ("the swarm had taught it... the wolf had taught it"), but the passage that follows over-explains the web's mechanism. Trust the accumulated body-knowledge of the reader.

**Where it lands:**
- Ch. 11 (dissolve) is perfectly paced. The dissolution is neither rushed nor dragged. The present-tense passage is brief and controlled, exactly as the style guide requests.
- Chs. 12-13 earn their quietness. The pace opens beautifully. The dawn stream scene is the manuscript's most quietly affecting passage. The closing is precise and complete without being pat.

### Tonal Consistency

The tonal arc is one of the manuscript's achievements. The progression from eerie disorientation (Ch. 1) through mounting dread (Chs. 2-3) to identity-level confrontation (Chs. 5-7) to grief (Ch. 9) to stillness and integration (Chs. 11-13) feels intentional and earned.

**Where the tone is strongest:**
- The shift from external threat (swarm, wolf) to internal exposure (stag, snake, owl) is seamless. The reader transitions from fear-for-Ada to something more complex without a jarring gear change.
- The boar chapter (Ch. 9) maintains emotional rawness without melodrama. The grief stays grounded in sensory fragments rather than expository backstory. This is exactly what the style guide demands.
- Chs. 12-13 achieve the "quiet exhale" promised in theme.md. The warmth and tenderness feel earned rather than imposed.

**Where the tone wavers:**
- Ch. 4's composure-reassembling passage ("You can handle this") risks sounding slightly too knowing in its narrative commentary. The line "She did not recognize it as armor. It just felt more like herself" is excellent. But the passage that precedes it ("The raw, gasping creature on the moss had been an aberration...") editorializes slightly more than the close-POV demands. We are inside Ada's head; the passage should feel like Ada's self-narrative, not the narrator's commentary on it.
- Ch. 7 (owl) pushes the intensity to a point where Ada's weeping, combined with the glass-wall imagery and the chapter's extended perceptual distortion, risks tipping from "emotionally raw" into "overwhelming." This is subjective, but a first read through seven consecutive high-intensity chapters will tax even an engaged reader. The owl chapter could benefit from one additional beat of physical grounding (a sensory detail unrelated to the confrontation) to give the reader a breath.
- The spider's voice in Ch. 10 ("You built this. Every strand is something you told yourself") is the most didactic line of dialogue in the manuscript. It is saved by the spider's delivery (quiet, factual) and by the fact that Ada's body has already understood this. But it comes close to the style guide's prohibition against animals explaining their own symbolism. Direction: consider softening the second sentence. The first ("You built this") is perfect.

### Voice and Dialogue Consistency

**Ada's internal voice** evolves appropriately across the manuscript. The clinical register in Ch. 1 is distinct and convincing. Its progressive erosion through Chs. 2-7 is well-tracked. The death of the clinical voice in Ch. 8 ("I need to find" failing to complete) is precisely the right moment. The replacement voice (sparse, somatic, honest) in Chs. 9-13 is a genuine shift, not merely the same voice with fewer words.

**Ada's spoken words** form a satisfying arc: "Stop" (Ch. 2, resistance) -> "I'm fine" (Ch. 5, deflection) -> "I know" (Ch. 7, acknowledgment) -> "I don't know" / "That I'd fall through" (Ch. 8, vulnerability) -> sub-verbal sounds only (Ch. 9) -> near-silence (Chs. 10-13). This arc is visible only at manuscript scale and is one of the book's subtle achievements.

**The frog's voice** is consistent and well-modulated. It grows from absence (Ch. 1) to terse guidance ("This way," Ch. 3) to warmth ("You're still here," Ch. 6) to teaching ("Solid ground doesn't have to be hard. It just has to hold," Ch. 8) to witness ("There you are," Ch. 11) to near-silence ("Almost morning," Ch. 12). The frog speaks slightly too much in Ch. 8 (seven lines). This is justified by the chapter's function as the teaching chapter, but 5-6 lines would achieve the same effect with more economy.

**The animal-demon voices** are distinct from each other and consistent with the style guide's prohibition against human conversation in animal costume. The strongest voices are the wolf ("I'm tired too" -- devastating in context), the stag ("You can put it down. You won't. But you can." -- perfect tonal precision), and the boar ("You never mourned" -- three words that carry the weight of the entire manuscript's emotional architecture). The weakest voice is the spider's, whose second line ("Every strand is something you told yourself") comes closest to explaining its own symbolism. The owl's questions ("What do you see?" etc.) are effective but less memorable than the other animals' statements.

## What Is Working (Manuscript-Level Strengths)

**1. Physical continuity as emotional record.** The devil's club scratches from Ch. 2 persist and are referenced in every subsequent chapter. The torn jeans, the waterlogged boots, the rain jacket -- every physical detail accumulates into a somatic record of Ada's journey. This is visible only at manuscript scale. The reader who has been tracking the scratches for twelve chapters feels the weight of Ada's night in a way that no individual chapter could achieve. When Ada examines the scratches by the dawn stream in Ch. 12, the passage resonates with ten chapters of accumulated reference. This is meticulous and effective.

**2. The fist-to-open-hands motif.** The arc from involuntary fists (Ch. 1: "Her hands were fists. She had not decided to make them fists") through exhaustion-opening (Ch. 2), attempted loosening that fails (Ch. 4-5), hands holding self (Ch. 6), hands open with nothing to grip (Ch. 7), hands on mycelium (Ch. 8), hands clawing earth in grief (Ch. 9), to open hands at Ada's sides in Ch. 13 is one of the most controlled and quietly powerful physical motifs in any contemporary manuscript. It is never explained. It is never named as a symbol. It simply unfolds across 13 chapters in a way that is invisible on first read and luminous on reread.

**3. The varied thrush bookend.** The opening sentence of Ch. 1 ("A bird called once from somewhere high in the Douglas fir canopy: a single sustained note, hollow and metallic, like a finger drawn along the rim of a wet glass") returns in Ch. 12 and Ch. 13 with the exact same descriptive phrase. This structural symmetry is earned, not forced. The dusk-to-dawn frame gives the manuscript a shape that feels inevitable. The thrush "did not know this, and that was part of it" (Ch. 13) is one of the book's finest sentences.

**4. The three kinds of tears (Chs. 7, 9, 11).** Each crying episode is physically and emotionally distinct: the owl tears are capacity/overflow, the boar tears are convulsive grief, the dissolution tears are warm and rising. This progression would be impossible to appreciate in a per-chapter review but at manuscript scale creates a three-part emotional architecture beneath the surface of the narrative.

**5. Sensory specificity sustains across the full arc.** The manuscript does not lose its grip on the physical world as it moves into more abstract territory. Ch. 11's dissolution passage is anchored by the mat's temperature against Ada's cheek, the crust on her nose, the silk in her hair. Ch. 13's clearing is rendered with the same precision as Ch. 1's trailhead: wood sorrel, wild violet, dew on petals. The style guide's demand for grounded symbolism is honored from first page to last.

**6. Backstory handled through fragments, never exposition.** Ada's past arrives in pieces: the hallway (Ch. 3), the kitchen table (Ch. 5), the phone and the door and the hand (Ch. 6), the linen and the November light and the parking-lot hand (Ch. 9). These fragments never resolve into a coherent narrative. They remain sensory impressions, not biography. This is exactly the approach the constraints and style guide demand, and it works beautifully at manuscript scale: the accumulated fragments create a mosaic that is more powerful for its incompleteness.

## What Feels Weak (Manuscript-Level Issues)

**1. The mycelium hum description cycle (priority: moderate).** The hum is introduced in Ch. 3, grows through Chs. 4-7, and dominates Chs. 8-11. This is correct. The problem is that the hum's description follows the same pattern in nearly every chapter: it is present beneath her feet, it enters her bones, it matches her heartbeat, it is a frequency she can feel but not hear. By Ch. 8, the reader has received this information five or six times. Each individual description is well-written. But the cumulative effect is of a single paragraph about the hum being re-rendered with slight variations across eight chapters. Direction: establish the hum fully in Ch. 4 and Ch. 8 (its two major escalations). In the intervening chapters, reference it briefly (a sentence, a clause) rather than re-describing it. Trust the reader's memory.

**2. Chapter openings rely too heavily on environmental description (priority: moderate).** Chs. 1, 4, 6, 7, 8, 9, and 12 open with sensory environmental detail. The style guide explicitly warns against repetitive chapter openings. The manuscript avoids identical openings (each chapter's first sentence is distinct), but the strategy of opening with a sensory-environmental observation is repeated often enough to become a default. Chs. 2 ("She was counting trees"), 5 ("The voice returned in fragments"), 10 ("Her body was the first thing that returned"), and 13 ("Her boot found grass") break this pattern effectively. Direction: revise the openings of Chs. 4, 7, and 12 to begin with action, thought, or a character beat rather than environmental description. This would give the manuscript more varied entry points.

**3. The middle chapters (6-8) could be tightened as a sequence (priority: moderate).** Individually, Chs. 6 (snake), 7 (owl), and 8 (roots) are strong. Read consecutively, they produce a slight sense of repetition in their emotional rhythm: encounter -> Ada breaks or yields -> aftermath -> walking -> next encounter. The walking-between-encounters passages, while necessary for pacing, follow a similar template: Ada walks, the forest is described, the hum is mentioned, Ada's physical state is catalogued, something shifts. These transitional passages could be varied more. In Ch. 7's approach, Ada could interact with the environment rather than simply observing it. In Ch. 8's opening, the description of the cedars could be shorter, arriving more quickly at the root-hollow sitting.

**4. Ada's physical inventory is over-detailed in some chapters (priority: low).** The continuity notes show extraordinary attention to physical state, and the prose reflects this with regular inventory passages (Ch. 4 post-crossing, Ch. 5 opening, Ch. 9 close, Ch. 12 stream). These are grounding and necessary, but the manuscript could trust the reader to carry more physical details from chapter to chapter. The devil's club scratches, for instance, are re-described in nearly identical terms across at least eight chapters. After Ch. 4's detailed examination, subsequent chapters could reference them with a single clause rather than re-describing the embedded spines and the swelling. Direction: choose three key inventory moments (Ch. 4, Ch. 9, Ch. 12) and make those the full examinations. In other chapters, use single-clause references.

**5. The dissolution passage (Ch. 11) risks being too smooth (priority: low).** This is a subtle issue. The dissolution -- Ada's ego dissolving into the mycelium network -- is the manuscript's climactic moment. The prose executes it cleanly: the present-tense shift, the merged syntax, the frog's croak as tether. But the passage is almost too graceful. The style guide asks for "controlled" use of unconventional syntax during peak psychedelic moments, and the manuscript complies. But the dissolution could benefit from one beat of friction, of resistance, of the terrifying aspect of losing the self. The terror is present in the earlier chapters but has been fully processed by Ch. 10 (spider). By Ch. 11, Ada arrives at dissolution with no resistance. This is psychologically accurate (the prior encounters have stripped her defenses), but dramatically it means the climactic moment has less tension than the encounters that preceded it. Direction: consider adding one brief beat of terror or vertigo during the dissolution (2-3 sentences, not a full sequence) -- the moment where dissolution goes past comfortable and into genuine groundlessness -- before the frog's croak pulls her back. This would make the dissolution feel more earned and more dangerous.

## Prose Notes

### Sentence Variety Across Chapters
The prose demonstrates excellent sentence variety within individual chapters. The rhythm tracks Ada's state as specified in the style guide: fragments during fear (Chs. 2-3), longer sentences during immersion (Chs. 8, 11), clean direct sentences during integration (Chs. 12-13). This is well-executed. At manuscript scale, one minor pattern emerges: the tendency to end paragraphs with a short, declarative sentence used as a punctuation beat. "She walked." "She breathed." "She followed." "She stayed." "She stood." These are individually powerful. But they appear across every chapter, and the cumulative effect is of a prose technique that becomes a signature move rather than a contextual choice. Direction: vary about 30% of these terminal declarations with differently structured endings (a dependent clause, an image, an environmental detail).

### Imagery and Specificity Patterns
The manuscript's strongest sense is touch (the texture of bark, the temperature of water, the feel of mud and mycelium). The second strongest is smell (each zone has a distinct olfactory profile). Sound is well-handled through the insect chorus, the hum, and the birdsong. Taste is used effectively but less frequently. Sight is, surprisingly, the most uneven sense -- not because it is poorly rendered, but because the bioluminescent glow descriptions become repetitive ("pale green," "blue-green," "the glow," "fungal light"). The visual palette of the forest is limited by the darkness and the single light source, which is realistic but makes the visual descriptions feel samey across the middle chapters. Direction: vary the glow descriptions by focusing on what the light reveals (texture, shape, movement) rather than on the light itself. The reader knows the forest is lit by bioluminescence by Ch. 3.

### Rhythm Consistency
Rhythm is consistent with the style guide's specifications. The key test passages -- the swarm flight in Ch. 2 (kinetic, fragmented), the wolf circling in Ch. 3 (tightening), the dissolution in Ch. 11 (opening, dissolving syntax), the dawn stream in Ch. 12 (clean, warm, direct) -- all demonstrate appropriate rhythmic variation. The manuscript passes the rhythm test.

### Redundancy at Scale
The primary redundancies at manuscript scale are: (1) the hum description cycle, (2) the "Not X" negation cascade, (3) the departure formula, (4) "something" as vague referent, and (5) the body-before-mind commentary. All are addressed above. An additional minor redundancy: the phrase "patient and old" or "old and patient" appears in at least four chapters to describe various aspects of the forest. Direction: use the phrase once (Ch. 1 is the best placement) and find alternatives elsewhere.

## Emotional Arc Assessment

The emotional arc is the manuscript's highest achievement. The progression:

1. **Unease and curiosity** (Ch. 1): perfectly calibrated. Ada's control is established as character, not just circumstance.
2. **Mounting dread** (Chs. 2-3): the swarm and wolf deliver genuine tension. The reader's impulse to flee with Ada is visceral.
3. **Deepening confrontation** (Chs. 4-7): the shift from external threat to internal exposure is the manuscript's most sophisticated structural move. By the time the owl collapses Ada's analytical distance, the reader understands that the forest's dangers are Ada's own.
4. **Crisis and surrender** (Chs. 8-9): the frog's teaching in Ch. 8 and the boar's devastation in Ch. 9 form the emotional climax. The boar lying beside Ada after the grief is the manuscript's most powerful and most surprising image. The surprise comes from the tenderness -- after the violence of the charges, the gentleness is devastating.
5. **Quiet revelation** (Chs. 10-11): the spider and the dissolution land with appropriate weight. The spider is the quietest confrontation and the dissolution is the most interior. Both are correct choices for their position in the arc.
6. **Integration and peace** (Chs. 12-13): the "deep, quiet exhale" promised in theme.md is delivered. The varied thrush bookend, the dawn stream, the stone in the pocket, the frog on its stone -- these details compose a resolution that is specific and embodied rather than abstract. The final sentence ("Ada walked into the ordinary world, carrying everything, wounded and whole and awake") earns every word.

The emotional arc delivers on the reader promise. The one risk is that the middle chapters (6-8) sustain such high emotional intensity that the reader may experience fatigue. The valley chapter (Ch. 4) provides one respite, but there is no equivalent break between the snake (Ch. 6) and the boar (Ch. 9). The transitions between these chapters are smooth but relentless. Direction: consider whether Ch. 7 or Ch. 8's opening could include a brief beat of non-confrontational beauty or rest (similar to the Mycena cascade in Ch. 2) to give the reader a micro-respite before the next intensity peak.

## Style-Guide Compliance

The manuscript demonstrates strong compliance with the style guide across all 13 chapters. Specific assessments:

- **Register (literary but accessible):** Consistently maintained. The prose is precise without being showy.
- **Density (medium-high):** Appropriate throughout. No paragraph feels like filler. Some paragraphs in Chs. 8-9 could be tightened.
- **Rhythm tracking Ada's nervous system:** Well-executed. Fear chapters are clipped. Immersion chapters breathe. Integration chapters open.
- **Dialogue style:** Compliant. Ada's speech is brief and unpolished. Animal speech is dreamlike and psychedelically inflected. No witty banter.
- **Sensory-first description:** Every scene opens through the senses. Compliant throughout.
- **Grounded symbolism:** Symbols emerge from physical details. The reader never needs to decode a symbol consciously.
- **Selective focus:** Ada's attention shifts from threats (early) to textures (late). The style guide's "shift in perception IS the arc" is realized.
- **Environmental interiority:** The forest reflects Ada's state through description, not statement.
- **Close third person locked to Ada:** Maintained throughout. No narrator editorializing, with minor exceptions noted above (Ch. 4 composure passage).
- **Past tense with present-tense exceptions:** Ch. 11's dissolution uses present tense briefly and returns to past tense. Ch. 9 has a single paragraph of present tense during the grief. Both are controlled and infrequent. Compliant.
- **Em-dashes:** None found in prose. Compliant.
- **Latin/scientific names:** None found in prose. Compliant.
- **Filter words:** Heavily reduced across all chapters per revision logs. Occasional instances remain ("She could not see its top" in Ch. 8 is technically a filter but is used for spatial information rather than perception-framing). Near-compliant.
- **Said-bookisms:** None. The animals' speech arrives without tags. Ada's rare speech uses "said" or no tag. Compliant.
- **Explaining symbolism:** Near-compliant. The spider's second line ("Every strand is something you told yourself") is the closest the manuscript comes to the prohibited "I am your shame" construction. It is saved by the web-as-self-narrative framing (the spider is not explaining the symbol; it is stating a fact about construction), but it warrants watching.

## Revision Suggestions (Prioritized, Manuscript-Level)

1. **Reduce "Not X. Not Y." negation cascades by 30-40% across the manuscript.** This is the most visible stylistic tic at manuscript scale. Keep the strongest instances. Convert weaker instances to affirmative descriptions.

2. **Compress the hum description cycle.** Establish fully in Ch. 4 and Ch. 8. In Chs. 5, 6, 7, 9, and 10, reduce to single-clause references.

3. **Vary chapter openings.** Revise openings of Chs. 4, 7, and 12 to begin with action, thought, or character rather than environmental description.

4. **Audit "something" across all chapters.** Replace approximately 30% with specific descriptors or restructured sentences.

5. **Reduce the "body knew before mind" commentary.** After Ch. 5, present somatic knowledge through action rather than narrative annotation.

6. **Vary animal departure formula.** The snake and/or wolf departures should be structurally distinct from the stag/owl/boar departures.

7. **Add one beat of friction/terror to Ch. 11's dissolution.** The dissolution is too smooth. A brief moment of genuine groundlessness would raise the stakes.

8. **Tighten transitional walking passages in Chs. 6-8.** These are well-written but follow a similar template. Introduce variety in what Ada does between encounters.

9. **Reduce paragraph-ending declarative sentences ("She walked." "She breathed.") by approximately 30%.** Replace with varied endings.

10. **Soften the spider's second line of dialogue** (Ch. 10). "Every strand is something you told yourself" approaches the prohibited zone of an animal explaining its symbolism. Consider: "Every strand. Yours." -- shorter, less expository, trusts the reader.

11. **Consider a micro-respite between Ch. 6 and Ch. 9.** The emotional intensity from the snake through the boar is sustained across four consecutive chapters. A brief moment of non-confrontational beauty (30-50 words) in Ch. 7 or Ch. 8's opening would give the reader a breath.

12. **Tighten Ch. 4's far-bank recovery passage** by approximately 200 words and **Ch. 8's root-hollow sitting passage** by approximately 300-500 words.
