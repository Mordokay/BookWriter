## Issues

### Issue 1
- **Severity:** MODERATE
- **Location:** Line 155 -- "a cricket testing the air with a single chirp before committing"
- **Problem:** The Ch. 1 Revision Log specifically documents: "'Crickets' corrected: Replaced with seasonally appropriate insect sounds (beetles, unidentified whine) for late-autumn PNW." Crickets are not seasonally appropriate for late autumn in the Pacific Northwest. Temperatures are in the upper 30s F. Crickets are cold-blooded and inactive at these temperatures.
- **Why it matters:** This was explicitly flagged and corrected in Ch. 1's revision. Reintroducing a cricket in Ch. 3 contradicts the established seasonal biology and the revision standard set for the manuscript.
- **Suggested fix:** Replace "a cricket testing the air with a single chirp" with a seasonally appropriate insect sound. Options: "a beetle clicking tentatively in the undergrowth," or "a thin whine rising from the leaf litter," or "a single click from the dark, too brief to identify."

### Issue 2
- **Severity:** MINOR
- **Location:** Line 63 -- "Hold eye contact. Something from a survival manual"
- **Problem:** The wolf in characters.md is a gray wolf (Canis lupus). Standard wolf-encounter survival guidance is actually mixed on eye contact -- some sources say maintain it (to assert dominance), others say avoid direct eye contact (it can be read as a challenge). The chapter addresses this ambiguity by having Ada try both ("She locked her gaze on the amber eyes" then "She broke eye contact"), which is effective dramatically. However, the initial instruction "Hold eye contact" is more typically associated with mountain lion encounters than wolf encounters. This is a minor naturalistic detail.
- **Why it matters:** The story's world is grounded in real biology. The survival manual reference invokes real-world knowledge. The inaccuracy is slight and the dramatic purpose (Ada trying everything, nothing working) is well-served.
- **Suggested fix:** No change required for the narrative -- Ada trying multiple strategies is the point, and the failure of each is intentional. If desired for naturalistic precision, the initial instruction could be reframed: "Eye contact. Make herself big. Something from a survival manual, arriving with the force of an instruction from outside herself." This allows both strategies to be filed together without attributing a specific technique.

### Issue 3
- **Severity:** MINOR
- **Location:** Line 29, line 31 -- Description of the clearing and forest silence
- **Problem:** The ch03 brief specifies the clearing is "perhaps twenty feet across." The chapter delivers "not wide, perhaps twenty feet across." However, the brief also describes the wolf encounter as occurring with the wolf circling at twelve to fifteen feet initially. In a twenty-foot clearing, a twelve-to-fifteen-foot orbit around a person standing roughly center would place the wolf right at the tree line. The geometry works but is tight. When the orbit tightens to five feet, the wolf is close enough, but the initial wide arc in a twenty-foot clearing means the wolf is essentially walking the perimeter. This is internally consistent but barely.
- **Why it matters:** Spatial geometry contributes to the reader's proprioceptive sense of the encounter. The tightening spiral is the encounter's central mechanic.
- **Suggested fix:** No change needed. The geometry works if Ada is near one edge (backed against a tree), giving the wolf room for its initial wider arc on the opposite side. The chapter positions Ada against a tree early, which resolves the spatial question.

### Issue 4
- **Severity:** MINOR
- **Location:** Line 87 -- "the quadriceps first, the same fine involuntary vibration that had started after the swarm"
- **Problem:** This accurately references the Ch. 2 adrenaline-withdrawal trembling. However, it then says "except now it was deeper, originating not in muscle fatigue but in something more fundamental." The running-state.md notes from Ch. 2 describe "fine involuntary muscle spasms in thighs, jaw, webbing between fingers" as adrenaline withdrawal. The chapter correctly escalates the trembling's significance. No contradiction, but worth flagging: the post-wolf trembling (line 143) is described as "a systemic shaking -- not the localized muscle spasm of adrenaline withdrawal but a full-body vibration." The term "localized muscle spasm" partially contradicts Ch. 2's running-state description, which lists trembling in thighs, jaw, AND webbing between fingers (three locations, not localized).
- **Why it matters:** Minor semantic precision. The word "localized" implies the Ch. 2 trembling was confined to one area, when it was actually distributed across multiple body areas.
- **Suggested fix:** Change "not the localized muscle spasm of adrenaline withdrawal" to "not the scattered muscle spasms of adrenaline withdrawal" or "not the involuntary muscle spasms of adrenaline withdrawal."

### Issue 5
- **Severity:** MINOR
- **Location:** Line 101 -- "each inhale catching at the top, never filling the lungs, the kind of breathing that fed the panic cycle"
- **Problem:** The phrasing "the kind of breathing that fed the panic" echoes very similar language from Ch. 2, line 109: "too fast, too shallow -- the kind of breathing that fed the panic instead of starving it." Per style-guide.md: "minimal repetition." This is a near-duplicate construction across chapters.
- **Why it matters:** The style guide warns against repetitive patterns. The reader who has just read Ch. 2's version may notice the echo. However, since it accurately describes the same physiological response, it is a borderline case.
- **Suggested fix:** Rephrase the Ch. 3 version to avoid the identical construction. For example: "each inhale catching at the top, never filling, the rhythm itself a signal to her body that the threat was still present, still here." This conveys the same feedback loop without the repeated "kind of breathing that fed the panic" formula.

### Issue 6

### Issue 7
- **Severity:** MINOR
- **Location:** Line 91 -- Wolf's eyes described as "not uniform gold but striated, the color of old sap layered over darker rings"
- **Problem:** Wolf eye color is described as "amber" throughout the chapter (lines 49, 53, 63, 93, 111, 133, 137) and as "pale yellow" in world.md and characters.md. The specific description on line 91 ("old sap layered over darker rings") is stated to be a psilocybin-amplified perception, which is consistent with the psychedelic layer distorting real features. No contradiction, but worth noting that "amber" and "pale yellow" are slightly different colors. The chapter uses "amber" exclusively while canon uses "pale yellow" or "yellow-eyed."
- **Why it matters:** The description is richer and more specific than canon, but the difference between "pale yellow" and "amber" is a tonal shift. Under psilocybin-amplified perception, color shifts are expected, so this is defensible.
- **Suggested fix:** No change required. The color shift from "pale yellow" (canon) to "amber" (as-perceived under psilocybin) is consistent with the psychedelic layer. If desired for strict canon adherence, one early reference could use "yellow" before shifting to "amber" as Ada's perception intensifies.

### Issue 8
- **Severity:** MINOR
- **Location:** Lines 79-81, 89-91, 129-131 -- Wolf dialogue delivery mechanism
- **Problem:** The wolf's first line: "The wolf's jaw did not move." The wolf's second line: "Same flat delivery. Same absence of a moving mouth." The wolf's third line: "without a mouth, without a source." This is consistent with characters.md: "A wolf 'speaks' but its jaw does not move in human ways. The words arrive in Ada's mind as the animal watches her." However, the frog's speech on line 183 uses the same delivery mechanism: "without a visible source, without a mouth that moved in any way she could verify." The parallelism is intentional (the psilocybin-mediated communication is the same for all animals/frog), but the nearly identical phrasing for both the wolf and frog within the same chapter risks making the device feel mechanical. Style-guide.md cautions against repetitive patterns.
- **Why it matters:** The speech-delivery description is used four times in one chapter (three for the wolf, one for the frog). The reader may register the mechanism as formula by the fourth instance.
- **Suggested fix:** Vary the frog's delivery description. Since the frog's communication is already established as different in tone ("low, unhurried, flat in a way that was not tonelessness but simplicity"), the delivery mechanism could be compressed: "The words arrived simply, the way the wolf's had, though the quality was nothing alike." This acknowledges the shared mechanism while avoiding the full restatement.

### Issue 9

- **Why it matters:** The physical reality of embedded spines + continuous mechanical irritation from brushing against stems should register as more than "burned." It is consistent but slightly underplayed.
- **Suggested fix:** No change required. The pain is noted. Ada's pushing through despite it serves her characterization (stubbornness, body-over-mind). If desired, one additional beat of specific pain escalation during a particularly rough salal passage could reinforce the cumulative injury.
