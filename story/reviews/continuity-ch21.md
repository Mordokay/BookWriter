## Issues Found

### Critical

**1. Nursery staffing count contradicts Ch 7.**

Ch 21 line 5 states: "The nursery's morning shift had run six caretakers when she first visited."

Ch 7 (Marsh's first nursery visit) shows only **two caretakers on shift** (line 55: "Two caretakers were working. One, a broad olive frog with steady hands... The other, younger, brown with pale yellow markings"). The olive caretaker then mentions "Rotating shifts. Twelve of us for this nursery" (line 65), meaning twelve total across all shifts, not six per morning shift.

Marsh's first visit in Ch 7 showed two caretakers working, not six. The "six" figure in Ch 21 has no established basis. It may be that Ch 21 intends the morning shift to have been six at some earlier baseline (before Ch 7), but the prose says "when she first visited," which points to Ch 7, where only two were present.

**Recommended fix:** Change "six caretakers when she first visited" to a number consistent with Ch 7 (e.g., "the nursery had carried twelve caretakers across its rotating shifts when she first visited" or adjust the math to work from a different baseline). Alternatively, revise to say Marsh's reference is to the total rotating pool thinning, not the per-shift count. The simplest fix: "The nursery's rotating pool had been twelve when she first visited. Now the morning shift had three."

**2. Timeline.md is out of sync with the actual story.**

Timeline.md Day 32 (Ch 20) states: "Noll invites the biologists to the Pools. He delivers the ultimatum: remain as honored witnesses or undergo dignified cessation."

The actual Ch 20 prose is entirely the consciousness-merging ritual. The ultimatum is planned for Ch 25 per the chapter outline ("The Administrative Courtesy").

Similarly, timeline.md Day 33-34 (Ch 21) states: "Each biologist processes the ultimatum through their frog counterpart." The actual Ch 21 contains no reference to any ultimatum. It covers nursery mortality, the archive's political management, and the biologists' debrief on institutional memory.

The timeline.md entries for Ch 20-22 appear to reflect an earlier plot structure that was revised when the chapter outline expanded. This is not a Ch 21 prose error but a canon-file staleness issue that should be corrected to prevent downstream confusion.

**Recommended fix:** Update timeline.md Days 32-35+ to match the actual chapter outline and written prose. The ultimatum moves to Ch 25's timeline slot.

### Minor

**3. "Two caretakers at the far end" (line 53) -- minor spatial ambiguity.**

Line 5 establishes three caretakers on shift: Oona, the older caretaker, and the younger one at the shallow end. Line 53 references "Two caretakers at the far end of the nursery exchanged a look." This is internally consistent if the two are the older and younger caretakers (not Oona), but the younger one was earlier placed at "the shallow end" (line 5), not "the far end." The spatial shift is minor and could reflect the nursery's layout (the shallow end may be at the far end from Oona's position), but it creates a small navigational hiccup for attentive readers.

**Recommended fix:** Consider clarifying with "The older caretaker and the younger one exchanged a look" to eliminate any ambiguity about whether additional unnamed caretakers are present.

**4. Quill calls Marsh "Ellie" (line 157) -- first occurrence in the book.**

Quill says: "This is not new information, Ellie." This is the first time anyone uses this nickname for Eleanor Marsh in the entire manuscript. While first-name usage exists elsewhere (Marsh calls Reed "Elias" in Ch 15 line 167; Vale calls Hart "Simone" in Ch 21 line 137), "Ellie" is a diminutive not established in the character sheet or any previous chapter.

This is not necessarily an error -- it could be an intentional signal of intimacy between Quill and Marsh that has deepened over the weeks. But if "Ellie" is to land as a character beat, it should either (a) be introduced slightly earlier in the book or (b) be acknowledged as notable here. If unintentional, consider replacing with "Eleanor" or "Marsh."

**5. Pem addresses Marsh as "Dr. Marsh" -- consistent but unverified pattern.**

Pem addresses Marsh as "Dr. Marsh" on lines 75 and 121. In Ch 15, Pem does not address Quill by any title (just speaks directly). However, Pem's formality is variable and the "Dr." could reflect institutional courtesy toward a less-familiar visitor (Pem has spoken with Quill more). No contradiction, but worth noting that this is a new form of address from Pem.

**6. Vale present at debrief despite being absent from Ch 20.**

Running state for Ch 20 notes "Vale -- Not present in Ch 20." He appears at the Ch 21 debrief without any mention of where he was during the merging ritual or what he has been doing. His arrival in the debrief scene is unremarkable -- he is simply there, sitting on the stone ledge. This is consistent with the ensemble approach (characters come and go), but a single grounding detail about Vale's recent activities (e.g., a reference to the governance sessions, or to his absence from the ritual) would strengthen spatial continuity.

## New Facts Established in Ch 21

1. **Elderly mortality acceleration during Pruning preparations.** Four mourning rituals in six days (baseline: ~one per 4-5 days). Deaths are among elderly, not operators. Caused by intensified breathing sessions changing water chemistry, stress, displacement, disrupted routine. Systematically underemphasized by the archive. This is established as a historical pattern, not unique to this Pruning.

2. **The archive has "different rooms."** Pem reveals the archive records both what the civilization needs to remember and what it needs to forget, in different places, accessed differently. Public memory for morale; private memory for continuity. This is Pem's most politically revealing statement in the book.

3. **Pem's framing of curation as survival.** "A civilization that remembers everything equally remembers nothing. Emphasis is a form of survival." The archive selects what to bring forward. This extends Pem's Ch 15 statement ("the story the archive tells this year") into explicit institutional policy.

4. **The older caretaker's mother died during a previous Pruning.** Not from operations but from stress in the water. Grounds the institutional pattern in personal history.

5. **Nursery thinning under Pruning mobilization.** Caretakers reassigned to Pruning support work (logistics, food preparation for staging teams). Eastern nursery reduced to three on the morning shift.

6. **Tadpole distress behavior.** Tadpoles moving in tight circles near pool edges, crowding toward walls, agitated tail-flicking. Behavioral stress response consistent with water chemistry changes.

7. **Quill calls Marsh "Ellie."** First use of this diminutive. Signals deepened intimacy within the group under pressure.

8. **Hart's institutional comparison.** "The most advanced civilization on Earth manages public health data the same way every government on ours does." NHS/CDC analogy establishes Hart's frame for the archive's political management.

9. **Vale's characterization of the archive as "narrative control."** Distinguishes his reading (a single institution with total control and no external oversight) from Hart's reading (familiar institutional behavior) and Quill's reading (the frogs believe it is necessary).

10. **Marsh's empty notebook page.** Final image: the field naturalist who has documentation that is complete and changes nothing. A shift from her earlier pattern of always writing.
