### Minor

1. **Operator casualty count discrepancy (seventeen vs. three).**
   Talla states: "The coordination office reports seventeen operator casualties across two displacement waves." In Ch 21 (line 85), Pem tells Marsh: "The displacement teams have lost three operators to coherence failure since the preparations began." The Ch 18 timeline entry and chapter outline reference "three operators pulled unconscious; one does not recover." The gap between three coherence failures and seventeen casualties is large. It is defensible if "casualties" in Talla's usage means all operators injured, incapacitated, or pulled unconscious (a broader category than Pem's "coherence failure," which implies permanent loss), and the number seventeen accumulates across the full multi-day Pruning operation including drilling phases described in Ch 19. But Talla uses the word "casualties" where Pem used "coherence failure," and the reader has no prior reference to seventeen as a number. Recommend either: (a) establishing the broader casualty count earlier in Ch 19 or Ch 21 (e.g., "seventeen operators pulled from the pools, three of whom did not recover"), or (b) adjusting Talla's line to use a figure that connects more explicitly to the three permanent losses Pem disclosed, e.g., "three operators who did not return, and fourteen more who required medical intervention."

2. **Timeline.md describes Ch 22 as an epilogue chapter.**
   Timeline.md entry for "Day 35 and beyond (Ch. 22)" describes weeks passing and the biologists being absorbed into frog life -- content that belongs to Ch 27 in the chapter outline. The chapter as written correctly follows the chapter-outline.md (the authoritative structural plan), not the timeline. This is a pre-existing timeline.md discrepancy already flagged in the continuity-ch17 review. It does not affect the chapter prose, but timeline.md should be updated to reflect the actual chapter content: Ch 22 = Talla's Wager (Day 34-35), political peak of Act 2B.

3. **Noll's speech register slightly more direct than character sheet.**
   Noll's character sheet specifies he never uses "I decide" and prefers "it was felt" or "the consensus emerged." In Ch 22, Noll uses first-person phrasing more directly: "I come with a request," "I apologize for the hour," "I do not press. I ask." This is a minor note rather than a contradiction -- the scene is a private visit to five humans in a cramped room, not a formal assembly, and a slightly more personal register is justifiable. But it is worth noting that Noll's signature deflective constructions ("the general feeling," "it was felt," "one might observe") are absent from this appearance. Consider adding one or two of these constructions to his dialogue without losing the intimate tone.

4. **Mourning ritual numbers: compatible but reader may stumble.**
   Ch 21 (Marsh's count): "four mourning rituals in the past six days" with a baseline of "one every four or five days." Ch 22 (Talla's figure): "seven mourning rituals in twelve days against a baseline of roughly one per week." These are mathematically compatible -- the twelve-day window is broader than Marsh's six-day window, and "roughly one per week" is consistent with "one every four or five days" as approximate baselines. But the reader encounters two different data sets (4-in-6 vs. 7-in-12) with slightly different baselines in consecutive chapters. Talla sourcing her data to Oona Reedmoss provides a clear provenance distinct from Marsh's personal count, which helps. No change strictly required, but the reader's math sense may briefly snag.

## New Facts Established in Ch 22

1. **Seventeen operator casualties across two displacement waves** -- the coordination office's public figure. This is the first time a specific cumulative operator casualty count is stated in dialogue. (See Minor Issue 1 regarding reconciliation with Ch 21's "three coherence failures.")

2. **Talla's public refusal to attend breathing circles** -- delivered at a communal meal, loudly enough for half the residential warrens to hear. This is described as the most overtly political act in the settlement's recent memory. Her line: "If my absence endangers the Pruning, then the Pruning has a design problem, not a participation problem."

3. **Noll visits the biologists' quarters for the first time.** He is accompanied by Pem. This is the first time Noll has come to them rather than summoning or encountering them in communal spaces.

4. **Noll asks the biologists to speak publicly in support of community cohesion.** The request is framed as invitation, not instruction. All five refuse.

5. **Five distinct refusals documented:** Vale (immediate, clipped "No"), Marsh (gentle, principled), Reed (uncertain, shakes head without speaking), Hart (short, hard laugh), Quill (silence -- uninterpreted, taken by Noll without confirmation).

6. **Hart predicted this request in her field notes three weeks ago.** The entry has not been shown to anyone. This establishes Hart's pattern-recognition as a recurring capability and plants a potential callback for later chapters.

7. **Pem explicitly catalogues all five humans' reactions** before leaving. The archival function is confirmed as operating in real time on the biologists themselves -- they are not just witnesses but subjects of the archive.

8. **A likely informant is present at Talla's Third Pool meeting** -- a frog who entered without speaking, whose colors were flat and controlled, and whose presence will likely be reported.

9. **The communal meal's visual ripple response to Talla's refusal** -- colors shifting in a wave across forty to fifty frogs, conversations stopping and restarting in clusters. This is the first depiction of a large-scale involuntary chromatic event in a domestic (non-ritual) setting.

10. **Hart's self-awareness about her own investment:** She has been "waiting for this since the first pool tour" and recognizes the system's extension to the humans as predictable institutional behavior. Her laugh is diagnostic, not emotional.

11. **Noll's phrase: "I do not press. I ask. The difference matters to us."** Establishes Noll's framing of the request-vs-instruction distinction as culturally significant to the Threnn, consistent with the "voluntary-mandatory" paradox running through the book.

## Summary

Chapter 22 is factually sound and well-grounded in established canon. Character voices, physical descriptions, political dynamics, and world-building details are consistent with prior chapters and source documents. The only item approaching a substantive concern is the seventeen-operator-casualty figure, which represents a significant escalation from the three coherence failures cited by Pem in Ch 21 and should be reconciled or contextualized. The timeline.md discrepancy is pre-existing and does not affect the chapter itself. Noll's slightly more direct speech register is a stylistic note worth considering but does not break character. The mourning-ritual numbers are compatible across chapters but may create a brief reader hesitation.
