## Issues

### Issue 1: Stone size inconsistency between planting and payoff
- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Location:** Ch. 6, line 129 (planting) vs. Ch. 13, line 63 (payoff)
- **Problem:** In Ch. 6, the stone is described as "the size of an apricot pit." In Ch. 13, it is described as "the size of a large almond." An apricot pit and a large almond are similar in size but not identical -- an apricot pit is roughly twice the volume of an almond. More importantly, an apricot pit is round and ridged, while an almond is elongated and smooth. The Ch. 13 description ("smooth, oval") matches "almond" but not "apricot pit" (which would be rough-surfaced and more spherical).
- **Why it matters:** This is the payoff of a thread that has been carefully maintained across seven chapters. A careful reader who remembers the Ch. 6 description will notice the mismatch. It undermines the precision that makes the rest of the object-tracking so effective.
- **Suggested fix:** Harmonize the two descriptions. Either change Ch. 6 to "the size of a large almond" (matching Ch. 13's "smooth, oval" description) or change Ch. 13 to "the size of an apricot pit" (matching Ch. 6). The almond comparison works better with the Ch. 13 description of "smooth, oval, gray with a vein of white quartz," so changing Ch. 6 is the cleaner fix.

### Issue 2: "Fourteen hours ago" in Ch. 13 may overstate duration
- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Location:** Ch. 13, line 41
- **Problem:** Ada thinks of the nurse log frog as "A lifetime ago. Fourteen hours ago." The timeline in timeline.md maps the journey from approximately 5:00 PM (ingestion) to 7:00 AM (emergence), which is 14 hours. However, the frog's first appearance was approximately 5:00-5:30 PM (during onset, before the trip fully began), and Ch. 13 is mapped to 6:15-7:00 AM. This puts the interval at 12.5-14 hours, so "fourteen hours" is at the outer edge of accuracy. The bigger concern is that Ada has no watch and the psilocybin has distorted her time perception all night. Her ability to produce a precise figure like "fourteen hours" is inconsistent with the established rule that she cannot track time.
- **Why it matters:** The story has carefully maintained temporal ambiguity throughout. Ada never knows what time it is. Producing a specific duration undermines this. The line works emotionally ("A lifetime ago. Fourteen hours ago.") but the specificity of "fourteen" clashes with the temporal framework.
- **Suggested fix:** Replace "Fourteen hours ago" with something vaguer that preserves the emotional contrast: "A lifetime ago. A single night ago." or "A lifetime ago. Hours ago." Either preserves the "objective brevity vs. subjective eternity" juxtaposition without requiring Ada to have time-tracking capability she has been denied all night.

### Issue 3: Owl species terminology -- "spotted owl" vs. encounter description
- **Severity:** LOW
- **Location:** Ch. 12, line 89 vs. Ch. 7 prose
- **Problem:** Ch. 12 refers to "the spotted owl of the encounter," distinguishing it from a barred owl heard at dawn. The Ch. 7 continuity notes correctly identify the species as "Northern spotted owl (Strix occidentalis caurina)." However, in the Ch. 7 prose itself, the owl is never explicitly named as a spotted owl -- it is described by physical details (dark brown plumage, white spots, round facial disc) but the species name does not appear in the narrative text. This means the Ch. 12 reference to "the spotted owl of the encounter" introduces a species name that Ada never used or thought during the actual encounter. This is a minor issue because the prose-level omission of the name in Ch. 7 is a deliberate stylistic choice (no Latin names in prose per humanizer rules), and "spotted owl" is a common name, not technical jargon.
- **Why it matters:** Very minor. The average reader will not notice. A careful reader might wonder how Ada knows it was a spotted owl if she never identified it during the encounter, but this is easily attributable to her general ecological knowledge (established in Ch. 1).
- **Suggested fix:** No change required. If desired, a small fix: change Ch. 12's reference to "Not the owl of the encounter" (dropping the species name).

### Issue 4: Rain jacket zipper state -- minor tracking gap
- **Severity:** LOW
- **Location:** Ch. 8 through Ch. 13
- **Problem:** In Ch. 4, Ada zips the rain jacket "to her chin." In Ch. 8, she "unzips the jacket partway" (in response to Zone 4's warmth). In Ch. 9, the jacket is described as "twisted, half-unzipped" after the boar encounter. In Ch. 10 and 11, the jacket is described in continuity notes as still in this state. In Ch. 12, Ada "tugged the zipper up against the cold and the teeth caught and held partway" (web silk clogging the zipper). In Ch. 13, the zipper is "jammed halfway." This tracking is quite good. However, the transition from "unzipped partway" (Ch. 8) through the boar encounter to "twisted, half-unzipped" (Ch. 9) happens off-screen -- there is no moment in the Ch. 9 prose where the jacket's state changes. The "twisted" state implies physical disruption during the boar encounter, but the encounter's prose focuses on emotional experience and does not describe the jacket shifting.
- **Why it matters:** Minimal. The reader can infer that the jacket twisted during the physical events of the boar encounter (Ada on the ground, boar lying beside her). This is a tracking note rather than a true error.
- **Suggested fix:** No change required. The inference is natural.

### Issue 5: Backpack position -- left shoulder vs. both shoulders

- **Why it matters:** Minimal. The shift is consistent once it occurs and can be read as character-driven. No fix needed.
- **Suggested fix:** None required. The detail works as implicit characterization.

## Character Consistency Notes

- **Ada's emotional progression never resets.** Each chapter builds on the previous state. The one moment where the composure "snaps back" (Ch. 4, after the crossing) is explicitly designed and narrated as the habitual default reasserting itself, setting up the stag encounter. This is the only regression, and it is structurally intentional and short-lived.

- **Ada's relationship to the forest evolves irreversibly.** She tries to escape in Ch. 1-3, endures in Ch. 4-5, is forced into stillness in Ch. 6-7, stops trying to escape in Ch. 8 (midpoint reversal), and moves inward willingly in Ch. 9-11. She never reverts to "get me out of here" after Ch. 8. This is correct per the plot design.

- **The frog's voice is consistent.** Short, grounded sentences. Never didactic. "This way" (Ch. 3). "You're still here" / "That's the hardest one to look at" (Ch. 6). "Where do you want to go?" (Ch. 8). "There you are" (Ch. 11). "Almost morning" (Ch. 12). The frog does not speak in Ch. 1 (observation only), Ch. 4 (sound only), Ch. 10 (absent), or Ch. 13 (trills once, no words). The voice is consistent in register and never crosses into lecturing or exposition.

- **Animal-demon speech patterns are distinct.** Wolf: blunt, tired, flat ("You've been running your whole life" / "I'm tired too"). Stag: cold, formal, precise ("Is this who you are? Or who you've practiced being?"). Snake: intimate, close, accusatory ("You know what you did" / "You could have stayed"). Owl: clinical, relentless, question-based. Boar: pre-verbal (grief sounds, not words). Spider: quiet, factual, measured ("You built this" / "You can stop spinning. You won't disappear."). Each voice is distinct and consistent with its psychological wound.

- **No secondary human characters appear in the forest.** This constraint is maintained perfectly across all 13 chapters. Humans exist only in Ada's memory fragments: the hallway/childhood scanning (Ch. 3), the kitchen table / "Are you okay?" (Ch. 5), the phone not answered / the door walked through / the hand (Ch. 6, echoed in Ch. 11). No human is named or given sustained dialogue.

## World Rule Notes

- **The forest never speaks in human language.** This rule is maintained. All speech comes from animals (wolf, stag, snake, owl, boar sounds, spider, frog). The forest communicates through physical response only (paths shifting, bioluminescence, temperature, spatial rearrangement).

- **Ada cannot die or be physically killed.** Maintained. She is scratched, muddied, cold, exhausted, and dehydrated, but never in mortal danger.

- **Ada cannot be rescued.** Maintained. No other humans enter the forest. The journey is hers alone.

- **Animals speak under the psychedelic layer.** The mechanism is consistent: jaws do not move in human ways; words arrive in the space between the creature's presence and Ada's perception. This is maintained for the wolf (Ch. 3), stag (Ch. 5), snake (Ch. 6), owl (Ch. 7), spider (Ch. 10), and frog (Ch. 3, 6, 8, 11, 12). The boar does not speak (Ch. 9), which is correct -- grief is pre-verbal.

- **The forest responds to Ada's emotional state through physical changes.** This is shown consistently: trees lean in / canopy closes during fear (Ch. 2-3), paths close during attempted control (Ch. 1-2), clearings open for confrontation (Ch. 5-7), the mycelium hum rises during surrender (Ch. 8-11). The response patterns match the table in world.md.

- **The psychedelic layer warps perception but starts from real biology.** Every perceptual distortion in the manuscript is grounded in documented psilocybin effects: surfaces breathing, color saturation, synesthesia, ego dissolution, time distortion, pattern recognition amplification. No effect violates real phenomenology.

- **Physical injuries accumulate and persist.** Verified across all 13 chapters (see Confirmed Strengths #1).

- **No deus ex machina.** The backpack recovery in Ch. 11 is the closest the story comes to this, and it is handled well by invoking the established world-rule of spatial rearrangement (demonstrated in Ch. 1-2).
