## Issues

### Issue 1
- **Severity:** low
- **Location:** Line 27 -- "waxing gibbous"
- **Problem:** Ada identifies the moon as "waxing gibbous" and notes it is visible through a "narrow gap in the canopy." In Ch. 1, she "checked" the moon phase (waxing gibbous) before the trip. This is consistent. However, the ch02 brief specifies that the canopy is closing as she moves deeper and that moonlight arrives only in "rare shafts." The chapter handles this correctly -- Ada finds one gap, looks through it, then the gap "seals" while she is looking. No issue with the moon being briefly visible. The brief also says "moon (waxing gibbous) may be visible through occasional canopy gaps." This is fine.
- **Why it matters:** A false alarm on review. No fix needed.
- **Suggested fix:** None. Confirmed consistent.

### Issue 2
- **Severity:** low
- **Location:** Line 49 -- "the orange-green foxfire of the Omphalotus"
- **Problem:** The chapter names Omphalotus as "foxfire" and contrasts it with the Mycena chlorophos of Zone 2. In Ch. 1, Omphalotus olearius was described as "jack-o'-lantern mushrooms" with a glow "the color of an ember seen through green glass." The Ch. 2 reference to "foxfire of the Omphalotus" introduces the term "foxfire" for the first time in the prose. Technically, "foxfire" is a general term for bioluminescent fungi and is most commonly associated with Armillaria and Panellus species, not Omphalotus. World.md uses "foxfire" in the Zone 2 description ("foxfire on rotting wood"), not in the Zone 1 Omphalotus entry.
- **Why it matters:** Minor biological terminology issue. A reader with mycological knowledge might note that foxfire is not the standard term for Omphalotus bioluminescence. However, the term is used colloquially for any glowing fungal wood, so this is defensible.
- **Suggested fix:** Consider changing "the orange-green foxfire of the Omphalotus" to "the orange-green glow of the Omphalotus" to avoid the slight terminological imprecision. Or leave it -- the colloquial usage is common enough.

### Issue 3
- **Severity:** low
- **Location:** Line 63 -- mosquitoes described as landing and not biting
- **Problem:** Late autumn (late October/November) in the Pacific Northwest is very late for active mosquitoes. Most mosquito species in PNW old-growth are inactive by this time of year. Crane flies and moths are more seasonally plausible. Ch. 1's revision log explicitly corrected "crickets" to "seasonally appropriate insect sounds (beetles, unidentified whine) for late-autumn PNW," showing awareness of seasonal accuracy. Mosquitoes in the hundreds at this time of year would be unusual.
- **Why it matters:** The story has established a commitment to ecological accuracy (real species, real behaviors). Ch. 1 already corrected a seasonal insect error. A swarm of hundreds of mosquitoes in late autumn is a stretch. However, the story's structural ambiguity allows for the psilocybin layer amplifying or distorting what is actually present -- a few mosquitoes could feel like hundreds under the psychedelic layer.
- **Suggested fix:** This is a judgment call. The swarm is also a psychedelic/psychological phenomenon, not purely ecological. If ecological accuracy is prioritized, reduce the mosquito presence and increase moths, crane flies, and beetles (all more seasonally plausible). If the psychedelic layer is prioritized, the current treatment is defensible -- Ada perceives a swarm that may or may not match biological reality. Consider adding a brief sensory note that the mosquitoes' presence feels "wrong" or "impossible" to deepen the ambiguity, or lean into moths and beetles as the dominant species with mosquitoes as a minor, unsettling element.
